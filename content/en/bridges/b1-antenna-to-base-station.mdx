<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why this bridge?

You've designed a 64-element phased array with 23 dBi gain, HPBW of 12°, and cross-pol isolation >25 dB. Beautiful RF work. But where does that antenna connect? What signals does it carry? Why does the network architect care about your beamwidth?

This bridge connects RF antenna engineering to 5G network architecture. By the end, you'll see the gNB (5G base station) through both lenses — and understand why fronthaul option 7.2x looks the way it does.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

An antenna is the physical interface between guided waves (cables, waveguides) and radiated waves (free space). A gNB is the logical entity that terminates the radio protocol stack.

They meet at the **radio unit (RU)** — where digital baseband signals become analog RF, and where your antenna pattern matters.

The split between "RF stuff" and "network stuff" isn't a clean line. Beamforming decisions affect MAC scheduling. Antenna correlation affects MIMO rank. Interference patterns affect handover. Understanding both sides makes you dangerous (in a good way).

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## The gNB architecture

### Functional split

3GPP defines the gNB as three logical components:

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                     gNB                         │
│  ┌──────────┐   ┌──────────┐   ┌──────────┐   │
│  │   CU     │   │   DU     │   │   RU     │   │
│  │ (Central)│   │ (Distrib)│   │ (Radio)  │   │
│  └────┬─────┘   └────┬─────┘   └────┬─────┘   │
│       │              │              │         │
│       └──────F1──────┘              │         │
│                      └────Fronthaul─┘         │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                                      │
                                   Antenna
```

**CU (Central Unit):** RRC, PDCP — control plane, user plane termination.

**DU (Distributed Unit):** RLC, MAC, upper PHY — scheduling, HARQ.

**RU (Radio Unit):** Lower PHY, RF front-end — beamforming, D/A conversion, amplification.

### Where antennas live

The antenna is physically part of (or connected to) the RU. Your antenna design directly affects:

- **Beamforming weights** in the RU
- **Antenna port mapping** visible to the DU
- **CSI-RS patterns** that the UE measures
- **Coverage footprint** that the CU's RRM uses for mobility

### Option 7.2x: The RF-network boundary

The most common fronthaul split in 5G RAN:

| Split point | In DU | In RU |
|-------------|-------|-------|
| Upper PHY | Resource mapping, precoding | iFFT, cyclic prefix, RF |
| Beamforming | Weights computed | Weights applied |
| Data format | IQ samples (frequency domain) | IQ samples (time domain) → RF |

**Why 7.2x?** It moves beamforming to the RU — where your antenna is. The RU applies beamforming weights without sending 64 antenna streams over fronthaul. Fronthaul carries layer-mapped streams (≤8), not antenna ports (≤64).

This is where antenna design meets network architecture.

## RF parameters that matter to the network

### Antenna gain and coverage

Your 23 dBi gain enters the link budget:

$$\text{MAPL} = P_{tx} + G_{tx} + G_{rx} - L_{body} - L_{penetration} - M_{fade} - M_{interference}$$

The CU uses coverage predictions to decide:
- Cell radius (determines inter-site distance)
- Handover thresholds (depends on overlap)
- Load balancing (steers traffic to less loaded cells)

### Beamwidth and beam management

Your 12° HPBW determines:
- Number of SSB beams to cover the sector
- Beam sweeping periodicity
- Beam failure detection threshold

**Example:** 120° sector with 12° beams → 10-12 SSB beams. Each beam covers ~10° azimuth × ~10° elevation.

The MAC scheduler uses beam correspondence to:
- Schedule PDCCH in the correct beam
- Trigger beam failure recovery
- Handover between beams (L1 mobility) or cells (L3 mobility)

### Polarization and MIMO rank

Your cross-pol antenna enables:
- Rank-2 transmission (dual-polarized MIMO)
- Polarization diversity against fading
- 4 or 8 antenna ports for SU-MIMO

The DU's scheduler adapts rank based on channel conditions. If your antenna's cross-pol isolation is poor (&lt;20 dB), rank-2 becomes unreliable.

### Array correlation and channel capacity

Dense antenna spacing (λ/2) → low correlation → high capacity.
Wide antenna spacing (>λ) → beam steering range but correlation issues.

The CU doesn't directly see this — but it shows up in throughput statistics and CQI reports.

## The signal chain: RF → Network

### Downlink (gNB → UE)

1. **CU:** Receives IP packets, maps to DRBs, applies PDCP.
2. **DU:** Segments into RLC PDUs, schedules on MAC, maps to resource elements, generates precoding weights.
3. **RU:** Applies beamforming (from precoding matrix), performs iFFT, adds CP, upconverts to RF.
4. **Antenna:** Radiates the signal with your designed pattern.
5. **UE:** Receives, performs channel estimation, reports CSI back.

### Uplink (UE → gNB)

1. **UE:** Transmits using allocated resources, applies precoding.
2. **Antenna:** Receives with pattern gain, feeds to RU.
3. **RU:** Amplifies (LNA), downconverts, ADC, removes CP, FFT, applies combining weights.
4. **DU:** Decodes, reassembles, delivers to CU.
5. **CU:** Terminates PDCP, delivers to core.

### Where your antenna design appears

| Parameter | Appears in... |
|-----------|---------------|
| Gain | Link budget, coverage prediction |
| Pattern | Beam correspondence, interference |
| Polarization | MIMO rank, diversity |
| Bandwidth | Supported bands, carrier aggregation |
| EIRP | Regulatory compliance, cell planning |

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Massive MIMO panel for TDD**

64 elements (8H × 8V), dual-pol → 128 antenna elements.
Beamforming in RU → 8 layer-mapped streams over fronthaul (not 128).

Antenna engineer's view:
- Design for 3.5 GHz band
- λ/2 spacing (42.8 mm)
- Per-element EIRP = +26 dBm
- Array gain = 10log₁₀(64) = 18 dBi

Network architect's view:
- 8 SSB beams cover 120° sector
- 64T64R capable of rank-8 SU-MIMO
- Fronthaul capacity: ~25 Gbps (Option 7.2x)
- Supports 100 MHz NR carrier

**Example: Why fronthaul bandwidth matters**

If RU sends raw time-domain samples:
- 100 MHz × 30.72 Msps × 2 (I/Q) × 16 bits × 64 antennas = **196 Gbps**

With Option 7.2x (frequency domain, after beamforming):
- 8 layers × ~25 Gbps = **~25 Gbps**

Your antenna design (64 elements) would be impractical without functional split.

**Example: Antenna tilt and coverage**

Mechanical downtilt: 6° (fixed).
Electrical downtilt: 0-15° (configurable via RU).

CU's RRM uses electrical tilt for:
- Coverage optimization
- Interference mitigation
- Load balancing between cells

Antenna engineer sets mechanical tilt at installation; network optimizes electrical tilt continuously.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Designing antennas without understanding the split.** Your antenna doesn't exist in isolation — it's part of a fronthaul-connected RU. Impedance, connectors, and timing matter.

2. **Ignoring calibration.** Massive MIMO requires per-element calibration for coherent beamforming. The RU performs this, but antenna phase/amplitude stability is your responsibility.

3. **Forgetting about interference.** Your beautiful beam still creates interference in neighboring cells. The CU's ICIC features depend on predictable patterns.

4. **Treating beamforming as pure RF.** Beamforming weights come from the DU's CSI feedback processing. The RU applies them. The antenna shapes them. All three must align.

5. **Ignoring thermal constraints.** 64 elements × 26 dBm each = serious heat. Power amplifier design affects both RF performance and network availability.
