<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why phasors?

Every RF signal is a sinusoid — or a sum of sinusoids. Analyzing sinusoids directly means wrestling with trigonometric identities. Phasors replace sines and cosines with complex exponentials, turning differentiation into multiplication and addition of waves into addition of vectors.

The entire field of AC circuit analysis, filter design, and antenna impedance matching rests on this one trick: represent sinusoids as rotating complex numbers.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A sinusoid like $v(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t + \phi)$ oscillates endlessly. But its essential information is just two numbers: amplitude $V_0$ and phase $\phi$. A phasor captures exactly that: a complex number $\tilde{V} = V_0 e^{j\phi}$.

Picture a rotating arrow (vector) in the complex plane. Its length is the amplitude; its angle is the phase. As time advances, the arrow spins at angular frequency $\omega$. The projection onto the real axis traces out the cosine wave.

Adding two sinusoids of the same frequency? Just add their phasors as complex numbers. The arrow lengths and angles combine naturally — no trig identities needed.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Euler's formula

The bridge between exponentials and sinusoids:

$$e^{j\theta} = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta$$

Therefore:
$$\cos\theta = \text{Re}(e^{j\theta}), \quad \sin\theta = \text{Im}(e^{j\theta})$$

### Phasor representation

For a sinusoidal signal:
$$v(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t + \phi)$$

The phasor is:
$$\tilde{V} = V_0 e^{j\phi} = V_0 \angle \phi$$

The full time-domain signal is recovered by:
$$v(t) = \text{Re}(\tilde{V} e^{j\omega t})$$

### Operations become simple

| Time domain | Phasor domain |
|-------------|---------------|
| $\frac{d}{dt}$ | $\times j\omega$ |
| $\int dt$ | $\div j\omega$ |
| Add same-frequency sinusoids | Add complex numbers |

### Rectangular vs. polar form

- **Rectangular:** $\tilde{V} = a + jb$ (good for addition)
- **Polar:** $\tilde{V} = V_0 \angle \phi$ or $V_0 e^{j\phi}$ (good for multiplication)

Convert: $V_0 = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$, $\phi = \arctan(b/a)$

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Adding two voltages**

Two voltage sources in series:
- $v_1(t) = 10\cos(\omega t)$ → $\tilde{V}_1 = 10\angle 0°$
- $v_2(t) = 5\cos(\omega t + 90°)$ → $\tilde{V}_2 = 5\angle 90° = 5j$

Sum of phasors:
$$\tilde{V}_\text{total} = 10 + 5j$$

Magnitude: $\sqrt{10^2 + 5^2} = 11.18$ V
Phase: $\arctan(5/10) = 26.6°$

Result: $v_\text{total}(t) = 11.18\cos(\omega t + 26.6°)$

**Example: Capacitor voltage-current relationship**

For a capacitor: $i = C\frac{dv}{dt}$

In phasor form: $\tilde{I} = j\omega C \tilde{V}$

Current leads voltage by 90° — the factor of $j$ rotates the phasor.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Same frequency only.** Phasor addition only works for sinusoids at the same frequency. Mixing 1 kHz and 2 kHz signals requires separate phasor analysis or Fourier methods.

2. **$j$ vs. $i$.** Engineers use $j = \sqrt{-1}$ because $i$ denotes current. Physicists use $i$. Don't mix conventions.

3. **Peak vs. RMS.** A phasor can represent peak amplitude or RMS amplitude — be consistent. Power calculations typically use RMS phasors.

4. **Phase reference.** Phasors are relative. Saying "$\tilde{V} = 10\angle 30°$" only makes sense if you define what $0°$ means (usually the source or a reference signal).

5. **DC signals.** A DC signal has $\omega = 0$ and no phase — it's just a real constant. Phasor analysis doesn't add value for DC.

</section>
