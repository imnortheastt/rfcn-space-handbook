## Why S-parameters?

At RF frequencies, you can't easily measure voltage and current — the wavelengths are too short and the probe itself disturbs the circuit. Instead, we measure **traveling waves**: what goes in, what bounces back, what passes through.

S-parameters (scattering parameters) describe an RF network entirely in terms of these waves. Every amplifier, filter, cable, and antenna is characterized by its S-parameters. Network analyzers measure them directly. If impedance is the language of lumped circuits, S-parameters are the language of distributed RF systems.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine throwing a ball at a wall. Some energy bounces back (reflection); the rest passes through or is absorbed. The reflection coefficient $\Gamma$ is the ratio of the reflected wave to the incident wave.

For a two-port network (like an amplifier with input and output):

- $S_{11}$: reflection at port 1 (input match)
- $S_{21}$: transmission from port 1 to port 2 (gain or loss)
- $S_{12}$: transmission from port 2 to port 1 (reverse isolation)
- $S_{22}$: reflection at port 2 (output match)

A perfect amplifier has $S_{11} = S_{22} = 0$ (no reflections) and large $|S_{21}|$ (high gain).

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Reflection coefficient

For a load $Z_L$ on a line with characteristic impedance $Z_0$:

$$\Gamma = \frac{Z_L - Z_0}{Z_L + Z_0}$$

- $\Gamma = 0$: matched load ($Z_L = Z_0$), no reflection
- $|\Gamma| = 1$: total reflection (open or short)
- $0 < |\Gamma| < 1$: partial reflection

### Return loss and VSWR

**Return loss** (in dB):
$$\text{RL} = -20\log_{10}|\Gamma|$$

Higher return loss = better match. A return loss of 20 dB means only 1% of power reflects.

**VSWR**:
$$\text{VSWR} = \frac{1 + |\Gamma|}{1 - |\Gamma|}$$

VSWR of 1:1 is perfect; 2:1 is acceptable for many systems; 3:1 is poor.

### S-parameter matrix

For an N-port network, S-parameters form an N×N matrix. For two ports:

$$\begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} S_{11} & S_{12} \\ S_{21} & S_{22} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

where $a_i$ = incident wave at port $i$, $b_i$ = reflected wave at port $i$.

### Common S-parameter metrics

| Parameter | Meaning | Ideal for amplifier |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|
| $S_{11}$ | Input reflection | $\to 0$ (good match) |
| $S_{21}$ | Forward gain | Large (20–30 dB) |
| $S_{12}$ | Reverse isolation | $\to 0$ (−30 dB or less) |
| $S_{22}$ | Output reflection | $\to 0$ (good match) |

### Converting between $Z$ and $\Gamma$

$$\Gamma = \frac{Z - Z_0}{Z + Z_0}, \quad Z = Z_0\frac{1 + \Gamma}{1 - \Gamma}$$

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Antenna return loss**

An antenna has $S_{11} = -15$ dB at 2.4 GHz.

Return loss: 15 dB
$|\Gamma| = 10^{-15/20} = 0.178$
Reflected power: $|\Gamma|^2 = 3.2\%$
VSWR: $(1 + 0.178)/(1 - 0.178) = 1.43$

This is acceptable for most applications.

**Example: Amplifier gain**

An LNA has $S_{21} = 18$ dB. This means the output power is $10^{18/10} = 63$ times the input power (in linear scale).

If $S_{12} = -25$ dB, the reverse isolation is good — signal leaking backward is only $0.3\%$ of the forward signal.

**Example: Cable loss**

A coaxial cable has $S_{21} = -3$ dB at 1 GHz. Half the input power reaches the output; half is lost to attenuation.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Reference impedance.** S-parameters are defined relative to $Z_0$ (usually 50 Ω). The same device has different S-parameters in a 75 Ω system.

2. **Frequency dependence.** S-parameters vary with frequency. A filter that passes 1 GHz ($S_{21} \approx 0$ dB) may block 2 GHz ($S_{21} = -40$ dB). Always check the frequency.

3. **Magnitude vs. complex value.** $|S_{11}| = 0.2$ tells you the reflection magnitude. The full complex $S_{11}$ tells you the phase too — needed for matching network design.

4. **Active vs. passive.** Passive devices have $|S_{21}| \leq 1$ (can't create power). Amplifiers have $|S_{21}| > 1$. Oscillators can have $|S_{11}| > 1$ under certain conditions.

5. **Measurement conditions.** S-parameters assume all other ports are terminated in $Z_0$. If your actual circuit has different terminations, the behavior changes.

</section>
