## Why you can't use a voltmeter at RF

At 1 kHz, probing a circuit with a voltmeter is harmless — the probe wires are a tiny fraction of a wavelength and barely disturb anything. At 10 GHz, the same probe wire is roughly 3 cm long — comparable to or longer than the signal wavelength of 3 cm. The probe itself becomes an antenna, radiating energy and completely distorting the circuit's behavior.

This is not a minor inconvenience. It is fundamental: at RF and microwave frequencies, the classical distinction between "component" and "measurement instrument" breaks down. Measurement requires a different paradigm.

The solution is to characterize circuits by how they interact with traveling waves — power flowing in and power bouncing back or passing through. **S-parameters** (scattering parameters) formalize this. Instead of measuring voltage and current, a network analyzer injects a calibrated traveling wave at each port and measures the waves that emerge. The ratio of output wave to input wave at each port combination is an S-parameter.

S-parameters work at any frequency. They are the universal language of RF circuit characterization.

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Picture throwing a ball at different surfaces:

- A soft foam wall: ball barely bounces back, mostly absorbed. Reflection is small.
- A concrete wall: ball bounces almost completely back. Reflection is large.
- An open doorway: ball passes through with nothing bouncing back. Perfect transmission, zero reflection.

The **reflection coefficient** $\Gamma$ is the ratio of the "bounced-back" wave amplitude to the incident wave amplitude. $|\Gamma| = 0$ means no reflection (perfect match). $|\Gamma| = 1$ means total reflection (open or short circuit).

For a two-port network (any device with an input and an output — amplifier, filter, cable):

- **$S_{11}$** = "How much bounces back at port 1?" = input reflection coefficient. Tells you the input match.
- **$S_{21}$** = "How much passes from port 1 to port 2?" = forward transmission. Tells you the gain (or loss).
- **$S_{12}$** = "How much passes from port 2 to port 1?" = reverse transmission. Tells you isolation.
- **$S_{22}$** = "How much bounces back at port 2?" = output reflection coefficient. Tells you the output match.

For a cloud engineer analogy: $S_{11}$ is the HTTP response code for "request rejected at the front door." $S_{21}$ is the successful throughput from input to output. $S_{12}$ is reverse traffic leaking from output back to input. An ideal component has $S_{11} = S_{22} = 0$ (no rejections) and large $|S_{21}|$ (high throughput).

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Reflection coefficient definition

For a load impedance $Z_L$ connected to a transmission line with characteristic impedance $Z_0$:

$$\Gamma = \frac{Z_L - Z_0}{Z_L + Z_0}$$

Properties:
- $\Gamma = 0$: $Z_L = Z_0$ — perfectly matched, no reflection
- $\Gamma = -1$: $Z_L = 0$ — short circuit, total reflection (phase reversed)
- $\Gamma = +1$: $Z_L \to \infty$ — open circuit, total reflection (phase preserved)
- $|\Gamma| < 1$: all passive loads (cannot reflect more than incident)
- $|\Gamma| > 1$: only active devices (amplifiers with feedback, oscillators)

### Return loss

Return loss quantifies the reflected power in dB:

$$\text{RL} = -20\log_{10}|\Gamma| \quad \text{dB}$$

The negative sign makes return loss a positive number for the usual case of partial reflection. Higher return loss = better match = less power reflected.

| $|\Gamma|$ | Return loss | Reflected power | Delivered power |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.5 | 6 dB | 25% | 75% |
| 0.316 | 10 dB | 10% | 90% |
| 0.178 | 15 dB | 3.2% | 96.8% |
| 0.1 | 20 dB | 1% | 99% |

For antenna matching, 10 dB return loss (VSWR ≈ 2:1) is acceptable in many applications. LNA inputs typically need 15 dB or better.

### VSWR

The voltage standing wave ratio is a classic metric from the era of waveguide and slotted-line measurements:

$$\text{VSWR} = \frac{1 + |\Gamma|}{1 - |\Gamma|}$$

VSWR = 1:1 is a perfect match. VSWR = 2:1 means $|\Gamma| = 0.333$, return loss ≈ 9.5 dB, 11% power reflected. VSWR = 3:1 means $|\Gamma| = 0.5$, 25% power reflected.

VSWR is always ≥ 1. For a matched load, VSWR = 1. For a short or open, VSWR = $\infty$.

### S-parameter matrix

For a general N-port network, S-parameters form an N×N matrix. Each element $S_{ij}$ is the ratio of the wave exiting port $i$ to the wave entering port $j$, with all other ports terminated in $Z_0$:

$$S_{ij} = \frac{b_i}{a_j}\bigg|_{a_k = 0 \text{ for } k \neq j}$$

For the important case of a two-port network:

$$\begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} S_{11} & S_{12} \\ S_{21} & S_{22} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

where $a_i$ = incident (ingoing) wave amplitude at port $i$, $b_i$ = scattered (outgoing) wave amplitude at port $i$.

In practice, $|S_{ij}|^2$ represents the power ratio (fraction of incident power at port $j$ that exits at port $i$). In dB: $S_{21,\text{dB}} = 20\log_{10}|S_{21}|$.

### Key S-parameter metrics for common devices

**Amplifier:**
- $S_{21}$: forward gain — typically +15 to +30 dB
- $S_{11}$: input match — want below −10 dB (ideally −20 dB)
- $S_{22}$: output match — want below −10 dB
- $S_{12}$: reverse isolation — want below −25 dB (prevents oscillation)

**Low-pass filter:**
- $|S_{21}|$ in passband: near 0 dB (ideally 0, reality −0.5 to −2 dB insertion loss)
- $|S_{21}|$ in stopband: −20 to −60 dB (rejection)
- $|S_{11}|$ in passband: below −15 dB (good match)

**Coaxial cable (lossless model):**
- $|S_{21}|$ = 1 (0 dB, no loss)
- $|S_{12}|$ = 1 (reciprocal)
- $|S_{11}|$ = 0 (matched)
- Phase of $S_{21}$: $-\beta \ell$ where $\beta = 2\pi/\lambda$ and $\ell$ is cable length

### Converting between $Z$ and $\Gamma$

$$\Gamma = \frac{Z - Z_0}{Z + Z_0}, \qquad Z = Z_0 \cdot \frac{1 + \Gamma}{1 - \Gamma}$$

These are inverses of each other. The first maps impedance to the Smith chart. The second reads impedance off the Smith chart.

### Properties of S-parameter matrices

**Reciprocal networks** (passive, no magnetic materials): $S_{ij} = S_{ji}$, so $S_{12} = S_{21}$. An isolator (one-directional device) is non-reciprocal: $|S_{21}| \approx 1$ but $|S_{12}| \approx 0$.

**Lossless networks**: the S-matrix is unitary — $S^\dagger S = I$. For a lossless two-port, $|S_{11}|^2 + |S_{21}|^2 = 1$ (power in equals power out).

**Passive networks**: $|S_{ij}| \leq 1$ for all $i, j$. Active devices (amplifiers) can have $|S_{21}| > 1$.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example 1: Antenna with S11 = −15 dB**

A 2.4 GHz patch antenna has measured $S_{11} = -15$ dB.

$|\Gamma| = 10^{-15/20} = 10^{-0.75} = 0.178$

Reflected power: $|\Gamma|^2 = 0.032 = 3.2\%$

Delivered to antenna: $1 - 0.032 = 96.8\%$

VSWR: $(1 + 0.178)/(1 - 0.178) = 1.178/0.822 = 1.43$

This is an excellent antenna match. Consumer Wi-Fi chips target $S_{11}$ below −10 dB over the operating band.

**Example 2: LNA characterization**

An LNA (low-noise amplifier) at 900 MHz has:
- $S_{21} = +18$ dB: the output power is $10^{18/10} = 63$ times the input power.
- $S_{11} = -12$ dB: input match is acceptable (6.3% reflected).
- $S_{12} = -28$ dB: reverse isolation — only $10^{-28/10} = 0.16\%$ of output leaks back to input.
- $S_{22} = -10$ dB: output match, 10% power reflected at output.

The $S_{12} = -28$ dB tells us the LNA is unconditionally stable — insufficient reverse signal to sustain oscillation.

**Example 3: Cable insertion loss**

A 1-meter SMA cable at 6 GHz has $S_{21} = -1.5$ dB.

Power delivered through cable: $10^{-1.5/10} = 0.708 = 70.8\%$ (29.2% lost to attenuation).

$S_{11} = -25$ dB: the cable is well-matched (0.3% reflected at input).

Phase of $S_{21}$: if the cable is 1 meter and the signal's wavelength in the cable is about 6 cm (at 6 GHz with velocity factor 0.7), the cable is about 16.7 wavelengths long. Phase rotation: $16.7 \times 360° \approx 6000°$ — this is why phase-coherent systems use phase-stable cables.

**Example 4: Filter rejection ratio**

A 1 GHz bandpass filter has:
- $S_{21}$ at 1 GHz: −1 dB (in-band insertion loss)
- $S_{21}$ at 2 GHz: −45 dB (out-of-band rejection)

Rejection ratio: $-45 - (-1) = 44$ dB of rejection at the second harmonic. This determines whether the transmitter's second harmonic meets regulatory spectral emission limits.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **S-parameters depend on $Z_0$.** The same physical device has different S-parameters in a 50 Ω system versus a 75 Ω system. Always verify the reference impedance. When comparing datasheets, confirm both use 50 Ω.

2. **S-parameters are frequency-dependent.** $S_{21} = +18$ dB at 900 MHz may be $+12$ dB at 1.8 GHz and $-3$ dB at 3 GHz. A single-frequency measurement tells you nothing about bandwidth. Always look at the full frequency sweep.

3. **Magnitude tells only part of the story.** $|S_{11}| = 0.2$ gives the reflection magnitude. The full complex $S_{11}$ (magnitude and phase) tells you whether the mismatch is inductive or capacitive — essential for designing a correction network. Discarding the phase loses half the information.

4. **Active vs. passive boundaries.** For passive devices, $|S_{21}| \leq 1$ (no power gain). An amplifier has $|S_{21}| > 1$. An oscillating circuit can exhibit $|S_{11}| > 1$ at certain frequencies (more power reflected than incident — the device is generating power). This is a stability warning sign, not a measurement error.

5. **Measurement conditions must match use conditions.** S-parameters assume all ports not being driven are terminated in $Z_0$. In a real circuit, if your LNA output sees a mismatched filter input, the effective $S_{21}$ differs from the datasheet value. This mismatch interaction is why "cascading S-parameters" requires proper de-embedding.

6. **dB conversion: 20 log, not 10 log.** S-parameters are voltage wave ratios. Converting $|S_{21}|$ to dB uses $20\log_{10}|S_{21}|$, not $10\log_{10}$. Since power = voltage squared, $10\log_{10}|S_{21}|^2 = 20\log_{10}|S_{21}|$ — same result, but the factor is 20.
