## Why impedance?

At DC, a capacitor blocks current and an inductor passes it freely. At high frequencies, the roles reverse. Resistance alone cannot capture this frequency-dependent behavior. **Impedance** extends Ohm's law to AC: $\tilde{V} = \tilde{I} Z$, where $Z$ is a complex number encoding both magnitude and phase shift.

Every RF component — antennas, filters, transmission lines — has an impedance. Matching impedances is how you transfer power efficiently and avoid reflections.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Impedance $Z = R + jX$ has two parts:

- **Resistance $R$** (real part): dissipates energy as heat. Always positive.
- **Reactance $X$** (imaginary part): stores and releases energy without dissipating it.
  - $X > 0$: inductive (current lags voltage)
  - $X < 0$: capacitive (current leads voltage)

Think of resistance as friction and reactance as springiness. A purely resistive load absorbs power; a purely reactive load bounces power back and forth.

The magnitude $|Z| = \sqrt{R^2 + X^2}$ tells you the ratio of voltage to current amplitudes. The phase angle $\theta = \arctan(X/R)$ tells you the phase shift between voltage and current.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Impedance of basic elements

| Element | Impedance | At DC | At high $\omega$ |
|---------|-----------|-------|------------------|
| Resistor | $Z_R = R$ | $R$ | $R$ |
| Capacitor | $Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C} = -\frac{j}{\omega C}$ | $\infty$ (open) | $\to 0$ (short) |
| Inductor | $Z_L = j\omega L$ | $0$ (short) | $\to \infty$ (open) |

### Series and parallel combinations

**Series:** $Z_\text{total} = Z_1 + Z_2 + \cdots$

**Parallel:** $\frac{1}{Z_\text{total}} = \frac{1}{Z_1} + \frac{1}{Z_2} + \cdots$

Or use admittance $Y = 1/Z$ for parallel: $Y_\text{total} = Y_1 + Y_2 + \cdots$

### Admittance

Admittance $Y = G + jB$ where:
- $G$ = conductance (real part)
- $B$ = susceptance (imaginary part)

Useful when components are in parallel.

### Power in complex impedance

Instantaneous power oscillates, but average power depends only on the resistive part:

$$P_\text{avg} = \frac{1}{2}|I|^2 R = \frac{1}{2}\text{Re}(\tilde{V}\tilde{I}^*)$$

Reactive power (stored/returned, not dissipated):
$$Q = \frac{1}{2}|I|^2 X$$

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: RC low-pass filter**

A resistor $R$ in series with a capacitor $C$ to ground. Input voltage $\tilde{V}_\text{in}$, output taken across $C$.

Capacitor impedance: $Z_C = 1/(j\omega C)$

Voltage divider:
$$\tilde{V}_\text{out} = \tilde{V}_\text{in} \cdot \frac{Z_C}{R + Z_C} = \tilde{V}_\text{in} \cdot \frac{1}{1 + j\omega RC}$$

At low $\omega$: $|\tilde{V}_\text{out}| \approx |\tilde{V}_\text{in}|$ (passes through).
At high $\omega$: $|\tilde{V}_\text{out}| \to 0$ (filtered out).
Cutoff frequency: $\omega_c = 1/(RC)$, where output is $1/\sqrt{2}$ of input (−3 dB).

**Example: Antenna feed impedance**

A half-wave dipole has feed impedance $Z \approx 73 + j42.5$ Ω at resonance. The real part (73 Ω) represents radiation resistance — power delivered to the antenna radiates. The imaginary part (42.5 Ω inductive) can be canceled with a series capacitor for impedance matching.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Impedance is frequency-dependent.** A capacitor's impedance at 1 MHz is 100× smaller than at 10 kHz. Always specify the frequency.

2. **Resistance ≠ impedance magnitude.** A purely reactive element has $R = 0$ but $|Z| \neq 0$. Don't confuse the two.

3. **Phase matters for power.** Two devices with the same $|Z|$ but different phases deliver different real power. Only the resistive part dissipates.

4. **Complex conjugate matching.** Maximum power transfer requires $Z_\text{load} = Z_\text{source}^*$ (conjugate match), not $Z_\text{load} = Z_\text{source}$.

5. **Units.** Impedance is in ohms (Ω). Admittance is in siemens (S). Reactance and resistance are both in ohms.

</section>
