## Why the Smith chart?

Before computers, RF engineers needed to solve impedance matching problems graphically. Philip Smith invented a chart that maps all possible complex impedances onto a unit circle. Even today, with simulation software everywhere, the Smith chart remains the standard visualization for impedance, reflection, and matching networks.

Every network analyzer displays results on a Smith chart. Every antenna datasheet includes one. If you can't read a Smith chart, you can't speak RF.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

The Smith chart is a transformed complex plane. Instead of plotting impedance $Z$ directly, we plot the **reflection coefficient** $\Gamma$, which always lies inside or on the unit circle.

Key landmarks:
- **Center** ($\Gamma = 0$): perfect match (load equals characteristic impedance)
- **Right edge** ($\Gamma = 1$): open circuit
- **Left edge** ($\Gamma = -1$): short circuit
- **Top half**: inductive loads
- **Bottom half**: capacitive loads

Moving around the chart corresponds to adding transmission line length or matching components. Clockwise rotation = moving toward the generator along a line. The chart makes visible what equations hide.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Normalized impedance

To use the Smith chart, first normalize impedance by the characteristic impedance $Z_0$ (usually 50 Ω):

$$z = \frac{Z}{Z_0} = r + jx$$

where $r = R/Z_0$ and $x = X/Z_0$.

### Reflection coefficient

The reflection coefficient relates to normalized impedance:

$$\Gamma = \frac{z - 1}{z + 1} = \frac{Z - Z_0}{Z + Z_0}$$

This maps the entire right half of the complex $z$-plane (all passive impedances) into the unit circle.

### Reading the chart

- **Constant resistance circles**: Pass through the right edge ($\Gamma = 1$), centered on the real axis.
- **Constant reactance arcs**: Curves connecting the right edge to the center.

To find $\Gamma$ from $Z$: locate the intersection of the $r$ circle and $x$ arc.

### VSWR circles

The **voltage standing wave ratio** (VSWR) depends only on $|\Gamma|$:

$$\text{VSWR} = \frac{1 + |\Gamma|}{1 - |\Gamma|}$$

On the Smith chart, constant VSWR is a circle centered at the origin. The radius equals $|\Gamma|$.

### Moving along a transmission line

A lossless transmission line of electrical length $\theta$ rotates $\Gamma$ clockwise by $2\theta$:

$$\Gamma_\text{in} = \Gamma_L e^{-j2\theta}$$

One full rotation (360°) = half wavelength of line.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Finding reflection coefficient**

Load: $Z_L = 100 + j50$ Ω, $Z_0 = 50$ Ω

Normalized: $z = 2 + j1$

On the Smith chart: locate intersection of $r = 2$ circle and $x = 1$ arc.

Read off: $|\Gamma| \approx 0.45$, $\angle\Gamma \approx 27°$

Verify by formula:
$$\Gamma = \frac{(2+j1) - 1}{(2+j1) + 1} = \frac{1+j1}{3+j1} = 0.4 + j0.2$$
$$|\Gamma| = 0.447, \quad \angle\Gamma = 26.6°$$

**Example: Matching with a stub**

To match a mismatched load, find where the constant-VSWR circle intersects the $r = 1$ circle. At that point, add a reactive element (stub or lumped component) to cancel the remaining reactance. The Smith chart shows exactly how much line length and what reactance is needed.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Normalization.** The chart assumes a specific $Z_0$. Changing $Z_0$ changes where your impedance plots. Always normalize first.

2. **Direction convention.** Moving toward the generator = clockwise. Moving toward the load = counterclockwise. Get this wrong and your matching network fails.

3. **Admittance chart.** Rotating the Smith chart 180° gives an admittance chart. Some problems are easier in admittance (parallel components). Know which you're using.

4. **Lossy lines.** For lossy transmission lines, $|\Gamma|$ shrinks as you move toward the generator — you spiral inward, not just rotate.

5. **Reading precision.** A hand-drawn Smith chart is accurate to maybe 5%. For precise work, use simulation. The chart is for intuition and first-pass design.

</section>
