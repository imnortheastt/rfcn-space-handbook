<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why a helical antenna for satcom?

Satellite links use circular polarization — it eliminates Faraday rotation fading in the ionosphere and avoids polarization alignment issues as the satellite moves. The axial-mode helical antenna naturally produces circular polarization with high gain (10-15 dBi) and wide bandwidth (2:1). It's the classic choice for portable ground terminals.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A helix is a coiled wire. In axial mode, the circumference equals roughly one wavelength, so the current makes one full rotation per turn. This rotating current radiates a circularly polarized wave along the axis — like a corkscrew of electromagnetic field.

More turns = more gain = narrower beam.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Operating modes

**Normal mode (C < λ/2):**
- Omnidirectional like short dipole
- Low gain
- Not used for satcom

**Axial mode (C ≈ λ):**
- Endfire radiation along helix axis
- Circular polarization
- High gain, directive beam

### Design parameters

**Circumference:**
$$C = \pi D \approx \lambda$$

Optimal: $C = 0.75\lambda$ to $1.33\lambda$

**Pitch angle:**
$$\alpha = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{S}{\pi D}\right)$$

Optimal: $12° < \alpha < 14°$ (typically 13°)

**Turn spacing:**
$$S = C \tan(\alpha) \approx 0.23\lambda$$

**Number of turns:**
$$N = \frac{L_{helix}}{S}$$

More turns → higher gain, narrower beamwidth

### Gain

Empirical formula (Kraus):

$$G \approx 15 \cdot N \cdot C^2 \cdot S / \lambda^3 \; \text{(dimensionless)}$$

Or approximately:

$$G_{dBi} \approx 10.8 + 10\log_{10}(N C^2 S / \lambda^3)$$

For N = 10, C = λ, S = 0.25λ: G ≈ 14 dBi

### Input impedance

At helix input (over ground plane):

$$Z_{in} \approx 140 \cdot (C/\lambda) \; \Omega$$

For C = λ: $Z_{in} \approx 140 \; \Omega$

**Matching to 50Ω:**
- λ/4 transformer: $Z_1 = \sqrt{50 \times 140} = 84 \; \Omega$
- Tapered line matching
- External matching network

### Axial ratio

Measures circularity (1.0 = perfect CP):

$$AR \approx \frac{2N + 1}{2N}$$

For N = 10: AR ≈ 1.05 (0.4 dB)

For N = 5: AR ≈ 1.1 (0.8 dB)

### Beamwidth

Half-power beamwidth:

$$\theta_{HP} \approx \frac{52°}{\sqrt{N S/\lambda} \cdot (C/\lambda)}$$

For N = 10, C = λ, S = 0.25λ: $\theta_{HP} \appro$ 33°

### Polarization sense

- Right-hand wound helix → RHCP
- Left-hand wound helix → LHCP

Match satellite: most LEO/GEO downlinks use RHCP.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: L-band satcom terminal (1.6 GHz)**

Frequency: 1.6 GHz (Iridium, Inmarsat)
λ = 187.5 mm

Design:
- Diameter D = λ/π = 60 mm
- Pitch S = 0.25λ = 47 mm
- Turns N = 8
- Total length = 8 × 47 = 376 mm

Expected:
- Gain: ~13 dBi
- Beamwidth: ~38°
- Axial ratio: 1.06

**Example: S-band cubesat ground station (2.2 GHz)**

Frequency: 2.2 GHz
λ = 136 mm

Design:
- D = 43 mm
- S = 34 mm
- N = 12
- Length = 408 mm

Expected:
- Gain: ~15 dBi
- Beamwidth: ~28°
- Suitable for LEO tracking with motorized mount

**Example: Quadrifilar helix antenna (QHA)**

Four interwound helices, fed in quadrature:
- Cardioid pattern (hemispherical coverage)
- Lower gain (~3 dBi) but wide beamwidth
- Used for GPS, Iridium handhelds

Design trade-off: higher gain (long helix) vs. portability.

**Example: Ground plane design**

Minimum ground plane: 0.5λ diameter (reflector cup)

Larger (1λ) reduces backlobes.

Cup depth: 0.15λ improves pattern.

**Example: Matching network**

For 140Ω to 50Ω at 1.6 GHz:

λ/4 transformer:
- Z₁ = 84Ω
- Microstrip: W ≈ 2 mm on FR-4 (εr = 4.4, h = 1.6 mm)
- Length: λ/4 ÷ √εeff ≈ 23 mm

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Wrong polarization sense.** Winding direction matters. RHCP satellite needs RHCP ground antenna. Cross-polarization gives 3 dB loss.

2. **Insufficient ground plane.** Too small or absent ground plane causes pattern distortion and impedance mismatch.

3. **Operating outside axial mode.** If C < 0.7λ or C > 1.4λ, you get normal mode or higher-order modes with degraded performance.

4. **Ignoring feed region.** The first turn must be shaped carefully to launch the traveling wave. Abrupt transitions cause reflections.

5. **Forgetting mechanical stability.** Long helices need support structures. Wind loading can be significant for portable terminals.

</section>
