## Why a 2.4 GHz dipole?

The half-wave dipole is the workhorse antenna — simple, omnidirectional in azimuth, and well-understood. At 2.4 GHz (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, ZigBee), the dipole is compact (~6 cm total length) and easy to build. Every RF engineer should be able to design one from scratch.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A dipole is two wires fed from the center. Current flows out, reaches the ends, and reflects back. At resonance, the antenna is λ/2 long — current distribution is sinusoidal with maximum at the center, zero at the ends.

The radiation pattern looks like a donut around the antenna axis.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Frequency and wavelength

At 2.4 GHz:

$$\lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{3 \times 10^8}{2.4 \times 10^9} = 125 \text{ mm}$$

Half wavelength: $\lambda/2 = 62.5$ mm

### Physical length adjustment

Due to end effects and finite wire diameter, actual resonant length is shorter:

$$L_{physical} \approx 0.95 \times \frac{\lambda}{2} = 0.95 \times 62.5 = 59.4 \text{ mm}$$

Each arm: ~30 mm

### Wire diameter effect

Thicker wire → lower resonant length (more end capacitance)
Thinner wire → higher Q, narrower bandwidth

For 2.4 GHz with 1 mm diameter wire: factor ≈ 0.95

### Input impedance

At resonance, dipole impedance:
- Free space: $Z_{in} \approx 73 + j0 \; \Omega$
- Above ground plane (h = λ/4): varies with height

### Matching to 50Ω

Options:
1. **Folded dipole**: $Z_{in} \approx 292 \; \Omega$, use 4:1 balun
2. **Gamma match**: shunt capacitor + series inductor
3. **Direct feed**: accept ~1.5:1 VSWR (73Ω to 50Ω)
4. **Sleeve balun**: coax with outer sleeve

### Bandwidth

Q ≈ 10-15 for thin wire dipole

Bandwidth (VSWR < 2): 
$$BW \approx \frac{f_0}{Q} \approx \frac{2400}{12} = 200 \text{ MHz}$$

Covers full ISM band (2400-2483.5 MHz).

### Radiation pattern

- **E-plane (vertical)**: Figure-8 pattern, nulls at axis
- **H-plane (horizontal)**: Omnidirectional
- **Gain**: 2.15 dBi

### Polarization

Linear polarization aligned with dipole axis.

Mount vertically for vertical polarization (standard for omnidirectional coverage).

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: PCB dipole design**

FR-4 substrate (εr = 4.4), 2.4 GHz.

Effective wavelength: $\lambda_{eff} = \lambda_0 / \sqrt{\epsilon_{eff}}$

For edge traces: $\epsilon_{eff} \approx 2.5$, so $\lambda_{eff} \approx 79$ mm

Half-wave: 39.5 mm total, 19.7 mm each arm

**Example: Whip antenna**

Flexible rubber antenna on 2.4 GHz radio module.

Internal helix loading shortens physical length to ~35 mm while maintaining electrical λ/2.

Trade-off: lower efficiency (75% vs. 95% for full-size).

**Example: Coax sleeve dipole**

Materials:
- RG-58 coax, 30 mm exposed center conductor
- 30 mm sleeve over outer conductor

Total length: 60 mm center-to-center.

Sleeve acts as balun, preventing common-mode current.

**Example: S11 measurement**

Connect to VNA, sweep 2-3 GHz.

Target: S11 < -10 dB at 2.44 GHz (center of band).

If resonance is high: lengthen arms.
If resonance is low: shorten arms.

**Example: Radiation pattern measurement**

Anechoic chamber or outdoor range.

Rotate antenna, measure received power every 5°.

Expected: -3 dB beamwidth ≈ 78° in E-plane.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Forgetting the balun.** Coax is unbalanced; dipole is balanced. Without a balun, common-mode currents radiate from the feedline, distorting the pattern.

2. **Ignoring mounting effects.** Nearby metal (phone case, ground plane, mounting bracket) shifts resonance. Always tune in the final environment.

3. **Using wrong velocity factor.** Air dipole vs. PCB dipole vs. helix-loaded all have different effective lengths.

4. **Confusing dBi and dBd.** Dipole gain is 0 dBd = 2.15 dBi. Specs often mix these up.

5. **Over-engineering for simple applications.** A bent wire dipole achieves 90% of theoretical performance. Perfection isn't always needed.
