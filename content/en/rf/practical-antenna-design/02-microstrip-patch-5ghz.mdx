<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why a 5 GHz patch antenna?

Patch antennas are flat, low-profile, and PCB-integrated — perfect for laptops, access points, and embedded devices. At 5 GHz (Wi-Fi 5/6/6E), the patch is small enough (~15 mm) for tight integration while providing 6-8 dBi directional gain.

This is the go-to antenna for any product that needs a planar radiator.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A patch antenna is a resonant cavity between a metal patch and ground plane. The patch edges act as two radiating slots. When the patch length equals λ/2 (accounting for substrate), the slots radiate in phase, creating a broadside beam.

It's like a flattened dipole sandwiched in a PCB.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Frequency and dimensions

At 5.5 GHz (mid-band):

$$\lambda_0 = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{3 \times 10^8}{5.5 \times 10^9} = 54.5 \text{ mm}$$

For FR-4 (εr = 4.4, h = 1.6 mm):

$$\epsilon_{eff} \approx \frac{\epsilon_r + 1}{2} + \frac{\epsilon_r - 1}{2} \left(1 + 12\frac{h}{W}\right)^{-0.5}$$

For W ≈ 15 mm: $\epsilon_{eff} \approx 3.9$

Patch length:

$$L = \frac{c}{2f\sqrt{\epsilon_{eff}}} - 2\Delta L \approx \frac{54.5}{2\sqrt{3.9}} - 1.6 = 12.2 \text{ mm}$$

### Patch width

For efficient radiation:

$$W = \frac{c}{2f} \sqrt{\frac{2}{\epsilon_r + 1}} \approx 16.6 \text{ mm}$$

### Feed techniques

**Inset feed (microstrip):**
- Notch into patch edge for 50Ω match
- Inset depth: $y_0 = \frac{L}{\pi}\cos^{-1}\sqrt{\frac{Z_0}{R_{in}}}$
- Simple, but introduces cross-polarization

**Coaxial probe feed:**
- Pin through substrate connects to patch
- Clean impedance control
- Probe inductance limits bandwidth

**Aperture coupling:**
- Slot in ground plane couples energy
- Separate feed and radiating layers
- Best bandwidth, more complex

**Proximity coupling:**
- Feed line under patch, no contact
- Good bandwidth
- Requires multilayer PCB

### Input impedance

At patch center: $Z = 0$ (short circuit)
At edge: $Z_{edge} \approx 150-300 \; \Omega$

For 50Ω match, feed at intermediate point (~L/3 from edge).

### Bandwidth

Patch bandwidth is limited by substrate height:

$$BW \approx 3.77 \frac{\epsilon_r - 1}{\epsilon_r^2} \frac{h}{\lambda_0} \frac{W}{L}$$

For FR-4, h = 1.6 mm: BW ≈ 3-4%

At 5.5 GHz: 165-220 MHz — covers single Wi-Fi channel, not full band.

**Wideband techniques:**
- Thicker substrate (h = 3.2 mm): BW → 6-8%
- U-slot patch: BW → 20-30%
- Stacked patches: BW → 15-25%

### Radiation pattern

- **Broadside beam**: Maximum normal to patch
- **E-plane beamwidth**: ~80-90°
- **H-plane beamwidth**: ~100-120°
- **Gain**: 6-8 dBi

### Polarization

Linear polarization along the patch length.

For circular polarization: truncate corners or use dual feed with 90° phase shift.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 5.5 GHz patch on FR-4**

Substrate: FR-4, εr = 4.4, h = 1.6 mm, tan δ = 0.02

Dimensions:
- Length L = 12.2 mm
- Width W = 16.6 mm
- Inset depth y₀ = 3.5 mm
- Inset width = 1 mm (50Ω line width)

Expected:
- Resonance: 5.5 GHz
- Gain: 6 dBi
- Bandwidth: ~180 MHz (VSWR < 2)

**Example: Wi-Fi 5 GHz full-band coverage**

Band: 5150-5850 MHz (700 MHz span, 13% BW)

Single patch can't cover — use:
- U-slot patch: 25% BW achievable
- Or: two overlapping patches (5.2 GHz + 5.7 GHz)

**Example: 2×2 patch array**

For higher gain (12-14 dBi):

Element spacing: λ₀/2 ≈ 27 mm
Array size: ~60 × 60 mm

Feed network: Wilkinson dividers or corporate feed.

**Example: Low-loss substrate**

Rogers RO4003C (εr = 3.55, tan δ = 0.0027):
- Higher efficiency: 90% vs. 70% for FR-4
- Larger patch: L ≈ 14.5 mm
- Cost: ~10× FR-4

Use for products where efficiency matters (battery-powered, high-power).

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Using FR-4 for narrowband applications.** FR-4 loss tangent (0.02) limits efficiency to 70-80%. Okay for Wi-Fi, not for high-power or sensitive receivers.

2. **Ignoring feed line radiation.** Microstrip feed lines radiate and can distort patterns. Keep feeds short or use stripline.

3. **Forgetting ground plane size.** Finite ground plane affects resonance and pattern. Minimum 2λ × 2λ for predictable behavior.

4. **Assuming ideal εr.** FR-4 has ±10% tolerance and varies with frequency. Prototype and tune.

5. **Neglecting manufacturing tolerances.** PCB etching has ±0.1 mm tolerance. At 5 GHz, this shifts resonance by ~100 MHz.
