<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why dB?

You are multiplying gains across five stages. Instead of multiplying five numbers together, you add five numbers. That is the entire reason the decibel exists.

A decibel is a logarithmic unit expressing a ratio. Because signal chains are long and power levels span twelve orders of magnitude — from a picowatt at a receiver input to a kilowatt at a broadcast transmitter — every RF engineer thinks in dB by default. Once you internalize three reference points (×2 ≈ +3 dB, ×10 = +10 dB, ×0.5 ≈ −3 dB), you can decompose any link budget in your head.

Think of it exactly like a distributed system's latency budget. Each service hop adds milliseconds; you sum them. Each RF stage adds or subtracts dB; you sum them. The math is the same: logarithms turn a chain of multiplications into a chain of additions.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

```
Signal power journey from transmitter to receiver:

  TX amp       Cable      Antenna     Free space    Antenna     LNA
  +30 dBm  →  −2 dB   →  +12 dBi  →  −120 dB   →  +8 dBi  →  −1 dB  →  RX

  Linear:  1 W × 0.63 × 15.8 × 10^{-12} × 6.3 × 0.79  ≈  ?   (painful)
  dB:      30  −  2   +  12  −  120      +  8  −  1   = −73 dBm  (trivial)
```

The three mental anchors that cover 90 % of link budget arithmetic:

```
  Power change   |  Ratio  |  dB value
  ───────────────┼─────────┼──────────
  Double         |   2 ×   |  +3 dB
  Half           |  0.5 ×  |  −3 dB
  Ten times      |  10 ×   |  +10 dB
  One tenth      |  0.1 ×  |  −10 dB
  Four times     |   4 ×   |  +6 dB  (= double twice)
  One hundred    | 100 ×   |  +20 dB (= 10 × 10)
  One million    |  10^6 × |  +60 dB (= 6 × 10 dB)
```

A 5G base station transmits at 43 dBm (20 W). A phone receiver's sensitivity is −100 dBm (10 femtowatts). The link must survive a 143 dB budget — fourteen orders of magnitude. In linear arithmetic that's a ratio of $10^{14.3}$. In dB it's just 143. That's the entire point.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### The definition

The decibel expresses the ratio of two power quantities:

$$L_\text{dB} = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{P}{P_\text{ref}}\right)$$

Because power is proportional to voltage squared ($P \propto V^2$), for voltage ratios the factor becomes 20:

$$L_\text{dB} = 20 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{V}{V_\text{ref}}\right)$$

The factor-of-2 difference matters enormously. A voltage gain of 10 is 20 dB; a power gain of 10 is only 10 dB. Mixing them up produces a systematic 6 dB error — a classic bug that makes a passing test fail in the field.

### Absolute references — the suffixes

A bare "dB" is a dimensionless ratio. To express an absolute level, the suffix pins the reference:

```
  Suffix  |  Reference  |  Formula                      |  Where used
  ────────┼─────────────┼───────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────
  dBm     |  1 mW       |  10 log10(P / 1 mW)           |  RF power levels
  dBW     |  1 W        |  10 log10(P / 1 W)            |  Satellite, broadcast
  dBi     |  isotropic  |  10 log10(G / G_isotropic)    |  Antenna gain
  dBd     |  dipole     |  dBi − 2.15                   |  Antenna datasheets
  dBc     |  carrier    |  10 log10(P_spur / P_carrier) |  Phase noise, spurs
  dBHz    |  1 Hz BW    |  10 log10(noise density)      |  PSD specs
```

The relationship between dBm and dBW: since 1 W = 1000 mW, $\text{dBm} = \text{dBW} + 30$.

### Converting between linear and dB

To go from a linear ratio $r$ to dB: $10 \log_{10}(r)$.

To go from dB back to linear: $r = 10^{L/10}$.

Common values worth memorizing:

$$0\ \text{dBm} = 1\ \text{mW}, \quad 30\ \text{dBm} = 1\ \text{W}, \quad -30\ \text{dBm} = 1\ \mu\text{W}$$

### Why you cannot add dBm values

dBm encodes a logarithm of power. Adding two dBm numbers produces a logarithm of a product, not of a sum. When two equal signals combine:

$$10\ \text{dBm} + 10\ \text{dBm} \neq 20\ \text{dBm}$$

The correct procedure:

1. Convert each to milliwatts: $10^{10/10} = 10\ \text{mW}$ each.
2. Add linearly: $10 + 10 = 20\ \text{mW}$.
3. Convert back: $10 \log_{10}(20) = 13\ \text{dBm}$.

The answer is 13 dBm, not 20 dBm. Coherent signal addition is a different story — but that requires phase information, not just power.

### The EIRP computation

Effective isotropic radiated power (EIRP) is the workhorse of link budgets:

$$\text{EIRP}_\text{dBm} = P_\text{TX,dBm} - L_\text{cable,dB} + G_\text{antenna,dBi}$$

It is the power a hypothetical isotropic antenna would need to produce the same field strength in the direction of maximum gain. Every link budget starts here.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

### Walking a real link budget

A small-cell base station: TX power 24 dBm, cable + connector loss 1.5 dB, antenna gain 14 dBi.

```
  EIRP = 24 dBm − 1.5 dB + 14 dBi = 36.5 dBm
```

Free-space path loss at 3.5 GHz over 200 m (urban micro):

$$L_\text{FSPL} = 20 \log_{10}(d) + 20 \log_{10}(f) + 92.4\ \text{dB}$$

$$= 20 \log_{10}(0.2) + 20 \log_{10}(3500) + 92.4 = -14 + 70.9 + 92.4 = 149.3\ \text{dB}$$

Received power at UE (0 dBi antenna):

```
  P_RX = 36.5 − 149.3 + 0 = −112.8 dBm
```

UE sensitivity for 5G NR QPSK: typically −110 dBm. Link margin = −112.8 − (−110) = −2.8 dB. The link is tight — add a wall loss or move to cell edge and it fails. This is the moment where dB thinking pays off: you instantly see which term to improve (increase EIRP by 3 dB, pick a closer site, or accept narrower MCS).

### Mental math trick: the 3-10 rule

Any ratio can be approximated by combining 3 dB steps and 10 dB steps:

```
  Want to know: what power ratio is 7 dB?
  7 = 10 − 3
  10 dB = ×10, −3 dB = ×0.5
  So 7 dB ≈ 10 × 0.5 = 5 ×  (exact: 5.01 ×)

  Want to know: what power ratio is 13 dB?
  13 = 10 + 3
  10 dB = ×10, +3 dB = ×2
  So 13 dB ≈ 20 ×  (exact: 19.95 ×)
```

### The AWS/cloud analogy

Think of dB like decibels in a request-tracing system where each service has a "latency gain" (added ms) or "latency loss" (optimization). The total end-to-end latency is the sum of all contributions. dB in RF is identically structured — the end-to-end SNR is the algebraic sum of all gains and losses along the chain. The tooling differs; the arithmetic is the same.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **dB vs dBm.** dB is a dimensionless ratio. dBm references 1 milliwatt. Saying "the power is 10 dB" is meaningless without a reference. Say "10 dBm" or "10 dBW."

2. **10 log vs 20 log.** Power ratios use factor 10. Voltage or field-amplitude ratios use factor 20. A voltage gain of 10 × is 20 dB, not 10 dB. Mixing them up produces a 6 dB error — common and embarrassing.

3. **Adding dBm values.** You can subtract dBm values (to get a dB ratio), but you cannot add them meaningfully. Two −90 dBm noise sources sum to −87 dBm, not −180 dBm.

4. **dBi vs dBd.** An antenna spec saying "12 dBd" is actually 14.15 dBi. Always check which reference the datasheet uses; 2.15 dB matters when your link margin is 3 dB.

5. **Reference must be stated.** dBi references an isotropic radiator. dBd references a dipole. dBc references the carrier. Always specify.

6. **Negative dB is not negative power.** −30 dBm means $10^{-3}$ mW = 1 µW. It does not mean the signal is absent or negative. A signal at −100 dBm is very weak but very real.
