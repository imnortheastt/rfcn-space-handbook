## Why dB?

You are multiplying gains across five stages. Instead of multiplying five numbers together, you add five numbers. That is the entire reason the decibel exists.

A decibel is a logarithmic unit expressing a ratio. Because adding is easier than multiplying, and because signal chains are long, every RF engineer thinks in dB by default.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Doubling power adds roughly 3 dB. Halving power subtracts 3 dB. Multiplying power by 10 adds 10 dB. These three reference points let you reason about most link budgets mentally.

The glossary term **decibel** pops up everywhere in RF because it compresses five orders of magnitude into a manageable single-digit number.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

The formula for power ratio in dB:

$$L_\text{dB} = 10 \log_{10}(P/P_\text{ref})$$

For voltage ratios (impedance must be equal), the factor changes to 20:

$$L_\text{dB} = 20 \log_{10}(V/V_\text{ref})$$

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

A transmitter outputs 1 W (0 dBW). After a cable loss of 3 dB, the power reaching the antenna is 0.5 W (−3 dBW). The antenna has a gain of 10 dB. The effective isotropic radiated power (EIRP) is:

```
EIRP = 0 dBW − 3 dB + 10 dB = 7 dBW ≈ 5 W
```

Adding and subtracting. No multiplication needed.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **dB vs dBm.** dB is a dimensionless ratio. dBm references 1 milliwatt. Saying "the power is 10 dB" is meaningless without a reference. Say "10 dBm" or "10 dBW."
2. **10 log vs 20 log.** Power ratios use factor 10. Voltage or field-amplitude ratios use factor 20. Mixing them up produces a 6 dB error — common and embarrassing.
3. **Reference must be stated.** dBi references an isotropic radiator. dBd references a dipole. dBc references the carrier. Always specify.

</section>
