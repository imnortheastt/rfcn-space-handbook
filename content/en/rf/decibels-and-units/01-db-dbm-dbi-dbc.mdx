## Why decibels?

You are designing a cellular base station. The transmit power is 40 W. By the time the signal reaches a phone 2 km away, the received power is 0.000 000 000 4 W — a factor of 100 billion smaller. If you try to track this in a spreadsheet using watts, you multiply and divide numbers spanning 11 orders of magnitude. One arithmetic slip and your link budget is wrong by a factor of 1000.

RF engineers solved this problem over a century ago: use logarithms. Decibels compress that 11-order range into a span of about 110. Multiplication becomes addition. A cascade of amplifiers and cables is just a column of additions. A link budget is a simple sum.

Every RF datasheet, every network analyzer screen, every 3GPP specification uses dB and its variants. Before you can read any of them, you need to understand this notation fluently — not just the formula, but the mental arithmetic.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A decibel expresses a ratio on a logarithmic scale. The core formula is:

$$\text{dB} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)$$

The key insight: every factor of 10 in power is 10 dB, and every factor of 2 is approximately 3 dB. Once you internalize those two anchors, you can mentally compute most RF numbers.

**The ×10 / ×2 ladder:**
- +10 dB = 10× power
- +3 dB ≈ 2× power (exact: 3.01 dB)
- +6 dB ≈ 4× power (two doublings)
- +20 dB = 100× power
- +30 dB = 1000× power

**Going negative:**
- −3 dB ≈ half power
- −10 dB = one-tenth power
- −30 dB = one-thousandth power

**The suffix tells you the reference point:**
- **dB** alone = a dimensionless ratio (gain, loss)
- **dBm** = referenced to 1 milliwatt (absolute power)
- **dBW** = referenced to 1 watt (absolute power)
- **dBi** = antenna gain referenced to an isotropic radiator
- **dBd** = antenna gain referenced to a half-wave dipole
- **dBc** = power referenced to a carrier signal

Think of dBm like "decibels measured from 1 mW." It converts an absolute power value into a logarithmic number. Think of dB (plain) like a multiplier — "+20 dB gain" means the output is 100× the input, regardless of actual power levels.

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### dB — pure dimensionless ratio

For power quantities:
$$G_{\text{dB}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{out}}{P_{in}}\right)$$

For voltage or current (because power ∝ V², the factor becomes 20):
$$G_{\text{dB}} = 20 \log_{10}\left(\frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}}\right)$$

The factor-of-2 difference between 10 and 20 trips up beginners constantly. The rule: use 10 when you are comparing powers (watts); use 20 when comparing field quantities (volts, amps, field strengths). Both express the same physical power ratio — they just start from different measured quantities.

### dBm — absolute power referenced to 1 mW

$$P_{\text{dBm}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{\text{mW}}}{1\ \text{mW}}\right)$$

Conversion table every RF engineer knows by heart:

| Linear power | dBm |
|---|---|
| 1 pW | −90 dBm |
| 1 nW | −60 dBm |
| 1 μW | −30 dBm |
| 1 mW | 0 dBm |
| 10 mW | +10 dBm |
| 100 mW | +20 dBm |
| 1 W | +30 dBm |
| 10 W | +40 dBm |
| 100 W | +50 dBm |

Converting back from dBm to milliwatts: $P_{\text{mW}} = 10^{P_{\text{dBm}}/10}$

### dBW — absolute power referenced to 1 W

$$P_{\text{dBW}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{\text{W}}}{1\ \text{W}}\right)$$

Relationship to dBm: $\text{dBm} = \text{dBW} + 30$

dBW appears mostly in satellite and high-power transmitter specifications (e.g., EIRP of a geostationary satellite is often given as +50 dBW or higher).

### dBi — antenna gain relative to isotropic

An isotropic radiator is a theoretical point source that radiates equally in all directions. Real antennas focus energy in preferred directions, so they appear to have "gain" compared to this ideal sphere.

$$G_{\text{dBi}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{G_{\text{actual}}}{G_{\text{isotropic}}}\right)$$

The isotropic radiator has $G_{\text{isotropic}} = 1$ (by definition), so dBi is just the log of the gain number. A half-wave dipole has a gain of about 1.64 linear, which equals 2.15 dBi. A typical patch antenna might be 7 dBi. A high-gain dish could be 40 dBi.

### dBd — antenna gain relative to dipole

$$G_{\text{dBd}} = G_{\text{dBi}} - 2.15$$

So a 10 dBd antenna is 12.15 dBi. This matters when reading base-station antenna datasheets: some list gain in dBd, which makes the number look smaller. Always check which reference is used.

### dBc — relative to a carrier

Used to characterize harmonics, spurious outputs, and phase noise — all relative to the carrier power.

$$P_{\text{dBc}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{\text{spur}}}{P_{\text{carrier}}}\right)$$

Example: "Phase noise: −120 dBc/Hz at 1 MHz offset" means the noise power density 1 MHz away from the carrier is 120 dB below the carrier, per hertz of bandwidth. This is always a negative number (the spur is weaker than the carrier) unless something is very wrong.

### Cascaded gains: why dB makes link budgets trivial

In linear units, a chain of components multiplies:

$$P_{\text{out}} = P_{\text{in}} \times G_1 \times G_2 \times L_1 \times L_2 \times \ldots$$

where gains $G > 1$ and losses $L < 1$. In dB:

$$P_{\text{out,dBm}} = P_{\text{in,dBm}} + G_{1,\text{dB}} + G_{2,\text{dB}} + L_{1,\text{dB}} + L_{2,\text{dB}} + \ldots$$

All you do is add. Losses are negative dB values, gains are positive. A complete link budget is a column sum.

### The critical rule: you cannot add dBm values

**dBm + dBm is meaningless.** dBm represents a power level; two power levels add in watts (linear domain), not in dBm (log domain).

To add two powers expressed in dBm:
1. Convert each to milliwatts: $P_{\text{mW}} = 10^{P_{\text{dBm}}/10}$
2. Add the milliwatt values
3. Convert the sum back to dBm

Example: +10 dBm + +10 dBm
- 10 mW + 10 mW = 20 mW
- $10 \log_{10}(20) = 13$ dBm

The answer is +13 dBm, not +20 dBm. This is the single most common dB mistake.

### Quick mental-math table

| Linear change | dB equivalent |
|---|---|
| ×2 | +3 dB |
| ×4 | +6 dB |
| ×8 | +9 dB |
| ×10 | +10 dB |
| ×100 | +20 dB |
| ×1000 | +30 dB |
| ÷2 | −3 dB |
| ÷10 | −10 dB |

For intermediate values: combine the anchors. ×5 = ×10 ÷ 2 = +10 − 3 = +7 dB. ×20 = ×10 × 2 = +10 + 3 = +13 dB.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example 1: Transmitter output in dBm**

A Wi-Fi router transmits at 100 mW.

$$P = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{100}{1}\right) = 10 \times 2 = +20 \text{ dBm}$$

Shortcut: 100 mW = 10 × 10 mW = +10 dB above 10 mW = +10 + 10 = +20 dBm. Or: 100 mW = +10 dBm + 10 dB (since 10 mW is +10 dBm and 100 mW is 10× that).

**Example 2: Complete link budget**

A 900 MHz IoT sensor link:
- TX power: +20 dBm (100 mW)
- TX cable loss: −1 dB
- TX antenna gain: +3 dBi
- Free-space path loss at 500 m: −84 dB
- RX antenna gain: +0 dBi (omni)
- RX cable loss: −0.5 dB

Received power: $20 - 1 + 3 - 84 + 0 - 0.5 = -62.5$ dBm

If the receiver sensitivity is −90 dBm, the link margin is $-62.5 - (-90) = 27.5$ dB. Healthy.

**Example 3: Amplifier cascade**

LNA gain: +18 dB. Driver amplifier gain: +12 dB. Power amplifier gain: +30 dB.

Total gain: $18 + 12 + 30 = +60$ dB = $10^6$ = 1,000,000× power.

Input: −40 dBm. Output: $-40 + 60 = +20$ dBm = 100 mW.

**Example 4: Spur specification**

A transmitter at +33 dBm has a second harmonic at −45 dBc.

Spur power: $33 + (-45) = -12$ dBm.

In watts: $10^{-12/10} = 10^{-1.2} \approx 63$ μW.

**Example 5: Antenna gain comparison**

An antenna is specified as 9 dBd.

In dBi: $9 + 2.15 = 11.15$ dBi.

In linear gain: $10^{11.15/10} \approx 13$.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Adding dBm values directly.** This is the cardinal sin of RF arithmetic. +10 dBm and +10 dBm is +13 dBm, not +20 dBm. The only time you add logarithmic values is when they are dimensionless ratios (dB, not dBm).

2. **Using 10 log for voltage ratios.** Voltage is a field quantity. Power ∝ V², so the dB factor for voltage is 20, not 10. Saying "10 dB voltage gain" when you mean "20 dB voltage gain" understates the gain by 2× in field amplitude and 4× in power.

3. **Mixing dBi and dBd without checking.** An antenna claiming 12 dBd is actually 14.15 dBi. In a link budget using dBi throughout, using the dBd number directly gives you a 2.15 dB error. Verify the reference for every antenna gain value.

4. **Treating dBm as a relative unit.** "The signal dropped 3 dB" and "the signal is at −80 dBm" are different statements. The first is a change (dimensionless ratio). The second is an absolute level. You can add dB ratios to dBm levels, but not two dBm values.

5. **Forgetting the factor-of-2 frequency.** Mental arithmetic with ×2 = +3 dB is handy, but 3.01 dB is exact. For precision cascade calculations, small rounding errors accumulate. When the margin is tight (less than 3 dB), use exact values.

6. **Interpreting negative dB as negative power.** −20 dB means 1/100 of the reference, not "negative power." Power is always positive; dB can be negative, zero, or positive.
