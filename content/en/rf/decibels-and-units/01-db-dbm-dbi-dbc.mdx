<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why decibels?

RF engineers deal with enormous dynamic ranges — signals vary from picowatts to kilowatts, antenna gains span factors of 1000+. Working in linear units means multiplying 10^12 numbers. Decibels convert multiplication to addition, making link budgets tractable.

Understanding dB variants (dBm, dBi, dBc) is the first step to reading any RF datasheet or specification.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A decibel is just a ratio expressed logarithmically:

$$\text{dB} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)$$

- **+3 dB** ≈ double power
- **+10 dB** = 10× power
- **−3 dB** ≈ half power
- **−10 dB** = 1/10 power

The suffix tells you what the reference is:
- **dBm** = relative to 1 milliwatt
- **dBW** = relative to 1 watt
- **dBi** = relative to isotropic antenna
- **dBc** = relative to carrier

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### dB (pure ratio)

Power ratio:
$$G_{\text{dB}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{out}}{P_{in}}\right)$$

Voltage/current ratio (power ∝ V²):
$$G_{\text{dB}} = 20 \log_{10}\left(\frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}}\right)$$

### dBm (absolute power)

Reference: 1 mW.

$$P_{\text{dBm}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P}{1\ \text{mW}}\right)$$

| Power | dBm |
|-------|-----|
| 1 μW | −30 dBm |
| 1 mW | 0 dBm |
| 10 mW | +10 dBm |
| 100 mW | +20 dBm |
| 1 W | +30 dBm |

### dBW (absolute power)

Reference: 1 W.

$$P_{\text{dBW}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P}{1\ \text{W}}\right)$$

Conversion: $\text{dBm} = \text{dBW} + 30$.

### dBi (antenna gain)

Reference: isotropic radiator (hypothetical antenna radiating equally in all directions).

$$G_{\text{dBi}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{G}{G_{iso}}\right)$$

A dipole has gain ≈ 2.15 dBi.

### dBd (antenna gain)

Reference: half-wave dipole.

$$G_{\text{dBd}} = G_{\text{dBi}} - 2.15$$

Datasheets use both — always check which reference.

### dBc (relative to carrier)

Used for spurious signals, phase noise, harmonics.

$$P_{\text{dBc}} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{signal}}{P_{carrier}}\right)$$

Example: "Second harmonic at −40 dBc" means 40 dB below carrier.

### Addition rules

| Operation | Linear | dB |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Cascade gains | $G_{total} = G_1 \times G_2$ | $G_{total} = G_1 + G_2$ |
| Power ratio | $P_2 / P_1$ | $P_{2,\text{dBm}} - P_{1,\text{dBm}}$ |

**You cannot add dBm values directly.** To add powers in dBm:
1. Convert to linear (mW)
2. Add
3. Convert back to dBm

### Quick mental math

| Change | dB | Exact |
|--------|-----|-------|
| ×2 | +3 dB | 3.01 dB |
| ×4 | +6 dB | 6.02 dB |
| ×10 | +10 dB | exact |
| ×100 | +20 dB | exact |
| ×1000 | +30 dB | exact |

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Amplifier cascade**

Amplifier 1: +20 dB gain.
Amplifier 2: +15 dB gain.

Total gain: $20 + 15 = 35$ dB = 3162× power.

**Example: Power in dBm**

Transmitter outputs 2 W.

$P = 10 \log_{10}(2000\ \text{mW}) = 10 \times 3.3 = 33$ dBm.

**Example: Link budget**

TX power: +30 dBm.
TX antenna gain: +10 dBi.
Path loss: −100 dB.
RX antenna gain: +5 dBi.

Received power: $30 + 10 - 100 + 5 = -55$ dBm.

**Example: Adding powers**

Two signals: +10 dBm and +10 dBm.

Linear: 10 mW + 10 mW = 20 mW = +13 dBm.

(NOT +20 dBm — that's the multiplication mistake.)

**Example: dBc to absolute**

Carrier: +20 dBm.
Spur at −30 dBc.

Spur power: $20 + (-30) = -10$ dBm.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Adding dBm values.** dBm + dBm is meaningless. Convert to mW first, add, convert back.

2. **Confusing dBi and dBd.** An antenna listed as "12 dBd" is actually 14.15 dBi. Always check the reference.

3. **Using 10× for voltage.** For voltage or current ratios, use 20 log₁₀, not 10 log₁₀.

4. **Forgetting dBm is absolute.** "The signal dropped 3 dB" is relative. "The signal is −80 dBm" is absolute.

5. **Calculator precision.** 3 dB ≈ 2× is fine for estimates. For precise work, use 10^(x/10).

6. **Negative dB confusion.** −3 dB means half power (÷2), not −2× power.

</section>
