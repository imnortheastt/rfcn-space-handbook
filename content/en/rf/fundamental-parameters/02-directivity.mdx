<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why directivity?

Gain tells you how well an antenna focuses power, but it mixes two effects: focusing and losses. **Directivity** isolates the focusing part — how much the radiation pattern concentrates power in the preferred direction, assuming a lossless antenna.

Directivity is the theoretical upper limit of gain. It's what you'd get if your antenna had perfect efficiency.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Think of directivity as a shape property of the radiation pattern, independent of how much power gets lost as heat.

An omnidirectional antenna spreads power uniformly — low directivity. A narrow pencil beam concentrates power in one direction — high directivity.

Mathematically, directivity compares the peak radiation intensity to the average radiation intensity over all directions. A tight beam has a peak much higher than the average.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Definition

Directivity $D(\theta, \phi)$ in direction $(\theta, \phi)$:

$$D(\theta, \phi) = \frac{U(\theta, \phi)}{U_{avg}} = \frac{4\pi U(\theta, \phi)}{P_{rad}}$$

where:
- $U(\theta, \phi)$ = radiation intensity (W/sr)
- $U_{avg}$ = average radiation intensity = $P_{rad}/(4\pi)$
- $P_{rad}$ = total radiated power

### Peak directivity

$$D_0 = \max_{\theta, \phi} D(\theta, \phi) = \frac{4\pi U_{max}}{P_{rad}}$$

### Beam solid angle

Directivity relates to beam solid angle $\Omega_A$:

$$D_0 = \frac{4\pi}{\Omega_A}$$

where:

$$\Omega_A = \iint_{4\pi} \frac{F(\theta, \phi)}{F_{max}} d\Omega$$

$F(\theta, \phi)$ is the normalized radiation pattern, and the integral is over all solid angles.

### Approximate formulas

For antennas with well-defined beamwidths $\theta_{HP}$ and $\phi_{HP}$ (in radians):

$$D_0 \approx \frac{4\pi}{\theta_{HP} \cdot \phi_{HP}}$$

Or with beamwidths in degrees:

$$D_0 \approx \frac{41253}{\theta_{HP}^\circ \cdot \phi_{HP}^\circ}$$

This assumes a single main lobe with negligible sidelobes.

### Directivity vs. gain

$$G = \eta_{rad} \cdot D$$

where $\eta_{rad}$ = radiation efficiency (typically 0.5–0.95 for practical antennas).

| Parameter | Includes losses? | Upper bound |
|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| Directivity | No | Pattern-limited |
| Gain | Yes | $\leq D$ |

### Common directivities

| Antenna | Directivity |
|---------|-------------|
| Isotropic | 1 (0 dB) |
| Hertzian dipole | 1.5 (1.76 dB) |
| Half-wave dipole | 1.64 (2.15 dB) |
| Uniformly illuminated aperture | $4\pi A / \lambda^2$ |

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Estimating directivity from beamwidth**

An antenna has 3 dB beamwidths of 10° × 15°.

$$D_0 \approx \frac{41253}{10 \times 15} = 275 = 24.4 \text{ dB}$$

If efficiency is 70%, gain ≈ 24.4 − 1.5 = 22.9 dBi.

**Example: Aperture antenna**

A 1 m² uniformly illuminated aperture at 10 GHz ($\lambda = 0.03$ m):

$$D = \frac{4\pi \times 1}{0.03^2} = 13,963 = 41.5 \text{ dB}$$

With 55% aperture efficiency: gain ≈ 38.9 dBi.

**Example: Why directivity matters for arrays**

A phased array's directivity is approximately $N \times D_{element}$ where $N$ = number of elements. A 64-element array with 6 dB elements has directivity ≈ $6 + 10\log_{10}(64) = 6 + 18 = 24$ dB.

Actual gain depends on feed network losses, element coupling, and scan angle.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Directivity ≠ gain.** Low-efficiency antennas have directivity much higher than gain. A highly directive antenna with 10% efficiency has poor gain.

2. **Beamwidth approximation limits.** The $41253/(\theta \cdot \phi)$ formula assumes a single lobe. High-sidelobe antennas have lower directivity than this predicts.

3. **Aperture efficiency.** Real apertures don't achieve $4\pi A/\lambda^2$. Non-uniform illumination, edge diffraction, and feed blockage reduce effective directivity.

4. **Pattern shape matters.** Two antennas with the same beamwidth can have different directivities if their sidelobe structures differ.

5. **Partial directivity.** For dual-polarized antennas, total directivity splits between polarizations. High XPD concentrates directivity in the intended polarization.

6. **Frequency dependence.** Like gain, directivity varies with frequency as the electrical size of the antenna changes.

</section>
