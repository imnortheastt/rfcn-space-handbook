<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why antenna efficiency?

Directivity measures the antenna's focusing ability. But not all power delivered to the antenna gets radiated — some is lost to heat, reflections, and mismatch.

**Efficiency** quantifies these losses. It's the bridge between directivity (theoretical maximum) and gain (what you actually get). Understanding efficiency is essential for system design and antenna selection.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine a garden hose with a spray nozzle. The nozzle pattern is directivity — how focused the spray is. But if the hose has leaks or the nozzle is partially blocked, less water comes out than goes in.

Efficiency measures how much of what you put in actually comes out where you want it. A 50% efficient antenna radiates only half the power you feed it; the rest becomes heat.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Total efficiency

Total antenna efficiency $\eta_0$ combines all loss mechanisms:

$$\eta_0 = \eta_{refl} \times \eta_{cond} \times \eta_{diel}$$

or equivalently:

$$\eta_0 = (1 - |\Gamma|^2) \times \eta_{rad}$$

where:
- $\eta_{refl} = (1 - |\Gamma|^2)$ = reflection (mismatch) efficiency
- $\eta_{cond}$ = conduction efficiency (ohmic losses)
- $\eta_{diel}$ = dielectric efficiency (dielectric losses)
- $\eta_{rad} = \eta_{cond} \times \eta_{diel}$ = radiation efficiency
- $\Gamma$ = reflection coefficient at the feed

### Radiation efficiency

Radiation efficiency considers only ohmic and dielectric losses:

$$\eta_{rad} = \frac{P_{rad}}{P_{accepted}} = \frac{R_{rad}}{R_{rad} + R_{loss}}$$

where:
- $R_{rad}$ = radiation resistance
- $R_{loss}$ = loss resistance (conductor + dielectric)

For wire antennas:

$$R_{loss} \propto \sqrt{f} \quad \text{(skin effect)}$$

Higher frequency → higher loss resistance → lower efficiency.

### Aperture efficiency

For aperture antennas (horns, reflectors):

$$\eta_{ap} = \frac{G}{G_{max}} = \frac{G}{\frac{4\pi A}{\lambda^2}}$$

This combines:
- Illumination efficiency (tapered vs. uniform)
- Spillover efficiency (feed pattern vs. reflector size)
- Surface errors
- Blockage (feed, struts)

Typical values:
- Horn antenna: 50–80%
- Parabolic reflector: 55–75%
- Phased array: 60–80%

### Gain and efficiency

The fundamental relationship:

$$G = \eta_0 \times D$$

or in dB:

$$G_{dB} = D_{dB} + 10\log_{10}(\eta_0)$$

A 90% efficient antenna loses 0.46 dB from directivity to gain. A 50% efficient antenna loses 3 dB.

### Electrically small antennas

For antennas much smaller than a wavelength:

$$\eta_{rad} \approx \frac{(ka)^2}{(ka)^2 + \text{loss terms}}$$

where $a$ is the antenna size and $k = 2\pi/\lambda$.

Small antennas → low radiation resistance → efficiency dominated by loss resistance.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Dipole efficiency**

Half-wave dipole with $R_{rad} = 73\ \Omega$ and $R_{loss} = 2\ \Omega$:

$$\eta_{rad} = \frac{73}{73 + 2} = 97.3\% = -0.12 \text{ dB}$$

With VSWR = 2.0 ($|\Gamma| = 0.33$):

$$\eta_{refl} = 1 - 0.33^2 = 0.89 = -0.5 \text{ dB}$$

Total efficiency: $0.973 \times 0.89 = 86.6\%$

**Example: Chip antenna**

A 2.4 GHz chip antenna in a phone might have:
- Radiation efficiency: 30%
- Mismatch loss: 1 dB (80%)

Total efficiency: $0.30 \times 0.80 = 24\%$ = −6.2 dB

This is why phones struggle with weak signals despite sophisticated modulation.

**Example: Large reflector**

4 m dish at 12 GHz, 65% aperture efficiency:

$$G = 0.65 \times \frac{4\pi \times 4^2 \times \pi}{0.025^2} = 0.65 \times 1,006,000 = 654,000 = 58.2 \text{ dBi}$$

Without efficiency factor: 60 dBi (theoretical max).

**Example: Phased array element efficiency**

64-element array with per-element efficiency 80% and array efficiency 70% (feed network losses):

System efficiency: $0.80 \times 0.70 = 56\%$

Array gain = $56\% \times 64 \times D_{element}$

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Radiation vs. total efficiency.** Datasheets sometimes quote radiation efficiency without mismatch loss. Total efficiency is lower.

2. **Frequency dependence.** Efficiency varies with frequency — often worse at band edges where mismatch increases.

3. **Measurement difficulty.** Efficiency requires comparing radiated power to input power. Wheeler cap or reverberation chamber methods have uncertainties.

4. **Gain includes efficiency.** When someone says "antenna gain," efficiency is already baked in. Don't double-count losses.

5. **Small antenna tradeoffs.** Electrically small antennas trade efficiency for bandwidth and size. A −6 dB efficient antenna may be acceptable if the alternative is no antenna at all.

6. **Environmental effects.** Nearby objects (human body, metal enclosure) can dramatically change efficiency. Free-space specs don't apply in installed conditions.

7. **Aperture efficiency vs. radiation efficiency.** Different concepts. An aperture antenna can have 60% aperture efficiency (illumination) and 95% radiation efficiency (ohmic loss).

</section>
