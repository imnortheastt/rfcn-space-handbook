## Why antenna gain?

A flashlight concentrates light into a beam, delivering far more intensity in one direction than a bare bulb. A candle radiates light equally in all directions — what you might call "isotropic" illumination. The flashlight does not create more energy; it redirects energy that would have spread in all directions into a focused cone.

Antenna gain works identically. A high-gain antenna does not amplify the transmitter output — it redistributes radiated power into a narrower region of space. A dish antenna pointed at a satellite delivers 10,000× more power density at the satellite than a bare isotropic radiator with the same transmit power.

For a cloud engineer, gain is the single most important number in a wireless link budget. It directly sets the received signal level, the maximum communication range, and the coverage footprint of a base station. Every dB of gain is a dB of link margin — and in wireless systems, link margin is availability.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine a sphere surrounding the transmitting antenna. The total radiated power spreads over the surface of that sphere. An isotropic antenna spreads power uniformly — the power density at any point is:

$$S_{iso} = \frac{P_{rad}}{4\pi r^2}$$

A directive antenna concentrates power: in its preferred direction, the power density is $G$ times higher:

$$S_{directive} = G \cdot \frac{P_{rad}}{4\pi r^2}$$

The gain $G$ is a dimensionless multiplier — how much more power density you get compared to an isotropic source radiating the same total power.

Energy conservation still holds: where the gain is $G$ in one direction, it must be less than $G$ somewhere else. A 20 dBi dish that concentrates power into a 2° beam has nearly zero gain outside that beam. High gain and wide coverage are mutually exclusive.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Formal definition

Gain $G(\theta, \phi)$ at direction $(\theta, \phi)$:

$$G(\theta, \phi) = \frac{4\pi U(\theta, \phi)}{P_{in}}$$

where:
- $U(\theta, \phi)$ = radiation intensity in direction $(\theta, \phi)$, in W/sr
- $P_{in}$ = total power accepted by the antenna (not just radiated — $P_{in}$ includes ohmic losses)

The ratio $4\pi / P_{in}$ normalizes to what an isotropic radiator would produce if the same power were distributed uniformly over $4\pi$ steradians.

**Peak gain** $G_0$ is the maximum over all directions and is what antenna datasheets report unless otherwise stated.

### Gain vs. directivity

$$G = \eta_{rad} \cdot D$$

where $\eta_{rad}$ is radiation efficiency (0 to 1) and $D$ is directivity. Directivity measures the focusing ability of the radiation pattern alone. Gain also accounts for losses: a highly directive antenna that dissipates half its input power as heat has gain 3 dB below its directivity.

For the link budget, always use gain, not directivity.

### Reference antennas and units

**dBi** — gain relative to an isotropic radiator:
$$G_{dBi} = 10\log_{10}(G)$$

**dBd** — gain relative to a half-wave dipole (which has 2.15 dBi gain):
$$G_{dBd} = G_{dBi} - 2.15$$

The conversion: a 10 dBd antenna is 12.15 dBi. Always verify the reference when reading a specification. Marketing often uses dBd because the number is smaller and sounds more conservative.

### Common antenna gains

| Antenna type | Typical gain |
|---|---|
| Isotropic (theoretical) | 0 dBi |
| Half-wave dipole | 2.15 dBi |
| Quarter-wave monopole | 2–3 dBi |
| Rubber duck (helical whip) | 1–3 dBi |
| Patch (single element) | 6–9 dBi |
| Yagi (5 elements) | 10–12 dBi |
| Horn antenna | 10–25 dBi |
| Cellular sector (65° beam) | 15–18 dBi |
| Parabolic dish (1 m, 10 GHz) | 38–42 dBi |
| Massive MIMO panel (64 elements) | 25–35 dBi |
| Large dish (10 m, 30 GHz) | 60+ dBi |

### Effective aperture relation

A larger physical aperture captures more of an incoming wavefront, producing higher gain. The relationship:

$$G = \frac{4\pi A_e}{\lambda^2}$$

where $A_e$ is the effective aperture. For a parabolic dish:

$$A_e = \eta_{ap} \cdot A_{physical} = \eta_{ap} \cdot \frac{\pi D^2}{4}$$

where $\eta_{ap}$ is aperture efficiency (typically 0.55–0.75). The gain scales as $D^2/\lambda^2$: doubling the dish diameter quadruples the gain. Halving the wavelength (doubling frequency) also quadruples the gain for the same dish.

**Worked example — dish sizing:**

Need $G = 40$ dBi at $f = 12$ GHz ($\lambda = 25$ mm), with $\eta_{ap} = 0.6$.

$$G = 10^{40/10} = 10{,}000$$

$$A_e = \frac{G\lambda^2}{4\pi} = \frac{10{,}000 \times (0.025)^2}{4\pi} = \frac{6.25}{12.57} = 0.497\ \text{m}^2$$

$$A_{physical} = A_e / \eta_{ap} = 0.497/0.6 = 0.828\ \text{m}^2 \Rightarrow D = \sqrt{4 \times 0.828 / \pi} = 1.03\ \text{m}$$

A 1 m dish provides ~40 dBi at 12 GHz.

### EIRP: Gain in the transmit link budget

Effective Isotropic Radiated Power (EIRP) combines transmit power and antenna gain into a single metric:

$$\text{EIRP} = P_t \cdot G_t \quad \text{or in dB:} \quad \text{EIRP}_{dBm} = P_{t,dBm} + G_{t,dBi}$$

A 1 W (30 dBm) transmitter into a 20 dBi dish antenna produces EIRP = 50 dBm = 100 W equivalent. The satellite does not know whether that signal came from a 100 W isotropic radiator or a 1 W directional one — it sees the same power density in that direction.

EIRP is regulated: FCC and ITU set EIRP limits to prevent interference, regardless of how you achieve it (more power vs. more gain).

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Link budget example — Wi-Fi**

Transmitter: 20 dBm into a 3 dBi omnidirectional antenna. EIRP = 23 dBm.

Free space path loss at 2.4 GHz, distance 50 m:
$$L_{fs} = 20\log_{10}(50) + 20\log_{10}(2.4 \times 10^9) + 20\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{4\pi}{c}\right) = 34 + 187.6 - 147.6 = 74\ \text{dB}$$

Receiver antenna gain: 3 dBi.

Received power: $23 - 74 + 3 = -48\ \text{dBm}$.

Receiver sensitivity: −90 dBm. Link margin: 42 dB. The link easily closes.

**Coverage vs. gain tradeoff**

A cellular base station must cover a 120° sector. It can use:
- Option A: 3 dBi omni — covers 360° but weak signal
- Option B: 17 dBi sector antenna — 65° beam, 14 dB more gain in that sector

Option B delivers 14 dB better signal per UE within the 65° sector. Three sectors cover 360°. This is why every modern base station uses sector antennas.

**Gain degradation with frequency**

A patch antenna designed for 2.4 GHz peak gain of 8 dBi.

At 2.45 GHz (edge of ISM band): gain may drop to 6 dBi as the patch drifts off resonance.

At 5.8 GHz: same physical patch has completely different resonance — gain could be near 0 dBi.

Always check gain vs. frequency plots, not just the peak spec.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **dBi vs. dBd.** A 10 dBd antenna is 12.15 dBi. Using dBd as dBi overstates link budget by 2.15 dB — enough to cause a link to fail at range that was calculated to close.

2. **Gain does not create power.** A 20 dBi antenna fed with 1 W radiates 1 W total — just concentrated in a narrow beam. The peak power density is 100× an isotropic radiator, but the total radiated power is unchanged.

3. **Realized gain includes mismatch.** IEEE defines two gain variants: *gain* (ignores feedline mismatch) and *realized gain* (includes mismatch). Datasheets often report the higher "gain" value. For link budgets, use realized gain or add mismatch loss separately.

4. **Pattern vs. peak.** Peak gain is one number. The radiation pattern shows how gain varies with angle — essential for interference analysis, sidelobe management, and coverage planning. Never substitute peak gain for a directional analysis.

5. **Near-field invalidates gain.** Gain is a far-field quantity. If the link is shorter than the far-field boundary $2D^2/\lambda$, gain cannot be applied directly.

6. **Frequency variation.** Gain varies with frequency. A 15 dBi antenna at 900 MHz may be 12 dBi at 850 MHz and 10 dBi at 800 MHz. Always check gain over the full operating band.
