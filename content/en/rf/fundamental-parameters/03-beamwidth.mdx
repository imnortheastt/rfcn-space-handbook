<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why beamwidth?

Gain tells you how much power goes in the main direction. Beamwidth tells you how narrow that main beam is — the angular region where the antenna is effective.

A narrow beam means high directivity but requires precise pointing. A wide beam covers more area but with less gain. Beamwidth is central to coverage planning, tracking systems, and interference analysis.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Think of a spotlight vs. a floodlight. The spotlight has a narrow beam — intense but covers a small angle. The floodlight covers a wide angle but is dimmer at any point.

Antenna beamwidth works the same way. A satellite dish has a 1° beam — miss by 2° and you lose the signal. A Wi-Fi router has a 60° beam — you can move around the room and stay connected.

Beamwidth quantifies the transition from "in the beam" to "out of the beam."

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Half-power beamwidth (HPBW)

The most common definition. HPBW is the angle between the directions where radiation intensity drops to half its maximum value (−3 dB).

$$\text{HPBW} = \theta_{-3dB}$$

For many antennas, HPBW in azimuth and elevation differ — beams are often elliptical, not circular.

### First-null beamwidth (FNBW)

The angle between the first nulls on either side of the main lobe. Useful for interference analysis — outside the FNBW, there's a null before the first sidelobe.

$$\text{FNBW} \approx 2 \times \text{HPBW}$$

The exact ratio depends on aperture illumination.

### Beamwidth and aperture

For a uniformly illuminated aperture of width $D$:

$$\text{HPBW} \approx 0.886 \frac{\lambda}{D} \text{ (radians)} = 50.8 \frac{\lambda}{D} \text{ (degrees)}$$

For tapered illumination (reduced sidelobes), the coefficient increases to 1.0–1.2.

### Beamwidth and directivity

Approximate relationship:

$$D_0 \approx \frac{41253}{\theta_{HP}^\circ \times \phi_{HP}^\circ}$$

Narrower beam → higher directivity.

### Array beamwidth

For a uniform linear array of $N$ elements with spacing $d$:

$$\text{HPBW} \approx 0.886 \frac{\lambda}{Nd}$$

More elements → narrower beam.

### Beam scanning

When an array scans off-boresight by angle $\theta_s$:

$$\text{HPBW}(\theta_s) = \frac{\text{HPBW}(0)}{\cos(\theta_s)}$$

The beam broadens at scan angles — a 10° beam at boresight becomes 11.5° at 30° scan.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Satellite dish pointing**

A 1 m dish at 12 GHz ($\lambda = 0.025$ m):

$$\text{HPBW} \approx 50.8 \times \frac{0.025}{1.0} = 1.27°$$

Pointing accuracy must be better than ±0.5° to stay within 3 dB. Motorized mounts typically achieve 0.1° accuracy.

**Example: Cellular sector antenna**

A 65° horizontal beamwidth sector antenna covers one-third of a cell. Three sectors cover 360° with some overlap.

Vertical beamwidth of 8° tilts down 5° to cover the cell without spilling energy to distant cells.

**Example: Phased array scanning**

64-element linear array, $d = λ/2$ spacing:

$$\text{HPBW} = 50.8 \times \frac{1}{64 \times 0.5} = 1.6°$$

At 45° scan: HPBW ≈ 1.6°/cos(45°) = 2.3°.

**Example: Radar resolution**

A radar with 1° beamwidth at 100 km range has cross-range resolution:

$$\Delta_{cross} = R \times \theta = 100 \text{ km} \times \frac{\pi}{180} = 1.75 \text{ km}$$

Two targets closer than 1.75 km at the same range appear as one.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **3 dB vs. other definitions.** Some specifications use −6 dB or −10 dB points. Always verify the definition.

2. **Azimuth vs. elevation.** Antennas often have different horizontal and vertical beamwidths. Both matter for coverage.

3. **Sidelobes.** Beamwidth describes the main lobe only. Sidelobes can cause interference even outside the HPBW.

4. **Frequency dependence.** Beamwidth scales with wavelength. A 5 GHz antenna has half the beamwidth of the same aperture at 2.5 GHz.

5. **Pointing loss.** Being off-axis by half the HPBW costs 3 dB. For high-gain systems, pointing accuracy is critical.

6. **Near-field effects.** Beamwidth is defined in the far field. Close to the antenna, the beam may not be well-formed.

</section>
