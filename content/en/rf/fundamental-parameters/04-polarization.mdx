<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why antenna polarization?

An antenna doesn't just radiate power in a direction — it radiates with a specific electric field orientation. This is **polarization**.

Matching polarization between transmitter and receiver is essential. Mismatch causes loss. Deliberate polarization diversity enables MIMO and interference rejection. Understanding polarization is fundamental to antenna selection and system design.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Picture a rope tied to a wall. You can shake it up-and-down (vertical), side-to-side (horizontal), or in a circle. Radio waves behave similarly — the electric field oscillates in a specific pattern.

- **Linear:** E-field stays in one plane (vertical, horizontal, or tilted)
- **Circular:** E-field rotates as the wave travels
- **Elliptical:** E-field traces an ellipse (imperfect circular)

The receiving antenna must be oriented to capture the incoming polarization. A vertical antenna captures vertically polarized waves; a horizontal one captures horizontally polarized waves.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Polarization states

The electric field of a plane wave can be written:

$$\mathbf{E} = E_x \hat{x} e^{j\phi_x} + E_y \hat{y} e^{j\phi_y}$$

The polarization depends on the amplitude ratio $E_y/E_x$ and phase difference $\Delta\phi = \phi_y - \phi_x$.

| Polarization | Condition |
|--------------|-----------|
| Linear horizontal | $E_y = 0$ |
| Linear vertical | $E_x = 0$ |
| Linear slant | $\Delta\phi = 0$ or $\pi$, both nonzero |
| Circular (RH) | $E_x = E_y$, $\Delta\phi = -90°$ |
| Circular (LH) | $E_x = E_y$, $\Delta\phi = +90°$ |
| Elliptical | All other cases |

### Axial ratio

For elliptical polarization, the axial ratio (AR) measures eccentricity:

$$\text{AR} = \frac{E_{major}}{E_{minor}}$$

- AR = 1: perfect circular
- AR = ∞: perfect linear
- AR < 3 dB: "good" circular polarization in practice

### Polarization purity

**Cross-polarization discrimination (XPD):** ratio of co-pol to cross-pol power.

$$\text{XPD} = 10\log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{co}}{P_{cross}}\right)$$

Typical values: 20–30 dB for well-designed antennas.

**Cross-polarization isolation (XPI):** in dual-pol systems, isolation between ports.

### Antenna polarization types

| Antenna | Polarization |
|---------|--------------|
| Dipole | Linear (along axis) |
| Patch | Linear or circular (with feed design) |
| Helix | Circular (axial mode) |
| Spiral | Circular (wideband) |
| Horn | Linear (depends on feed) |
| Parabolic | Depends on feed |

### Faraday rotation

Linearly polarized waves passing through the ionosphere experience rotation. The angle depends on frequency and total electron content.

$$\Delta\theta \propto \frac{TEC}{f^2}$$

Circular polarization is immune to Faraday rotation — a major reason satellites use it.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Satellite uplink**

Satellite uses RHCP; ground terminal must use RHCP (same sense in both directions due to reflection convention).

If the terminal uses LHCP by mistake: total loss (orthogonal polarizations).

**Example: Dual-polarized MIMO**

LTE and 5G use ±45° slant polarization for 2×2 MIMO on a single antenna panel. The two polarizations provide independent channels with 20+ dB isolation.

**Example: FM radio**

FM broadcast typically uses circular polarization. Car antennas (often whips, linearly polarized) lose 3 dB but work at any orientation.

**Example: Rain depolarization**

Raindrops are slightly oblate. Horizontal polarization experiences more attenuation than vertical. At mmWave frequencies, rain can significantly degrade cross-pol isolation, affecting dual-pol systems.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Sense convention.** IEEE defines circular polarization sense looking **into** the wave (from source). Optics convention is opposite. Verify which convention your equipment uses.

2. **Off-axis degradation.** An antenna's polarization may change off-axis. A circularly polarized antenna at boresight may become elliptical at scan angles.

3. **Reflection flips sense.** Circularly polarized waves reverse sense upon reflection. RHCP becomes LHCP after a single bounce.

4. **Cross-pol in arrays.** Array elements may have worse XPD at scan angles. System XPD is often worse than element XPD.

5. **Measurement challenges.** Measuring polarization accurately requires rotating lineups or dual-pol reference antennas. Field measurements often have errors.

6. **Multipath depolarizes.** Indoor and urban environments scatter waves randomly. Originally polarized signals become partially depolarized.
