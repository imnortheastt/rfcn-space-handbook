<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why these three phenomena?

Radio waves don't just travel in straight lines. They bounce off buildings, bend around corners, and penetrate walls. Understanding reflection, refraction, and diffraction explains why you get a signal indoors, around corners, and in valleys — and why sometimes you don't.

These three mechanisms are the physical foundation beneath every path loss model. COST-231 and TR 38.901 are statistical summaries of millions of interactions governed by exactly these three processes. Understanding the physics lets you reason about when models will fail — and what to do when they do.

For satellite links, diffraction explains why a handset behind a building sometimes maintains a weak connection. For mmWave 5G, reflection explains why street canyons can paradoxically improve coverage. For rural LTE, the Fresnel zone clearance rule determines whether a hilltop relay link will close.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### Three behaviors at boundaries

All three phenomena arise from how electromagnetic waves interact with boundaries between materials:

- **Reflection**: energy bounces back from a surface — like light in a mirror, but the amount reflected depends on material, frequency, and angle.
- **Refraction**: the wave bends when entering a new medium — like a straw appearing bent in water, caused by the change in wave speed across the boundary.
- **Diffraction**: the wave bends around obstacles or through openings — why you can hear someone shouting around a corner even when you cannot see them.

The key physical insight is **wavelength relative to obstacle size**. When an obstacle is much larger than the wavelength, the wave reflects cleanly. When it is comparable in size, diffraction dominates. When it is much smaller, the wave passes through almost undisturbed (scattering, not diffraction). At 900 MHz ($\lambda \approx 33$ cm), buildings are many wavelengths wide so reflection and diffraction both matter. At 28 GHz ($\lambda \approx 10$ mm), even small obstacles cause reflection and diffraction is weaker.

### The Fresnel zone intuition

Before treating each phenomenon in detail, appreciate the Fresnel zone concept. Any direct radio path between TX and RX needs clearance not just of the geometric line-of-sight, but an ellipsoidal volume around it. Energy that grazes this volume diffracts and can partially cancel the direct path. The first Fresnel zone radius at the midpoint quantifies the minimum clearance needed for near-free-space propagation. This ellipse grows with lower frequency and longer path — the reason UHF links need tower heights of tens of meters even in flat terrain.

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Reflection

When a plane wave hits a planar boundary between two media, part of the energy reflects and part transmits. The reflection coefficient depends on polarization, incident angle, and the electrical properties (permittivity $\varepsilon_r$, conductivity $\sigma$) of both media.

**Perpendicular (TE) polarization reflection coefficient:**
$$\Gamma_\perp = \frac{\eta_2 \cos\theta_i - \eta_1 \cos\theta_t}{\eta_2 \cos\theta_i + \eta_1 \cos\theta_t}$$

**Parallel (TM) polarization reflection coefficient:**
$$\Gamma_\parallel = \frac{\eta_2 \cos\theta_t - \eta_1 \cos\theta_i}{\eta_2 \cos\theta_t + \eta_1 \cos\theta_i}$$

where $\eta = \sqrt{\mu/\varepsilon}$ is the intrinsic impedance of each medium, $\theta_i$ is the angle of incidence, and $\theta_t$ is the angle of transmission (refraction).

**Special cases:**

- **Perfect conductor** ($\sigma \to \infty$): $|\Gamma| = 1$, total reflection with 180° phase reversal for TE polarization. Wet concrete and metal approximate this.
- **Brewster angle** (TM polarization only): there exists an angle $\theta_B = \arctan(n_2/n_1)$ at which $\Gamma_\parallel = 0$ — no reflection at all. Radar absorbers exploit this.
- **Grazing incidence** ($\theta_i \to 90°$): both polarizations give $\Gamma \to -1$, meaning near-total reflection with phase reversal. This is the ground reflection regime in the two-ray model.

**Practical reflection coefficients for common materials at 1–10 GHz:**

| Material | $\varepsilon_r$ | $|\Gamma|$ normal incidence |
|----------|----------------|----------------------------|
| Concrete | 4–8 | 0.33–0.55 |
| Brick | 3–4 | 0.27–0.33 |
| Glass | 5–7 | 0.38–0.46 |
| Metal (aluminium) | $\sim \infty$ | ~1.0 |
| Dry earth | 3–5 | 0.27–0.38 |
| Sea water | ~80 | ~0.80 |

Reflected power fraction = $|\Gamma|^2$. Transmitted power = $1 - |\Gamma|^2$ (before absorption in the material).

### Refraction

When a wave crosses from medium 1 to medium 2, the transmission angle follows **Snell's law**:

$$n_1 \sin\theta_i = n_2 \sin\theta_t$$

where the refractive index $n = \sqrt{\varepsilon_r \mu_r}$ (for non-magnetic materials $\mu_r = 1$, so $n = \sqrt{\varepsilon_r}$).

**Critical angle and total internal reflection:** when $n_1 > n_2$, there is an angle above which no energy transmits:

$$\theta_c = \arcsin\left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right)$$

For air ($n = 1$) to earth ($\varepsilon_r \approx 15$, $n \approx 3.87$): $\theta_c = \arcsin(1/3.87) \approx 15°$. Below this grazing angle, the ground reflects essentially all energy — consistent with the two-ray model assumption.

**Atmospheric refraction**: the refractive index of air decreases with altitude due to decreasing pressure and humidity. This gradient bends signals slightly toward earth under normal conditions, extending radio horizons beyond optical line-of-sight. An anomalous temperature inversion (warm layer over cool layer) creates **ducting** — the wave traps between layers and can travel hundreds of kilometers with little loss, causing severe interference in clear-weather conditions. Satellite and fixed microwave links must account for ducting in tropical coastal regions.

### Diffraction

Waves bend around obstacles or through apertures via Huygens' principle: every point on a wavefront acts as a new point source. Energy from the original wavefront reconstructs around corners, allowing reception behind obstacles.

**Knife-edge diffraction (Fresnel-Kirchhoff model):**

The diffraction parameter $\nu$ characterizes how much an obstacle protrudes into the direct path:

$$\nu = h \sqrt{\frac{2(d_1 + d_2)}{\lambda d_1 d_2}}$$

where:
- $h$ = obstacle height above the direct line-of-sight (positive = above LOS, negative = below)
- $d_1$ = distance from transmitter to obstacle
- $d_2$ = distance from obstacle to receiver
- $\lambda$ = wavelength

Knife-edge diffraction loss (dB) as a function of $\nu$:

| $\nu$ | Loss (dB) | Interpretation |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| $< -1$ | $\approx 0$ | Obstacle well clear of LOS |
| $-1$ | 0 | Tangent to first Fresnel zone |
| $0$ | 6 | Grazing — obstacle at LOS level |
| $1$ | ~13 | Obstacle 1 Fresnel zone above LOS |
| $2.4$ | ~20 | Substantial blockage |
| $> 2.4$ | $13 + 20\log_{10}(\nu)$ | Deep shadow region |

For $\nu > 0$, a compact approximation:
$$L_d \approx 6.02 + 9.11\nu + 1.27\nu^2 \quad \text{(dB, valid for } 0 \leq \nu \leq 2.4\text{)}$$

**Multiple knife-edge:** real terrain has multiple ridges. The Deygout method adds up diffraction losses from individual obstacles in sequence, accounting for the fact that each obstacle changes the effective incident angle for subsequent ones.

### Fresnel zones

The Fresnel zones are concentric ellipsoidal shells around the direct path. The $n$-th zone boundary is where the path via the zone edge is exactly $n\lambda/2$ longer than the direct path. Signals reflected from even-numbered zones tend to cancel the direct path (path difference = integer $\lambda$); odd zones reinforce it.

First Fresnel zone radius at a point distance $d_1$ from TX and $d_2$ from RX:

$$r_1 = \sqrt{\frac{\lambda d_1 d_2}{d_1 + d_2}}$$

At the midpoint of a $d$-km link:

$$r_1 = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\lambda d} \approx \frac{17.3\sqrt{d}}{2\sqrt{f}} \text{ m} \quad \text{(d in km, f in GHz)}$$

**Clearance rule**: the obstacle or terrain should not enter the first Fresnel zone. A common design rule is 0.6 $r_1$ clearance (60% of the first Fresnel zone), which gives less than 0.5 dB of additional diffraction loss compared to free space. Taller tower heights in flat terrain are often driven entirely by this Fresnel zone clearance requirement, not by the geometric horizon.

**Example**: 5 GHz, 10 km link (midpoint at 5 km):

$$r_1 = 17.3\sqrt{5/(4 \times 5)} = 17.3 \times 0.5 = 8.65 \text{ m}$$

The midpoint clearance requirement is 0.6 × 8.65 = 5.2 m above any obstacle at the path midpoint.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

### Example 1: Ground reflection coefficient

2.4 GHz over dry earth ($\varepsilon_r = 5$, low conductivity) at grazing incidence ($\theta_i \approx 85°$).

At grazing, both polarizations approach $\Gamma \approx -1$. The reflected wave is nearly as strong as the incident wave but inverted in phase. This is the physical basis for the two-ray model's -1 ground reflection assumption.

### Example 2: Building penetration loss

1 GHz signal entering a concrete wall ($\varepsilon_r = 6$, thickness = 30 cm) at normal incidence.

Refractive index: $n = \sqrt{6} = 2.45$.

Entry reflection coefficient: $\Gamma = (1 - 2.45)/(1 + 2.45) = -0.42$.

Transmitted power fraction per surface: $1 - 0.42^2 = 0.82$ (−0.86 dB per surface).

Two surfaces: $0.82^2 = 0.67$, i.e., −1.7 dB from reflection alone. Add absorption: at 1 GHz, concrete absorbs roughly 1–3 dB per 30 cm. Total penetration loss: 3–5 dB typical.

At 28 GHz, both reflection and absorption increase significantly — concrete can exceed 25 dB total penetration loss, which is why mmWave coverage requires outdoor base stations or IRS/repeater solutions for indoor penetration.

### Example 3: Hilltop knife-edge diffraction

5 GHz microwave link, total distance 10 km, obstacle at midpoint (5 km from each end), obstacle 15 m above the direct LOS.

$$\nu = 15 \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 10000}{0.06 \times 5000 \times 5000}} = 15 \sqrt{\frac{20000}{1500000}} = 15 \times 0.1155 = 1.73$$

Loss: $13 + 20\log_{10}(1.73) = 13 + 4.8 = 17.8$ dB. This is a significant additional loss that must be budgeted.

### Example 4: Fresnel zone clearance for a rural microwave hop

900 MHz backhaul link, 30 km, antenna towers at each end, terrain rises 20 m at midpoint.

First Fresnel zone radius at midpoint:

$$r_1 = 17.3\sqrt{\frac{15}{4 \times 0.9}} = 17.3\sqrt{4.17} = 35.3 \text{ m}$$

Required clearance: 0.6 × 35.3 = 21.2 m above terrain.

If terrain is 20 m high at midpoint and towers are 30 m, link height at midpoint = 30 m (assuming flat average height). The link just meets the clearance requirement. In practice, earth curvature also lowers the effective link height at the midpoint, so engineers add an effective earth radius correction (typically k = 4/3 factor). This may require taller towers.

### Example 5: Atmospheric ducting impact

Ka-band satellite terminal in a tropical coastal region. During late afternoon, a temperature inversion at 500 m altitude creates a ducting layer. Terrestrial interference from a microwave link 300 km away can propagate via ducting and appear at the satellite terminal's uplink frequency, causing interference that clear-sky models would never predict. This is why satellite terminals in ducting-prone regions deploy interference-mitigation techniques and frequency coordination is mandatory.

## Pitfalls

1. **Ignoring frequency scaling of diffraction.** Diffraction loss increases with frequency for the same obstacle geometry (larger $\nu$). A 900 MHz link that grazes a hilltop with 6 dB loss will see 13+ dB loss at 2.4 GHz for the same geometry. Never scale a UHF link budget to SHF without recomputing Fresnel zones.

2. **Treating reflection as total.** Real surfaces absorb some energy. $|\Gamma| < 1$ always for non-ideal conductors. Using $\Gamma = -1$ everywhere overestimates reflected power by 1–6 dB depending on material and frequency.

3. **Single knife-edge for multiple ridges.** The knife-edge method applied to the single worst obstacle underestimates loss by 3–10 dB when multiple ridges are present. Use Deygout or Epstein-Peterson methods for actual terrain.

4. **Forgetting phase in multipath.** Reflection creates a copy of the signal with a phase shift. Two paths adding constructively gives +6 dB; destructively gives a deep fade. Propagation models that track only power lose all phase information and cannot predict these interference patterns.

5. **Polarization mismatch at reflection.** Perpendicular and parallel polarizations reflect with different coefficients and phase shifts. A vertically polarized transmit antenna reflecting off a tilted glass facade does not produce a vertically polarized reflected wave. This can cause 10–20 dB polarization mismatch loss in reflected paths.

6. **Neglecting Fresnel zone clearance.** Engineers who place antennas just above the geometric LOS without checking Fresnel zone clearance will find 3–6 dB more loss than expected. This is especially significant for lower-frequency microwave links (< 2 GHz) where the first Fresnel zone is large.
