<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why these three phenomena?

Radio waves don't just travel in straight lines. They bounce off buildings, bend around corners, and penetrate walls. Understanding reflection, refraction, and diffraction explains why you get signal indoors, around corners, and in valleys — and why sometimes you don't.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

All three phenomena arise from wave behavior at boundaries:

- **Reflection**: wave bounces back (mirror effect)
- **Refraction**: wave bends when entering different medium (straw-in-water effect)
- **Diffraction**: wave bends around obstacles or through openings (sound around corners)

The wavelength relative to obstacle size determines which dominates.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Reflection

When a wave hits a boundary between two media with different electrical properties:

**Reflection coefficient (perpendicular polarization):**
$$\Gamma_\perp = \frac{\eta_2 \cos\theta_i - \eta_1 \cos\theta_t}{\eta_2 \cos\theta_i + \eta_1 \cos\theta_t}$$

**Reflection coefficient (parallel polarization):**
$$\Gamma_\parallel = \frac{\eta_2 \cos\theta_t - \eta_1 \cos\theta_i}{\eta_2 \cos\theta_t + \eta_1 \cos\theta_i}$$

where η = intrinsic impedance = $\sqrt{\mu/\varepsilon}$.

**Perfect conductor:** $\Gamma = -1$ (total reflection, 180° phase shift).

**Brewster angle:** For parallel polarization, $\Gamma_\parallel = 0$ when:
$$\theta_B = \arctan\left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right)$$

At Brewster angle, no reflection for parallel polarization — used in radar absorbers.

### Refraction

**Snell's law:**
$$n_1 \sin\theta_i = n_2 \sin\theta_t$$

where n = refractive index = $\sqrt{\varepsilon_r \mu_r}$.

**Critical angle:** When $n_1 > n_2$, total internal reflection occurs above:
$$\theta_c = \arcsin\left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right)$$

For air-to-earth: $\theta_c \approx 10°$ (earth εᵣ ≈ 15).

### Diffraction

Waves bend around obstacles comparable to or larger than their wavelength.

**Knife-edge diffraction (Fresnel-Kirchhoff):**

Diffraction parameter:
$$\nu = h \sqrt{\frac{2(d_1 + d_2)}{\lambda d_1 d_2}}$$

where h = obstacle height above LOS, d₁ and d₂ = distances to TX and RX.

Diffraction loss (dB):
- $\nu < -1$: $L_d \approx 0$ (clearance, no loss)
- $\nu = 0$: $L_d = 6$ dB (grazing)
- $\nu = 1$: $L_d = 16$ dB
- $\nu > 1$: $L_d \approx 13 + 20\log_{10}\nu$

### Fresnel zones

Ellipsoids where path length differs by nλ/2:

$$r_n = \sqrt{\frac{n \lambda d_1 d_2}{d_1 + d_2}}$$

First Fresnel zone radius at midpoint:
$$r_1 = 17.3\sqrt{\frac{d}{4f}}$$

where d in km, f in GHz, r₁ in meters.

**Rule:** 60% of first Fresnel zone should be clear for negligible diffraction loss.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Ground reflection**

2.4 GHz over grass (εᵣ = 15, σ = 0.005 S/m).

At grazing incidence (θᵢ ≈ 90°): $\Gamma \approx -1$ regardless of polarization.

Ground reflection nearly perfectly inverts the wave.

**Example: Building penetration**

1 GHz into concrete wall (εᵣ = 5).

$$n_{concrete} = \sqrt{5} = 2.24$$

At normal incidence:
$$\Gamma = \frac{377/2.24 - 377}{377/2.24 + 377} = \frac{168 - 377}{168 + 377} = -0.38$$

Transmitted power: $1 - |\Gamma|^2 = 86\%$ per surface.

Two surfaces: $0.86^2 = 74\%$ → 1.3 dB penetration loss (excluding absorption).

**Example: Hilltop diffraction**

5 GHz link, 10 km total, obstacle at midpoint, 10 m above LOS.

$$\nu = 10 \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 10000}{0.06 \times 5000 \times 5000}} = 10 \sqrt{0.0133} = 1.15$$

Loss: $13 + 20\log_{10}(1.15) = 14.2$ dB.

**Example: Fresnel clearance**

2.4 GHz, 5 km link.

$$r_1 = 17.3\sqrt{\frac{5}{4 \times 2.4}} = 17.3\sqrt{0.52} = 12.5 \text{ m}$$

Need 7.5 m clearance (60%) at midpoint for negligible loss.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Ignoring frequency dependence.** Diffraction loss increases with frequency. A 900 MHz design may fail at 2.4 GHz.

2. **Perfect conductor assumption.** Real surfaces have $|\Gamma| < 1$. Some energy penetrates or absorbs.

3. **Single knife-edge model.** Real terrain has multiple obstacles. Use multiple knife-edge methods (Deygout, Epstein-Peterson).

4. **Forgetting phase.** Reflection can be in-phase or out-of-phase. Two paths can cancel (destructive) or add (constructive).

5. **Overlooking polarization.** Perpendicular and parallel polarizations reflect differently. Mismatch costs up to 20 dB.
