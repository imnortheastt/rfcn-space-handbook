<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why the two-ray model?

Free-space path loss predicts signal falls as 1/d². But measurements over flat ground show 1/d⁴ beyond a breakpoint distance. The two-ray model explains this: direct wave plus ground-reflected wave interfere, creating alternating peaks and nulls, transitioning to steeper loss at long range.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine two paths: direct line-of-sight and a bounce off the ground. Near the transmitter, these paths have different lengths and phases — they can add or cancel. Far away, both paths become nearly horizontal, the phase difference stabilizes at 180°, and they partially cancel — giving the 1/d⁴ law.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Geometry

TX at height $h_t$, RX at height $h_r$, separated by distance d.

Direct path length:
$$d_1 = \sqrt{d^2 + (h_t - h_r)^2} \approx d + \frac{(h_t - h_r)^2}{2d}$$

Reflected path length:
$$d_2 = \sqrt{d^2 + (h_t + h_r)^2} \approx d + \frac{(h_t + h_r)^2}{2d}$$

Path difference:
$$\Delta = d_2 - d_1 \approx \frac{2h_t h_r}{d}$$

Phase difference:
$$\Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\Delta = \frac{4\pi h_t h_r}{\lambda d}$$

### Received power

Including ground reflection coefficient Γ ≈ -1:

$$P_r = P_t G_t G_r \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^2 \left|1 + \Gamma e^{-j\Delta\phi}\right|^2$$

The interference term $|1 - e^{-j\Delta\phi}|^2 = 4\sin^2(\Delta\phi/2)$.

### Two regimes

**Near field (d < d_c):**

$$P_r \propto \frac{1}{d^2} \times \text{oscillations}$$

Alternating constructive/destructive interference.

**Far field (d > d_c):**

When $\Delta\phi << 1$: $\sin(\Delta\phi/2) \approx \Delta\phi/2$

$$P_r = P_t G_t G_r \frac{h_t^2 h_r^2}{d^4}$$

Fourth-power law — 40 dB/decade.

### Crossover distance

Transition occurs when first Fresnel zone touches ground:

$$d_c = \frac{4 h_t h_r}{\lambda}$$

Or equivalently, when $\Delta\phi = \pi/2$.

### Path loss equation

Beyond crossover:

$$L = 40\log_{10}(d) - 20\log_{10}(h_t) - 20\log_{10}(h_r)$$

Note: no frequency dependence in far-field two-ray.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Cellular tower**

Frequency: 900 MHz (λ = 0.33 m)
Tower height: 30 m
Mobile height: 1.5 m

Crossover distance:
$$d_c = \frac{4 \times 30 \times 1.5}{0.33} = 545 \text{ m}$$

At 100 m (< dc): free-space-like with oscillations.
At 1 km (> dc): two-ray 1/d⁴ applies.

Path loss at 1 km:
$$L = 40\log_{10}(1000) - 20\log_{10}(30) - 20\log_{10}(1.5) = 120 - 29.5 - 3.5 = 87 \text{ dB}$$

**Example: Wi-Fi across parking lot**

2.4 GHz, both antennas at 2 m height.

$$d_c = \frac{4 \times 2 \times 2}{0.125} = 128 \text{ m}$$

Under 128 m: expect nulls and peaks.
Over 128 m: two-ray loss dominates.

At 50 m (before dc): null spacing ≈ $\frac{\lambda d}{2h_t h_r} = \frac{0.125 \times 50}{2 \times 4} = 0.78$ m.

Moving 0.78 m can swing signal by 20+ dB.

**Example: Air-to-ground**

Aircraft at 3000 m, ground terminal at 10 m, 1 GHz.

$$d_c = \frac{4 \times 3000 \times 10}{0.3} = 400 \text{ km}$$

Within 400 km: two-ray doesn't apply, use free-space.

**Example: Path loss comparison**

At 2 km, 30 m tower, 1.5 m mobile:

Free-space (900 MHz): $32.4 + 20\log(0.9) + 20\log(2) = 32.4 - 0.9 + 6 = 37.5$ dB? 

Correct: $20\log(4\pi \times 2000/0.33) = 20\log(76000) = 97.6$ dB

Two-ray: $40\log(2000) - 20\log(30) - 20\log(1.5) = 132 - 29.5 - 3.5 = 99$ dB

Two-ray predicts ~1.5 dB more loss — close match confirms two-ray region.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Applying two-ray too close.** Below crossover distance, use free-space with oscillations, not 1/d⁴.

2. **Forgetting ground imperfection.** Real ground isn't flat or perfectly reflecting. Roughness scatters energy.

3. **Assuming Γ = -1.** Ground reflection coefficient depends on frequency, polarization, and soil type. Range: -0.3 to -1.

4. **Ignoring multipath.** Two-ray is minimum complexity. Real environments have buildings, trees, terrain.

5. **Using two-ray indoors.** Multiple reflections dominate. Two-ray is an outdoor flat-earth model.
