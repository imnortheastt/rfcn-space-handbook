## Why the two-ray model?

Free-space path loss predicts signal power falls as $1/d^2$ — 20 dB per decade. But measurements over flat ground consistently show $1/d^4$ beyond a certain distance. A cell at 2 km sees roughly 6 dB more loss than free space would predict; at 5 km the discrepancy is over 10 dB. Ignoring this error wipes out half your link margin before you deploy a single site.

The two-ray model explains this discrepancy precisely: a ground-reflected wave interferes with the direct path. Near the transmitter the two paths alternately add and cancel, creating oscillating signal strength. Beyond the **breakpoint distance** the reflected wave is nearly 180° out of phase with the direct path and partially cancels it — transitioning to the steeper $1/d^4$ law.

This matters for every terrestrial link: macrocell base stations, point-to-point microwave hops, and air-to-ground links all operate in regimes where two-ray geometry is the dominant propagation mechanism.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### Two paths, one receiver

Picture a transmitter on a tower and a receiver on the ground separated by distance $d$. Two paths exist:

1. **Direct path**: straight line from TX to RX, length $d_1$.
2. **Ground-reflected path**: bounces off the ground, length $d_2 > d_1$.

Because the ground reflection coefficient at grazing incidence is approximately $-1$ (180° phase flip, as derived in the reflection lesson), the reflected wave arrives inverted. The direct and reflected waves superimpose at the receiver.

**Close to the transmitter** ($d \ll d_b$): the path length difference $\Delta = d_2 - d_1$ is large relative to wavelength. As the receiver moves, $\Delta$ changes rapidly relative to $\lambda$, causing the two waves to alternately add (constructive) and cancel (destructive). Signal strength oscillates by ±20 dB or more over distances of a few wavelengths.

**Far from the transmitter** ($d \gg d_b$): both paths are nearly horizontal. The path difference $\Delta \approx 2h_t h_r / d$ becomes much smaller than $\lambda$. The phase difference $\Delta\phi = 2\pi\Delta/\lambda \approx 4\pi h_t h_r / (\lambda d)$ approaches zero as $d$ increases — but since the reflection inverts the wave (adds $\pi$ radians), the total phase difference between direct and reflected approaches $\pi$. The two waves nearly cancel. The cancellation worsens as $d$ increases, giving the steeper $1/d^4$ decay.

```
Two-Ray Geometry:

TX (height ht)          RX (height hr)
      |                      |
      |     Direct path d1   |
      |----------------------|
      |                      |
      |  d'  |     d''       |
      |______ground__________|
             reflected path d2 > d1

Path difference: Δ = d2 - d1 ≈ 2·ht·hr / d  (far field)
Phase difference: Δφ = (2π/λ)·Δ + π (π from reflection)
```

The $\pi$ from reflection means that as $d$ grows and $\Delta \to 0$, the total phase difference approaches $\pi$ — destructive interference. The two-ray cancellation gets worse with distance, not better.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Geometry and path lengths

TX at height $h_t$, RX at height $h_r$, horizontal separation $d$.

Direct path (exact):
$$d_1 = \sqrt{d^2 + (h_t - h_r)^2} \approx d + \frac{(h_t - h_r)^2}{2d}$$

Reflected path (via image point at $-h_t$):
$$d_2 = \sqrt{d^2 + (h_t + h_r)^2} \approx d + \frac{(h_t + h_r)^2}{2d}$$

Path difference:
$$\Delta = d_2 - d_1 \approx \frac{2h_t h_r}{d}$$

Phase difference due to path length plus 180° from reflection:
$$\Delta\phi_{total} = \frac{4\pi h_t h_r}{\lambda d} + \pi$$

### Received power — two-ray interference formula

Including ground reflection coefficient $\Gamma \approx -1$:

$$P_r = P_t G_t G_r \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^2 \left|1 - e^{-j\frac{4\pi h_t h_r}{\lambda d}}\right|^2$$

The interference term $\left|1 - e^{-j\psi}\right|^2 = 4\sin^2(\psi/2)$ where $\psi = 4\pi h_t h_r / (\lambda d)$.

**Near field** ($\psi$ large, many oscillations): received power swings between 0 and $4 \times$ the free-space value — a 6 dB peak above Friis and complete nulls.

**Far field** ($\psi \ll 1$, $\sin(\psi/2) \approx \psi/2$):

$$P_r \approx P_t G_t G_r \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^2 \cdot \psi^2 = P_t G_t G_r \frac{h_t^2 h_r^2}{d^4}$$

Note: $\lambda$ cancels out entirely. The far-field two-ray power depends only on heights and distance, **not on frequency**.

### Breakpoint (crossover) distance

The transition from oscillating to monotone $1/d^4$ occurs at:

$$d_b = \frac{4 h_t h_r}{\lambda}$$

Equivalently, $d_b$ is where $\psi = \pi$ (first null of the oscillation), or where the first Fresnel zone just touches the ground.

At the breakpoint the two-ray and free-space predictions agree (to first order). Beyond $d_b$ two-ray gives more loss; the additional loss vs. free-space grows as $20\log_{10}(d/d_b)$ dB.

### Two-ray path loss formula (beyond breakpoint)

$$L_{2ray}[\text{dB}] = 40\log_{10}(d) - 20\log_{10}(h_t) - 20\log_{10}(h_r)$$

Key properties:
- **40 dB/decade** of distance (vs. 20 dB/decade for free space)
- **No frequency dependence** — frequency effects disappear in the far field
- **Height dependence**: raising both antennas by factor $k$ reduces path loss by $40\log_{10}(k)$ — e.g., doubling both heights gives 12 dB improvement

### Breakpoint distance reference table

| Frequency | $h_t = 30$ m, $h_r = 1.5$ m | $h_t = 10$ m, $h_r = 1.5$ m |
|-----------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| 900 MHz | 545 m | 182 m |
| 1.8 GHz | 1090 m | 363 m |
| 2.1 GHz | 1273 m | 424 m |
| 28 GHz | 16,800 m | 5600 m |

Note: at 28 GHz with typical macro heights, $d_b$ exceeds typical cell sizes — mmWave microcells often operate in the free-space regime, which is one reason mmWave path loss behaves closer to $n=2$ than $n=4$ within cells.

### Complete picture: near field vs. far field

$$L(d) = \begin{cases} L_{fs}(d) + \text{oscillations} & d < d_b \\ 40\log_{10}(d) - 20\log_{10}(h_t) - 20\log_{10}(h_r) & d > d_b \end{cases}$$

For careful work, the oscillation peaks in the near field can be found by setting $\psi = 2\pi k$ (constructive) and $\psi = (2k-1)\pi$ (destructive), giving peak/null distances:

$$d_{peak,k} = \frac{4h_t h_r}{(2k-1)\lambda}, \quad d_{null,k} = \frac{4h_t h_r}{2k\lambda}$$

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

### Example 1: Macrocell at 900 MHz — transition from free space to two-ray

Tower $h_t = 30$ m, mobile $h_r = 1.5$ m, 900 MHz ($\lambda = 0.333$ m).

$$d_b = \frac{4 \times 30 \times 1.5}{0.333} = 540 \text{ m}$$

Path loss at 500 m (just inside $d_b$): use FSPL.
$$L_{fs} = 32.45 + 20\log_{10}(0.5) + 20\log_{10}(900) = 32.45 - 6 + 59.1 = 85.5 \text{ dB}$$

Path loss at 2 km (well beyond $d_b$): use two-ray.
$$L_{2ray} = 40\log_{10}(2000) - 20\log_{10}(30) - 20\log_{10}(1.5) = 132 - 29.5 - 3.5 = 99 \text{ dB}$$

FSPL at 2 km: $32.45 + 20\log_{10}(2) + 59.1 = 97.6$ dB.

Two-ray adds 1.4 dB at 2 km. At 5 km: two-ray = $40\log_{10}(5000) - 33 = 146 - 33 = 113$ dB; FSPL = $97.6 + 8 = 105.6$ dB. Difference: 7.4 dB. The gap widens with distance.

### Example 2: Effect of tower height on coverage

Same scenario. Compare $h_t = 30$ m vs. $h_t = 60$ m at 5 km (both beyond $d_b$).

$$L_{30m} = 40\log_{10}(5000) - 20\log_{10}(30) - 20\log_{10}(1.5) = 146 - 29.5 - 3.5 = 113 \text{ dB}$$
$$L_{60m} = 40\log_{10}(5000) - 20\log_{10}(60) - 20\log_{10}(1.5) = 146 - 35.6 - 3.5 = 106.9 \text{ dB}$$

Doubling tower height saves 6 dB in path loss — equivalent to 4× increase in transmit power, or a 40% increase in coverage radius.

### Example 3: Wi-Fi across a parking lot at 2.4 GHz

Both antennas at $h = 2$ m, 2.4 GHz ($\lambda = 0.125$ m).

$$d_b = \frac{4 \times 2 \times 2}{0.125} = 128 \text{ m}$$

Within 128 m: signal oscillates. Null spacing at 50 m:
$$\Delta d_{null} = \frac{\lambda d}{2h_t h_r} = \frac{0.125 \times 50}{2 \times 2 \times 2} = 0.78 \text{ m}$$

Moving less than 1 m can swing received power by 20 dB — explaining the "dead spots" in parking lot Wi-Fi.

### Example 4: Air-to-ground link — two-ray doesn't apply

Aircraft at $h_t = 3000$ m, ground terminal at $h_r = 10$ m, 1 GHz.

$$d_b = \frac{4 \times 3000 \times 10}{0.3} = 400{,}000 \text{ m} = 400 \text{ km}$$

Within 400 km: two-ray model does not apply — use free-space Friis. This is why air-to-ground links at medium-to-high altitudes are well modeled by simple FSPL within their operational range.

### Example 5: Satellite to ground — always free space

LEO satellite at 600 km altitude. Even at $h_r = 100$ m (elevated ground terminal):

$$d_b = \frac{4 \times 600000 \times 100}{0.15} = 1.6 \times 10^9 \text{ m} \gg 600 \text{ km}$$

Satellite links are firmly in the free-space regime. Ground reflection is only relevant for very low elevation angles (below ~5°), where the reflected wave and direct wave path lengths converge.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Applying two-ray before the breakpoint.** The $1/d^4$ law only holds beyond $d_b$. Below the breakpoint, use free-space with oscillation corrections. Naively applying two-ray at short distances predicts far too little loss.

2. **Assuming Gamma = -1 always.** Real ground reflection coefficients depend on soil type, moisture, frequency, and polarization. Wet soil has $|\Gamma| \to 1$ (good approximation). Dry sand: $|\Gamma| \approx 0.5$. Using $\Gamma = -1$ for dry ground overestimates the cancellation effect.

3. **Ignoring rough surface scattering.** Real ground is not flat — it has surface roughness. The Rayleigh criterion $\sigma_h > \lambda / (8\cos\theta_i)$ determines when roughness matters. At higher frequencies (>3 GHz), ground roughness disperses the reflection into a wide lobe rather than a specular reflection, weakening the two-ray cancellation. The true exponent becomes closer to $n = 3$ than $n = 4$.

4. **Using two-ray indoors.** Indoors, dozens of reflections from walls, floor, and ceiling dominate. Two-ray is an outdoor, flat-earth, single-reflection model. Applying it indoors is incorrect — use statistical indoor path loss models.

5. **Forgetting frequency cancellation.** The surprising result that two-ray path loss has no frequency dependence in the far field misleads some engineers into thinking antenna height is all that matters. This is true only beyond $d_b$. Below $d_b$, frequency strongly affects both the oscillation pattern and the path loss.

6. **Misinterpreting "no frequency dependence."** The statement refers to **received power for given antenna gains**. If you compare isotropic antennas at different frequencies, the different effective apertures reintroduce frequency dependence. The two-ray result assumes the same $G_t$ and $G_r$ regardless of frequency.

</section>
