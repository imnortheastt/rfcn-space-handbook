## Why multipath matters

In real environments, signals arrive via multiple paths — direct, reflected from buildings, scattered from vehicles, diffracted around corners. These paths have different lengths, so they arrive with different delays. The receiver sees not one clean copy of the signal, but dozens of superimposed copies shifted in time.

This time dispersion is the reason Wi-Fi uses OFDM, LTE uses a cyclic prefix, and 5G NR has multiple subcarrier spacing options. Without understanding multipath, you cannot understand why these architectural choices were made — or why a single-carrier system that works perfectly in an open field fails catastrophically in a city.

For cloud engineers building systems over radio: multipath determines the coherence bandwidth of the channel. If your signal bandwidth exceeds the coherence bandwidth, different frequency components of your signal fade independently — a fundamentally different failure mode from AWGN.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### Echoes in a canyon

Imagine shouting in a canyon. You hear your voice arrive directly, then echoes returning from the canyon walls at different delays — 50 ms, 100 ms, 200 ms after the original. If you shout a word every 80 ms (125 words per second), the 100 ms echo of word 1 overlaps with word 2 — you can no longer distinguish them. This is **inter-symbol interference (ISI)**.

Radio signals in cities behave identically. A building 150 m away creates an echo arriving 1 μs after the direct path (300 m extra / 3×10⁸ m/s). If your symbol period is 0.5 μs (2 Msps), that echo lands on the next symbol — ISI occurs.

### Frequency selectivity — the colored noise view

Multipath also creates a frequency-selective channel. Different frequencies in your signal experience different amounts of constructive and destructive interference from the echo paths. A channel with 1 μs delay spread has constructive interference at some frequencies and destructive at others, with the pattern repeating approximately every $1/\tau_{rms} \approx 1$ MHz. If your signal bandwidth is 10 MHz, some sub-bands are strong while others are weak — your channel looks like a comb filter, not a flat attenuator.

OFDM handles this by dividing the signal into narrow subcarriers, each narrow enough that the channel appears flat within that subcarrier. Each subcarrier then needs only a simple one-tap equalizer (a single complex multiply) rather than a multi-tap time-domain equalizer.

```
Frequency-selective channel (delay spread = 1 μs):

Signal power (dB)
    0  |---\    /---\    /---
  -10  |    \  /     \  /
  -20  |     \/       \/
       +---+---+---+---+----> Frequency
           1   2   3   4  MHz

Coherence bandwidth Bc ≈ 1/(5 × 1μs) = 200 kHz
Signal bandwidth (10 MHz) >> Bc → frequency selective
Each 200 kHz chunk fades independently
```

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Channel impulse response

The multipath channel is characterized as a linear time-varying filter. Its impulse response is a sum of delayed, scaled, phase-shifted copies of the input:

$$h(\tau, t) = \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} a_i(t) \, \delta(\tau - \tau_i) \, e^{j\phi_i(t)}$$

where:
- $a_i$ = amplitude of path $i$ (fading over time)
- $\tau_i$ = propagation delay of path $i$
- $\phi_i$ = phase of path $i$ (includes carrier phase from path length)
- $N$ = number of significant multipath components

The received signal is the convolution of the transmitted signal with $h(\tau, t)$.

### Power delay profile (PDP)

The power delay profile averages the squared magnitude of the channel impulse response over small-scale fading:

$$P(\tau) = E[|h(\tau)|^2]$$

Commonly modeled as exponentially decaying:
$$P(\tau) = \frac{1}{\bar{\tau}} e^{-\tau/\bar{\tau}}, \quad \tau \geq 0$$

where $\bar{\tau}$ is related to the RMS delay spread.

### Delay spread metrics

**Mean excess delay** — power-weighted mean arrival time (relative to first significant path):
$$\bar{\tau} = \frac{\sum_k P(\tau_k)\, \tau_k}{\sum_k P(\tau_k)}$$

**RMS delay spread** — the standard deviation of arrival times weighted by power:
$$\tau_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_k P(\tau_k)\, \tau_k^2}{\sum_k P(\tau_k)} - \bar{\tau}^2}$$

RMS delay spread is the single most important channel parameter for waveform design. It tells you whether your signal will suffer ISI and how complex your equalizer needs to be.

### Typical delay spreads

| Environment | $\tau_{rms}$ typical | Notes |
|-------------|----------------------|-------|
| Indoor (small office) | 10–50 ns | Short path lengths |
| Indoor (large building, open plan) | 50–200 ns | Longer reflections |
| Urban microcell | 100–500 ns | Street-level reflections |
| Urban macrocell | 1–5 μs | Distant building reflections |
| Suburban | 200 ns–2 μs | Varies with clutter |
| Hilly terrain | 5–20 μs | Distant ridges |
| LEO satellite (near-LOS) | 5–20 ns | Almost no multipath |

### Coherence bandwidth

The coherence bandwidth $B_c$ is the frequency range over which the channel transfer function is approximately constant (correlated). It is inversely related to delay spread:

$$B_c \approx \frac{1}{5\tau_{rms}} \quad \text{(0.9 correlation definition)}$$

$$B_c \approx \frac{1}{2\pi\tau_{rms}} \quad \text{(0.5 correlation definition)}$$

The 0.5-correlation definition is more commonly used in system design. The 0.9 definition gives a more conservative (smaller) $B_c$.

### Flat vs. frequency-selective fading

| Signal bandwidth $B_s$ vs. $B_c$ | Fading type | Effect on signal |
|----------------------------------|-------------|------------------|
| $B_s \ll B_c$ | Flat (non-selective) | All frequencies fade together; no ISI |
| $B_s \gg B_c$ | Frequency-selective | Different frequencies fade differently; ISI occurs |

Flat fading means the entire signal bandwidth sees the same channel coefficient — simple to handle. Frequency-selective fading means the channel acts as a filter with a complex frequency response — requires equalization or OFDM.

### ISI threshold

ISI occurs when the delay spread exceeds the symbol duration $T_s = 1/R_s$:

$$\tau_{rms} > T_s$$

Maximum ISI-free symbol rate for a single-carrier system:
$$R_s < \frac{1}{\tau_{rms}}$$

For urban macro ($\tau_{rms} = 3$ μs): maximum ISI-free rate = 333 ksps. LTE uses 7.68 Mcps physical layer — 23× above this threshold — which is why LTE needs a cyclic prefix of 4.7–16.7 μs.

### OFDM and the cyclic prefix

OFDM solves the multipath problem by:

1. **Dividing** the wideband signal into $N$ narrow subcarriers, each with bandwidth $B_s/N \ll B_c$. Each subcarrier sees flat fading.
2. **Adding** a cyclic prefix (CP) of duration $T_{cp} \geq \tau_{max}$ (maximum excess delay). The CP absorbs the ISI at the cost of overhead.
3. **Using** a single complex coefficient per subcarrier for equalization (divide received subcarrier by channel estimate — one multiplication).

CP overhead fraction: $T_{cp} / (T_{cp} + T_{symbol})$.

LTE normal CP: $T_{cp} = 4.7$ μs, $T_{symbol} = 66.7$ μs → overhead = 6.6%.
LTE extended CP: $T_{cp} = 16.7$ μs → overhead = 20% (for high-delay-spread environments).

## Apply

### Example 1: Why Wi-Fi uses OFDM

Indoor office, $\tau_{rms} = 50$ ns.

Coherence bandwidth:
$$B_c = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 50 \times 10^{-9}} = 3.2 \text{ MHz}$$

Wi-Fi 802.11n uses 20 MHz bandwidth — frequency selective ($20 \gg 3.2$ MHz).

OFDM subcarrier spacing: 312.5 kHz (each subcarrier bandwidth $\ll$ 3.2 MHz, so each sees flat fading).

CP duration: 800 ns (covers the 50 ns RMS spread with margin for 200–300 ns max excess delay).

Single-carrier alternative: would need a 7-tap equalizer ($T_s = 1/20\text{ MHz} = 50$ ns ≈ $\tau_{rms}$, so ISI spans ~6–7 symbol periods). OFDM is simpler.

### Example 2: LTE design parameters driven by urban multipath

Urban macro, $\tau_{rms} = 3$ μs, $\tau_{max} = 10$ μs.

Coherence bandwidth (0.5 correlation):
$$B_c = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 3 \times 10^{-6}} = 53 \text{ kHz}$$

LTE subcarrier spacing: 15 kHz $\ll$ 53 kHz → each subcarrier is flat-fading. ✓

LTE normal CP: 4.7 μs > typical $\tau_{rms}$, but $< \tau_{max}$ in hilly terrain. For rural/hilly deployments with $\tau_{max}$ up to 16 μs, the extended CP (16.7 μs) is used — at the cost of 20% overhead reduction in throughput.

### Example 3: Max symbol rate in a subway tunnel

Subway tunnel, $\tau_{rms} = 500$ ns (hard reflections from tunnel walls).

Maximum ISI-free single-carrier rate:
$$R_s < \frac{1}{500 \times 10^{-9}} = 2 \text{ Msps}$$

At 16-QAM (4 bits/symbol): max data rate without equalizer = 8 Mbps.

For LTE/5G: use OFDM with 15 kHz subcarriers and CP. No ISI concern because OFDM absorbs the delay spread.

### Example 4: Satellite channel — almost flat

LEO satellite at 30° elevation, $\tau_{rms} \approx 15$ ns (near-LOS, very little scattering).

Coherence bandwidth:
$$B_c = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 15 \times 10^{-9}} = 10.6 \text{ MHz}$$

A 5G NTN NB-IoT channel with 200 kHz bandwidth: $200 \text{ kHz} \ll 10.6 \text{ MHz}$ → flat fading. The satellite channel for narrowband IoT is essentially AWGN — delay spread is not the design driver (Doppler and propagation delay are).

### Example 5: Computing delay spread from a measured PDP

Measured PDP with three dominant paths:

| Path | Delay (ns) | Relative power (dB) | Linear power |
|------|------------|---------------------|--------------|
| 0 | 0 | 0 | 1.0 |
| 1 | 100 | -3 | 0.5 |
| 2 | 400 | -10 | 0.1 |

Mean excess delay:
$$\bar{\tau} = \frac{1.0 \times 0 + 0.5 \times 100 + 0.1 \times 400}{1.0 + 0.5 + 0.1} = \frac{90}{1.6} = 56.25 \text{ ns}$$

Second moment:
$$\overline{\tau^2} = \frac{1.0 \times 0 + 0.5 \times 10000 + 0.1 \times 160000}{1.6} = \frac{21000}{1.6} = 13125 \text{ ns}^2$$

RMS delay spread:
$$\tau_{rms} = \sqrt{13125 - 56.25^2} = \sqrt{13125 - 3164} = \sqrt{9961} = 99.8 \text{ ns}$$

Coherence bandwidth: $1/(2\pi \times 100\text{ ns}) = 1.6$ MHz.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing $\tau_{rms}$ with $\tau_{max}$.** RMS delay spread is a power-weighted statistic. Maximum excess delay $\tau_{max}$ (the delay of the last significant path) is what determines the required CP length. $\tau_{max}$ can be 3–5× larger than $\tau_{rms}$ for exponential PDPs.

2. **Assuming flat fading because the channel is narrowband.** "Narrowband" relative to signal bandwidth is not the same as narrowband relative to coherence bandwidth. A 200 kHz IoT signal in an urban macro channel ($B_c = 53$ kHz) is frequency-selective even though 200 kHz seems narrow.

3. **Forgetting power weighting in delay spread calculation.** Weak late paths contribute little to $\tau_{rms}$. A path arriving at 10 μs with -40 dB power barely affects the delay spread calculation. Noise paths included in the sum corrupt the result — threshold the PDP before computing.

4. **OFDM solves ISI but not fading.** OFDM converts frequency-selective fading into flat fading per subcarrier — it does not eliminate fading. Each subcarrier still fades as Rayleigh or Rician. OFDM simply makes equalization tractable; you still need coding, diversity, or MIMO to combat the remaining flat fading.

5. **Single $\tau_{rms}$ value for a complex environment.** Delay spread is highly location-dependent. A model giving $\tau_{rms} = 3$ μs for "urban" is a median — individual locations may range from 0.5 to 15 μs. Design for the worst-case deployment scenario, not the median.

6. **Ignoring inter-carrier interference (ICI) from Doppler.** OFDM orthogonality depends on channel being static over one OFDM symbol period. Fast mobility (high Doppler) causes the channel to vary within a symbol, breaking orthogonality and introducing ICI. This is the main challenge for OFDM in high-speed vehicular and satellite scenarios.
