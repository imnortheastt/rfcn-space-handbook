## Why multipath matters?

In real environments, signals arrive via multiple paths — direct, reflected, scattered, diffracted. These paths have different delays, creating copies of your signal that arrive at different times. This causes inter-symbol interference, frequency-selective fading, and determines whether your system needs equalization or OFDM.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine shouting in a canyon. You hear echoes arriving at different times. Radio signals do the same in cities — bouncing off buildings, arriving as ghosts of the original. If echoes arrive within one symbol period, they blur the symbol. If they span multiple symbols, they cause inter-symbol interference (ISI).

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Channel impulse response

The multipath channel is characterized by:

$$h(\tau) = \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} a_i \delta(\tau - \tau_i) e^{j\phi_i}$$

- $a_i$ = amplitude of path i
- $\tau_i$ = delay of path i
- $\phi_i$ = phase of path i (from path length and carrier)

### Power delay profile (PDP)

Average power vs. delay:

$$P(\tau) = E[|h(\tau)|^2]$$

Often modeled as exponentially decaying:

$$P(\tau) = \frac{1}{\tau_{rms}} e^{-\tau/\tau_{rms}}$$

### Delay spread metrics

**Mean excess delay:**
$$\bar{\tau} = \frac{\sum_k P(\tau_k) \tau_k}{\sum_k P(\tau_k)}$$

**RMS delay spread:**
$$\tau_{rms} = \sqrt{\overline{\tau^2} - (\bar{\tau})^2}$$

where $\overline{\tau^2} = \frac{\sum_k P(\tau_k) \tau_k^2}{\sum_k P(\tau_k)}$

### Coherence bandwidth

Frequency range over which channel is approximately flat:

$$B_c \approx \frac{1}{5\tau_{rms}} \quad \text{(0.9 correlation)}$$

$$B_c \approx \frac{1}{2\pi\tau_{rms}} \quad \text{(0.5 correlation)}$$

### Frequency-selective vs. flat fading

| Condition | Type | Effect |
|-----------|------|--------|
| $B_s << B_c$ | Flat fading | All frequencies fade together |
| $B_s >> B_c$ | Frequency-selective | Different frequencies fade differently |

where $B_s$ = signal bandwidth.

### Typical delay spreads

| Environment | τ_rms |
|-------------|-------|
| Indoor (small office) | 10-50 ns |
| Indoor (large building) | 50-200 ns |
| Urban cellular | 1-5 μs |
| Suburban | 200 ns - 2 μs |
| Hilly terrain | up to 20 μs |

### ISI condition

ISI occurs when delay spread exceeds symbol time:

$$\tau_{rms} > T_s$$

Maximum ISI-free symbol rate:

$$R_s < \frac{1}{\tau_{rms}}$$

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Wi-Fi indoors**

τ_rms = 50 ns (typical office).

Coherence bandwidth:
$$B_c = \frac{1}{5 \times 50 \times 10^{-9}} = 4 \text{ MHz}$$

Wi-Fi uses 20 MHz bandwidth — frequency-selective.

This is why Wi-Fi uses OFDM with 312.5 kHz subcarriers (< 4 MHz).

**Example: LTE design**

Urban τ_rms = 3 μs.

$$B_c = \frac{1}{5 \times 3 \times 10^{-6}} = 67 \text{ kHz}$$

LTE uses 15 kHz subcarriers (< 67 kHz) — each subcarrier sees flat fading.

Cyclic prefix: 4.7 μs (normal) — covers 3 μs delay spread with margin.

**Example: Single-carrier limit**

τ_rms = 1 μs.

Maximum ISI-free symbol rate: 1 Msps.

For higher rates without OFDM: need equalization.

10 Msps with 1 μs spread: equalizer needs ~10 taps.

**Example: Satellite link**

LEO satellite, τ_rms ≈ 10 ns (near-LOS).

$$B_c = \frac{1}{5 \times 10 \times 10^{-9}} = 20 \text{ MHz}$$

Satellite channels are essentially flat-fading up to ~20 MHz bandwidth.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Ignoring delay spread.** A system designed for AWGN fails in multipath. Always characterize the channel.

2. **Confusing coherence bandwidth and signal bandwidth.** Bc is channel property; Bs is system design choice.

3. **Single τ_rms value.** Delay spread varies with environment and link geometry. Use appropriate model.

4. **Forgetting power weighting.** Delay spread uses power-weighted delays. Weak late paths contribute less.

5. **OFDM solves everything.** OFDM converts frequency-selective to flat per subcarrier — but you still need CP > τ_max.
