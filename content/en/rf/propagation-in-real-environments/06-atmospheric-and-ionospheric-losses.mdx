## Why atmospheric effects matter

Your satellite link works perfectly on a clear day. A tropical thunderstorm 200 km away reduces the signal by 30 dB — the link goes dark. A 5G mmWave small cell delivers gigabit speeds over 150 m. At 200 m it drops to unusable — not because of path loss, but because oxygen absorbs 15 dB/km at 60 GHz. An L-band GPS signal arrives with a timing error of 10 nanoseconds, translating to 3 meters of ranging error — from the ionosphere, not hardware.

These are atmospheric and ionospheric effects: phenomena that have nothing to do with buildings or terrain but impose hard frequency- and weather-dependent limits on any radio link above a few gigahertz (or, for ionospheric effects, below ~3 GHz).

For cloud engineers learning RF, this lesson draws a direct analogy: atmospheric losses are like variable network latency and packet loss injected by the physical medium — not by the endpoints, not by the path geometry, but by the transmission medium itself. The difference is that you can model and budget for them with ITU-R standards, just as you would model packet loss in a WAN link budget.

## Intuition

### Three distinct impairment mechanisms

```
Frequency bands and dominant atmospheric effects:

0.1 GHz ─── 1 GHz ─── 3 GHz ─── 10 GHz ─── 30 GHz ─── 100 GHz ─── 300 GHz
    │              │           │           │             │              │
    ├── IONOSPHERE ─────────── │           │             │              │
    │   (Faraday, delay,       │           │             │              │
    │   scintillation)         │           │             │              │
                               │           │             │              │
                               ├── RAIN ATTENUATION ──────────────────▶│
                               │   (dominates above ~3 GHz)            │
                                           │             │              │
                                           ├─ GASEOUS ───────────────▶ │
                                           │  ABSORPTION               │
                                           │  (O₂ peak: 60 GHz)        │
                                           │  (H₂O peaks: 22, 183 GHz) │
```

**Rain attenuation**: Raindrops are liquid water spheroids with diameter comparable to mm-wave wavelengths. They scatter and absorb energy. Attenuation is catastrophic above 10 GHz and negligible below 3 GHz.

**Gaseous absorption**: O₂ and H₂O molecules have quantum resonance modes at specific frequencies. At resonance, the molecule absorbs photon energy. The 60 GHz O₂ absorption band is so strong it limits terrestrial links to ~1 km — which is exactly why 5G uses it for dense small-cell frequency reuse without interference between cells.

**Ionospheric effects**: The ionosphere (60–1000 km altitude) contains free electrons that interact with RF signals below ~3 GHz. Electron density varies with solar activity, time of day, latitude, and season. Effects include group delay (GPS errors), Faraday rotation (polarization rotation), and scintillation (amplitude/phase fluctuations).

### The rain rate distribution insight

Rain attenuation cannot be budgeted at average rain rate — that would be meaningless. What matters is the statistical distribution: what rain rate is exceeded for only 0.01%, 0.1%, or 1% of an average year. A "1% exceedance" rain rate in London (≈ 10 mm/hr) is very different from Kuala Lumpur (≈ 50 mm/hr). Satellite link budgets are fundamentally statistical: "99% link availability" means the margin exceeds rain attenuation 99% of the time — and that 1% of outage is engineered in.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Rain attenuation — ITU-R P.838 model

Total rain attenuation along a path:

$$A_{rain} = \gamma_R \cdot d_{eff} \quad [\text{dB}]$$

Specific attenuation (dB/km):

$$\gamma_R = k \cdot R^\alpha$$

where $R$ = rain rate (mm/hr), $k$ and $\alpha$ are frequency- and polarization-dependent empirical coefficients from ITU-R P.838.

**ITU-R P.838 coefficients (horizontal polarization, selected frequencies):**

| Frequency | $k_H$ | $\alpha_H$ | $\gamma_R$ at 5 mm/hr | $\gamma_R$ at 25 mm/hr | $\gamma_R$ at 50 mm/hr |
|-----------|--------|------------|----------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| 6 GHz | 0.00175 | 1.308 | 0.012 dB/km | 0.08 dB/km | 0.19 dB/km |
| 10 GHz | 0.0101 | 1.276 | 0.063 dB/km | 0.42 dB/km | 0.98 dB/km |
| 20 GHz | 0.0751 | 1.099 | 0.34 dB/km | 1.85 dB/km | 3.87 dB/km |
| 30 GHz | 0.187 | 1.021 | 0.95 dB/km | 4.82 dB/km | 9.74 dB/km |
| 40 GHz | 0.350 | 0.939 | 1.59 dB/km | 7.26 dB/km | 13.9 dB/km |
| 100 GHz | 1.12 | 0.669 | 3.36 dB/km | 11.6 dB/km | 19.5 dB/km |

Key insight: the exponent $\alpha \approx 1$ near 30 GHz, so attenuation is roughly proportional to rain rate. At lower frequencies $\alpha > 1$ (super-linear); the difference between light and heavy rain is more dramatic.

### Effective path length for satellite links

Rain is not uniformally distributed. For a satellite path:

$$d_{eff} = \frac{L_S \cdot r_p}{\sin(\theta)}$$

where:
- $\theta$ = elevation angle of satellite
- $L_S$ = vertical extent of rain cell (≈ 5 km for tropical, ≈ 3 km for temperate)
- $r_p$ = path reduction factor from ITU-R P.618:

$$r_p = \frac{1}{1 + 0.78\sqrt{L_S \cdot \gamma_R / f} - 0.38(1 - e^{-2L_S})}$$

For a GEO satellite at 20° elevation from London through 3 km rain height, the effective rain path can reach 8–12 km of rain even though the vertical rain column is only 3 km — because of the slant geometry.

### Gaseous absorption — ITU-R P.676

Total gaseous attenuation:

$$A_{atm} = (\gamma_O + \gamma_W) \cdot d \quad [\text{dB}]$$

where $\gamma_O$ and $\gamma_W$ are specific attenuations from oxygen and water vapor (dB/km), computed from the P.676 line-by-line model.

**Zenith attenuation at sea level (standard conditions):**

| Frequency | $\gamma_O$ (dB/km) | $\gamma_W$ (dB/km) | Total (dB/km) | Notes |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|----------------|-------|
| 10 GHz | 0.007 | 0.002 | 0.009 | Negligible |
| 22.2 GHz | 0.006 | 0.18 | 0.186 | H₂O resonance |
| 30 GHz | 0.009 | 0.08 | 0.089 | Ka-band |
| 60 GHz | 15.0 | 0.07 | 15.07 | O₂ resonance |
| 90 GHz | 0.14 | 0.07 | 0.21 | W-band window |
| 183 GHz | 0.3 | ~50 | ~50 | H₂O resonance |

The 60 GHz absorption makes terrestrial line-of-sight range self-limiting to ~1 km — adjacent 60 GHz small cells cannot interfere with each other even with high-gain antennas. This is the entire frequency reuse rationale for 5G WiGig/802.11ad.

For satellite links (looking up through the full atmosphere), the **zenith total absorption** is:
- 20 GHz: ~0.5 dB
- 30 GHz: ~0.4 dB
- 60 GHz: ~11 dB (total for zenith path through troposphere)

### Wet antenna effect

A practical source often missed in textbooks: when rain is falling, water films form on antenna reflectors and radomes. This adds:
- Dry radome: 0.1–0.3 dB
- Wet radome (heavy rain): 1–3 dB extra

At Ka-band, wet antenna can add 2–4 dB on top of the rain attenuation calculated for the path. ITU-R P.618 includes this implicitly in its margin recommendations.

### Ionospheric effects (below ~3 GHz)

The ionosphere is a plasma layer containing free electrons. The electron density $N_e$ (electrons/m³) determines the magnitude of all ionospheric effects.

**Faraday rotation** — polarization plane rotates as the wave traverses the ionosphere:

$$\Omega = \frac{2.36 \times 10^4}{f^2} \int_0^L N_e B \cos\theta \, dl \quad [\text{rad}]$$

where $B$ is the geomagnetic field strength (T), $\theta$ is the angle between the wave direction and the field. Rotation scales as $f^{-2}$, so doubling frequency reduces rotation by 4×.

**Typical Faraday rotation values:**

| Frequency | Daytime (mid-lat) | Solar max | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|-------|
| 0.1 GHz | 108 rad (17 turns!) | 1000 rad | Unusable linear polarization |
| 0.5 GHz | 4.3 rad | 40 rad | Severe |
| 1.5 GHz (GPS L1) | 0.5 rad (28°) | 4.5 rad | Requires circular pol or correction |
| 3 GHz | 0.12 rad (7°) | 1.1 rad | Usually manageable |
| 10 GHz | 0.004 rad | 0.04 rad | Negligible |

**Mitigation**: Use circular polarization (immune to rotation angle), or apply ionospheric correction using a model (NeQuick, IRI).

**Ionospheric group delay** — electrons slow the group velocity of the wave:

$$\Delta t = \frac{40.3}{c \cdot f^2} \int N_e \, dl = \frac{40.3 \cdot TEC}{c \cdot f^2} \quad [\text{s}]$$

where TEC = Total Electron Content (electrons/m²), measured in TECU (1 TECU = $10^{16}$ el/m²). Typical daytime TEC: 10–50 TECU.

At GPS L1 (1.575 GHz): $\Delta t \approx 7–30$ ns, corresponding to 2–9 m of ranging error — significant for precision navigation.

**Ionospheric scintillation** — rapid amplitude and phase fluctuations caused by electron density irregularities:
- Worst at equatorial regions (±15° latitude) around sunset local time
- Also severe at high latitudes during geomagnetic storms
- Characterized by S4 index (amplitude scintillation) and $\sigma_\phi$ (phase scintillation)
- S4 > 0.5 is strong scintillation; S4 = 1 means deep fades to near-zero signal
- Most severe at UHF (400 MHz – 2 GHz); negligible above 6 GHz

### Total path loss for a satellite link

$$L_{total} = L_{FS} + A_{rain} + A_{atm} + A_{ion} + A_{misc}$$

where:
- $L_{FS}$ = free-space path loss (dominant term)
- $A_{rain}$ = rain attenuation (0–35 dB depending on frequency, rain rate, elevation)
- $A_{atm}$ = gaseous attenuation (0.3–2 dB for Ka/Ku zenith, more at low elevation)
- $A_{ion}$ = ionospheric delay/absorption (dB-equivalent; usually < 0.5 dB above 1 GHz)
- $A_{misc}$ = wet antenna, pointing error, radome (~1–3 dB total)

## Apply

### Example 1: Ka-band GEO satellite, tropical region

Link: 30 GHz downlink, elevation 30°, tropical Brazil (rain rate exceeded 1% of year: 65 mm/hr).

**Step 1 — Specific rain attenuation:**
$$\gamma_R = 0.187 \times 65^{1.021} = 0.187 \times 68.5 = 12.8 \text{ dB/km}$$

**Step 2 — Rain height and effective path:**
Rain height in tropics: $h_R = 5$ km.
Slant path through rain: $L_S = (h_R - h_s)/\sin(30°) = 5/0.5 = 10$ km.
Path reduction factor (simplified): $r_p \approx 0.62$ for 10 km at this rain rate.
Effective path: $d_{eff} = 10 \times 0.62 = 6.2$ km.

**Step 3 — Rain attenuation:**
$$A_{rain} = 12.8 \times 6.2 = 79 \text{ dB}$$

This is extreme — a tropical 1% rain event on a 30° elevation Ka-band link can produce 20–80 dB of attenuation. In practice, Ka-band systems in tropical regions target 99% availability (not 99.9%), use ACM (adaptive coding and modulation) to survive 10–20 dB rain events, and rely on site diversity for high-availability applications.

**Step 4 — Gaseous attenuation:**
At 30 GHz, zenith: 0.089 dB/km. For slant path at 30° elevation: effective atmospheric thickness ≈ $10/\sin(30°) = 20$ km.
$A_{atm} = 0.089 \times 20 = 1.8$ dB.

**Step 5 — Total excess loss:** Rain (for 1% exceedance) + gaseous = 79 + 1.8 ≈ 81 dB. This dominates any other link budget term. Ka-band in the tropics is only viable with very high EIRP, ACM, and appropriate availability targets.

### Example 2: Ka-band in temperate Europe (UK)

Same 30 GHz, elevation 30°, UK (1% exceedance rain rate ≈ 22 mm/hr).

$$\gamma_R = 0.187 \times 22^{1.021} = 4.23 \text{ dB/km}$$

Rain height: $h_R = 3$ km, slant path = 6 km, $r_p \approx 0.7$, $d_{eff} = 4.2$ km.

$$A_{rain} = 4.23 \times 4.2 = 17.8 \text{ dB}$$

This is much more manageable. A typical Ku/Ka satellite in Europe budgets 10–20 dB for 99% rain availability — achievable with moderate EIRP and ACM.

### Example 3: 5G at 60 GHz for indoor backhaul

Inter-floor backhaul link, 100 m distance, indoor environment.

Gaseous absorption at 60 GHz: 15 dB/km.

Path loss contribution from O₂: $15 \times 0.1 = 1.5$ dB. Acceptable for 100 m link.

For 300 m (three floors in a large building): $15 \times 0.3 = 4.5$ dB from O₂ alone, before any building material losses. Beyond ~500 m, the 60 GHz O₂ window effectively prevents terrestrial operation without extremely high-gain directional antennas.

### Example 4: L-band satellite terminal — ionospheric margin

LEO satellite at 1.6 GHz (Iridium-like), tropical region, daytime.

Faraday rotation at 1.6 GHz: $\Omega \approx 0.44$ rad = 25°. This is tolerable with circular polarization but causes ~0.3 dB of effective loss due to polarization mismatch if linear polarization is used.

Ionospheric scintillation (S4 = 0.4, moderate): peak amplitude variation ±3 dB. Link budget must include a 3–5 dB ionospheric scintillation margin for equatorial terminals.

### Example 5: Link availability decision — site diversity

High-availability Ka-band enterprise link in Malaysia, 99.99% required.

Rain rate exceeded 0.01% of year in Malaysia: ≈ 100+ mm/hr.

At 30 GHz, effective rain attenuation at 0.01% exceedance: 60–80 dB. No practical link budget can absorb this with a single site.

**Site diversity**: Two ground stations separated by 20+ km. Probability of simultaneous heavy rain at both sites is much lower (heavy rain cells are typically 5–10 km diameter). The joint outage probability drops from 0.01% to below 0.001%. This is the only viable engineering solution for very-high-availability Ka-band in tropical regions — implemented in practice by VSAT network operators with multiple hub sites.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Budgeting at average rain rate.** Average rain is irrelevant. Link budgets use exceedance statistics (0.01%, 0.1%, 1% of year). The rain rate exceeded 1% of a year in London (~10 mm/hr) vs. Singapore (~65 mm/hr) determines whether Ka-band is viable in that region.

2. **Ignoring elevation angle.** Rain attenuation scales inversely with $\sin(\theta)$. A satellite at 10° elevation has 5× more rain path than one at 90° (zenith). GEO satellites visible at low elevation from high-latitude sites suffer dramatically more rain attenuation.

3. **Forgetting wet antenna and radome.** Rain attenuation calculations model the atmosphere. They do not include the water film on the antenna. A wet dish radome adds 1–4 dB at Ka-band — this must be in the link budget as a separate term.

4. **Ignoring gaseous absorption for long mmWave links.** For a 1 km 60 GHz backhaul, the free-space path loss is roughly 140 dB but O₂ adds another 15 dB — 10% more loss from gas alone. Engineers used to sub-6 GHz planning who don't know the absorption spectrum will design links that fail in deployment.

5. **Assuming ionospheric effects are GPS-only.** Faraday rotation affects any linearly polarized link below ~3 GHz — including cellular, satellite IoT (Iridium, Orbcomm), and amateur satellite. A linearly polarized patch antenna on a 1.5 GHz NTN terminal may experience 30–60° of polarization rotation, causing 3–6 dB of polarization loss on a circularly polarized satellite signal.

6. **Not accounting for seasonal and solar cycle variation.** Ionospheric TEC is 3–5× higher at solar maximum than minimum. A satellite system designed during solar minimum that experiences a solar maximum year may see Faraday rotation 3–5× higher than modeled. ITU-R models include solar activity parameters — use worst-case values for satellite link design.

7. **Using the wrong rain availability model for the region.** ITU-R P.837 provides global rain rate statistics. Regional variations within a country can be large — a satellite operator serving both the UK and West Africa with the same Ka-band margin will systematically underserve the African terminals.
