<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why diversity?

Fading causes deep signal nulls — moments when the channel gain drops by 20-40 dB. A single link fails during these nulls. Diversity provides multiple independent copies of the signal, so when one path fades, another likely doesn't.

Diversity is the fundamental technique for reliable wireless communication.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine betting on a single coin flip versus averaging ten independent flips. One flip can be unlucky; ten flips average out. Diversity does the same for fading channels — multiple independent "bets" on the channel smooth out the randomness.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Spatial diversity

Use multiple antennas separated by λ/2 or more.

**Receive diversity (SIMO):** Multiple RX antennas combine signals. Fading at one antenna is independent of fading at another if spacing ≥ λ/2.

**Transmit diversity (MISO):** Multiple TX antennas send redundant copies. Space-time codes (Alamouti) achieve full diversity without channel knowledge at TX.

**Full MIMO:** Both TX and RX have multiple antennas. Achieves both diversity and multiplexing gains.

### Time diversity

Transmit the same symbol at different times.

**Requirement:** Time separation > coherence time $T_c$.

**Implementation:** Interleaving + coding across time slots.

**Limitation:** Introduces delay. Not suitable for real-time applications with slow fading.

### Frequency diversity

Transmit the same symbol on different frequencies.

**Requirement:** Frequency separation > coherence bandwidth $B_c$.

**Implementation:** OFDM with interleaving across subcarriers, frequency hopping.

**Relationship:** $B_c \approx 1/(2\pi\tau_{rms})$ where $\tau_{rms}$ is delay spread.

### Polarization diversity

Use orthogonal polarizations (H/V or LHCP/RHCP).

**Independence:** Cross-polar discrimination (XPD) typically 6-15 dB in urban environments.

**Benefit:** Two independent paths without extra antenna spacing. Useful for compact devices.

### Angle/pattern diversity

Antennas with different radiation patterns see different multipath clusters.

**Implementation:** Switching between directional antennas, or using antennas with orthogonal patterns.

### Cooperative diversity

Multiple users relay each other's signals.

**Virtual MIMO:** Single-antenna devices form distributed antenna array.

**Protocols:** Amplify-and-forward, decode-and-forward.

### Comparison

| Type | Independence requirement | Overhead | Typical use case |
|------|--------------------------|----------|------------------|
| Spatial | λ/2 spacing | Hardware | Base stations, flagship phones |
| Time | > $T_c$ separation | Delay | Slow fading, FEC |
| Frequency | > $B_c$ separation | Bandwidth | OFDM, frequency hopping |
| Polarization | Orthogonal pol | Hardware | Compact devices, satellite |
| Cooperative | Separate nodes | Coordination | IoT, relaying |

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 5G NR antenna configuration**

Smartphone with 4 antennas at 3.5 GHz (λ ≈ 86 mm):
- λ/2 = 43 mm — achievable spacing in phone form factor
- 2 antennas at each end of phone, cross-polarized
- Combines spatial and polarization diversity

**Example: Wi-Fi 6E**

Operating at 6 GHz (λ = 50 mm):
- Indoor $B_c$ ≈ 10-20 MHz (delay spread ~50 ns)
- 160 MHz channel spans 8-16 coherence bandwidths
- OFDM with LDPC coding across subcarriers provides frequency diversity

**Example: Satellite communications**

LEO satellite link:
- Polarization diversity: LHCP/RHCP
- Time diversity: interleaving over 100+ ms (handles Doppler variation)
- No spatial diversity at handheld terminal (single antenna)

**Example: LTE-M IoT**

Narrowband (180 kHz channel):
- No frequency diversity within channel
- Relies on time diversity via repetitions
- Up to 2048 repetitions for coverage enhancement

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Assuming independence.** Diversity requires independent fading paths. Correlated paths (e.g., antennas too close, frequencies within $B_c$) provide reduced diversity gain.

2. **Confusing diversity with multiplexing.** Diversity improves reliability (same data on multiple paths). Multiplexing improves rate (different data on multiple paths). They trade off.

3. **Ignoring cost.** Each diversity branch costs hardware (spatial), bandwidth (frequency), or latency (time). Diminishing returns after 2-4 branches.

4. **Over-engineering.** High-SNR scenarios don't need much diversity — errors are rare anyway. Diversity matters most at moderate SNR.

5. **Forgetting combining method.** How branches combine matters. MRC requires channel knowledge; selection combining doesn't but is suboptimal.
