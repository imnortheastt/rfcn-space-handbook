<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why diversity gain?

Adding antennas improves reliability — but by how much? Diversity gain quantifies this: it's the SNR reduction you can afford while maintaining the same error rate. A 10 dB diversity gain means you can operate at 10 dB lower SNR with the same BER.

This metric drives antenna system design decisions.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

In Rayleigh fading, deep fades (where signal drops 20+ dB) occur with probability ~1%. With two independent branches, both fading simultaneously has probability ~(1%)² = 0.01%. The tail of the error probability shrinks dramatically.

Diversity gain captures this tail compression.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Definition

Diversity gain compares outage probability (or BER) curves:

$$G_d = \lim_{\text{SNR} \to \infty} \frac{\log P_e^{(1)}}{\log P_e^{(L)}}$$

For L-branch diversity with order-d diversity:

$$P_e \propto \text{SNR}^{-d}$$

Without diversity (d = 1): $P_e \propto 1/\text{SNR}$
With L-branch MRC (d = L): $P_e \propto 1/\text{SNR}^L$

### Combining techniques

**Selection Combining (SC)**
- Select branch with highest SNR
- Simplest implementation
- Diversity gain: L (same as MRC asymptotically)
- 1-3 dB loss vs. MRC at finite SNR

**Maximal Ratio Combining (MRC)**
- Weight each branch by its channel coefficient
- Optimal linear combiner
- Output SNR = sum of branch SNRs
- Requires channel knowledge

**Equal Gain Combining (EGC)**
- Co-phase branches, sum with equal weights
- 1 dB loss vs. MRC
- Simpler than MRC (no amplitude weighting)

### SNR gain from combining

For L i.i.d. Rayleigh branches with average SNR $\bar{\gamma}$:

| Combining | Mean output SNR | Gain vs. no diversity |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| MRC | $L \bar{\gamma}$ | L (array gain) |
| EGC | $\bar{\gamma}(1 + (L-1)\pi/4)$ | ~0.88L |
| SC | $\bar{\gamma} \sum_{k=1}^{L} 1/k$ | ~$\ln L + 0.577$ |

### BER improvement

For BPSK over Rayleigh fading:

No diversity: $P_b \approx \frac{1}{4\bar{\gamma}}$ (high SNR)

L-branch MRC: $P_b \approx \binom{2L-1}{L} \frac{1}{(4\bar{\gamma})^L}$

At $\bar{\gamma}$ = 20 dB:
- No diversity: $P_b \approx 2.5 \times 10^{-3}$
- 2-branch MRC: $P_b \approx 1.5 \times 10^{-5}$
- 4-branch MRC: $P_b \approx 4 \times 10^{-9}$

### Effective diversity gain

Real systems achieve less than ideal diversity gain due to:
- Correlation between branches
- Imperfect channel estimation
- Implementation losses

Effective gain: $G_{eff} = G_{ideal} \times \rho_{corr} \times \rho_{CSI}$

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 2-branch receive diversity**

Target BER: $10^{-4}$

No diversity: requires SNR ≈ 17 dB
2-branch MRC: requires SNR ≈ 8 dB

Diversity gain: 17 - 8 = 9 dB

This 9 dB can translate to:
- 2.8× range extension (free space)
- 9 dB lower TX power
- Move from 90% to 99.9% coverage

**Example: Wi-Fi MIMO receiver**

2×2 MIMO at 5 GHz:
- Spatial correlation: ρ = 0.3 (0.5λ spacing)
- CSI accuracy: 0.9 (realistic estimation)
- Ideal diversity gain: 3 dB (2 branches)
- Effective gain: 3 × 0.85 × 0.9 ≈ 2.3 dB

Still substantial improvement over SISO.

**Example: Diminishing returns**

Adding branches beyond 2-4:

| Branches | BER at 10 dB SNR | Incremental gain |
|----------|------------------|------------------|
| 1 | $10^{-2}$ | baseline |
| 2 | $10^{-4}$ | 2 orders |
| 4 | $10^{-7}$ | 3 orders |
| 8 | $10^{-12}$ | 5 orders |

Doubling antennas beyond 4 adds ~1-2 dB. Cost usually not justified.

**Example: Outage probability**

For 1% outage (99% reliability):

1 branch: requires 20 dB SNR margin
2 branches: requires 10 dB SNR margin
4 branches: requires 5 dB SNR margin

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing array gain with diversity gain.** Array gain is the increase in mean SNR. Diversity gain is the improvement in the SNR distribution tail. MRC provides both; selection combining provides only diversity gain.

2. **Assuming high-SNR regime.** Diversity gain definitions use asymptotic expressions. At finite SNR (practical systems), the gains are smaller.

3. **Ignoring correlation.** Correlated branches provide reduced effective diversity. Two antennas with ρ = 0.9 correlation behave almost like one antenna.

4. **Forgetting implementation cost.** Each branch needs RF chain, ADC, and processing. 4→8 branches rarely worth the cost.

5. **Using wrong combining.** SC is simple but leaves ~2-3 dB on the table vs. MRC. Choose based on hardware constraints.

</section>
