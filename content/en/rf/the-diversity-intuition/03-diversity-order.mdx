## Why diversity order?

Diversity order is the exponent in the error probability expression: $P_e \propto \text{SNR}^{-d}$. It determines how fast errors vanish as SNR increases. Order 1 means errors drop linearly; order 4 means they drop as the fourth power. This single number predicts system reliability behavior.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Think of diversity order as the number of independent fades that must all be bad simultaneously for an error to occur. With 4 independent branches, all 4 must fade together — probability drops as the fourth power of SNR.

Higher order = faster error decay = more reliable system.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Definition

At high SNR, average error probability follows:

$$P_e = (G_c \cdot \text{SNR})^{-d} + o(\text{SNR}^{-d})$$

- $d$ = diversity order
- $G_c$ = coding gain (horizontal shift of error curve)

### Maximum achievable order

For $N_t$ TX and $N_r$ RX antennas:

$$d_{max} = N_t \cdot N_r$$

This is the full diversity order — achieved by optimal space-time codes.

### Space-time codes and diversity order

| Code | $N_t$ | $N_r$ | Rate | Diversity order |
|------|-------|-------|------|-----------------|
| SISO | 1 | 1 | 1 | 1 |
| MRC (1×L) | 1 | L | 1 | L |
| MISO MRT (L×1) | L | 1 | 1 | L |
| Alamouti | 2 | 1 | 1 | 2 |
| Alamouti | 2 | 2 | 1 | 4 |
| V-BLAST | N | N | N | 1 (per stream) |
| D-BLAST | N | N | ~N | N (with coding) |

### Alamouti code

The canonical 2×1 transmit diversity scheme:

$$\mathbf{X} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & -x_2^* \\ x_2 & x_1^* \end{bmatrix}$$

Time 1: send $x_1, x_2$ from antennas 1, 2
Time 2: send $-x_2^*, x_1^*$ from antennas 1, 2

Properties:
- Orthogonal design: $\mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^H = (|x_1|^2 + |x_2|^2)\mathbf{I}$
- Achieves full diversity d = 2
- Rate 1 (no rate loss)
- ML decoding is linear (simple receiver)

### Diversity vs. multiplexing tradeoff

Full diversity requires repetition; high rate requires independent streams.

**Zheng-Tse tradeoff:**
$$d(r) = (N_t - r)(N_r - r)$$

- $r$ = multiplexing gain
- $d(r)$ = achievable diversity order at multiplexing gain r

At full multiplexing ($r = \min(N_t, N_r)$): $d = 0$
At full diversity ($r = 0$): $d = N_t N_r$

### Partial diversity

Sometimes full diversity isn't achieved:

- V-BLAST: d = 1 per stream (interference-limited)
- Selection combining: d = L (same as MRC)
- Correlated channels: $d_{eff} < d_{ideal}$

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 2×2 Alamouti**

$N_t = 2$, $N_r = 2$, using Alamouti code.

Full diversity: $d = 2 \times 2 = 4$

At SNR = 20 dB: $P_e \propto (10^2)^{-4} = 10^{-8}$

Compare to SISO ($d = 1$): $P_e \propto 10^{-2}$

Six orders of magnitude improvement!

**Example: V-BLAST vs. Alamouti**

4×4 system at 10 dB SNR:

V-BLAST (4 streams, d = 1 each):
- Per-stream: $P_e \approx 10^{-1}$
- Very high throughput, poor reliability

Alamouti (rate 1, d = 8):
- $P_e \approx 10^{-8}$
- Low throughput, excellent reliability

Choose based on application: bulk data transfer (V-BLAST) vs. control signaling (Alamouti).

**Example: 5G NR PDCCH**

Control channel uses transmit diversity (d = 2 or 4):
- Critical signaling must be ultra-reliable
- Full diversity justifies rate loss

Data channel (PDSCH) uses spatial multiplexing:
- Throughput prioritized
- Lower diversity acceptable

**Example: Effective diversity with correlation**

2×2 system with TX correlation ρ = 0.7:

Full diversity: $d = 4$

Effective diversity: approximately $d_{eff} \approx 4 \times (1 - \rho^2) \approx 2$

Half the ideal diversity order due to correlation.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Assuming full diversity automatically.** V-BLAST, ZF receivers, and many practical schemes don't achieve full diversity. Check the actual order.

2. **Confusing diversity order with branch count.** 8 correlated branches might give d = 2. Order depends on effective independence.

3. **Ignoring coding gain.** Two schemes with same diversity order can differ by 3-6 dB due to coding gain differences.

4. **Optimizing wrong metric.** Low diversity order isn't always bad — high multiplexing gain may be more valuable.

5. **Forgetting practical SNR range.** Asymptotic diversity order matters less at moderate SNR (0-20 dB) where most systems operate.

</section>
