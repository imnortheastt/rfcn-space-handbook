## Why NTN-specific models?

5G NR extends to non-terrestrial networks (NTN) — satellites and high-altitude platforms. 3GPP developed new channel models in TR 38.811 and TR 38.821 specifically for NTN, accounting for the unique propagation characteristics: long delays, high Doppler, and different fading statistics.

These models are the official reference for 5G satellite system design, replacing or supplementing older ITU-R and academic models.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

NTN models adapt terrestrial 5G channel models for space. They keep the cluster/ray structure from TR 38.901 but modify parameters for satellite geometry: higher K-factors (more LOS), different delay spreads, and elevation-dependent behavior.

Think of NTN models as "TR 38.901 for satellites" — same framework, different numbers.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### NTN scenarios

3GPP defines four NTN deployment scenarios:

| Scenario | Altitude | Round-trip delay |
|----------|----------|------------------|
| GEO | 35,786 km | 540 ms |
| MEO | 7,000–20,000 km | 50–130 ms |
| LEO | 600–1,500 km | 4–10 ms |
| HAPS | 20 km | <1 ms |

### TR 38.811 channel model

Based on TR 38.901 structure:

$$h(t, \tau) = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \sum_{m=1}^{M} a_{n,m} \cdot \delta(\tau - \tau_n) \cdot e^{j2\pi\nu_n t}$$

- $N$ = number of clusters
- $M$ = rays per cluster
- $a_{n,m}$ = ray amplitude
- $\tau_n$ = cluster delay
- $\nu_n$ = Doppler frequency

### Environment categories

| Environment | Description | Typical elevation |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Dense urban | High-rise, NLOS dominant | All |
| Urban | Mixed buildings | All |
| Suburban | Low-density buildings | All |
| Rural | Open terrain, scattered trees | All |

### Elevation-dependent parameters

K-factor increases with elevation:

$$K(\theta) = K_{10} + K_{el} \cdot (\theta - 10°)$$

For rural: $K_{10} = 7$ dB, $K_{el} = 0.15$ dB/degree.

At 50° elevation: $K = 7 + 0.15 \times 40 = 13$ dB.

### Delay spread

Much smaller than terrestrial (no distant scatterers):

| Environment | Delay spread (ns) |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Rural | 20–50 |
| Suburban | 50–100 |
| Urban | 100–200 |
| Dense urban | 200–400 |

Compare to terrestrial urban: 300–1000 ns.

### Doppler characteristics

LEO satellite at 600 km altitude:

Maximum Doppler at horizon: ~40 kHz at S-band.

At 30° elevation: ~25 kHz.

Doppler rate: up to 500 Hz/s (must track continuously).

### Clutter loss

Additional loss from local environment:

$$L_{clutter} = A_{clutter} \cdot (1 - e^{-B_{clutter} \cdot \theta})$$

Parameters vary by environment and frequency.

### Atmospheric effects

Included in NTN models:
- Tropospheric scintillation (amplitude fluctuation)
- Faraday rotation (polarization rotation in ionosphere)
- Ionospheric delay (GPS-like effects at low frequencies)

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: LEO S-band link budget**

Frequency: 2 GHz, altitude: 600 km, elevation: 45°.

Free-space loss: $L_{fs} = 170$ dB.

Additional NTN model factors:
- Clutter loss (suburban): 3 dB
- Shadow fading margin (10%): 6 dB
- Atmospheric loss: 0.5 dB

Total: $170 + 3 + 6 + 0.5 = 179.5$ dB path loss.

**Example: GEO Ka-band**

Frequency: 20 GHz, elevation: 35°.

K-factor (urban): $K = 5 + 0.1 \times 25 = 7.5$ dB.

Rain fade at 99.9%: ~8 dB (location-dependent).

System must handle both fading (fast) and rain (slow).

**Example: Doppler compensation**

LEO at 550 km, UE stationary, 2 GHz.

Satellite passes overhead in 10 minutes.

Doppler range: −40 kHz to +40 kHz.

5G NR subcarrier spacing: 15 kHz → severe ICI without compensation.

Required: pre-compensation at gNB based on ephemeris + UE location.

**Example: Timing advance**

GEO round-trip: 540 ms >> LTE max TA (667 μs).

NTN extends TA range and adds GNSS-based timing.

Residual timing error: <1 μs with GNSS assistance.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Using terrestrial models.** TR 38.901 parameters don't apply directly. Use TR 38.811/38.821 for NTN.

2. **Ignoring Doppler.** LEO Doppler exceeds terrestrial by 1000×. OFDM orthogonality breaks without compensation.

3. **Forgetting timing.** NTN propagation delays require extended timing procedures. Standard RACH doesn't work.

4. **Elevation as constant.** Elevation changes during pass (especially LEO). Channel statistics are time-varying.

5. **Rain at Ka-band.** Atmospheric effects dominate at higher frequencies. Clear-sky assumptions break in tropical regions.

6. **Frequency reuse.** Different satellites in constellation see different channel states. Coordination is harder than terrestrial.

</section>
