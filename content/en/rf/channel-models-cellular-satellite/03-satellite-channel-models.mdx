<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why satellite channel models?

Satellite links differ from terrestrial. The direct path is (usually) clear, but the mobile terminal sees shadowing from buildings, trees, and terrain. Traditional Rayleigh/Rician models don't capture the slow shadowing plus fast fading combination unique to satellite mobile.

Specialized satellite channel models like Loo and Lutz capture this dual-timescale behavior — essential for designing satellite handsets, maritime terminals, and IoT devices.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Picture a satellite phone in a car. Sometimes you're in a clear area — strong LOS to the satellite, some weak multipath. Sometimes you're under trees or near buildings — the LOS is shadowed, multipath dominates. The channel switches between these states as you move.

Satellite channel models capture this state-switching behavior. They're not just about average loss — they model the dynamics of good/bad channel conditions.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Channel components

Satellite mobile channel has three components:

1. **Direct (LOS):** Stable path to satellite, possibly shadowed
2. **Diffuse multipath:** Scattering from surroundings
3. **Specular multipath:** Strong reflections (ground, buildings)

### Rician factor

In clear conditions, ratio of LOS to diffuse power:

$$K = \frac{P_{LOS}}{P_{diffuse}}$$

Typical values:
- Clear sky: $K = 10$–$20$ dB
- Light shadowing: $K = 0$–$5$ dB
- Heavy shadowing: $K < 0$ dB (NLOS dominates)

### Loo model

The classic land-mobile satellite model:

$$r(t) = A(t) e^{j\phi_0} + z(t)$$

- $A(t)$ = lognormally shadowed LOS amplitude
- $z(t)$ = Rayleigh-distributed multipath

LOS amplitude (dB) is Gaussian: $20\log_{10}(A) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$

Three parameters: $\mu$ (mean LOS level), $\sigma$ (shadow spread), $M_p$ (multipath power).

### Lutz model

Two-state Markov model:

- **Good state:** Rician fading (strong LOS)
- **Bad state:** Rayleigh fading (shadowed)

Transition probabilities define state duration:
- $P_{gg}$ = probability of staying good
- $P_{bb}$ = probability of staying bad

State durations follow geometric distribution.

### Corazza-Vatalaro model

Combines Loo-type shadowing with state transitions:

Received amplitude:
$$r = S \cdot A \cdot e^{j\phi} + S \cdot z$$

- $S$ = slow shadowing (lognormal)
- $A$ = LOS component
- $z$ = multipath

Unifies Loo (single state) and Lutz (state switching).

### Doppler spectrum

Satellite geometry gives different Doppler than terrestrial:

$$f_d = \frac{v}{\lambda} \cos\alpha \cos\beta$$

- $v$ = mobile velocity
- $\alpha$ = satellite elevation
- $\beta$ = mobile direction relative to satellite azimuth

Low elevation satellites → higher Doppler spread.

### Environment classification

| Environment | $K$ (dB) | $\sigma$ (dB) | Good state % |
|-------------|----------|---------------|--------------|
| Open rural | 15–20 | 1–2 | 95% |
| Suburban | 5–10 | 3–5 | 70% |
| Urban | 0–5 | 5–8 | 40% |
| Indoor | −5 to 0 | 8–12 | 10% |

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Maritime satellite**

Ship at sea, 40° elevation, L-band (1.5 GHz).

Environment: open (no shadowing), sea surface reflections.

Model: Rician with $K \approx 15$ dB, specular reflection causing frequency-selective fading.

BER degradation vs. AWGN: ~0.5 dB at $10^{-3}$.

**Example: Vehicular Ku-band**

Car with roof-mounted antenna, urban environment.

Loo parameters: $\mu = -3$ dB, $\sigma = 4$ dB, $M_p = -10$ dB.

Deep fades (>10 dB below mean): ~5% of time.

Required: 3–5 dB extra margin vs. clear-sky link budget.

**Example: State duration calculation**

Lutz model, suburban, 50 km/h.

Average "bad" state duration: 2 seconds (correlates with building blockage).

Average "good" state duration: 8 seconds.

Time fraction in bad state: 2/(2+8) = 20%.

For ARQ system: retransmission timeout should exceed typical bad-state duration.

**Example: LEO vs. GEO**

GEO at 35° elevation vs. LEO at 70° average elevation.

Higher LEO elevation → less shadowing, shorter fade durations.

But LEO Doppler is much higher: must track satellite motion.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Using terrestrial models.** Rayleigh/Rician alone don't capture shadowing dynamics. Use satellite-specific models.

2. **Ignoring elevation.** Shadowing probability is strongly elevation-dependent. 20° elevation has 3× more blockage than 50°.

3. **Static vs. dynamic.** Models give instantaneous statistics. Time-series generation needs proper Doppler filtering.

4. **Frequency scaling.** Models are usually L/S-band. Ka-band has different scattering behavior.

5. **Antenna pattern.** Narrower terminal antennas reduce multipath but don't eliminate shadowing.

6. **Indoor penetration.** Most satellite models assume outdoor or vehicle. Indoor needs additional penetration loss (10–25 dB).

</section>
