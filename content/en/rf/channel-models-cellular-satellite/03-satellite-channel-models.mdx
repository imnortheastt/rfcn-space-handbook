## Why satellite channel models?

A Rayleigh fading model predicts your link performance in an urban canyon. You apply the same model to a satellite handset in a car driving through a suburban neighborhood. The simulation looks fine — but your BER measurements are 10 dB worse than predicted. Why?

Because the satellite channel is fundamentally different from the terrestrial channel, and using terrestrial fading models for satellite links produces systematically wrong predictions.

In a terrestrial cellular link, you're looking horizontally through a city. There's rarely a direct line-of-sight path — multipath dominates. The signal envelope is Rayleigh-distributed. In a satellite link, you're looking upward at a steep angle. You almost always have a direct LOS path to the satellite, but that path is interrupted periodically by trees, buildings, and terrain as the terminal moves. The resulting channel has two simultaneous timescales: slow shadowing (your LOS path gets blocked for seconds) and fast fading (multipath from nearby scatterers varies over centimeters of movement).

Classic terrestrial models can't represent this. Satellite-specific models — Loo, Lutz, Corazza-Vatalaro — were developed precisely to capture this dual-timescale behavior.

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### The dual-timescale problem

```
Signal received by a satellite handset in a moving car (S-band, 1.5 GHz):

Power (dBm)                           Good state        Bad state
    -80 |                        ___________
    -90 |                   ____/           \____
   -100 |              ____/                    \___
   -110 |    ____/____/   ↑ fast fading             \___________
   -120 |___/             within good/bad state
   -130 |
        +---|---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|---> Time (s)
        0   2         4         6   ↑     8         10        12
                                    shadowing transition
                                    (building/tree blockage)

Timescale separation:
  Slow: good/bad state transitions — seconds (shadow)
  Fast: amplitude ripple within a state — milliseconds (multipath)
```

Within the "good state" (clear LOS), the envelope follows a Rician distribution — strong LOS plus weak multipath. Within the "bad state" (shadowed LOS), the LOS component is attenuated by a random building or tree, and the envelope approaches Rayleigh. The switching between states is Markovian — state durations follow an exponential distribution.

### Why terrestrial models fail

A Rayleigh model says the LOS component is zero at all times — every location is NLOS. This is pessimistic for satellite links (the satellite is overhead, not blocked by buildings on the same level). A pure Rician model says the LOS amplitude is deterministically fixed — no long-duration shadowing. This is optimistic in any environment with trees or buildings. Neither is correct.

The Loo model is the key insight: let the LOS amplitude itself be a random variable. Specifically, let $A(t)$ — the LOS amplitude — be drawn from a log-normal distribution (in dB, it's Gaussian). Then add Rayleigh-distributed multipath on top. This two-level hierarchy produces the satellite channel's dual-timescale behavior analytically.

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Channel component decomposition

Every satellite mobile channel has three additive components:

| Component | Nature | Timescale | Distribution |
|-----------|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Direct (LOS) | Stable path from satellite | Slow variation (shadowing) | Log-normal amplitude |
| Diffuse multipath | Scattering from surroundings | Fast ($\sim\lambda$) | Rayleigh |
| Specular multipath | Specific strong reflectors (ground, buildings) | Medium | Discrete rays |

The ratio of LOS to diffuse power is the K-factor:
$$K = \frac{P_{LOS}}{P_{diffuse}}$$

Typical values by environment and elevation:

| Environment | Elevation 10° | Elevation 40° | Elevation 70° |
|-------------|--------------|--------------|--------------|
| Open rural | 8 dB | 14 dB | 18 dB |
| Suburban | 3 dB | 8 dB | 12 dB |
| Urban | −2 dB | 3 dB | 7 dB |
| Dense urban | −8 dB | −2 dB | 2 dB |
| Indoor | −15 dB | −10 dB | −5 dB |

K-factor increases with elevation because higher elevation means less building blockage and a more stable LOS path.

### Loo model (single-state)

The canonical land-mobile satellite channel model (Loo, 1985):

$$r(t) = A(t) e^{j\phi_0} + z(t)$$

where:
- $r(t)$ = complex received envelope
- $A(t)$ = LOS amplitude, log-normally distributed: $20\log_{10}(A) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_A, \sigma_A^2)$
- $\phi_0$ = fixed LOS phase
- $z(t)$ = complex Gaussian multipath with power $2\sigma_z^2$ (Rayleigh envelope)

Three model parameters:
- $\mu_A$ (dB): mean LOS amplitude — positive means strong LOS, negative means shadowing
- $\sigma_A$ (dB): LOS amplitude standard deviation — spread of the shadowing
- $M_p = 2\sigma_z^2$ (dBW or dBm): diffuse multipath power

The PDF of the received signal amplitude $r$ from the Loo model is:

$$p(r) = \frac{r}{\sigma_z^2} \int_0^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_A A} \exp\left(-\frac{(\ln A - \mu_A)^2}{2\sigma_A^2}\right) \exp\left(-\frac{r^2 + A^2}{2\sigma_z^2}\right) I_0\left(\frac{rA}{\sigma_z^2}\right) dA$$

The inner integral is over all possible LOS amplitudes, weighted by the log-normal distribution. Numerically, this is computed by Monte Carlo or closed-form approximations.

**Loo model parameter table (L-band, representative environments):**

| Environment | $\mu_A$ (dB) | $\sigma_A$ (dB) | $M_p$ (dB relative) |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Open (light trees) | −0.5 | 0.9 | −15 |
| Suburban | −2.0 | 2.5 | −12 |
| Urban | −5.5 | 4.5 | −8 |
| Heavy shadowing | −12 | 6.0 | −5 |

### Lutz two-state Markov model

The Lutz model (1991) divides the channel into two discrete states:

- **Good state (G)**: Rician fading with K-factor $K_G$ — LOS present
- **Bad state (B)**: Rayleigh fading — LOS blocked, multipath only

State transitions follow a first-order Markov chain:

$$\mathbf{P} = \begin{pmatrix} P_{GG} & 1-P_{GG} \\ 1-P_{BB} & P_{BB} \end{pmatrix}$$

where $P_{GG}$ is the probability of staying in the good state and $P_{BB}$ of staying in the bad state.

State duration statistics:
- Duration in good state: mean $\bar{T}_G = 1/(1-P_{GG})$ (in symbol or time units)
- Duration in bad state: mean $\bar{T}_B = 1/(1-P_{BB})$

Fraction of time in bad state: $A = \bar{T}_B/(\bar{T}_G + \bar{T}_B)$

**Lutz model parameters (S-band, vehicular, various environments):**

| Environment | $A$ (bad fraction) | $K_G$ (dB) | $\bar{T}_G$ (s at 50 km/h) | $\bar{T}_B$ (s) |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| Open | 0.05 | 15 | 12 | 0.6 |
| Suburban | 0.20 | 8 | 5 | 1.2 |
| Urban | 0.45 | 3 | 2 | 1.6 |
| Dense urban | 0.75 | 0 | 0.5 | 1.5 |

### Corazza-Vatalaro model

Unifies the Loo continuous-shadowing model with Lutz-style state switching:

$$r(t) = S(t) \cdot [A \cdot e^{j\phi_0} + z(t)]$$

where $S(t)$ is a slow log-normal shadowing process that modulates both the LOS and multipath components together. This represents the case where the entire path (not just the LOS component) is modulated by a large obstacle.

The Corazza-Vatalaro model reduces to:
- **Loo model** when $S(t)$ modulates only the LOS (special case)
- **Lutz model** when $S(t)$ is binary (two-state approximation)

It is more general and provides better fits to measurements in environments with large building blockages that shadow the entire signal, not just the LOS component.

### Doppler spectrum in satellite channels

The Doppler spectrum for satellite links differs from the Clarke/Jakes spectrum used for terrestrial channels. The satellite geometry constrains the angular distribution of arriving waves:

$$f_D = \frac{v}{\lambda}[\cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta_m - \beta_s)]$$

where:
- $v$ = terminal velocity
- $\alpha$ = satellite elevation angle
- $\beta_m$ = direction of mobile motion (azimuth)
- $\beta_s$ = satellite azimuth

At high elevation angles ($\alpha \to 90°$), $\cos(\alpha) \to 0$ — the LOS component from a nearly-overhead satellite has near-zero Doppler regardless of UE velocity. The multipath, arriving from scattered objects at low elevation angles, has the full $v/\lambda$ Doppler spread. This creates a composite spectrum with a strong spectral spike at near-zero Doppler (LOS) plus a broader diffuse spread (multipath).

For LEO satellites, the satellite motion itself adds a dominant Doppler component that dwarfs terminal motion — this is covered in the NTN-specific models lesson.

### Environment classification for model selection

| Environment | Recommended model | Key parameters to calibrate |
|-------------|------------------|---------------------------|
| Maritime (open sea) | Rician + specular ground reflection | K-factor, reflection coefficient |
| Open rural | Loo (light shadowing) | $\mu_A$, $\sigma_A$ from measurement |
| Suburban vehicular | Lutz two-state or Loo | State fractions, K-factor |
| Urban vehicular | Lutz two-state | State fractions, bad-state duration |
| Pedestrian urban | Corazza-Vatalaro | Shadow depth, correlation distance |
| Indoor | Penetration loss + Rician | Penetration loss statistics |

## Apply

### Example 1: Maritime satellite — performance estimation

Ship at sea, L-band (1.55 GHz), elevation 40°, ship roll ±10°.

Environment: open, no shadowing. Sea surface specular reflection creates a two-ray geometry.

Model: Rician K-factor model.

At 40° elevation over calm sea, specular reflection coefficient for horizontal polarization ≈ −0.85. The reflected path adds destructive interference at some geometries.

For statistical performance: $K = 15$ dB. Rician BER at $E_b/N_0 = 10$ dB is approximately $10^{-6}$ for BPSK — essentially AWGN performance. The maritime satellite channel is benign in open conditions; ship antenna stabilization is sufficient.

Ship pitch/roll causes elevation angle variation of ±10°, changing K from 14–16 dB. Performance impact is less than 0.5 dB — acceptable without adaptive coding.

### Example 2: Suburban vehicular Lutz model

Car at 60 km/h, S-band (2 GHz), suburban, satellite at 35° elevation.

Lutz parameters (suburban): $A = 0.20$ (bad fraction), $K_G = 8$ dB, $\bar{T}_G = 4$ s, $\bar{T}_B = 1$ s.

**State durations**: At 60 km/h, 1 second = 16.7 m of travel. Bad states last ~1 s (= ~17 m, about half a building width). Good states last ~4 s (= ~67 m, about one to two building separations in suburban).

**System design implication**: ARQ (Automatic Repeat reQuest) retransmission timeout must exceed the bad state duration. If the timeout is 0.2 s but bad states last 1 s, retransmissions are lost in the same fade — a burst error, not an independent retry. Set ARQ timeout to 2–3× the mean bad state duration (~2–3 s).

**BER in bad state**: Rayleigh at S-band, received $E_b/N_0 = 5$ dB: BER ≈ $8 \times 10^{-2}$. In good state (Rician, K = 8 dB): BER ≈ $10^{-4}$. Average BER = $0.20 \times 0.08 + 0.80 \times 10^{-4} = 0.016 + 0.0001 \approx 1.6\%$ — the bad state dominates despite being 20% of time.

### Example 3: Loo model — fade depth estimation

LEO satellite at L-band (1.5 GHz), urban environment.

Loo parameters (urban): $\mu_A = -5.5$ dB, $\sigma_A = 4.5$ dB, $M_p = -8$ dB.

Probability of received signal being below threshold $T = \mu_A - 15$ dB = −20.5 dB (relative to free-space):

Using Gaussian approximation for the log-normal LOS: $P(A < T_{linear}) \approx Q((20\log_{10}(T_{linear}) - \mu_A)/\sigma_A) = Q((-15)/4.5) = Q(-3.33) = 99.96\%$.

That means only 0.04% of the time is the LOS amplitude within 15 dB of the mean — 99.96% of time the LOS is stronger than −20.5 dB. The deep fades come from moments when both the LOS is heavily shadowed AND the multipath is in a destructive phase. The joint probability is roughly 5–10% of time for >10 dB below mean in urban environments.

### Example 4: Elevation angle effect

LEO terminal at two elevation angles during a pass: 20° (low pass) vs. 70° (high pass).

From the Lutz parameter table (urban):
- At 20° elevation: bad fraction ≈ 0.55, $\bar{T}_B = 2$ s
- At 70° elevation: bad fraction ≈ 0.15, $\bar{T}_B = 0.8$ s

The high-elevation pass has 3.7× less shadowing fraction AND shorter fade durations. This explains why:
1. LEO constellation designs target minimum elevation angles of 30°–40° for reliable service
2. Higher-orbit LEO (1200 km) provides longer durations at high elevation than lower-orbit LEO (550 km), even though both have high peak elevations

### Example 5: Indoor penetration loss as additional state

Pedestrian moving from outdoor to indoor in suburban (office building, glass facade).

Outdoor Lutz model: $A = 0.20$, $K_G = 8$ dB. When indoor, add 8 dB penetration loss (glass) — effectively shifts both states 8 dB lower.

New effective parameters indoors: $K_G^{indoor} = 8 - 8 = 0$ dB (Rayleigh-like), bad fraction increases to ~0.45 (many internal walls now also block the satellite path). The indoor link budget needs 15–25 dB additional margin beyond the outdoor model.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Applying terrestrial Rayleigh/Rician models directly to satellite links.** Pure Rayleigh assumes no LOS — wrong for most satellite scenarios. Pure Rician assumes constant LOS amplitude — wrong when shadowing changes the LOS level over seconds. Use Loo or Lutz for land-mobile satellite.

2. **Ignoring elevation-angle dependence.** Shadowing probability decreases strongly with elevation angle. A model calibrated at 20° elevation will overestimate fading at 50° by 3–5 dB. Always use elevation-parameterized models or apply elevation correction.

3. **Using static channel statistics for ARQ design.** The bad state in the Lutz model persists for 1–4 seconds. Retransmission protocols designed for independent symbol errors (terrestrial micro-cells) fail in satellite channels with burst-error durations of seconds. Design ARQ for the burst statistics.

4. **Frequency scaling without re-parameterization.** Loo and Lutz parameters are measured at L/S-band. At Ka-band, rain and atmospheric effects dominate over multipath; the statistical distribution changes completely. Do not frequency-scale these models to Ka-band without new measurement campaigns.

5. **Assuming the antenna pattern eliminates multipath.** A narrow-beam terminal antenna (e.g., 20° beamwidth on a boat dish) does reduce multipath from ground reflections. But it does not eliminate LOS shadowing when a wave breaks over the bow or a ship structure blocks the satellite. Shadowing and multipath are independent phenomena requiring separate treatment.

6. **Generating time-series with wrong Doppler filtering.** Statistical Monte Carlo drops give correct average statistics but cannot simulate burst-error patterns. Time-series simulation requires proper Doppler filtering of the LOS and multipath components separately to reproduce the correct autocorrelation function — especially important for FEC decoder testing.
