## Why COST-231?

Free-space path loss tells you propagation in a vacuum. Real cellular environments have buildings, terrain, vehicles, and foliage. At 1800 MHz in a dense city, the actual signal loss at 2 km is about 147 dB — versus 107 dB in free space. That 40 dB gap is entirely due to the urban environment, and it must be accounted for accurately in every link budget.

The COST-231 Hata model is the standard empirical formula for this task. It was derived from the Okumura measurement campaigns in Tokyo and extended to 2 GHz by the European COST-231 consortium. The result is a closed-form equation — no simulation required — that predicts median urban path loss to within ±8 dB based solely on frequency, antenna heights, and distance.

For cloud/backend engineers: COST-231 is like a production-grade regression model trained on millions of signal measurements. You feed it network parameters (frequency, height, distance) and it returns median path loss. You then add statistical margins (shadow fading) on top. It is the starting point for every real cellular coverage plan below 2 GHz.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### How empirical models are built

Engineers drive a city with calibrated receivers, recording received signal strength at thousands of locations. They repeat this at multiple frequencies, with different antenna heights, in different city types. Then they fit a regression equation to the data:

$$L_{dB} \approx A + B\log_{10}(f) + C\log_{10}(h_b) + D\log_{10}(d) + \text{corrections}$$

The coefficients encode the physics of urban propagation without modeling individual buildings. "On average, in medium cities, at 900 MHz, with a 30 m base station, 1 km gives X dB loss." That average behavior is exactly what link budgets need.

### Why log-distance scaling

The distance exponent for urban environments (typically $n = 3.5$ to $4$) is higher than free space ($n = 2$) because signal doesn't just spread spherically — it also gets absorbed, reflected, and diffracted by buildings on every path. Each decade of distance (1 km → 10 km) adds $10n \approx 35$–40 dB more loss than free space (20 dB per decade).

### The model family tree

```
Measurement campaign (Okumura, Tokyo, 1968)
    → Okumura curves (graphical lookup, 150–1500 MHz)
        → Hata formula (1980) — analytical version of Okumura curves
            → COST-231 Hata (1999) — extended to 2 GHz
                → 3GPP TR 38.901 UMa — modern version, 0.5–100 GHz
```

COST-231 sits in this lineage: it is the mature, standardized form of Okumura-Hata valid through the GSM/UMTS/LTE era. For frequencies above 2 GHz (3.5 GHz NR, 28 GHz mmWave), use 3GPP TR 38.901 instead.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Okumura-Hata model (150–1500 MHz)

The original Hata formula for urban environments:

$$L_{urban} = 69.55 + 26.16\log_{10}(f) - 13.82\log_{10}(h_b) - a(h_m) + (44.9 - 6.55\log_{10}(h_b))\log_{10}(d)$$

Parameters:
- $f$ = carrier frequency (MHz), range 150–1500 MHz
- $h_b$ = base station antenna height above ground (m), range 30–200 m
- $h_m$ = mobile antenna height (m), range 1–10 m
- $d$ = distance between BS and mobile (km), range 1–20 km

### COST-231 Hata extension (1500–2000 MHz)

$$L = 46.3 + 33.9\log_{10}(f) - 13.82\log_{10}(h_b) - a(h_m) + (44.9 - 6.55\log_{10}(h_b))\log_{10}(d) + C_m$$

The structure is identical to Hata but with modified coefficients that better fit measurements at 1.5–2 GHz. Key differences:
- Constant term: 46.3 vs. 69.55 (COST-231 starts lower because frequency dependence is captured separately in the $33.9\log_{10}(f)$ term)
- Frequency coefficient: 33.9 vs. 26.16
- Additional metropolitan correction: $C_m = 0$ dB (medium city/suburban), $C_m = 3$ dB (metropolitan dense urban)

### Mobile antenna correction factor $a(h_m)$

For medium-sized cities and suburban environments:

$$a(h_m) = (1.1\log_{10}(f) - 0.7)h_m - (1.56\log_{10}(f) - 0.8) \quad [\text{dB}]$$

For large cities at $f \geq 400$ MHz (dense urban):

$$a(h_m) = 3.2(\log_{10}(11.75 h_m))^2 - 4.97 \quad [\text{dB}]$$

At $h_m = 1.5$ m and $f = 1800$ MHz: medium-city correction $a = 0.97$ dB; large-city correction $a = 0.16$ dB. The difference is small at low mobile height but grows at higher mobile heights.

### Sensitivity to each parameter

| Parameter change | Effect on path loss | Intuition |
|-----------------|---------------------|-----------|
| Double $d$ (1 km → 2 km) | +10.5 to +13.5 dB | $n = 3.5$ to 4.5 |
| Double $f$ (900 → 1800 MHz) | +10 dB | More absorption and diffraction |
| Double $h_b$ (30 → 60 m) | −5.4 dB | Clears more obstacles |
| Double $h_m$ (1.5 → 3 m) | −2 to −3 dB | Higher mobile, fewer blockages |
| Urban → dense urban | +3 dB ($C_m$) | More building clutter |

### Environment corrections beyond COST-231

COST-231 is calibrated for urban medium cities. For other environments, apply correction factors:

| Environment | Correction vs. urban COST-231 |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Dense urban (metropolitan) | +3 dB ($C_m = 3$) |
| Suburban | $L_{su} = L_{urban} - 2[\log_{10}(f/28)]^2 - 5.4$ |
| Open rural | $L_{open} = L_{urban} - 4.78[\log_{10}(f)]^2 + 18.33\log_{10}(f) - 40.94$ |

The suburban and open corrections are large — suburban can be 10–15 dB less loss than urban; open rural 20–30 dB less. This captures the dramatic improvement when building density decreases.

### Model validity boundaries

| Parameter | Okumura-Hata | COST-231 Hata |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Frequency | 150–1500 MHz | 1500–2000 MHz |
| BS height | 30–200 m | 30–200 m |
| Mobile height | 1–10 m | 1–10 m |
| Distance | 1–20 km | 1–20 km |
| Environment | Quasi-flat urban | Quasi-flat urban |

Outside these ranges, use a different model. For 3.5 GHz sub-6 NR deployments, use 3GPP TR 38.901. For micro-cells (BS below rooftops), use WINNER II or 3GPP TR 38.901 UMi.

## Apply

### Example 1: Urban LTE at 1800 MHz — full calculation

BS: $h_b = 30$ m, $f = 1800$ MHz, mobile $h_m = 1.5$ m, distance $d = 2$ km, medium city.

Mobile correction (medium city):
$$a(h_m) = (1.1 \times 3.255 - 0.7)(1.5) - (1.56 \times 3.255 - 0.8)$$
$$= (3.58 - 0.7)(1.5) - (5.077 - 0.8) = (2.88)(1.5) - 4.277 = 4.32 - 4.28 = 0.04 \approx 0.97 \text{ dB}$$

Path loss:
$$L = 46.3 + 33.9 \times \log_{10}(1800) - 13.82 \times \log_{10}(30) - 0.97 + (44.9 - 6.55 \times \log_{10}(30)) \times \log_{10}(2) + 0$$
$$= 46.3 + 33.9 \times 3.255 - 13.82 \times 1.477 - 0.97 + (44.9 - 6.55 \times 1.477) \times 0.301$$
$$= 46.3 + 110.3 - 20.4 - 0.97 + (44.9 - 9.67) \times 0.301$$
$$= 46.3 + 110.3 - 20.4 - 0.97 + 35.23 \times 0.301$$
$$= 46.3 + 110.3 - 20.4 - 0.97 + 10.6 = 145.8 \text{ dB}$$

Free-space at same distance/frequency: $L_{fs} = 20\log_{10}(2000) + 20\log_{10}(1800 \times 10^6) - 147.55 = 107.6$ dB.

Urban excess loss: $145.8 - 107.6 = 38.2$ dB. This is the price of deploying in a city instead of open space.

### Example 2: Macro cell coverage radius

Urban LTE 900 MHz, $h_b = 40$ m, target: 99% link budget closes at 143 dB maximum path loss. What is the cell radius?

Solve COST-231 for $d$:
$$143 = 46.3 + 33.9\log_{10}(900) - 13.82\log_{10}(40) - 0.82 + (44.9 - 6.55\log_{10}(40))\log_{10}(d)$$
$$143 = 46.3 + 99.9 - 22.0 - 0.82 + (44.9 - 10.5)\log_{10}(d)$$
$$143 = 123.4 + 34.4\log_{10}(d)$$
$$\log_{10}(d) = (143 - 123.4)/34.4 = 0.569$$
$$d = 10^{0.569} = 3.7 \text{ km}$$

But this is the **median** path loss radius. At 90% location reliability with $\sigma = 8$ dB shadow margin (add 10.2 dB):

$$d_{90\%} = 10^{(143 - 10.2 - 123.4)/34.4} = 10^{0.274} = 1.88 \text{ km}$$

The 90% reliable cell radius is nearly half the median radius. This is the fundamental lesson: always add shadow fading margin, and it halves your coverage.

### Example 3: Height vs. additional power tradeoff

Current: $h_b = 30$ m. Question: is it better to raise the antenna to 60 m, or add 6 dB transmit power?

Height gain from 30 m → 60 m:
$$\Delta L = -13.82\log_{10}(60/30) - 6.55\log_{10}(d) \cdot \log_{10}(60/30)$$

At $d = 2$ km: $\Delta L = -13.82 \times 0.301 - 6.55 \times 0.301 \times 0.301 = -4.16 - 0.59 = -4.75$ dB.

So doubling tower height gives about 4.8 dB of effective path loss reduction. Adding 6 dB transmit power gives 6 dB — but only to the downlink. Tower height benefits both uplink and downlink simultaneously. For coverage-limited uplink (common in LTE), raising the tower is more valuable per dB than increasing BS transmit power.

### Example 4: COST-231 vs. 3GPP TR 38.901 at the boundary

At 2000 MHz exactly, COST-231 gives:
$$L_{COST231}(2 \text{ km}) = 46.3 + 33.9 \times 3.301 - 20.4 - 0.97 + 35.2 \times 0.301 = 146.8 \text{ dB}$$

3GPP TR 38.901 Urban Macro at 2 GHz gives approximately 148 dB for the same parameters (including the $\sigma = 4$ dB lower bound on distance-dependent path loss). The two models are consistent at the boundary — a good sanity check that COST-231 remains valid up to its stated limit.

### Example 5: Suburban deployment

Same 1800 MHz link, $d = 2$ km, but now in a suburban environment.

$$L_{su} = L_{urban} - 2[\log_{10}(1800/28)]^2 - 5.4$$
$$= 145.8 - 2 \times [\log_{10}(64.3)]^2 - 5.4$$
$$= 145.8 - 2 \times (1.808)^2 - 5.4$$
$$= 145.8 - 6.54 - 5.4 = 133.9 \text{ dB}$$

Suburban is 11.9 dB less loss than urban at the same distance — you can serve the same area with a significantly weaker transmitter, or extend coverage radius considerably.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Using COST-231 outside its frequency range.** The model is valid 1500–2000 MHz. Applying it at 3.5 GHz (5G NR sub-6) or 700 MHz (LTE Band 28) gives wrong results. Use Okumura-Hata below 1500 MHz; use 3GPP TR 38.901 above 2 GHz.

2. **Applying to micro-cells with BS below rooftops.** COST-231 assumes the base station antenna is above the surrounding roofline, providing a "macro-cell" geometry. Street-level small cells behave differently — use WINNER II UMi or 3GPP TR 38.901 UMi instead.

3. **Treating path loss output as instantaneous received power.** COST-231 outputs the **median** path loss. Individual locations have a log-normally distributed shadow component. Add 8–10 dB shadow margin for 90% location reliability. Forgetting this is the single most common link budget error.

4. **Ignoring indoor penetration.** COST-231 is an outdoor model. For indoor coverage, add building penetration loss (15–25 dB for concrete, 3–5 dB for glass) on top of the outdoor path loss.

5. **Using on hilly terrain.** The model assumes relatively flat terrain. Significant hills require separate diffraction loss calculations (Fresnel-Kirchhoff knife-edge) added to the COST-231 base.

6. **Confusing $C_m = 3$ dB for all urban deployments.** The +3 dB metropolitan correction is for very dense high-rise environments. A medium city (most European or Asian cities) uses $C_m = 0$. Over-applying the correction wastes link budget.

7. **Forgetting the distance lower bound of 1 km.** COST-231 was calibrated on measurements from 1–20 km. At 500 m, the model underestimates path loss because close-in behavior is dominated by near-field effects and street-canyon geometry not captured in the regression.
