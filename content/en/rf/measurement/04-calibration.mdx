<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why calibration?

Every measurement has uncertainty. Calibration quantifies and minimizes this uncertainty by comparing your instrument to a known standard. Without calibration, you don't know if your -20 dB is really -20 dB or could be anywhere from -18 to -22 dB.

Regulatory compliance and customer acceptance require traceable calibration.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Calibration is like setting your watch to atomic time. Your watch might run fast or slow, but once you know the error, you can correct it. Regular re-calibration catches drift before it causes problems.

The calibration chain connects your measurement to national/international standards — traceability.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Calibration chain

```
BIPM (International)
       ↓
National Metrology Institute (NIST, NPL, PTB)
       ↓
Accredited Calibration Lab
       ↓
Working Standards
       ↓
Your Instrument
```

Each step adds uncertainty. Total uncertainty is root-sum-square of all contributions.

### VNA calibration

**SOLT (Short-Open-Load-Thru):**
- 4 known standards
- Corrects for 12-term error model
- Most common for coaxial

**TRL (Thru-Reflect-Line):**
- 3 standards: thru, reflect (any), line
- Self-calibrating for line impedance
- Best for non-coaxial (waveguide, probes)

**Error terms corrected:**
- Directivity
- Source match
- Reflection tracking
- Load match
- Transmission tracking
- Cross-talk

### Power meter calibration

**Reference:** Calibrated power sensor (NIST-traceable)

**Process:**
1. Apply known power from calibrated source
2. Record meter reading
3. Calculate correction factor

**Typical uncertainty:** ±0.2 dB (k=2)

### Antenna gain calibration

**Standard gain horns:**
- Calculated gain from dimensions
- Uncertainty: ±0.15 dB
- Frequency: 1-40 GHz per horn

**Three-antenna method:**
- No standard antenna needed
- Three pairs of measurements
- Solve simultaneous equations
- Uncertainty: ±0.3 dB typical

### Spectrum analyzer calibration

**Frequency accuracy:**
- Compare to frequency standard (GPS-disciplined oscillator)
- Spec: ±1 ppm typical

**Amplitude accuracy:**
- Compare to calibrated signal generator
- Spec: ±0.5 dB typical

**Noise floor:**
- Verify with 50Ω termination
- DANL (Displayed Average Noise Level)

### Uncertainty budget

| Source | Uncertainty (k=1) |
|--------|-------------------|
| VNA calibration | 0.05 dB |
| Cable loss | 0.02 dB |
| Connector repeatability | 0.03 dB |
| Mismatch | 0.1 dB |
| Drift | 0.05 dB |
| **Combined (RSS)** | **0.13 dB** |
| **Expanded (k=2)** | **0.26 dB** |

### Calibration intervals

| Equipment | Typical interval |
|-----------|-----------------|
| VNA | 12 months |
| Power meter | 12 months |
| Signal generator | 12 months |
| Spectrum analyzer | 12 months |
| Calibration standards | 12-24 months |
| Reference antenna | 24-36 months |

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: VNA measurement uncertainty**

Measuring S21 = -30 dB through test fixture.

Uncertainty sources:
- VNA spec at -30 dB: ±0.3 dB
- Cable pair: ±0.1 dB (calibrated)
- Connector repeatability: ±0.05 dB (3 reconnects)
- Temperature: ±0.02 dB/°C × 2°C = ±0.04 dB

Combined: $\sqrt{0.3^2 + 0.1^2 + 0.05^2 + 0.04^2} = 0.32$ dB

Expanded (k=2): ±0.64 dB

Report: S21 = -30.0 ± 0.6 dB

**Example: Reference antenna gain verification**

Standard gain horn marked 15.0 dBi at 10 GHz.

Three-antenna measurement with horn A, B, C gives:
- A+B = 28.5 dB path loss
- A+C = 29.0 dB path loss
- B+C = 28.0 dB path loss

Solving: GA = 14.8 dBi, GB = 13.7 dBi, GC = 14.3 dBi

Horn A is 0.2 dB low from marked value — within spec.

**Example: Daily system check**

Before antenna measurement session:
1. Connect through cable to reference load
2. Verify S11 < -50 dB
3. Connect reference antenna
4. Verify gain within ±0.3 dB of reference value
5. Document results

If check fails, recalibrate or investigate.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Expired calibration.** Out-of-calibration equipment invalidates measurements. Check cal dates before testing.

2. **Wrong standards for frequency.** Calibration standards have limited bandwidth. Using SOLT standards outside their range adds error.

3. **Ignoring environmental conditions.** Standards are characterized at specific temperature/humidity. Correct for lab conditions.

4. **Forgetting connector care.** Worn connectors add uncertainty. Inspect, clean, and torque properly.

5. **Misunderstanding expanded uncertainty.** k=2 means 95% confidence level. k=1 is one standard deviation (~68%). Reports should specify k factor.

</section>
