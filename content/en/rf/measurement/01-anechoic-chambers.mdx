<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why anechoic chambers?

To measure an antenna's true radiation pattern, you need a reflection-free environment. Outdoor ranges require miles of clear space. Anechoic chambers create this environment indoors by absorbing electromagnetic waves on all surfaces.

This is the gold standard for antenna characterization.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine measuring your voice in a room with echoes — you'd hear the original sound plus reflections. An anechoic chamber is the RF equivalent of a soundproof room lined with acoustic foam. The absorbing material converts RF energy to heat instead of reflecting it.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Types of anechoic chambers

| Type | Frequency range | Description |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| Fully anechoic | > 100 MHz | All surfaces absorbing |
| Semi-anechoic | > 30 MHz | Conducting floor, absorbing walls/ceiling |
| GTEM cell | DC - 20 GHz | Transverse EM guided structure |
| Reverberation | > 80 MHz | Metal walls, mode stirring |

### Absorber materials

**Pyramidal foam absorbers:**
- Carbon-loaded polyurethane foam
- Pyramid shape: gradual impedance transition
- Length ≈ λ/4 to λ at lowest frequency
- Reflectivity: -20 to -50 dB

**Ferrite tiles:**
- For frequencies 30-1000 MHz
- Thin profile (6-12 mm)
- Magnetic loss mechanism
- Often combined with foam for broadband

**Hybrid absorbers:**
- Ferrite base + foam pyramids
- Coverage: 30 MHz - 40 GHz

### Chamber sizing

**Quiet zone**: the volume where reflectivity meets specification.

Far-field condition: $R \geq 2D^2/\lambda$

| Antenna size (D) | Frequency | Minimum R |
|------------------|-----------|-----------|
| 0.3 m | 10 GHz | 6 m |
| 1.0 m | 3 GHz | 20 m |
| 2.0 m | 1 GHz | 27 m |

Chamber dimensions: 2-3× quiet zone size.

### Performance specifications

| Parameter | Typical spec |
|-----------|--------------|
| Reflectivity | < -40 dB (quiet zone) |
| Cross-pol isolation | > 30 dB |
| Shielding effectiveness | > 100 dB |
| Temperature stability | ±2°C |
| Humidity control | 30-70% RH |

### Compact range alternative

For large antennas or low frequencies:

**Parabolic reflector**: converts spherical wave from feed to plane wave
- Quiet zone at focal region
- Chamber size reduced by 5-10×
- Reflector surface accuracy: λ/100 RMS

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Chamber sizing for 5G antenna**

Antenna aperture: 0.5 m × 0.5 m (D = 0.7 m diagonal)
Frequency: 28 GHz (λ = 10.7 mm)

Far-field distance: R = 2 × 0.7² / 0.0107 = 92 m

This requires a compact range — a direct far-field chamber would be impractical.

**Example: Absorber selection**

Test requirement: 100 MHz to 6 GHz

Solution:
- Floor and lower walls: ferrite tiles (100-1000 MHz)
- Upper walls and ceiling: 1m pyramidal foam (800 MHz - 6 GHz)
- Combined reflectivity: < -25 dB across band

**Example: Quiet zone verification**

Procedure:
1. Mount reference antenna at chamber center
2. Measure received power at grid of points (±30 cm)
3. Calculate amplitude variation
4. Specification: < ±0.5 dB across quiet zone

If spec fails, check:
- Absorber damage or degradation
- Cable routing reflections
- Support structure reflections

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Ignoring lower frequency limit.** Absorbers have minimum effective frequency. A 30 cm pyramid won't work well below 300 MHz.

2. **Forgetting the floor.** Semi-anechoic chambers have a conducting floor for EMC testing. Not suitable for full 3D pattern measurements.

3. **Cable and support reflections.** Every cable and mounting structure reflects. Use absorber-covered supports and route cables away from quiet zone.

4. **Temperature effects on absorbers.** Foam absorbers change properties with temperature. Specify operating range carefully.

5. **Assuming perfect conditions.** Real chambers have finite reflectivity. Include chamber uncertainty in measurement uncertainty budget.

</section>
