<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why pattern measurement?

Simulation predicts, measurement confirms. No matter how good your antenna model is, the physical antenna has manufacturing tolerances, feed effects, and environmental interactions. Pattern measurement reveals the truth — where your antenna actually radiates power.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Measuring a radiation pattern is like photographing a light bulb from all angles. You rotate around the source, recording intensity at each position. The collection of intensity values forms the 3D pattern.

The challenge is ensuring you're far enough away that the wavefront looks flat (far-field), and that reflections don't corrupt your measurement.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Far-field measurement

**Setup:**
- AUT (Antenna Under Test) on positioner
- Measurement antenna (horn or OEWG) at far-field distance
- VNA or signal generator + receiver
- θ-φ rotation system

**Procedure:**
1. Align AUT and measurement antenna boresights
2. Set frequency
3. Rotate AUT through θ, φ angles
4. Record amplitude (and optionally phase) at each angle
5. Plot polar or Cartesian pattern

**Far-field distance:**
$$R_{ff} \geq \frac{2D^2}{\lambda}$$

where D = largest dimension of AUT.

### Near-field measurement

**Why use it:** When $R_{ff}$ is impractically large.

**Types:**

| Geometry | Probe motion | Best for |
|----------|--------------|----------|
| Planar | XY scan | High-gain antennas |
| Cylindrical | φ rotation + Z scan | Omnidirectional |
| Spherical | θ-φ rotation | General purpose |

**Process:**
1. Sample E-field amplitude and phase on surface
2. Apply Fourier transform to get far-field pattern
3. Requires reference antenna for absolute gain

**Sampling density:** $\Delta < \lambda/2$ (Nyquist criterion)

### Pattern parameters to extract

| Parameter | Definition |
|-----------|------------|
| Main lobe direction | θ, φ of maximum |
| Half-power beamwidth | Angle between -3 dB points |
| First sidelobe level | dB below main lobe |
| Front-to-back ratio | Main lobe / 180° opposite |
| Null depth | dB at pattern minimum |
| Cross-polarization | Orthogonal component level |

### Coordinate systems

**Ludwig-3 (most common):**
- Co-pol: component aligned with principal polarization
- Cross-pol: orthogonal component
- Natural for linear polarization

**Spherical:**
- θ and φ components
- Natural for coordinate system, not polarization

### Gain measurement methods

**Gain comparison:**
$$G_{AUT} = G_{ref} + (P_{AUT} - P_{ref})$$

where $G_{ref}$ is known reference antenna gain.

**Two-antenna method:**
$$G = \sqrt{G_1 G_2} = \frac{4\pi R}{λ} \sqrt{\frac{P_r}{P_t}}$$

Requires two identical antennas.

**Three-antenna method:**
Three measurements with three different antennas, solve 3 equations for 3 unknowns.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Horn antenna pattern**

Standard gain horn at 10 GHz.
Aperture: 5λ × 4λ.

Far-field distance: R = 2 × (5 × 0.03)² / 0.03 = 1.5 m

Beamwidth estimate: θ ≈ 65°/5 = 13° (E-plane), φ ≈ 65°/4 = 16° (H-plane)

Angular sampling: 1° steps adequate (pattern varies slowly for horn)

**Example: Near-field transformation**

64-element phased array at 28 GHz (λ = 10.7 mm).
Array aperture: 8λ × 8λ = 86 mm × 86 mm.

Far-field distance: R = 2 × 0.086² / 0.0107 = 1.4 m

Seems manageable, but including radome and mounting: D → 200 mm.
New R = 2 × 0.2² / 0.0107 = 7.5 m

Near-field with planar scan at 0.5 m is more practical.

Scan plane: 300 mm × 300 mm (larger than array for edge effects)
Sample spacing: 5 mm (< λ/2 = 5.35 mm)
Points: 60 × 60 = 3600

**Example: Cross-polarization measurement**

Dual-polarized patch antenna.

Measure H-pol pattern with H-pol probe → co-pol pattern
Measure H-pol pattern with V-pol probe → cross-pol pattern

If cross-pol peak is -25 dB relative to co-pol peak, the antenna has 25 dB cross-polar discrimination.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Measuring in reactive near-field.** For R < λ/(2π), fields are reactive, not radiating. Far-field transform doesn't work.

2. **Aliasing in near-field scan.** If Δ > λ/2, you'll miss high-frequency spatial content. Grating lobes appear as artifacts.

3. **Cable effects.** Moving cables change pattern. Use rotary joints or dress cables consistently.

4. **Positioner reflections.** Metal positioners reflect. Use absorber-covered supports or subtract calibration.

5. **Phase drift.** Near-field measurements need stable phase. Temperature, vibration, and source drift cause errors.

</section>
