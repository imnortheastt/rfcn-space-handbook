<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why the Friis equation?

You have a transmitter with known power and antenna gain. A receiver sits some distance away with its own antenna. How much power reaches the receiver?

The Friis transmission equation answers this question for free-space propagation — the idealized case with no obstacles, no ground, no atmosphere. It's the starting point for every link budget, satellite communication calculation, and wireless system design.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A transmitting antenna radiates power outward. At distance $d$, that power spreads over a sphere of area $4\pi d^2$. The farther you are, the more diluted the signal.

The receiving antenna captures a portion of this spreading energy. A larger, higher-gain antenna captures more. The Friis equation combines these factors: transmit power, transmit antenna gain, spreading loss, and receive antenna effective area.

The key insight: free-space loss grows with the **square** of distance. Double the distance, lose 6 dB (factor of 4 in power). This is purely geometric spreading, not absorption.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### The Friis equation

$$P_r = P_t \cdot G_t \cdot G_r \cdot \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^2$$

where:
- $P_r$ = received power (W)
- $P_t$ = transmitted power (W)
- $G_t$ = transmit antenna gain (linear, not dB)
- $G_r$ = receive antenna gain (linear)
- $\lambda$ = wavelength (m)
- $d$ = distance (m)

### In decibels

$$P_r[\text{dBm}] = P_t[\text{dBm}] + G_t[\text{dBi}] + G_r[\text{dBi}] - L_{fs}[\text{dB}]$$

where free-space path loss (FSPL) is:

$$L_{fs} = 20\log_{10}\left(\frac{4\pi d}{\lambda}\right) = 20\log_{10}(d) + 20\log_{10}(f) + 20\log_{10}\left(\frac{4\pi}{c}\right)$$

### Practical FSPL formulas

With $d$ in km and $f$ in MHz:
$$L_{fs}[\text{dB}] = 32.45 + 20\log_{10}(d_\text{km}) + 20\log_{10}(f_\text{MHz})$$

With $d$ in meters and $f$ in GHz:
$$L_{fs}[\text{dB}] = 92.45 + 20\log_{10}(d_\text{m}) + 20\log_{10}(f_\text{GHz})$$

### Effective aperture relationship

Antenna gain relates to effective aperture:

$$G = \frac{4\pi A_e}{\lambda^2}$$

This explains the $\lambda^2$ in Friis: higher frequency means smaller effective aperture for the same gain, hence more loss.

### Reference values

| Frequency | 1 km FSPL | 10 km FSPL |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 100 MHz | 72 dB | 92 dB |
| 1 GHz | 92 dB | 112 dB |
| 10 GHz | 112 dB | 132 dB |
| 28 GHz | 121 dB | 141 dB |

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Wi-Fi link**

2.4 GHz, 100 mW transmitter (20 dBm), 3 dBi antennas, 100 m distance.

FSPL = 92.45 + 20log₁₀(100) + 20log₁₀(2.4) = 92.45 + 40 + 7.6 = 80 dB

$P_r$ = 20 + 3 + 3 − 80 = −54 dBm

This is well above typical Wi-Fi receiver sensitivity (−70 to −90 dBm).

**Example: Satellite link at GEO**

12 GHz downlink, satellite at 36,000 km, 50 W EIRP (47 dBW), 1 m dish (41 dBi at 12 GHz).

FSPL = 32.45 + 20log₁₀(36000) + 20log₁₀(12000) = 32.45 + 91.1 + 81.6 = 205.2 dB

$P_r$ = 47 + 41 − 205.2 = −117.2 dBW = −87.2 dBm

**Example: 5G mmWave at 28 GHz**

28 GHz, 30 dBm transmitter, 18 dBi antennas both ends, 200 m distance.

FSPL = 92.45 + 20log₁₀(200) + 20log₁₀(28) = 92.45 + 46 + 28.9 = 118.4 dB

$P_r$ = 30 + 18 + 18 − 118.4 = −52.4 dBm

High-gain antennas compensate for the higher path loss at mmWave.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Free space only.** Friis assumes no obstacles, reflections, or atmospheric effects. Real links have additional losses and multipath. Use Friis as the baseline, then add margins.

2. **Antenna gain reference.** Gains must be relative to isotropic (dBi). If specs give dBd (relative to dipole), add 2.15 dB to convert.

3. **Near-field invalid.** Friis applies in the far field ($d > 2D^2/\lambda$ for aperture dimension $D$). In the near field, the spreading law differs.

4. **Frequency "loss."** Higher frequency doesn't inherently lose more power — it just means smaller effective aperture for the same gain. A higher-gain antenna at higher frequency can have the same or lower path loss.

5. **Linear vs. dB.** The equation multiplies linear gains but sums dB gains. Mixing conventions causes errors. Convert consistently.

6. **EIRP confusion.** EIRP = $P_t \cdot G_t$. If given EIRP directly, don't add $G_t$ again.

</section>
