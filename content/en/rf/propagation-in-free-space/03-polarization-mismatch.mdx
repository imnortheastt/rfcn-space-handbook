## Why polarization matters?

A vertical antenna transmitting to a horizontal antenna loses nearly all its signal — not because of distance, but because of **polarization mismatch**. The electric field oscillates in one plane; the receiving antenna responds to a different plane.

Polarization is often overlooked in link budgets, but it can cause 3 dB loss (circular to linear) or near-total loss (orthogonal linear). Understanding it prevents mysterious link failures.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Electromagnetic waves have electric fields that oscillate in a specific direction. Think of polarization as the "orientation" of the wave:

- **Linear vertical:** E-field oscillates up and down
- **Linear horizontal:** E-field oscillates side to side
- **Circular:** E-field rotates as the wave travels

A receiving antenna extracts energy most efficiently when its polarization matches the incoming wave. Misalignment causes loss.

The extreme case: a vertical antenna receives nothing from a horizontally polarized wave (in theory — in practice, scattering and imperfect antennas create some coupling).

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Polarization loss factor

The polarization efficiency (or polarization loss factor) is:

$$\eta_p = |\hat{p}_t \cdot \hat{p}_r^*|^2$$

where $\hat{p}_t$ and $\hat{p}_r$ are the unit polarization vectors of transmit and receive antennas.

For linear polarizations at angle $\theta$:

$$\eta_p = \cos^2(\theta)$$

| Mismatch | $\theta$ | Loss (dB) |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Aligned | 0° | 0 |
| 45° tilt | 45° | 3 |
| Orthogonal | 90° | ∞ (theory) |

### Circular polarization

Circular polarization (CP) comes in two senses:
- **Right-hand circular (RHCP):** E-field rotates clockwise (looking into the wave)
- **Left-hand circular (LHCP):** E-field rotates counter-clockwise

Matching circular polarizations: $\eta_p = 1$ (0 dB loss)
Opposite circular polarizations: $\eta_p = 0$ (total loss)

### Circular to linear

A circularly polarized wave received by a linear antenna:

$$\eta_p = 0.5 \quad (-3\text{ dB})$$

Half the power is in the orthogonal component and is lost. This is why satellite links use circular polarization but lose 3 dB to handheld devices with linear (whip) antennas.

### Axial ratio

Real circularly polarized antennas aren't perfect. The **axial ratio (AR)** measures ellipticity:

$$\text{AR} = \frac{E_{max}}{E_{min}}$$

Perfect circular: AR = 1 (0 dB)
Linear: AR = ∞

For AR in dB:
$$\text{AR}_\text{dB} = 20\log_{10}(\text{AR})$$

Typical CP antennas achieve AR < 3 dB. Degraded AR increases polarization loss.

### Modified Friis equation

Including polarization loss:

$$P_r = P_t G_t G_r \eta_p \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^2$$

In dB:

$$P_r = P_t + G_t + G_r + L_p - L_{fs}$$

where $L_p = -10\log_{10}(\eta_p)$ is polarization loss.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Satellite to handheld**

GPS satellite transmits RHCP. Handheld receiver has a linear whip antenna.

Polarization loss: 3 dB

This is budgeted into GPS link calculations. High-precision receivers use CP antennas to recover this 3 dB.

**Example: Misaligned dipoles**

Two vertical dipoles, but one is tilted 30° from vertical.

$\eta_p = \cos^2(30°) = 0.75$
Loss: $-10\log_{10}(0.75) = 1.25$ dB

**Example: Cross-pol interference**

A dual-polarized antenna uses vertical and horizontal channels. Ideally, they're isolated. But imperfect antennas have **cross-polarization discrimination (XPD)**:

$$\text{XPD} = 10\log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{co-pol}}{P_{cross-pol}}\right)$$

Typical values: 20–30 dB. Lower XPD means more interference between polarization channels.

**Example: Faraday rotation**

Signals passing through the ionosphere experience Faraday rotation — the polarization plane rotates. For a linearly polarized wave, this causes time-varying loss as the rotation changes.

Solution: use circular polarization (immune to rotation) or dual-pol receivers that combine both orientations.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Scattering saves you.** In multipath environments, reflections depolarize the signal. A "mismatched" antenna may still receive scattered energy. Don't assume 90° mismatch means zero signal indoors.

2. **Axial ratio off-axis.** CP antennas have good AR only over limited angles. Pointing errors degrade AR and increase loss.

3. **Sense matters.** RHCP and LHCP are orthogonal. Using the wrong sense is as bad as linear orthogonal. Verify polarization sense in system design.

4. **Antenna orientation.** A nominally vertical mobile antenna isn't always vertical — users hold phones at various angles. Budget 3–6 dB margin for handheld orientation uncertainty.

5. **Cross-pol isolation.** In dual-pol MIMO systems, poor XPD reduces capacity by creating inter-stream interference. Aim for XPD > 20 dB.

6. **Measurement conventions.** Polarization definitions vary (IEEE vs. classical optics). Confirm the convention when reading specifications.

</section>
