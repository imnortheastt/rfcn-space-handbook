## Why path loss models?

Free-space path loss (FSPL) assumes a perfect vacuum with no obstacles. Real environments have ground reflections, buildings, foliage, and atmospheric effects. These add loss beyond FSPL and change how loss scales with distance.

Path loss models capture these effects empirically or semi-analytically. They're essential for coverage planning, interference analysis, and link budget calculations in real deployments.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

In free space, power falls off as $1/d^2$ (20 dB per decade of distance). But over real terrain:

- **Ground reflection** creates interference patterns that can make loss worse than free space
- **Obstructions** add diffraction and absorption losses
- **Multipath** causes fading that varies with position

Path loss models approximate these effects with a **path loss exponent** $n$ that modifies the distance dependency:

$$L \propto d^n$$

Free space has $n = 2$. Urban environments typically have $n = 3$ to $4$. Indoors can exceed $n = 4$.

The key insight: real environments are "lossier" than free space, and the loss grows faster with distance.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Log-distance path loss model

$$L(d) = L(d_0) + 10n \log_{10}\left(\frac{d}{d_0}\right) + X_\sigma$$

where:
- $L(d_0)$ = path loss at reference distance $d_0$ (often 1 m or 100 m)
- $n$ = path loss exponent
- $X_\sigma$ = shadow fading (log-normal, standard deviation $\sigma$)

| Environment | Typical $n$ | Typical $\sigma$ (dB) |
|-------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Free space | 2.0 | 0 |
| Urban LOS | 2.0–2.5 | 3–6 |
| Urban NLOS | 3.0–4.0 | 6–10 |
| Suburban | 2.5–3.5 | 4–8 |
| Indoor LOS | 1.6–2.0 | 3–6 |
| Indoor NLOS | 3.0–5.0 | 6–12 |

### Two-ray ground reflection model

For transmitter height $h_t$ and receiver height $h_r$ over a flat, reflective ground:

$$P_r = P_t G_t G_r \frac{h_t^2 h_r^2}{d^4}$$

At long distances ($d \gg \sqrt{h_t h_r}$), the direct and reflected paths nearly cancel, giving $n = 4$ instead of $n = 2$.

**Breakpoint distance:** $d_b = \frac{4 h_t h_r}{\lambda}$

Below $d_b$: loss follows free-space ($n \approx 2$). Above $d_b$: loss increases to $n \approx 4$.

### Reference path loss

Common convention for $L(d_0)$ at $d_0 = 1$ m:

$$L(1\text{ m}) = 20\log_{10}\left(\frac{4\pi}{\lambda}\right) = 20\log_{10}\left(\frac{4\pi f}{c}\right)$$

At 2.4 GHz: $L(1\text{ m}) \approx 40$ dB
At 28 GHz: $L(1\text{ m}) \approx 62$ dB

### Adding margins

Real link budgets include additional losses:

$$L_{total} = L_{path} + L_{shadow} + L_{body} + L_{penetration} + L_{rain} + \ldots$$

| Loss type | Typical value |
|-----------|---------------|
| Body loss (handheld) | 3–6 dB |
| Building penetration | 10–25 dB |
| Vehicle penetration | 3–10 dB |
| Foliage (per m) | 0.2–1 dB |
| Rain (at 28 GHz, heavy) | 5–15 dB/km |

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Outdoor cellular coverage**

2 GHz, 46 dBm base station, 18 dBi antenna, urban NLOS ($n = 3.5$, $\sigma = 8$ dB).

$L(1\text{ m}) = 38$ dB
At 500 m: $L = 38 + 35 \times \log_{10}(500) = 38 + 94.5 = 132.5$ dB

$P_r = 46 + 18 - 132.5 = -68.5$ dBm (median)

For 90% coverage reliability (shadowing margin = 1.28σ): add 10 dB margin.
Edge-of-cell receive power: −78.5 dBm

**Example: Indoor Wi-Fi**

5 GHz, 20 dBm AP, 0 dBi antennas, indoor NLOS ($n = 3.5$).

$L(1\text{ m}) = 47$ dB
At 30 m: $L = 47 + 35 \times \log_{10}(30) = 47 + 51.7 = 98.7$ dB

$P_r = 20 + 0 + 0 - 98.7 = -78.7$ dBm

This is marginal for high-throughput operation (typically needs > −70 dBm for good rates).

**Example: Two-ray breakpoint**

28 GHz mmWave, $h_t = 10$ m, $h_r = 1.5$ m.

$d_b = \frac{4 \times 10 \times 1.5}{0.0107} = 5607$ m

Below 5.6 km: $n \approx 2$. Above: $n \approx 4$. For urban deployments under 200 m, free-space model is reasonable for LOS.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Model validity range.** Models are calibrated for specific environments and frequencies. Don't extrapolate a 2 GHz urban model to 60 GHz.

2. **LOS vs. NLOS.** Most models assume one or the other. Real environments mix both. Separate calculations may be needed.

3. **Shadow fading.** $X_\sigma$ is random per location but correlated over distance (typically 10–50 m correlation). Don't treat it as independent at every point.

4. **Antenna height.** Two-ray and many empirical models are sensitive to antenna height. Ground-level measurements don't apply to rooftop deployments.

5. **Frequency dependence.** Some models include frequency terms; others require recalibration. Check model documentation.

6. **Indoor complexity.** Indoor propagation depends heavily on construction materials, layout, and furniture. Generic models may be off by 10+ dB.
