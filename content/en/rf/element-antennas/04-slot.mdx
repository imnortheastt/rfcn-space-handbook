<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why slot antenna?

A slot is the electromagnetic complement of a dipole — a narrow opening in a conducting surface rather than a wire in space. Cut a half-wavelength slot in a metal sheet, and it radiates like a dipole but with swapped E and H fields.

Slots are everywhere: waveguide slot arrays for radar, cavity-backed slots in aircraft, and slot antennas in smartphones. Understanding Babinet's principle connects slots to dipoles you already know.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine a dipole as a positive structure — metal wire surrounded by air. Now invert it: air slot surrounded by metal. This is the slot antenna.

Currents flow on the metal surface around the slot. The slot itself is where radiation escapes. Where a dipole has maximum electric field (at the ends), a slot has maximum magnetic field, and vice versa.

This complementary relationship means everything you know about dipoles translates to slots — with E and H interchanged.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Babinet's principle

For complementary structures (slot and dipole with same dimensions):

$$Z_{slot} \times Z_{dipole} = \frac{\eta^2}{4} \approx 35476\ \Omega^2$$

where $\eta \approx 377\ \Omega$ is free-space impedance.

For a half-wave slot:
$$Z_{slot} = \frac{35476}{73} \approx 486\ \Omega$$

High impedance compared to dipole's 73 Ω.

### Radiation pattern

A slot in an infinite ground plane radiates identically to a dipole, but:
- E-field and H-field patterns are swapped
- Polarization is rotated 90°
- A horizontal slot produces vertical polarization

### Half-wave slot

Length $\ell \approx \lambda/2$ produces resonance.

For a slot in a large ground plane:
- Impedance: ~486 Ω at center feed
- Pattern: figure-8 in plane of slot
- Directivity: 1.64 (2.15 dBi) — same as dipole

### Cavity-backed slot

Placing a cavity behind the slot:
- Forces unidirectional radiation (front only)
- Approximately doubles directivity
- Reduces back lobe significantly
- Commonly used in aircraft and flush-mount applications

### Waveguide slot arrays

Slots cut in waveguide walls create efficient arrays:
- Slot orientation controls coupling strength
- Slot spacing controls beam direction
- Used extensively in radar and satellite communication

### Feeding methods

- **Center feed:** High impedance (~486 Ω), requires transformer
- **Offset feed:** Lower impedance, better match to 50 Ω
- **Microstrip feed:** Couples to slot via electromagnetic coupling
- **Waveguide excitation:** Natural for waveguide slot arrays

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Flush-mount aircraft antenna**

Aircraft require aerodynamic antennas. A cavity-backed slot mounts flush with the fuselage, radiating through a thin dielectric cover.

Typical dimensions at 1 GHz ($\lambda = 30$ cm):
- Slot length: 15 cm
- Slot width: 1–2 cm
- Cavity depth: 7.5 cm (λ/4)

**Example: Waveguide slot array**

X-band radar at 10 GHz ($\lambda = 3$ cm):

Waveguide with 20 longitudinal slots, spaced λ/2 (1.5 cm apart), creates a narrow beam.

Slot offset from centerline controls coupling: larger offset = stronger coupling = more even illumination.

**Example: Smartphone slot**

Modern smartphones use slot antennas in the metal frame. The gap between frame sections acts as a slot, excited by a small coupling element.

Frame slot at 2.4 GHz: ~6 cm effective length.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Ground plane size.** Babinet assumes infinite ground plane. Finite ground modifies pattern and impedance.

2. **High impedance.** Center-fed half-wave slot is ~486 Ω. Matching to 50 Ω requires care.

3. **Slot width.** Narrow slots have high Q (narrowband). Wider slots have lower impedance and broader bandwidth.

4. **Bidirectional radiation.** A slot in a thin ground plane radiates both directions. Cavity backing or reflector needed for unidirectional.

5. **Complementary polarization.** A slot produces the orthogonal polarization to a dipole of the same orientation. Horizontal slot = vertical polarization.

6. **Cavity resonance.** Cavity-backed slots can have unwanted cavity modes if poorly designed.

</section>
