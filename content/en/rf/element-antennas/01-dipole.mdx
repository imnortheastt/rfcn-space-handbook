<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why dipole?

The dipole is the most fundamental antenna — a length of conductor fed at its center. It appears in every RF textbook for a reason: its behavior is analytically tractable, and most other antennas can be understood as variations or arrays of dipoles.

Understanding the dipole gives you a mental model for radiation resistance, impedance variation with length, and pattern formation.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A dipole works by driving current oscillations along a conductor. The oscillating current creates oscillating electromagnetic fields that propagate outward as radiation.

Picture it like this: you apply an alternating voltage at the center, current flows back and forth along the wire, and this accelerating charge radiates energy. The wire acts as a transformer between guided waves (transmission line) and free-space waves.

The dipole radiates most strongly broadside (perpendicular to the wire) and not at all off the ends — a donut-shaped pattern.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Hertzian dipole (infinitesimal)

An idealized dipole of length $\ell \ll \lambda$ with uniform current $I$. The far-field electric field:

$$E_\theta = j\eta \frac{k I \ell}{4\pi r} \sin\theta \, e^{-jkr}$$

where $\eta \approx 377\ \Omega$ is free-space impedance, $k = 2\pi/\lambda$.

Key properties:
- Radiation resistance: $R_{rad} = 80\pi^2 (\ell/\lambda)^2$
- Directivity: 1.5 (1.76 dBi)
- Pattern: $\sin^2\theta$ (donut-shaped)

### Short dipole

For dipoles shorter than $\lambda/10$, current distribution is approximately triangular (maximum at center, zero at ends):

$$R_{rad} = 20\pi^2 (\ell/\lambda)^2$$

The factor of 4 difference from Hertzian comes from the triangular vs. uniform current distribution.

### Half-wave dipole

Length $\ell = \lambda/2$. The most common dipole.

Current distribution is sinusoidal:
$$I(z) = I_0 \cos(kz)$$

Properties:
- Input impedance: $Z_{in} \approx 73 + j42\ \Omega$ (theoretical)
- In practice: ~70 Ω after shortening for resonance
- Directivity: 1.64 (2.15 dBi)
- Radiation pattern: similar to Hertzian but slightly more directive

### Impedance vs. length

| Length | Impedance | Character |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| $\ell \ll \lambda/2$ | Low R, high capacitive X | Hard to match |
| $\ell = \lambda/2$ | ~73 Ω, small inductive X | Easy to match |
| $\ell = \lambda$ | ~2000 Ω | High impedance |
| $\ell = 3\lambda/2$ | ~105 Ω | Multi-lobe pattern |

### Bandwidth

A thicker dipole has lower Q and wider bandwidth. The bandwidth-thickness tradeoff:

$$BW \propto \frac{a}{\lambda}$$

where $a$ is wire radius. Fat dipoles (cage, bow-tie) achieve octave+ bandwidth.

### Ground effects

A dipole above ground experiences image effects:
- At height $h = \lambda/4$: image reinforces, low-angle radiation enhanced
- At height $h = \lambda/2$: image cancels at horizon, high-angle lobe appears

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: FM broadcast dipole**

FM band: 88–108 MHz, center ~100 MHz ($\lambda = 3$ m).

Half-wave dipole length: $\ell = 0.5 \times 3 = 1.5$ m.

With velocity factor 0.95 for typical wire: $\ell_{actual} = 1.425$ m.

**Example: 2.4 GHz dipole**

$\lambda = 0.125$ m at 2.4 GHz.

Half-wave: 62.5 mm electrical, ~59 mm physical.

This is why Wi-Fi rubber duck antennas are about 6 cm long.

**Example: Dipole radiation resistance**

Short dipole with $\ell = \lambda/20$:

$$R_{rad} = 20\pi^2 (0.05)^2 = 0.49\ \Omega$$

This tiny radiation resistance means most power is lost in conductor resistance — why short dipoles are inefficient.

Half-wave: $R_{rad} \approx 73\ \Omega$ — much easier to drive efficiently.

**Example: Array element**

Dipoles are common array elements. A Yagi-Uda uses one driven dipole with parasitic director/reflector elements. Log-periodic arrays use many dipoles of varying length.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Physical vs. electrical length.** A dipole resonates at $\ell < \lambda/2$ physical length due to end effects. Typical shortening: 2–5%.

2. **Feed impedance variations.** The 73 Ω is theoretical at resonance. Actual impedance depends on wire diameter, nearby conductors, and exact frequency.

3. **Balun requirement.** Dipoles are balanced; coax is unbalanced. Without a balun, current flows on the coax outer conductor, distorting the pattern.

4. **Ground proximity.** A dipole near ground has modified impedance and pattern. The "free-space" values assume isolation.

5. **Directivity vs. gain.** Dipole directivity is 2.15 dBi. Gain is lower due to ohmic losses, especially for thin wires.

6. **Bandwidth assumptions.** A half-wave dipole has ~10% bandwidth for 2:1 VSWR. Wideband applications need fat dipoles or matching networks.

</section>
