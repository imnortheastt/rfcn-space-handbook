<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why monopole?

A monopole is half a dipole with a ground plane providing the other half via image theory. This simple configuration is everywhere: car antennas, handheld radios, cellular base stations, Wi-Fi routers.

The monopole's advantage is simplicity — one element, fed against ground, with no balun required. Understanding image theory connects monopole performance to dipole fundamentals.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine cutting a dipole in half and placing one arm against a perfect mirror (ground plane). The mirror creates a virtual image of the missing half. Electrically, the system behaves like a full dipole, but you only need to build half of it.

The ground plane is the mirror. Current flows into the monopole, and return current flows on the ground surface. The image current completes the radiating structure.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Image theory

A monopole of height $h$ over an infinite perfect ground plane is equivalent to a dipole of length $2h$ in free space.

The image current is equal in magnitude and direction, so radiation doubles in the upper hemisphere while the lower hemisphere is blocked by the ground.

### Quarter-wave monopole

Height $h = \lambda/4$, equivalent to half-wave dipole.

Properties:
- Input impedance: $Z_{in} \approx 36.5 + j21\ \Omega$ (half the dipole)
- Directivity: 3.28 (5.15 dBi) — double the dipole due to hemisphere concentration
- Pattern: maximum at horizon, null at zenith

### Impedance relationship

For any monopole over perfect ground:

$$Z_{monopole} = \frac{Z_{dipole}}{2}$$

This halving occurs because the ground eliminates half the radiating space while maintaining the same current.

### Radiation resistance

$$R_{rad,mono} = 40\pi^2 \left(\frac{h}{\lambda}\right)^2$$

for short monopoles ($h \ll \lambda/4$).

### Ground plane effects

**Finite ground plane:** Edges diffract, creating ripples in the pattern and modifying impedance. Rule of thumb: ground plane radius > λ/4 for reasonable performance.

**Real ground:** Conductivity matters. Poor ground (dry soil) increases losses and tilts the pattern upward. Good ground (sea water) approaches ideal.

**Elevated ground plane:** Radials at height create an artificial ground. Common in elevated installations.

### Radials

Ground plane radials substitute for an infinite ground:
- 4 radials at 90° spacing: adequate for many applications
- 16+ radials: approaches ideal performance
- Drooping radials (45°): raises impedance closer to 50 Ω

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: λ/4 vertical for 146 MHz**

$\lambda = 2.05$ m at 146 MHz.

Monopole height: $h = 0.514$ m ≈ 51 cm.

With 5% shortening for end effect: ~49 cm physical length.

Ground plane radials: 4 × 51 cm horizontal or 4 × 55 cm drooping at 45°.

**Example: AM broadcast tower**

AM stations (530–1700 kHz) use towers as monopoles. At 1 MHz ($\lambda = 300$ m):

Quarter-wave tower: 75 m tall.

Ground system: 120 buried radials, each 0.4λ (120 m) long.

**Example: Cellular base station**

Sectorized sites use arrays of monopoles or dipoles over ground planes. The ground plane (typically the mounting structure) provides the image.

**Example: Rubber duck antenna**

Handheld radios use helically wound monopoles — shorter than λ/4 but with loading. The human body acts as an imperfect ground plane.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Ground plane size.** A λ/4 monopole on a λ/8 ground plane has significantly degraded performance and raised impedance.

2. **Ground losses.** Real ground absorbs power. AM broadcast stations invest heavily in ground systems because ground losses dominate.

3. **Counterpoise vs. ground.** A few radials are a counterpoise, not a ground plane. Performance is adequate but not ideal.

4. **Vehicle mounting.** Car antennas work because the body is the ground plane. Mounting location affects pattern symmetry.

5. **Pattern tilt.** Over lossy ground, the pattern maximum tilts upward from horizon. The "horizon" gain may be 10+ dB below peak.

6. **Impedance bandwidth.** Like dipoles, thin monopoles are narrowband. Fat monopoles or matching networks extend bandwidth.

</section>
