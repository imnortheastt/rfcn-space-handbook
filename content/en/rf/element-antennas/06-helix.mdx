<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why helix antenna?

The helix antenna produces circular polarization naturally — no hybrid couplers, no dual feeds, just a wound wire. This makes it ideal for satellite communication, GPS, RFID, and any application requiring CP.

A helix can operate in two modes: normal mode (small helix, omnidirectional) or axial mode (large helix, directional endfire beam). Understanding both reveals how geometry controls radiation.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Picture a wire wound in a spiral, like a spring. Current travels along the wire, but the geometry means different parts of the current contribute at different phases and orientations.

In axial mode, the circumference is about one wavelength. Current traveling around each turn creates a rotating field vector — that's circular polarization. The turns add in phase along the axis, producing an endfire beam.

In normal mode, the helix is much smaller than a wavelength. It behaves like a short dipole and small loop combined, with radiation patterns that overlap.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Helix geometry

Key parameters:
- $D$ = diameter
- $S$ = spacing between turns
- $N$ = number of turns
- $C = \pi D$ = circumference
- $L = \sqrt{S^2 + C^2}$ = wire length per turn
- $\alpha = \arctan(S/C)$ = pitch angle

### Axial mode

Operating conditions:
$$0.75\lambda < C < 1.33\lambda$$

Optimal: $C \approx \lambda$, pitch angle $\alpha \approx 12–14°$.

**Properties:**
- Radiation: endfire (along helix axis)
- Polarization: circular (RHCP for right-hand winding)
- Directivity: $D \approx 15 \frac{C^2 S}{\lambda^3} N$
- Input impedance: $R_{in} \approx 140 \frac{C}{\lambda}$ Ω
- Beamwidth: $\theta_{HP} \approx \frac{52\lambda^{1.5}}{C\sqrt{NS}}$ degrees

For $C = \lambda$: $R_{in} \approx 140$ Ω — reasonable match to 50 Ω with simple transformer.

**Axial ratio:**
$$AR = \frac{2N + 1}{2N}$$

For N ≥ 4, AR < 1.2 (excellent circular polarization).

### Normal mode

Operating conditions:
$$C \ll \lambda, \quad S \ll \lambda$$

The helix acts as a combination of:
- Small loop (magnetic dipole) from the turns
- Short dipole (electric dipole) from the axial component

**Properties:**
- Radiation: omnidirectional in plane perpendicular to axis
- Polarization: typically elliptical, can be made circular
- Low radiation resistance (like small loop)
- Used in compact antennas (rubber duck)

### Ground plane

Axial-mode helices require a ground plane (reflector) at the feed end:
- Minimum diameter: ~0.75λ
- Often a cup or cavity for pattern improvement
- Ground plane prevents back radiation

### Bandwidth

Axial-mode helix is inherently wideband:
- 1.7:1 bandwidth typical for good CP
- Impedance relatively constant over band
- One of the widest-bandwidth CP antennas

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: S-band satellite helix**

2.2 GHz downlink, $\lambda = 13.6$ cm.

For axial mode with $C = \lambda$:
- Diameter: $D = 13.6/\pi = 4.3$ cm
- Pitch angle 13°: $S = C \tan(13°) = 3.1$ cm
- 10 turns: total length = 31 cm
- Directivity: ~15 dBi
- RHCP for right-hand winding

**Example: UHF RFID reader**

915 MHz, $\lambda = 32.8$ cm.

Compact 4-turn helix:
- $D = 10.4$ cm, $C = \lambda$
- $S = 7.5$ cm
- Total length: 30 cm
- Gain: ~10 dBi
- RHCP to match RFID tag response

**Example: Quadrifilar helix (QFH)**

For VHF satellite reception (137 MHz), QFH antennas use four interleaved helices with appropriate phasing:
- Produces near-hemispherical coverage
- Cardioid-like pattern
- RHCP for NOAA satellites

**Example: Rubber duck (normal mode)**

Handheld radio antenna: tightly wound helix, $C \ll \lambda$.

At 450 MHz, a 10 cm rubber duck uses normal-mode helix with loading effect to achieve resonance in compact form. Efficiency is lower than quarter-wave, but size is acceptable.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Mode confusion.** Axial and normal modes have completely different characteristics. Verify circumference is correct for intended mode.

2. **Winding sense.** Right-hand winding produces RHCP; left-hand produces LHCP. Satellite uplink/downlink may require matching sense.

3. **Ground plane size.** Too small a ground plane degrades pattern and increases back lobe. Minimum 0.75λ diameter.

4. **Bandwidth limits.** While wideband, performance degrades outside 0.75λ < C < 1.33λ range. Axial ratio worsens at band edges.

5. **Construction tolerance.** Turn spacing and diameter must be uniform. Variations degrade pattern and polarization purity.

6. **Feeding.** The ~140 Ω impedance requires matching. A tapered helix or quarter-wave transformer is common.

7. **Size.** Axial-mode helix is physically large at low frequencies. At 400 MHz, diameter is ~24 cm with length of 75+ cm for 10 turns.
