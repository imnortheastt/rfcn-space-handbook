<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why loop antenna?

The loop antenna is the magnetic dual of the dipole. While a dipole radiates from linear current, a loop radiates from circulating current — acting like a magnetic dipole.

Loops appear in two distinct forms: small loops (much smaller than λ) used for receiving and direction finding, and full-wave loops used as efficient radiating elements. Understanding both reveals how antenna geometry shapes radiation.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A small loop acts like a coil of wire with current flowing around it. This circulating current creates a magnetic field perpendicular to the loop plane. At RF frequencies, this oscillating magnetic field radiates.

Think of the small loop as a magnetic antenna — it responds primarily to the magnetic component of the wave, while a dipole responds to the electric component.

A full-wave loop is different: current distribution varies around the loop, creating a more complex pattern with higher directivity.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Small loop (electrically small)

Circumference $C \ll \lambda$ (typically $C < \lambda/10$). Current is uniform around the loop.

Far-field is identical to a magnetic dipole (small current loop):

$$E_\phi = j\eta \frac{k^2 A I}{4\pi r} \sin\theta \, e^{-jkr}$$

where $A$ = loop area.

Properties:
- Radiation resistance: $R_{rad} = 31171 \left(\frac{A}{\lambda^2}\right)^2 = 20\pi^2 \left(\frac{C}{\lambda}\right)^4$
- Directivity: 1.5 (1.76 dBi) — same as Hertzian dipole
- Pattern: figure-8, nulls perpendicular to loop plane

The extremely low radiation resistance (often milliohms) makes small loops inefficient transmitters but excellent receivers.

### Radiation resistance scaling

For small loops:

$$R_{rad} \propto \left(\frac{A}{\lambda^2}\right)^2 \propto \left(\frac{C}{\lambda}\right)^4$$

Doubling the circumference increases radiation resistance by 16×.

### Full-wave loop

Circumference $C = \lambda$. Current varies sinusoidally around the loop.

Properties:
- Input impedance: ~100–130 Ω
- Directivity: ~3.4 (5.3 dBi) — higher than dipole
- Pattern: broadside (perpendicular to loop plane)

Full-wave loops are practical transmitting antennas with performance comparable to or better than dipoles.

### Loop shapes

| Shape | Advantages |
|-------|------------|
| Circular | Maximum area for given perimeter, lowest loss |
| Square | Easier construction, similar performance |
| Triangle | Delta loop, compact, ~same performance |
| Multi-turn | Higher inductance for small loops, lower Q |

### Ferrite loading

Small loops wound on ferrite cores increase effective permeability, raising radiation resistance and efficiency. Used in AM radio receivers and low-frequency communication.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Direction-finding loop**

A small loop has sharp nulls perpendicular to its plane. Rotating the loop until null is found indicates the direction to the transmitter.

Used in aviation (ADF), marine, and wildlife tracking.

**Example: AM broadcast receiver**

The ferrite bar antenna in AM radios is a small magnetic loop. The ferrite increases inductance and sensitivity while maintaining small size.

**Example: Full-wave loop for HF**

7 MHz ($\lambda = 43$ m) full-wave loop:

Circumference = 43 m, shaped as square: 10.75 m per side.

Feed impedance ~100 Ω — 2:1 transformer to 50 Ω.

**Example: Magnetic loop transmitter**

An efficient small transmitting loop uses:
- Large diameter conductor (low loss)
- High-Q variable capacitor for tuning
- Small coupling loop for feed

Typical efficiency: 10–50% depending on size and construction.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Small loop efficiency.** Radiation resistance of milliohms means conductor loss dominates. Transmitting efficiency is very low unless the loop is well-constructed.

2. **Pattern orientation.** Small loop nulls are perpendicular to loop plane; full-wave loop maximum is perpendicular. Opposite behaviors.

3. **Tuning sensitivity.** Small transmitting loops are extremely narrowband. A high-Q tuning capacitor requires careful adjustment.

4. **Near-field exposure.** High-power transmitting loops can have dangerous near-field levels. Maintain distance.

5. **Ground effects.** Like all antennas, loops interact with ground. Height affects pattern and impedance.

6. **Bandwidth.** Small loops have Q of 100–1000+. Bandwidth may be 1% or less. Full-wave loops are more broadband.

</section>
