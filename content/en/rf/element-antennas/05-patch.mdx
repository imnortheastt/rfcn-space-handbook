<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why patch antenna?

The patch antenna revolutionized antenna design by making antennas flat, cheap, and integrable with PCBs. A simple metal rectangle on a dielectric substrate over a ground plane — that's it.

Patches dominate modern wireless: GPS receivers, Wi-Fi routers, cellular base stations, satellite terminals, RFID readers. Understanding patch behavior unlocks design for the planar antenna era.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A patch antenna is a resonant cavity with radiating edges. Picture it as two slot antennas: the open edges at opposite ends of the patch radiate like narrow slots fed in phase.

The patch acts like a half-wave resonator. Current flows along the length, and voltage peaks at the open edges where radiation occurs. The ground plane reflects radiation upward, creating a unidirectional pattern.

Think of it as a printed dipole: similar length (~λ/2), but flat and with inherent ground plane backing.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Basic rectangular patch

Patch length $L$ determines resonant frequency:

$$L \approx \frac{c}{2f\sqrt{\varepsilon_{eff}}} - 2\Delta L$$

where:
- $\varepsilon_{eff}$ = effective dielectric constant (accounts for fringing)
- $\Delta L$ = fringing extension (~0.412h for typical substrates)
- $h$ = substrate height

For thin substrates ($h \ll \lambda$):

$$\varepsilon_{eff} \approx \frac{\varepsilon_r + 1}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon_r - 1}{2}\left(1 + \frac{12h}{W}\right)^{-0.5}$$

### Radiation mechanism

The patch edges at $x = 0$ and $x = L$ act as two parallel slots separated by $L \approx \lambda/2$.

Fields at the edges are in phase, so radiation adds constructively broadside (perpendicular to patch).

### Input impedance

At resonance, a patch fed at the edge has:

$$R_{edge} \approx \frac{1}{2(G_1 + G_{12})}$$

where $G_1$ is slot conductance and $G_{12}$ is mutual conductance.

Typical values: 150–300 Ω at edge.

**Inset feed:** Feeding the patch inward from the edge lowers impedance:

$$R_{in}(y_0) = R_{edge} \cos^2\left(\frac{\pi y_0}{L}\right)$$

Setting $y_0$ appropriately achieves 50 Ω match.

### Bandwidth

Patch antennas are inherently narrowband:

$$BW \approx \frac{3.77(\varepsilon_r - 1)}{(\varepsilon_r)^2} \cdot \frac{W}{L} \cdot \frac{h}{\lambda_0}$$

Typical values: 1–5% for thin substrates.

**Increasing bandwidth:**
- Thicker substrate
- Lower ε_r
- Stacked patches
- Parasitic elements

### Directivity and gain

Single patch:
- Directivity: 5–8 dBi (depends on substrate)
- Efficiency: 70–95% depending on losses
- Beamwidth: 60–90° (moderately directive)

### Polarization

- **Linear:** Standard rectangular patch
- **Circular:** Square patch with truncated corners, or dual-feed with 90° phase shift
- **Dual linear:** Two orthogonal feeds

### Substrate selection

| Property | Low ε_r | High ε_r |
|----------|---------|----------|
| Size | Larger | Smaller |
| Bandwidth | Wider | Narrower |
| Efficiency | Higher | Lower |
| Typical use | High performance | Compact devices |

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: GPS L1 patch**

GPS L1: 1575.42 MHz, $\lambda_0 = 19$ cm.

On Rogers RO4003 (ε_r = 3.55, h = 1.52 mm):

Patch length: $L \approx 0.5 \times 19 / \sqrt{3.0} \approx 5.5$ cm (after fringing correction).

For RHCP: square patch with truncated opposite corners.

**Example: 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi patch**

$\lambda_0 = 12.5$ cm at 2.4 GHz.

On FR4 (ε_r = 4.4, h = 1.6 mm):
- Patch length: ~30 mm
- Patch width: ~38 mm
- Inset feed: ~10 mm from edge for 50 Ω

Gain: ~6 dBi, beamwidth ~80°.

**Example: Patch array for 5G**

28 GHz mmWave, $\lambda_0 = 10.7$ mm.

4×4 patch array on low-loss substrate:
- Element size: ~2.5 mm × 3 mm
- Element spacing: ~5 mm (0.5λ)
- Array gain: 6 (element) + 12 (array factor) = ~18 dBi

**Example: Circular polarization**

For GPS/satellite reception, use truncated-corner square patch:
- Truncation ratio: ~0.14 of corner diagonal
- Produces RHCP or LHCP depending on which corners are cut
- Axial ratio < 3 dB over 1–2% bandwidth

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Substrate losses.** FR4 has high loss at GHz frequencies. Use low-loss substrates (Rogers, Taconic) for high-efficiency applications.

2. **Narrow bandwidth.** Standard patches are 1–3% bandwidth. Multi-band or wideband applications need special techniques.

3. **Surface wave losses.** Thick, high-ε substrates launch surface waves that reduce efficiency and cause coupling.

4. **Manufacturing tolerance.** Patch dimensions are critical. Etching tolerances affect resonant frequency — allow for tuning.

5. **Feeding complexity.** Edge feed is high impedance; inset or probe feed requires calculation. Aperture-coupled feeds add layers.

6. **Ground plane size.** Finite ground affects pattern. Minimum ~λ/2 beyond patch edges.

7. **Dielectric variation.** ε_r varies with temperature and batch. Account for ±5% variation in design.

</section>
