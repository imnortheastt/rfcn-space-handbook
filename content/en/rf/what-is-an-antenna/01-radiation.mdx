<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why radiation matters

Every wireless link exists because charges accelerate. When electrons oscillate in a transmitting antenna, they shed energy as electromagnetic waves. Understanding radiation connects Maxwell's equations to practical RF engineering.

This lesson answers: why does current in a wire produce waves that travel through space?

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Static charges create electric fields. Moving charges (currents) create magnetic fields. But neither radiates — the fields stay attached to the source.

**Accelerating charges radiate.** When a charge speeds up or slows down, it "shakes off" some of its field. That detached field propagates outward as an electromagnetic wave.

An antenna is a structure designed to make charges accelerate efficiently at the desired frequency.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### The radiation condition

From Maxwell's equations, time-varying currents produce time-varying fields:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}$$

Radiation occurs when these time-varying fields propagate away from the source.

### Accelerating charge model

For a point charge with acceleration $\mathbf{a}$, the radiated electric field at distance $r$ (far field):

$$\mathbf{E} = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 c^2} \frac{\mathbf{a} \times \hat{r} \times \hat{r}}{r}$$

Key insights:
- Field strength ∝ acceleration
- Field strength ∝ 1/r (not 1/r²)
- Field is perpendicular to the acceleration direction
- Field is perpendicular to the propagation direction

### Larmor formula

Power radiated by an accelerating charge:

$$P = \frac{q^2 a^2}{6\pi\epsilon_0 c^3}$$

For sinusoidal acceleration at frequency $f$, power ∝ $f^4$. Higher frequencies radiate more efficiently.

### Hertzian dipole

The simplest radiator: an infinitesimal current element $I \cdot dl$.

Far-field electric field:

$$E_\theta = \frac{jk I dl \sin\theta}{4\pi r} \eta_0 e^{-jkr}$$

where:
- $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ (wave number)
- $\eta_0 = 377\ \Omega$ (free-space impedance)
- $\theta$ = angle from the dipole axis

### Radiation resistance

The antenna appears as a resistance to the source because it "consumes" power by radiating:

$$R_{rad} = \frac{2P_{rad}}{|I|^2}$$

For a Hertzian dipole:
$$R_{rad} = 80\pi^2 \left(\frac{dl}{\lambda}\right)^2$$

Very short antennas have very low radiation resistance → hard to match.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Why does a DC wire not radiate?**

DC current = constant velocity = zero acceleration. No acceleration → no radiation.

AC current = oscillating velocity = nonzero acceleration → radiation.

**Example: Frequency scaling**

A 1 GHz signal vs. a 10 GHz signal with the same current amplitude.

Power radiated ∝ $f^4$ for a given current element.

The 10 GHz signal radiates $10^4 = 10,000$ times more power per unit length.

This is why short antennas work better at high frequencies.

**Example: Radiation resistance of short dipole**

Dipole length: 0.1λ.

$R_{rad} = 80\pi^2 (0.1)^2 = 7.9\ \Omega$.

Very low — requires matching network, and losses dominate.

At λ/2: $R_{rad} \approx 73\ \Omega$ — much easier to match.

**Example: Why bent wires radiate**

A straight wire with uniform current has canceling contributions from opposite ends.

Bending the wire (like a loop) or using standing waves (like a dipole) creates net acceleration in a consistent direction → radiation.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing near and far fields.** Near-field components don't radiate — they store reactive energy. Only the far-field component carries power away.

2. **Thinking any current radiates.** A transmission line with equal and opposite currents has minimal radiation — the fields cancel. Differential mode currents in cables are designed not to radiate.

3. **Ignoring frequency dependence.** Radiation efficiency scales strongly with frequency. A 1 cm wire is a poor antenna at 100 MHz but effective at 10 GHz.

4. **Assuming radiation = power.** Radiation resistance describes power leaving as waves. Ohmic resistance describes power lost as heat. Both consume power from the source.

5. **Forgetting direction.** Radiation has a pattern. A dipole doesn't radiate along its axis.

</section>
