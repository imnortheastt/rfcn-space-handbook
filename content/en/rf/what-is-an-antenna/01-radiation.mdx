## Why radiation matters

Every wireless link exists because charges accelerate. When electrons oscillate in a transmitting antenna, they shed energy as electromagnetic waves — energy that travels across a room, a city, or 36,000 km to a geostationary satellite, all at the speed of light.

For a cloud engineer building distributed systems, antennas may feel like hardware magic. But the physics is surprisingly clean: one equation (the Larmor formula) connects the motion of a single charge to radio waves spanning continents. Once you see that connection, the entire RF stack makes sense from first principles.

This lesson answers: why does current in a wire produce waves that travel through space, and what determines how much power is radiated?

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Start with three facts from classical electromagnetism:

1. **Static charges** create electric fields that reach out to infinity — but these fields are "attached" to the charge. They follow it wherever it goes. No radiation.

2. **Charges moving at constant velocity** (a steady DC current) create both electric and magnetic fields. These fields also stay attached. Still no radiation.

3. **Accelerating charges** shed some of their field. The "shaken-off" field separates from the source and propagates outward independently. That propagating disturbance is electromagnetic radiation.

The key mental picture: imagine a charge sitting still, surrounded by a symmetric electric field. When you suddenly accelerate the charge, the news of that acceleration can only travel outward at the speed of light. Far away, the field hasn't "heard" about the movement yet — so you get a kink in the field lines where the old position meets the new position. That kink propagates outward forever. That is a radio wave.

An antenna is a structure designed to make charges accelerate in a controlled, efficient pattern at the desired frequency.

```
Static charge:          Moving (constant velocity):    Accelerating:
    E ──────              E ─────── v →                E ────kink───→
   / \                    │  field travels with         kink detaches,
  /   \                   │  the charge                 propagates at c
 │  q  │                  │
  \   /                   │
   \ /
  (attached field,        (still attached,             (radiation escapes!)
   no radiation)           no radiation)
```

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Maxwell's equations and time-varying fields

The foundation is Maxwell's two curl equations:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}$$

The first says a time-varying magnetic field creates an electric field. The second says a time-varying electric field (or a current) creates a magnetic field. Together they form a self-sustaining loop: each field drives the other. Once that loop detaches from its source, it propagates at $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}$.

### The Larmor formula — power from a single charge

For a non-relativistic accelerating point charge $q$ with acceleration $\mathbf{a}$, total radiated power is:

$$P_{rad} = \frac{q^2 a^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3}$$

This is the Larmor formula. Three immediate insights:

- Power scales as $a^2$: double the acceleration, quadruple the radiated power.
- Power scales as $q^2$: heavier charged particles (protons vs. electrons) radiate less for the same acceleration — this is why particle accelerators can be so lossy with electrons.
- Power is always positive: a decelerating charge also radiates (braking radiation, or *Bremsstrahlung*).

For sinusoidal motion $a(t) = a_0 \sin(2\pi f t)$, the time-averaged radiated power is $P = q^2 a_0^2 / 12\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3$. Since peak acceleration at frequency $f$ scales as $a_0 \propto f^2 x_0$ (where $x_0$ is displacement amplitude), $P \propto f^4$ for fixed displacement — higher frequencies radiate far more efficiently.

### Far-field electric field of a point charge

In the far field (distance $r \gg \lambda$), the radiated electric field of an accelerating charge is:

$$\mathbf{E}_{rad} = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 c^2} \frac{[\mathbf{a}_\perp]_{ret}}{r}$$

where $[\mathbf{a}_\perp]_{ret}$ is the component of acceleration perpendicular to the observation direction, evaluated at the retarded time. Key points:

- Field falls as $1/r$ (not $1/r^2$): power density falls as $1/r^2$, which is why the far field carries energy to infinity.
- Field is perpendicular to both the line of sight and the acceleration direction.
- The $1/r$ field is the only one that radiates; near-field components fall faster and don't carry net power away.

### The Hertzian dipole — simplest antenna model

The Hertzian dipole is an infinitesimal current element of length $dl$ carrying current $I$. It is the building block from which all antenna analysis begins. Its far-field electric field:

$$E_\theta = j\eta_0 \frac{k I\, dl}{4\pi r} \sin\theta \cdot e^{-jkr}$$

where $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wavenumber, $\eta_0 = 377\ \Omega$ is the free-space wave impedance, and $\theta$ is measured from the dipole axis. The $\sin\theta$ factor produces the characteristic donut pattern — maximum broadside, zero off the ends.

### Radiation resistance — the antenna as a resistor

From the source's perspective, a radiating antenna "consumes" power by launching waves into space. We model this as a resistance $R_{rad}$:

$$R_{rad} = \frac{2 P_{rad}}{|I_0|^2}$$

For a Hertzian dipole:

$$R_{rad} = 80\pi^2 \left(\frac{dl}{\lambda}\right)^2$$

**Worked example:** A short dipole with $dl = 0.1\lambda$:

$$R_{rad} = 80\pi^2 \times (0.1)^2 = 80 \times 9.87 \times 0.01 = 7.9\ \Omega$$

This is tiny compared to the 73 Ω of a half-wave dipole. The wire's ohmic resistance (typically 1–5 Ω) becomes a significant fraction, so efficiency is low. This is the fundamental reason short antennas are inefficient: not much of the input power actually escapes as waves.

### From Hertzian dipole to real antennas

Real antennas integrate the Hertzian dipole result over their current distribution. For a half-wave dipole, the sinusoidal current distribution $I(z) = I_0 \cos(kz)$ integrates to give:

$$R_{rad} \approx 73\ \Omega, \quad D = 1.64\ (2.15\ \text{dBi})$$

The factor 9× increase in radiation resistance over the $0.1\lambda$ case dramatically improves efficiency — most power now goes into radiation rather than heat.

## Apply

**Why a DC wire doesn't radiate**

DC current = constant charge velocity = zero acceleration. The Larmor formula gives $P = q^2 \cdot 0^2 / (\ldots) = 0$. No radiation. This is why power transmission lines don't broadcast radio signals (their radiation is negligible at 50/60 Hz for practical wire dimensions).

**Frequency scaling example**

Compare a 1 GHz vs. 10 GHz current element with identical current amplitude and length.

Since $P_{rad} \propto f^4$ for a short element (the Larmor term with sinusoidal acceleration):

$$\frac{P_{10\text{GHz}}}{P_{1\text{GHz}}} = \left(\frac{10}{1}\right)^4 = 10{,}000$$

A 10 GHz element radiates 10,000 times more power per unit current than a 1 GHz element of the same length. This is why millimeter-wave antennas can be tiny — at 28 GHz, even a 1 mm stub radiates efficiently.

**Radiation resistance of a 1 cm wire at 2.4 GHz**

$\lambda = 125$ mm at 2.4 GHz. Wire length $dl = 10$ mm $= 0.08\lambda$.

$$R_{rad} = 80\pi^2 \times (0.08)^2 = 5.1\ \Omega$$

If the wire has ohmic resistance $R_{loss} = 2\ \Omega$:

$$\eta = \frac{R_{rad}}{R_{rad} + R_{loss}} = \frac{5.1}{7.1} = 72\%$$

More than a quarter of the input power is wasted as heat. Lengthening to $\lambda/2$ (62.5 mm) gives $R_{rad} \approx 73\ \Omega$ and efficiency climbs above 97%.

**Why bent wires radiate but straight transmission lines don't**

A coaxial cable or twin-lead line carries equal and opposite currents in close proximity. Their radiation fields cancel almost perfectly (the net dipole moment is zero). Radiation leaks out only from unbalanced currents or bends. An antenna deliberately breaks this cancellation — it exposes one conductor while using the other as a ground return, or bends the current into a geometry where contributions add rather than cancel.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing near-field and far-field components.** The total field near an antenna contains terms falling as $1/r^3$, $1/r^2$, and $1/r$. Only the $1/r$ term carries power away to infinity. The $1/r^3$ and $1/r^2$ terms store reactive energy that oscillates in and out of the antenna. Applying far-field formulas in the near field gives wildly wrong answers.

2. **Thinking any current radiates.** A balanced transmission line with equal and opposite currents radiates essentially nothing — the fields cancel. EMC engineers spend enormous effort ensuring cables don't radiate accidentally. An antenna is the deliberate exception, not the rule.

3. **Ignoring frequency dependence.** A 1 cm piece of wire is a terrible antenna at 100 MHz ($\lambda = 3$ m, $dl/\lambda = 0.003$) but a reasonable radiator at 15 GHz ($\lambda = 20$ mm, $dl/\lambda = 0.5$). Same wire, completely different behavior.

4. **Equating radiation resistance with input impedance.** Input impedance includes radiation resistance, loss resistance, and reactance. At resonance, reactance goes to zero, but you still need to match $R_{rad} + R_{loss}$ to the feedline.

5. **Forgetting that radiation has direction.** A dipole does not radiate along its axis. Placing the null of one antenna in the direction of another is a useful interference-rejection technique — but it requires knowing the pattern, not just the gain number.
