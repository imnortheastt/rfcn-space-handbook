## Why a mental model?

Maxwell's equations describe everything about an antenna — and are usually intractable. Engineers need simpler models that capture the essential behavior without solving PDEs.

This lesson presents the mental models RF engineers actually use: the antenna as a transducer, as a two-port network, and as a spatial filter.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

An antenna is a **transducer** between guided waves (transmission lines) and radiated waves (free space). It converts between two domains:

| Domain | Quantity | Impedance |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Guided | Voltage, current | Z₀ (50Ω) |
| Radiated | E-field, H-field | η₀ (377Ω) |

The antenna matches these domains and shapes the radiation pattern.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Equivalent circuit model

At the feed point, the antenna looks like a lumped impedance:

$$Z_A = R_A + jX_A = (R_{rad} + R_{loss}) + jX_A$$

- $R_{rad}$ = radiation resistance (power radiated)
- $R_{loss}$ = loss resistance (power dissipated)
- $X_A$ = reactance (energy storage)

At resonance, $X_A = 0$ and $Z_A$ is purely real.

### Efficiency

$$\eta = \frac{R_{rad}}{R_{rad} + R_{loss}}$$

A good antenna: $\eta > 90\%$. Most power goes to radiation, not heat.

### Gain and directivity

**Directivity:** how much power is concentrated in the peak direction vs. isotropic.

$$D = \frac{4\pi}{\Omega_A}$$

where $\Omega_A$ is the beam solid angle.

**Gain:** directivity times efficiency.

$$G = \eta D$$

### Pattern as spatial filter

The antenna is a **spatial filter**: it amplifies signals from some directions and rejects others.

Pattern function $F(\theta, \phi)$: normalized field strength vs. angle.

Power pattern: $|F(\theta, \phi)|^2$.

### Effective aperture

How much power the antenna captures from an incident plane wave:

$$P_{received} = S \cdot A_e$$

where $S$ = power density (W/m²), $A_e$ = effective aperture (m²).

Related to gain: $A_e = G \lambda^2 / 4\pi$.

### Two-port network model

Antenna system as S-parameters:
- $S_{11}$ = reflection at antenna port (mismatch)
- Port 2 = "space port" (radiation)

Matching means $|S_{11}| \rightarrow 0$ at design frequency.

### Bandwidth

Range where $|S_{11}| < -10$ dB (or VSWR < 2):

$$BW = \frac{f_H - f_L}{f_0}$$

Bandwidth trades off with size and efficiency for small antennas.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Dipole analysis**

Half-wave dipole at resonance:
- $Z_A \approx 73 + j0\ \Omega$
- $R_{rad} \approx 73\ \Omega$, $R_{loss} \approx 0$ (if copper, air)
- Efficiency ≈ 100%
- Directivity = 1.64 (2.15 dBi)

Match to 50Ω line: slight mismatch ($S_{11} \approx -14$ dB).

**Example: Patch antenna budget**

Patch with $G = 6$ dBi at 2.4 GHz.

Effective aperture: $A_e = 4 \times 0.125^2 / 4\pi = 0.005$ m² ≈ 50 cm².

Incident power density: 1 μW/m².

Received power: $10^{-6} \times 0.005 = 5$ nW = −53 dBm.

**Example: Small antenna limitation**

Antenna much smaller than λ has:
- Low $R_{rad}$ (hard to radiate efficiently)
- High $X_A$ (reactive, hard to match)
- Narrow bandwidth

Fundamental limit: $Q \propto 1/(ka)^3$ where $a$ = antenna radius.

**Example: MIMO spatial filtering**

Each antenna in a MIMO array has a pattern. The array's combined pattern depends on element spacing and weighting. The antenna isn't just a transducer — it's a spatial multiplexer.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Ignoring loss.** Gain ≠ directivity for lossy antennas. Small antennas especially can have low efficiency.

2. **Frequency sensitivity.** The equivalent circuit model is frequency-dependent. $Z_A$ changes dramatically off resonance.

3. **Near-field complexity.** The simple impedance model applies at the feed. Near-field interactions (with ground, enclosure) affect the full system.

4. **Pattern idealization.** Real patterns have nulls, sidelobes, and cross-polarization. The main-beam model is only an approximation.

5. **Confusing bandwidth definitions.** Impedance bandwidth, pattern bandwidth, and gain bandwidth can differ. Specify which.

6. **Over-trusting simulation.** EM simulators give accurate results for defined geometry. Real antennas have manufacturing tolerances, mounting effects, and cable interactions.
