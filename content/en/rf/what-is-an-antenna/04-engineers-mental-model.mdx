## Why a mental model?

Maxwell's equations describe everything about an antenna. They are also largely intractable for engineering decisions. A working RF engineer does not solve PDEs every time they select or design an antenna — they use a set of abstractions that capture essential behavior at different levels of detail.

This lesson presents the three mental models RF engineers actually use:

1. **The transducer model:** the antenna converts between guided waves and free-space waves.
2. **The equivalent circuit model:** the antenna is a lumped impedance at its feed point.
3. **The spatial filter model:** the antenna selects signals from certain directions.

A cloud/backend engineer will recognize these immediately: they are the same kind of domain decomposition used to reason about network stacks — each layer has a simple interface hiding complex internal mechanics.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

An antenna sits at the boundary between two worlds:

| Domain | Signal form | Characteristic impedance |
|--------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Guided (transmission line, PCB trace) | Voltage, current | $Z_0 = 50\ \Omega$ |
| Free space (radio waves) | E-field, H-field | $\eta_0 = 377\ \Omega$ |

The impedance mismatch between these worlds (50 Ω vs. 377 Ω) is the central challenge of antenna engineering. The antenna's job is to:

1. **Match** the guided-wave impedance to free-space impedance — get power out of the cable and into the air.
2. **Shape** the spatial distribution of radiated power — send it where you want it.

Everything else (resonance, bandwidth, gain, polarization) is a consequence of how well the antenna accomplishes these two tasks.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Model 1: The equivalent circuit

At the antenna feed point, the antenna looks like a series circuit:

$$Z_A = R_A + jX_A = (R_{rad} + R_{loss}) + jX_A$$

- $R_{rad}$: radiation resistance — power that leaves as electromagnetic waves
- $R_{loss}$: loss resistance — power dissipated as heat in the conductor and dielectric
- $X_A$: reactance — energy stored in the near-field reactive region

The equivalent circuit is frequency-dependent: $Z_A(f)$ changes dramatically with frequency. This is what the VNA (Vector Network Analyzer) measures as $S_{11}$.

**Radiation efficiency** is the fraction of accepted power that actually radiates:

$$\eta_{rad} = \frac{R_{rad}}{R_{rad} + R_{loss}}$$

A dipole in copper wire at 2.4 GHz might have $R_{rad} = 73\ \Omega$, $R_{loss} = 0.5\ \Omega$: efficiency = 73/73.5 = 99.3%. A chip antenna in a phone at 2.4 GHz might have $R_{rad} = 10\ \Omega$, $R_{loss} = 30\ \Omega$: efficiency = 10/40 = 25%.

**Resonance** is where $X_A = 0$. At resonance, $Z_A$ is purely real and usually close to $R_{rad} + R_{loss}$. A half-wave dipole resonates naturally near 73 Ω — close enough to 50 Ω for direct connection.

### Model 2: S11, VSWR, and bandwidth

From the feedline's perspective, the antenna is a load with reflection coefficient:

$$\Gamma = \frac{Z_A - Z_0}{Z_A + Z_0}$$

The $S_{11}$ parameter (return loss) is just $\Gamma$ in dB:

$$S_{11} = 20\log_{10}|\Gamma|\ \text{dB}$$

The Voltage Standing Wave Ratio (VSWR — phản xạ điện áp, pronounced "V-S-W-R" or "vizwer") relates to $|\Gamma|$:

$$\text{VSWR} = \frac{1 + |\Gamma|}{1 - |\Gamma|}$$

The practical thresholds:

| $|S_{11}|$ | VSWR | Reflected power | Meaning |
|------------|------|-----------------|---------|
| 0 dB | ∞ | 100% | Total reflection (open or short) |
| −6 dB | 3.0:1 | 25% | Poor match |
| −10 dB | 1.93:1 | 10% | Acceptable (industry minimum) |
| −14 dB | 1.50:1 | 4% | Good match |
| −20 dB | 1.22:1 | 1% | Excellent |
| −∞ | 1.00:1 | 0% | Perfect (theoretical only) |

**Impedance bandwidth** is the frequency range over which $|S_{11}| < -10$ dB (VSWR < 2:1). A simple half-wave dipole achieves about 10% bandwidth. A fat dipole (bow-tie) achieves 50%+. A patch antenna typically achieves 2–5% on standard FR-4 substrate.

**Worked example — S11 and mismatch loss:**

A GPS antenna has $|S_{11}| = -12$ dB at 1575 MHz.

Reflected power fraction: $|\Gamma|^2 = 10^{-12/10} = 0.063 = 6.3\%$.

Mismatch loss: $10\log_{10}(1 - 0.063) = -0.28\ \text{dB}$.

Less than 0.3 dB lost to mismatch — acceptable for most systems.

### Model 3: Gain, directivity, and effective aperture

**Directivity** $D$ is a pure shape property of the radiation pattern:

$$D(\theta,\phi) = \frac{4\pi U(\theta,\phi)}{P_{rad}}$$

where $U(\theta,\phi)$ is radiation intensity (W/sr) and $P_{rad}$ is total radiated power. Peak directivity $D_0$ is what appears in link budget calculations.

**Gain** folds in radiation efficiency:

$$G = \eta_{rad} \cdot D$$

A perfectly directive antenna with 50% efficiency has gain 3 dB below its directivity.

**Effective aperture** — how much incident power the antenna captures when receiving:

$$P_{rx} = S \cdot A_e, \quad A_e = \frac{G\lambda^2}{4\pi}$$

A 10 dBi antenna at 2.4 GHz: $A_e = 10 \times (0.125)^2 / (4\pi) = 0.012\ \text{m}^2 = 124\ \text{cm}^2$. The antenna captures 124 cm² of the incident wavefront regardless of its physical size.

### Model 4: The spatial filter

An antenna is a **spatial filter**: it amplifies signals from some directions and attenuates signals from others. This is the model that connects antenna design to system-level performance:

- A base-station sector antenna with a 65° horizontal beam rejects interference from outside that beam by 20+ dB.
- A GPS antenna with a ground-plane choke ring attenuates multipath arriving from below the horizon.
- A massive MIMO array with 64 elements focuses a narrow 2° beam on a specific UE, rejecting all other users.

The normalized radiation pattern $F(\theta,\phi)$ is the transfer function of this spatial filter. The power pattern $|F(\theta,\phi)|^2$ gives the gain in each direction relative to peak gain.

### The Chu-Wheeler limit — why small antennas have narrow bandwidth

A fundamental result by Chu (1948): for an antenna that fits inside a sphere of radius $a$, the minimum achievable Q (and therefore maximum achievable bandwidth for a fixed efficiency) is:

$$Q_{min} \approx \frac{1}{(ka)^3} + \frac{1}{ka}, \quad k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$$

For electrically small antennas ($ka \ll 1$), $Q_{min} \propto 1/(ka)^3$, which grows rapidly as the antenna shrinks. High Q means narrow bandwidth. This is physics, not engineering failure — you cannot simultaneously make an antenna small, efficient, and wideband.

Engineers deal with this as a three-way trade-off: **size, efficiency, bandwidth — pick any two** (the third degrades).

## Apply

**Dipole system analysis from first principles**

Half-wave dipole at 2.4 GHz, copper wire 1 mm diameter, fed by 50 Ω coax.

- $R_{rad} \approx 73\ \Omega$, $R_{loss} \approx 0.4\ \Omega$ (skin-effect loss)
- Efficiency: $73/(73.4) = 99.4\%$
- $Z_A \approx 73 + j42\ \Omega$ (slightly above resonance; physical dipole is 5% shorter)
- Shorten to resonance: $Z_A \approx 70 + j0\ \Omega$
- $\Gamma = (70 - 50)/(70 + 50) = 20/120 = 0.167$; $S_{11} = 20\log(0.167) = -15.5\ \text{dB}$; VSWR = 1.4:1
- Gain: $G = 0.994 \times 1.64 = 1.63 = 2.12\ \text{dBi}$
- Effective aperture at 2.4 GHz: $A_e = 1.63 \times 0.0156 / (4\pi) = 2.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m}^2 = 20\ \text{cm}^2$

**Chip antenna in a phone — where the loss goes**

A typical 2.4 GHz chip antenna has:
- $G = 0\ \text{dBi}$ in free space (spec sheet value)
- Radiation efficiency: 20–40% when installed (body effects, ground plane interactions)
- Installed gain: −4 to −7 dBi

This 4–7 dB degradation from spec explains why phones in dense urban canyons (high path loss) struggle at cell edges. The antenna is not malfunctioning — it is operating as physics dictates.

**Matching network design**

Patch antenna at 5 GHz with $Z_A = 150 + j30\ \Omega$. Need to match to 50 Ω.

Using L-network: shunt element $B = -\text{Im}(Y_A)/G_A$... (full derivation belongs in a matching networks lesson). The key insight is that matching is possible but trades bandwidth: a perfect match at one frequency degrades away from that frequency. Wideband systems often accept slight mismatch across the band rather than narrow-band perfect match.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing gain with directivity.** Gain includes radiation efficiency; directivity does not. A highly directive small antenna with 10% radiation efficiency has gain 10 dB below its directivity. Never use directivity in a link budget.

2. **Treating S11 as the only figure of merit.** $S_{11} = -30\ \text{dB}$ is meaningless if the antenna's radiation efficiency is 5%. The power goes somewhere — into heat, into cable shield currents, into the user's hand. Efficiency and gain matter more than the match.

3. **Ignoring installation effects.** Antenna specs are measured in free space. A 5 dBi antenna mounted on a metal enclosure may have 3 dBi installed gain because ground-plane edge diffraction distorts the pattern. Always validate in the final mounting environment.

4. **Bandwidth definitions mismatch.** Impedance bandwidth ($|S_{11}| < -10\ \text{dB}$), pattern bandwidth (gain variation ≤ 3 dB), and efficiency bandwidth (efficiency ≥ spec) can all differ. A wideband impedance match does not guarantee wideband gain.

5. **Linear scale confusion.** VSWR = 2:1 sounds bad but only reflects 11% of power (−10 dB return loss). VSWR = 3:1 reflects 25% (−6 dB). Engineers sometimes panic over VSWR 2.5:1 (−7 dB return loss, 18% reflected) when the system can tolerate up to −6 dB.

6. **Forgetting that $A_e \neq A_{physical}$.** A patch antenna with $G = 6\ \text{dBi}$ at 2.4 GHz has $A_e \approx 50\ \text{cm}^2$, but its physical area is about 30 cm² of copper. Effective aperture can exceed physical area for directive antennas. This is not magic — it comes from the coherent phase relationship between energy captured across the aperture.
