<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why distinguish field regions?

The electromagnetic field around an antenna behaves very differently depending on distance. Near the antenna, fields store energy and couple strongly. Far from the antenna, fields radiate and behave like plane waves.

Knowing which region you're in determines whether you're doing near-field coupling, antenna measurement, or far-field communication.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Close to an antenna, the fields are complicated — they depend on antenna geometry, have reactive components, and don't follow simple spreading laws.

Far enough away, the fields simplify into spherical waves that look locally like plane waves. The pattern is stable and predictable.

The boundary between these regions is where antenna measurements must happen and where the radiation pattern "forms."

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Three field regions

1. **Reactive near field:** $r < 0.62\sqrt{D^3/\lambda}$
2. **Radiating near field (Fresnel):** $0.62\sqrt{D^3/\lambda} < r < 2D^2/\lambda$
3. **Far field (Fraunhofer):** $r > 2D^2/\lambda$

where $D$ = largest antenna dimension.

### Reactive near field

- Fields ∝ 1/r², 1/r³ (decay faster than radiation)
- Strong energy storage (reactive power)
- Fields depend heavily on antenna geometry
- E and H not in phase
- Power oscillates between antenna and field

Extends to roughly $\lambda/(2\pi)$ for small antennas.

### Radiating near field (Fresnel)

- Radiation fields dominate, but pattern varies with distance
- Phase differences across antenna aperture are significant
- Used in focused antennas, RFID, wireless charging

### Far field (Fraunhofer)

The region where:
- Fields ∝ 1/r (inverse distance)
- Angular pattern is independent of distance
- E and H are in phase and perpendicular
- Locally looks like a plane wave
- Power density ∝ 1/r²

### Far-field boundary

Standard criterion: $r > 2D^2/\lambda$

This ensures phase error across aperture < π/8.

| Antenna | Frequency | D | Far-field boundary |
|---------|-----------|---|-------------------|
| Dipole (λ/2) | 1 GHz | 15 cm | 0.15 m |
| Dish (1 m) | 10 GHz | 1 m | 67 m |
| Dish (3 m) | 30 GHz | 3 m | 1800 m |

### Field impedance

Far field: $Z = E/H = \eta_0 = 377\ \Omega$.

Near field: $Z$ depends on antenna type:
- Electric antenna (dipole): high impedance near field
- Magnetic antenna (loop): low impedance near field

### Additional criteria

For accurate measurements, all three must hold:
- $r > 2D^2/\lambda$ (phase criterion)
- $r > D$ (geometry criterion)
- $r > \lambda$ (wave criterion)

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Cell phone near head**

Phone antenna ≈ 5 cm, frequency = 2 GHz (λ = 15 cm).

Far-field boundary: $2 \times 0.05^2 / 0.15 = 0.03$ m = 3 cm.

Your head at 1 cm is in the reactive near field. SAR calculations use near-field analysis, not far-field power density.

**Example: Antenna test range**

Testing a 1 m dish at 30 GHz (λ = 1 cm).

Far-field boundary: $2 \times 1^2 / 0.01 = 200$ m.

Need 200 m range for far-field measurements, or use compact range/near-field probe.

**Example: RFID coupling**

NFC at 13.56 MHz (λ = 22 m).

Antenna ~5 cm, operating at ~5 cm.

$r << \lambda$ → purely reactive near field. This is inductive coupling, not radiation.

**Example: Radar antenna**

4 m dish at 10 GHz (λ = 3 cm).

Far-field boundary: $2 \times 16 / 0.03 = 1067$ m ≈ 1 km.

Target at 10 km is well in far field. At 500 m, pattern distortion may occur.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Using far-field formulas in near field.** Friis equation and gain calculations assume far field. Using them near the antenna gives wrong answers.

2. **Confusing near-field coupling with radiation.** RFID and wireless charging work via near-field coupling (reactive), not radiation. "Range" behaves very differently.

3. **Underestimating far-field distance.** Large antennas at high frequencies require surprisingly long test ranges. A 2 m dish at 30 GHz needs 800 m.

4. **Ignoring λ term for small antennas.** Very small antennas (D << λ) have far field starting at ~λ, not 2D²/λ. Use r > λ.

5. **Phase center errors.** In the radiating near field, the apparent phase center shifts with angle. Interferometry and ranging suffer.

6. **SAR vs. power density.** Near field: use SAR (W/kg) for safety. Far field: use power density (W/m²). Wrong metric → wrong assessment.

</section>
