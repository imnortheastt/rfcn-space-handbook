## Why distinguish field regions?

When you hold a phone near your head, the antenna is in your near field. When a base station contacts the same phone 500 m away, the phone is in the base station's far field. These two regimes are governed by completely different physics.

Near the antenna, fields store reactive energy, couple capacitively and inductively, and can induce significant currents in nearby conductors. Far from the antenna, fields behave like plane waves: they carry energy, obey simple inverse-square spreading, and can be characterized by a single radiation pattern independent of distance.

Confusing the two regions is one of the most common errors in RF system design — from wrong SAR calculations to invalid gain measurements. This lesson gives you the tools to identify which region you're in and what that means for your analysis.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Think of a loudspeaker playing a note. Right next to the speaker cone, the air pressure is complex — it depends on the exact shape of the cone, the room walls, and the distance. Step back to the other side of a concert hall, and the sound simplifies into a spherical wavefront; you can characterize it by direction and amplitude alone.

Antenna fields work the same way. Close to the antenna:
- Fields have strong $1/r^3$ and $1/r^2$ components that dominate over the $1/r$ radiation term.
- The E and H fields are out of phase (reactive power sloshes back and forth).
- The apparent impedance $Z = E/H$ differs wildly from 377 Ω and depends on the antenna type.

Far from the antenna:
- Only the $1/r$ radiation component survives (the others have decayed away).
- E and H are in phase and perpendicular — locally, it looks like a plane wave.
- $Z = E/H = \eta_0 = 377\ \Omega$ everywhere.
- The radiation pattern (gain vs. angle) is stable and distance-independent.

The transition between these two regimes is not a sharp boundary but a gradual shift. Engineers have defined practical criteria to mark the transition.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### The three field regions

For an antenna with largest dimension $D$:

**1. Reactive near field:** $r < 0.62\sqrt{D^3/\lambda}$

The reactive components of the field dominate. This region is important for:
- SAR (Specific Absorption Rate) calculations — energy absorbed by body tissue
- Near-field wireless charging and NFC coupling
- Coupling between closely spaced antenna elements
- EMC analysis of PCB traces

**2. Radiating near field (Fresnel region):** $0.62\sqrt{D^3/\lambda} \leq r < 2D^2/\lambda$

Radiation fields dominate, but the phase relationship between contributions from different parts of the antenna aperture varies with distance. The radiation pattern is not yet stable — it changes shape as you move away. This region matters for:
- Focused-beam systems (wireless power, some radar modes)
- RFID at intermediate ranges
- Near-field scanning for antenna diagnostics

**3. Far field (Fraunhofer region):** $r \geq 2D^2/\lambda$

The radiation pattern is stable: the angular distribution of power no longer changes with distance. All antenna specifications — gain, directivity, beamwidth, sidelobe level — are defined here. Communication systems, radar, and broadcasting all operate in this region.

### The far-field boundary criterion

The standard $r = 2D^2/\lambda$ comes from requiring that the maximum phase error across the antenna aperture be less than $\pi/8$ radians (22.5°). A larger phase error distorts the pattern enough to make far-field measurement invalid.

For a uniform aperture of width $D$, the path-length difference between the center and edge, as seen from distance $r$, is approximately $D^2/(8r)$ in wavelengths. Setting this less than $\lambda/16$ gives $r > 2D^2/\lambda$.

### Worked examples

**Cell phone near a head ($f = 2$ GHz, $\lambda = 150$ mm):**

Phone antenna $D \approx 50$ mm.

Far-field boundary: $2D^2/\lambda = 2 \times 0.05^2 / 0.15 = 0.033$ m $= 3.3$ cm.

Your head at 1 cm from the antenna is firmly in the reactive near field. SAR analysis must use near-field methods — Friis equation does not apply.

**Cellular sector antenna ($f = 1.8$ GHz, $D = 0.8$ m):**

Far-field boundary: $2 \times 0.64 / 0.167 = 7.7$ m.

A UE 100 m away is well in the far field. Pattern measurements at 10 m are adequate.

**Large dish for satellite ($f = 30$ GHz, $D = 3$ m, $\lambda = 10$ mm):**

Far-field boundary: $2 \times 9 / 0.01 = 1800$ m.

A 1 km test range is **not** far-field for this antenna. You either need a 2 km range, a compact antenna range (using a curved mirror to simulate far-field conditions), or near-field scanning with mathematical transformation.

**RFID reader ($f = 13.56$ MHz, $\lambda = 22$ m):**

Antenna $D = 20$ cm $= 0.2$ m. Far-field boundary: $2 \times 0.04 / 22 = 0.0036$ m $= 3.6$ mm.

But the operating range is 5–10 cm, and $r < \lambda$ (0.22 m). Apply the additional criterion $r > \lambda$: the reader at 5 cm is still in near field. This is intentional — NFC/RFID coupling relies on the reactive near field, not radiation.

### Field impedance and antenna type

In the near field, $Z = E/H$ depends on the antenna:

- **Electric (high-impedance) antennas** (dipoles, monopoles): the near field is dominated by the electric field. $Z \gg 377\ \Omega$ close to the antenna. Useful for measuring $E$-field close to RF sources.
- **Magnetic (low-impedance) antennas** (loops, ferrite rods): the near field is dominated by the magnetic field. $Z \ll 377\ \Omega$ close to the antenna. Better at measuring $H$-field near power electronics or motors.

In the far field, both types converge to $Z = \eta_0 = 377\ \Omega$.

### Multiple criteria for far field

For a valid far-field measurement, all three conditions must hold:
- $r > 2D^2/\lambda$ (phase criterion — most demanding for large apertures)
- $r > D$ (geometry criterion — stay outside the shadow region)
- $r > \lambda$ (wave criterion — ensure the field has organized into a wave)

For small antennas ($D \ll \lambda$), the wave criterion $r > \lambda$ dominates. A 1 cm dipole at 100 MHz ($\lambda = 3$ m) has far field starting at 3 m, not at the negligible $2D^2/\lambda = 0.7$ mm.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Checking whether a measurement is valid**

You need to characterize a 5G mmWave antenna module at 28 GHz. The module is 40 mm × 40 mm ($D = 57$ mm diagonal).

Far-field boundary: $\lambda = c/f = 10.7$ mm. $r_{FF} = 2 \times (0.057)^2 / 0.0107 = 0.61$ m.

With a 1 m measurement distance, you are in the far field. A 30 cm chamber would not be sufficient.

**Understanding 5G base-station coordination**

A massive MIMO panel at 3.5 GHz has $D = 500$ mm (64 elements in a 50 cm array). $\lambda = 86$ mm.

Far-field boundary: $2 \times 0.25 / 0.086 = 5.8$ m.

UEs connected at 50 m and beyond are firmly in the far field. Inter-site interference calculations use far-field gain patterns. In-building small cells at 5 m from the panel may be in the radiating near field — coordination algorithms that assume far-field patterns may be slightly off.

**Near-field SAR calculation**

A phone transmits at 23 dBm into a 5 cm antenna at 1.8 GHz ($\lambda = 167$ mm). Far-field boundary: $2 \times 0.05^2 / 0.167 = 30$ mm. Head at 15 mm is in the reactive near field.

The SAR limit (2 W/kg per 10 g of tissue in the EU) must be evaluated using full near-field EM simulation (FDTD or FEM), not a simple $1/r^2$ power density calculation. The reactive near field can concentrate energy in specific tissue volumes in ways that far-field spreading laws miss entirely.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Applying Friis in the near field.** The Friis transmission equation assumes far-field conditions: spherical wavefront, stable pattern, power density proportional to $1/r^2$. Using it when $r < 2D^2/\lambda$ gives optimistic received-power estimates.

2. **Confusing near-field coupling with radiation.** NFC and wireless charging work via reactive near-field coupling (essentially a transformer action). Efficiency falls as $1/r^6$ for small loops, not $1/r^2$. "Range" is limited to a few cm by this steep fall-off, not by transmitted power.

3. **Underestimating the far-field distance for large apertures.** A 2 m dish at 30 GHz ($\lambda = 10$ mm) needs $r_{FF} = 800$ m. Most engineers instinctively think "I'm far away" after 10 m, but 10 m is deep in the Fresnel zone for a large aperture at millimeter waves.

4. **Ignoring the $r > \lambda$ criterion for small antennas.** For very small antennas ($D \ll \lambda$), the formal $2D^2/\lambda$ formula gives a tiny distance, but the wave criterion $r > \lambda$ still applies. A patch antenna at 900 MHz ($\lambda = 333$ mm) needs $r > 333$ mm even though $D$ is only 30 mm.

5. **Assuming pattern stability in the radiating near field.** In the Fresnel region, the pattern shape depends on distance. Using a gain figure measured at one distance to predict power at a different distance (both in the Fresnel zone) introduces systematic errors.

6. **SAR vs. power density.** Near field: use SAR (W/kg) for safety assessment. Far field: use power density (W/m²) or field strength (V/m). Mixing them — especially applying ICNIRP far-field reference levels to a device held against the body — under-estimates exposure.
