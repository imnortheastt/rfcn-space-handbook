<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why massive MIMO uplink?

The uplink (user to base station) is where massive MIMO shines brightest. The base station has all the antennas, all the processing power, and — critically — can estimate channels from uplink pilots. Simple linear receivers (MRC, ZF) approach capacity as antenna count grows. No complex iterative detection needed.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

With M >> K, the channel matrix becomes "tall and skinny" — many more rows (antennas) than columns (users). This overdetermined system has a unique least-squares solution. Noise and interference average out across the antenna array. Simple linear processing suffices.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### System model

M antennas, K users, uplink transmission.

Received signal:
$$\mathbf{y} = \sqrt{p_u} \mathbf{H} \mathbf{s} + \mathbf{n}$$

- $\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{C}^M$ — received vector
- $\mathbf{H} \in \mathbb{C}^{M \times K}$ — channel matrix
- $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{C}^K$ — transmitted symbols ($E[|s_k|^2] = 1$)
- $p_u$ — uplink transmit power
- $\mathbf{n} \sim \mathcal{CN}(0, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$ — noise

### Maximum Ratio Combining (MRC)

$$\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{MRC} = \mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{y}$$

For user k:
$$\hat{s}_k = \mathbf{h}_k^H \mathbf{y} = \sqrt{p_u} \|\mathbf{h}_k\|^2 s_k + \sqrt{p_u} \sum_{j \neq k} \mathbf{h}_k^H \mathbf{h}_j s_j + \mathbf{h}_k^H \mathbf{n}$$

**SINR:**
$$\text{SINR}_k^{MRC} = \frac{p_u \|\mathbf{h}_k\|^4}{p_u \sum_{j \neq k} |\mathbf{h}_k^H \mathbf{h}_j|^2 + \sigma^2 \|\mathbf{h}_k\|^2}$$

**Asymptotic (M → ∞):**
$$\text{SINR}_k^{MRC} \to \frac{p_u M \beta_k}{\sigma^2}$$

Interference vanishes; SINR scales linearly with M.

### Zero-Forcing (ZF)

$$\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{ZF} = (\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{y}$$

Completely nulls interference (at cost of noise enhancement).

**SINR:**
$$\text{SINR}_k^{ZF} = \frac{p_u}{[(\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1}]_{kk} \sigma^2}$$

**Asymptotic (M → ∞):**
$$\text{SINR}_k^{ZF} \to \frac{p_u (M - K) \beta_k}{\sigma^2}$$

Scales as (M - K), slightly worse than MRC asymptotically.

### MMSE

$$\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{MMSE} = \left(\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H} + \frac{\sigma^2}{p_u} \mathbf{I}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{y}$$

Balances interference nulling and noise enhancement.

**Asymptotic:** Matches MRC and ZF as M → ∞.

### Achievable rate

Per-user ergodic rate:

$$R_k = E\left[\log_2(1 + \text{SINR}_k)\right]$$

With channel hardening, rate approaches:

$$R_k \approx \log_2\left(1 + \frac{p_u M \beta_k}{\sigma^2}\right)$$

### Sum spectral efficiency

$$SE = \sum_{k=1}^K R_k$$

Scales as $K \log_2(M)$ for fixed K, or approaches $K \log_2(M/K)$ when K scales with M.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: MRC vs ZF comparison**

64 antennas, 8 users, SNR = 10 dB per user.

MRC SINR: $\approx \frac{64 \times 10}{1 + 8 \times 10/64} = \frac{640}{2.25} = 284$ (24.5 dB)

ZF SINR: $\approx (64 - 8) \times 10 = 560$ (27.5 dB)

ZF is 3 dB better due to interference nulling.

At higher M, gap shrinks — interference naturally small with MRC.

**Example: Power scaling**

Target rate: 5 bits/s/Hz per user.
Need SINR ≈ 31 (15 dB).

With 64 antennas: $p_u \times 64 / \sigma^2 = 31 \to p_u/\sigma^2 = 0.5$ (-3 dB per user)

With 256 antennas: $p_u \times 256 / \sigma^2 = 31 \to p_u/\sigma^2 = 0.12$ (-9 dB per user)

4× more antennas allows 6 dB power reduction.

**Example: Sum rate**

64 antennas, 8 users, average SNR = 10 dB.

Per-user rate: $\log_2(1 + 640/8) = \log_2(81) = 6.3$ bits/s/Hz

Sum rate: $8 \times 6.3 = 50$ bits/s/Hz

50× spectral efficiency improvement over single-antenna!

**Example: When to use ZF vs MRC**

High SNR (>15 dB): ZF better — noise enhancement tolerable.
Low SNR (&lt;5 dB): MRC better — don't waste power nulling.
Moderate SNR: MMSE best — automatic trade-off.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Assuming perfect CSI.** Real systems estimate channels from pilots. Imperfect CSI degrades MRC/ZF performance.

2. **Ignoring pilot overhead.** Each user needs orthogonal pilot. K users need K pilot symbols per coherence interval.

3. **ZF with M ≈ K.** ZF requires M > K. When M = K, $(\mathbf{H}^H\mathbf{H})^{-1}$ amplifies noise dramatically.

4. **Forgetting large-scale fading.** Near-far effect: distant users need more power or suffer lower SINR.

5. **Assuming equal rates.** Real systems do scheduling, power control, and rate adaptation per user.

</section>
