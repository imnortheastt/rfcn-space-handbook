## The number that defines wireless reliability

In software, reliability is often expressed as availability — "five nines" means 99.999% uptime. For digital communication, the analogous metric is **bit error rate (BER)**: the fraction of bits that arrive with the wrong value.

BER = $10^{-3}$ means one bit in a thousand is wrong — barely acceptable for compressed voice, unusable for data.
BER = $10^{-6}$ means one bit in a million is wrong — acceptable for many data applications with upper-layer error correction.
BER = $10^{-12}$ is the target for fiber-optic links carrying financial transactions.

Every parameter in a wireless system — transmit power, antenna gain, modulation order, coding rate, bandwidth — ultimately feeds into the BER. Understanding how BER depends on SNR and modulation is the core skill for wireless system design.

The relationship is not linear. At low SNR, BER is near 0.5 (essentially random guessing). As SNR increases, BER falls dramatically — for BPSK, each 1 dB increase in $E_b/N_0$ near the $10^{-5}$ point reduces BER by a factor of roughly 5. This steep sensitivity is why link budgets need margin: a 3 dB fade can push BER from $10^{-6}$ to $10^{-2}$.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A digital receiver must make a binary decision: was this received sample from a "0" or a "1"? In BPSK, the transmitted symbols are $+\sqrt{E_s}$ and $-\sqrt{E_s}$. The decision boundary is at zero.

Noise shifts each received sample by a random amount. If the noise pushes a +1 sample below zero, the receiver decides "0" — a bit error. The probability of this is the probability that the Gaussian noise exceeds $\sqrt{E_s}$ in the wrong direction.

Geometrically: imagine two Gaussian "blobs" on the real line, centered at $+\sqrt{E_s}$ and $-\sqrt{E_s}$. The probability of error is the overlap between the two blobs — the area where the distributions share probability. Higher SNR = blobs farther apart = less overlap = lower BER. Wider signal constellation = more points packed in, blobs closer together = more overlap = higher BER.

This geometric picture — **signal space** — generalizes to all modulation schemes. QPSK is two-dimensional, 16-QAM has 16 points in 2D. The BER depends on how far apart the nearest neighbors are, relative to the noise standard deviation.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Definition

$$\text{BER} = P_b = P(\text{decision error}) = \frac{\text{number of bit errors}}{\text{total bits transmitted}}$$

BER is a probability — it applies to the statistical average over many bits.

### BPSK in AWGN — the fundamental formula

BPSK symbols: $x \in \{+\sqrt{E_s}, -\sqrt{E_s}\}$. Received: $y = x + n$ where $n \sim \mathcal{N}(0, N_0/2)$.

Decision: $\hat{x} = +1$ if $y > 0$, else $\hat{x} = -1$.

Probability of error (by symmetry, same for both symbols):
$$P_b = P(n < -\sqrt{E_s}) = Q\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{2E_s}{N_0}}\right) = Q\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{2E_b}{N_0}}\right)$$

The last equality holds because for BPSK, $E_s = E_b$ (one bit per symbol).

**This is the most important BER formula in communications.** Everything else is a variation on this theme.

### QPSK

QPSK sends two bits per symbol using four constellation points. The BER per bit equals BPSK:
$$P_b = Q\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{2E_b}{N_0}}\right)$$

QPSK has the same BER as BPSK for the same $E_b/N_0$, but twice the spectral efficiency (2 bits/symbol). This is why QPSK is ubiquitous — it doubles throughput at no reliability cost.

### M-PSK (M-ary phase shift keying)

For $M > 4$, symbol error probability approximately:
$$P_s \approx 2Q\!\left(\sqrt{2\frac{E_s}{N_0}}\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{M}\right)\right)$$

With Gray coding (adjacent symbols differ by one bit): $P_b \approx P_s / \log_2 M$.

As $M$ increases, adjacent symbols get closer together ($\sin(\pi/M)$ decreases), requiring higher $E_b/N_0$ for the same BER.

### M-QAM (M-ary quadrature amplitude modulation)

For square M-QAM:
$$P_s \approx 4\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}\right) Q\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{3E_s}{(M-1)N_0}}\right)$$

Bit error probability with Gray coding: $P_b \approx P_s / \log_2 M$.

**Required $E_b/N_0$ for BER = $10^{-5}$ by modulation (AWGN, no coding):**

| Modulation | Spectral eff. (bits/s/Hz) | Required $E_b/N_0$ |
|---|---|---|
| BPSK | 1 | 9.6 dB |
| QPSK | 2 | 9.6 dB |
| 16-QAM | 4 | 13.3 dB |
| 64-QAM | 6 | 17.1 dB |
| 256-QAM | 8 | 21.0 dB |
| 1024-QAM | 10 | 24.9 dB |

Each doubling of spectral efficiency costs roughly 4–5 dB in required SNR.

### BER in Rayleigh fading

In an AWGN channel, BER falls exponentially with $E_b/N_0$ (the Q-function tail). In Rayleigh fading, the channel amplitude $h$ varies randomly. The instantaneous $E_b/N_0 = |h|^2 \bar{E}_b/N_0$ also varies randomly.

Average BER for BPSK in Rayleigh fading (averaging over the exponential distribution of $|h|^2$):

$$\bar{P}_b = \frac{1}{2}\!\left(1 - \sqrt{\frac{\bar{E}_b/N_0}{1 + \bar{E}_b/N_0}}\right) \approx \frac{1}{4\bar{E}_b/N_0} \quad \text{at high SNR}$$

The high-SNR approximation shows BER decays as $1/\text{SNR}$ in Rayleigh fading, versus exponential decay in AWGN. This is the **diversity penalty**: at $\bar{E}_b/N_0 = 20$ dB, Rayleigh BPSK BER is about $1/(4 \times 100) = 0.25\%$, while AWGN BPSK BER at the same SNR is about $10^{-11}$. The fading channel needs roughly 30 dB more average SNR to achieve the same BER as AWGN at low BER targets.

### Diversity: the solution

With $L$-branch diversity (MRC combining), the BER approximation at high SNR becomes:

$$\bar{P}_b \approx \binom{2L-1}{L} \frac{1}{(4\bar{E}_b/N_0)^L}$$

Now BER falls as $1/\text{SNR}^L$ — the diversity order $L$ is the exponent. With $L = 2$: $1/\text{SNR}^2$. With $L = 4$: $1/\text{SNR}^4$. Each diversity branch roughly halves the required SNR (in dB, adds about 15 dB at BER = $10^{-6}$ for $L = 1 \to 2$).

Practical diversity: receive diversity (multiple antennas at base station), transmit diversity (Alamouti code in LTE), time diversity (coding + interleaving), frequency diversity (OFDM over wideband channel).

### Coding gain

Forward error correction (FEC) adds redundancy that allows error correction at the receiver. The **coding gain** is the reduction in required $E_b/N_0$ for the same BER (with the rate-reduced data rate accounted for):

- Convolutional codes (rate 1/2, constraint length 7): ~5 dB gain over uncoded QPSK at BER $10^{-5}$
- Turbo codes (LTE, rate 1/3): ~7 dB gain, approaching Shannon limit to within ~1 dB
- LDPC codes (Wi-Fi, 5G NR): ~7–8 dB gain, within 0.5 dB of Shannon limit

Coding trades bandwidth (lower spectral efficiency due to added redundancy) for SNR margin (lower required $E_b/N_0$). In power-limited systems, this trade is almost always worth making.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example 1: 5G NR uplink modulation selection**

A UE is 500 m from the base station. Measured SNR at the gNB: 22 dB.

From the table, 256-QAM requires 21 dB $E_b/N_0$ for uncoded BER $10^{-5}$.

With LDPC coding (rate 3/4, ≈ 6 dB gain): effective requirement ≈ 15 dB.

Available SNR: 22 dB. Margin: 7 dB. 256-QAM with coding is viable.

As the UE moves farther, SNR drops. When SNR falls below 15 dB, the eNB/gNB will instruct the UE to switch to 64-QAM or lower — this is **adaptive modulation and coding (AMC)**.

**Example 2: The cost of fading**

AWGN BPSK at BER = $10^{-6}$: requires $E_b/N_0 \approx 10.5$ dB.

Rayleigh fading BPSK (no diversity) at BER = $10^{-6}$: high-SNR approx gives $\bar{P}_b = 1/(4\text{SNR})$, so $\text{SNR} = 1/(4 \times 10^{-6}) = 250{,}000$ = 54 dB.

The fading penalty is $54 - 10.5 = 43.5$ dB. This is why no practical mobile system operates without diversity, coding, or both.

**Example 3: Implementation loss budgeting**

Theoretical QPSK at BER = $10^{-5}$: $E_b/N_0 = 9.6$ dB.

Measured BER = $10^{-5}$ in a real receiver at $E_b/N_0 = 11.4$ dB.

Implementation loss: $11.4 - 9.6 = 1.8$ dB. Typical sources: phase noise (0.5 dB), timing jitter (0.5 dB), I/Q imbalance (0.3 dB), ADC quantization (0.5 dB).

Total implementation loss of 1.8 dB is acceptable. Values above 3 dB indicate a hardware problem.

**Example 4: Comparison of modulations at fixed bandwidth**

Fixed bandwidth: 10 MHz. Required data rate: 40 Mbps.

40 Mbps in 10 MHz requires spectral efficiency = 4 bits/s/Hz → 16-QAM.

At BER = $10^{-5}$: 16-QAM needs $E_b/N_0 = 13.3$ dB (uncoded).

With rate-1/2 LDPC (5 dB gain, but spectral efficiency halved to 2 bits/s/Hz — not enough!).

With rate-3/4 LDPC (effective spectral efficiency = 3 bits/s/Hz from 16-QAM × 3/4 = 3 → insufficient for 4 bits/s/Hz).

Need 16-QAM rate-1 with mild coding, or 32-QAM with rate-4/5 coding. This trade-off analysis is exactly what AMC algorithms solve dynamically.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing $E_b/N_0$ and SNR.** $E_b/N_0$ is energy per bit normalized to noise density — independent of bandwidth. SNR = $P_s/(N_0 B)$ depends on bandwidth. They differ by the spectral efficiency factor. BER curves are plotted vs. $E_b/N_0$; link budgets often use SNR. Convert carefully.

2. **AWGN BER formulas in fading channels.** Looking up BPSK BER at 20 dB $E_b/N_0$ from an AWGN table gives $\approx 10^{-11}$. In Rayleigh fading at 20 dB average SNR, it is about $2.5 \times 10^{-3}$. The AWGN table is not applicable. Always specify the channel model.

3. **Ignoring implementation loss.** Real receivers have phase noise, timing error, I/Q imbalance, ADC quantization. These add 1–3 dB of implementation loss. Designing to the theoretical limit with no margin causes field failures.

4. **Gray coding assumed implicitly.** The BER formulas above assume Gray-coded constellations (nearest neighbors differ by one bit). Un-Gray-coded 16-QAM can have BER 4× worse. Modern systems always use Gray coding.

5. **BER target depends on application.** Voice over IP tolerates BER = $10^{-2}$ before quality degrades noticeably. TCP data transfer at $10^{-3}$ causes throughput collapse from retransmissions. Video streaming tolerates $10^{-4}$. FTP/file transfer needs $10^{-8}$ or lower for reasonable performance without upper-layer error correction. Match the target to the application.

6. **Coded BER vs. uncoded BER.** After LDPC or turbo decoding, the output BER is typically far lower than the channel BER before decoding. The decoder exploits block structure to correct errors. A channel BER of $10^{-2}$ before decoding can become $10^{-10}$ after LDPC decoding (when operating above the threshold). These are different quantities; do not compare them directly.

</section>
