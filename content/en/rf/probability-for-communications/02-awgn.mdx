## Why the AWGN channel?

The AWGN channel is the simplest useful channel model: transmitted signal plus Gaussian noise. It captures thermal noise in receivers, cosmic background radiation, and the cumulative effect of many small disturbances. Every communication system analysis starts here.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Thermal noise comes from random electron motion in resistors and semiconductors. At any temperature above absolute zero, electrons jiggle randomly, creating voltage fluctuations. These fluctuations have equal power at all frequencies (white) and Gaussian amplitude distribution.

The AWGN channel says: your signal arrives intact, but with this thermal fuzz added.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Channel model

$$y(t) = x(t) + n(t)$$

- $x(t)$ = transmitted signal
- $n(t)$ = AWGN noise
- $y(t)$ = received signal

### Noise properties

**Additive:** Noise adds to signal (vs. multiplicative fading).

**White:** Flat power spectral density across all frequencies:
$$S_n(f) = \frac{N_0}{2} \quad \text{(two-sided)}$$

Or $S_n(f) = N_0$ (one-sided, for $f \geq 0$).

**Gaussian:** Amplitude follows normal distribution:
$$n(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$

### Thermal noise power

Noise power in bandwidth B:
$$N = kTB$$

- $k$ = Boltzmann constant = $1.38 \times 10^{-23}$ J/K
- $T$ = absolute temperature (K)
- $B$ = bandwidth (Hz)

At room temperature (T = 290 K):
$$N_0 = kT = 4 \times 10^{-21} \text{ W/Hz} = -174 \text{ dBm/Hz}$$

### Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)

$$\text{SNR} = \frac{E_s}{N_0} \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{P_s}{N}$$

- $E_s$ = energy per symbol (J)
- $P_s$ = signal power (W)
- $N$ = noise power in signal bandwidth

In dB: $\text{SNR}_{\text{dB}} = P_{s,\text{dBm}} - N_{\text{dBm}}$

### Eb/N0

Energy per bit normalized to noise:

$$\frac{E_b}{N_0} = \frac{E_s}{N_0 \cdot \log_2 M}$$

where M = constellation size.

For BPSK: $E_b/N_0 = E_s/N_0$

For 16-QAM: $E_b/N_0 = E_s/N_0 - 6$ dB

### Discrete-time model

Sampled at symbol rate:
$$y_k = x_k + n_k$$

where $n_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, N_0/2)$ for real, or $\mathcal{CN}(0, N_0)$ for complex.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Wi-Fi receiver noise floor**

Bandwidth: 20 MHz
Temperature: 290 K

$$N = kTB = 4 \times 10^{-21} \times 20 \times 10^6 = 8 \times 10^{-14} \text{ W}$$

In dBm: $-174 + 10\log_{10}(20 \times 10^6) = -174 + 73 = -101$ dBm

With 6 dB noise figure: noise floor = -95 dBm.

**Example: Required SNR for BPSK**

Target BER: $10^{-5}$

From Q-function: need $E_b/N_0 \approx 9.6$ dB.

For BPSK with Rs = 1 Mbps in 1 MHz:
- Required SNR = 9.6 dB
- At -95 dBm noise floor: need -85.4 dBm signal

**Example: Link budget verification**

TX power: 20 dBm
Path loss: 80 dB
RX sensitivity: -70 dBm

Received power: 20 - 80 = -60 dBm

Margin: -60 - (-70) = 10 dB ✓

**Example: Complex baseband**

For QAM signals, noise is complex:
$$n = n_I + jn_Q$$

where $n_I, n_Q \sim \mathcal{N}(0, N_0/2)$ independent.

Total noise power: $E[|n|^2] = N_0$

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing one-sided and two-sided PSD.** One-sided $N_0$ (positive frequencies only) is 2× two-sided $N_0/2$. Be consistent.

2. **Forgetting noise figure.** Real receivers add noise beyond thermal. Total noise = kTB × F where F is noise figure.

3. **Wrong bandwidth.** Use noise bandwidth, not 3-dB bandwidth. They differ for non-ideal filters.

4. **Ignoring AWGN in presence of fading.** AWGN is always there. Fading channels are AWGN plus multiplicative effects.

5. **Assuming infinite precision.** At very high SNR, quantization noise dominates over AWGN.

</section>
