## The noise that is always there

Every electronic system that operates above absolute zero temperature generates thermal noise. It is not a design flaw. It is thermodynamics. As long as electrons exist and temperature is above 0 K, their random thermal motion creates fluctuating voltages across any resistive element.

For a cloud or backend engineer, noise is usually invisible — the digital abstraction layer handles it. But wireless communication operates below the abstraction layer. The signal arriving at a receiver is a tiny voltage — perhaps −100 dBm, or 10 picowatts — fighting against thermal noise that is always present at the receiver input. The question is never "is there noise?" but "is there enough signal to overcome the noise?"

The **AWGN channel** (Additive White Gaussian Noise) is the simplest model that captures this reality. It says: your transmitted signal arrives intact, but with a Gaussian noise voltage added. This one model — despite its simplicity — underlies most of communication theory. Shannon's capacity formula, BER curves, link budget calculations — they all assume AWGN as the baseline. More complex models (fading, interference, multipath) are built on top of it.

Understanding AWGN thoroughly means understanding *why* noise sets limits, *how* those limits scale with bandwidth and temperature, and *where* thermal noise fits relative to other impairments.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

**Additive:** The noise adds to the signal. The received signal $y = x + n$ where $x$ is what was sent and $n$ is noise. This is unlike fading (multiplicative: $y = hx + n$) where the channel also distorts the signal.

**White:** The noise has equal power at every frequency — a flat power spectral density. The term "white" comes from white light, which contains all visible frequencies equally. In practice, thermal noise is white over bandwidths far wider than any communication system, so this is an excellent approximation.

**Gaussian:** The amplitude distribution of the noise at any instant follows a Gaussian (normal) distribution centered at zero. This follows from the Central Limit Theorem: thermal noise is the sum of contributions from roughly $10^{23}$ electrons, so the sum converges to Gaussian regardless of the individual electron statistics.

The combination of these three properties makes AWGN mathematically tractable and physically accurate. Real receivers add noise figures on top of thermal noise, but the model structure remains.

**The key ratio:** All performance in AWGN depends on the **signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)**, or equivalently $E_b/N_0$ (energy per bit to noise spectral density). Higher SNR means higher reliability at the same data rate, or higher data rate at the same reliability. SNR is the single most important number in wireless link design.

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Channel model

The continuous-time AWGN channel:
$$y(t) = x(t) + n(t)$$

The discrete-time (sampled) model at symbol rate $1/T_s$:
$$y_k = x_k + n_k$$

where $n_k$ is drawn independently each symbol interval.

### Noise power spectral density

Thermal noise has a **two-sided power spectral density (PSD)**:
$$S_n(f) = \frac{N_0}{2} \quad \text{W/Hz, for all } f$$

The factor of 2 in the denominator is a convention to make the noise variance work out cleanly in bandpass analysis. The **one-sided PSD** is $N_0$ W/Hz (for $f \geq 0$).

At physical temperature $T$ (Kelvin):
$$N_0 = kT$$

where $k = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}$ J/K is Boltzmann's constant.

At the standard reference temperature $T_0 = 290$ K (17°C, a conservative room temperature):
$$N_0 = kT_0 = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 290 = 4.0 \times 10^{-21} \text{ W/Hz} = -174 \text{ dBm/Hz}$$

This number — **−174 dBm/Hz** — is the thermal noise floor. It is fundamental. No receiver can have a lower noise floor than this (at 290 K), regardless of how well it is designed.

### Noise power in a bandwidth

For a noise-limited receiver with bandwidth $B$ Hz:
$$N = N_0 B = kTB$$

In dBm:
$$N_\text{dBm} = -174 + 10\log_{10}(B) \quad \text{dBm}$$

Examples:

| Bandwidth | Thermal noise floor |
|---|---|
| 1 Hz | −174 dBm |
| 1 kHz | −144 dBm |
| 1 MHz | −114 dBm |
| 10 MHz | −104 dBm |
| 100 MHz | −94 dBm |
| 1 GHz | −84 dBm |

Every factor of 10 in bandwidth raises the noise floor by 10 dB. Wider bandwidth = more noise power admitted.

### Noise figure

Real receivers add noise beyond the thermal floor. The **noise figure** $F$ (or NF in dB) characterizes this:

$$N_\text{total} = F \cdot kTB$$

or equivalently, the noise floor rises by NF dB:
$$N_\text{receiver} = -174 + 10\log_{10}(B) + \text{NF} \quad \text{dBm}$$

Typical noise figures: 1–3 dB (high-quality LNA), 5–10 dB (integrated transceiver), 15–20 dB (system with lossy front-end components).

### Signal-to-noise ratio

The SNR at the receiver is:
$$\text{SNR} = \frac{P_s}{N} = \frac{P_s}{N_0 B}$$

In dB: $\text{SNR}_\text{dB} = P_{s,\text{dBm}} - N_\text{dBm}$

### Eb/N0 — the fundamental normalized metric

For digital communications, the most useful metric is $E_b/N_0$: energy per information bit normalized to noise spectral density. This removes the dependence on bandwidth and data rate, allowing fair comparison across modulation schemes.

$$\frac{E_b}{N_0} = \frac{P_s}{R_b N_0}$$

where $R_b$ is the bit rate in bits/s.

Relationship to SNR:
$$\frac{E_b}{N_0} = \text{SNR} \cdot \frac{B}{R_b}$$

For BPSK, $R_b = B$ (spectral efficiency 1 bit/s/Hz), so $E_b/N_0 = \text{SNR}$.

For QPSK, $R_b = 2B$, so $E_b/N_0 = \text{SNR}/2$ (3 dB lower for same SNR, because each bit carries 2 bits of information).

For M-QAM with $\log_2 M$ bits/symbol:
$$\frac{E_b}{N_0} = \frac{E_s}{N_0 \log_2 M}$$

where $E_s$ is energy per symbol.

### Complex baseband noise model

For bandpass signals analyzed in baseband (I/Q representation):

The complex noise $\tilde{n} = n_I + jn_Q$ has:
- $n_I, n_Q \sim \mathcal{N}(0, N_0/2)$ — independent, each with variance $N_0/2$
- Total complex noise variance: $E[|\tilde{n}|^2] = N_0$
- Written as complex Gaussian: $\tilde{n} \sim \mathcal{CN}(0, N_0)$

This model is used in all digital baseband signal processing.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example 1: Wi-Fi receiver noise floor**

802.11ac in 80 MHz channel, noise figure = 7 dB.

Thermal noise: $-174 + 10\log_{10}(80 \times 10^6) = -174 + 79 = -95$ dBm

With 7 dB NF: noise floor = $-95 + 7 = -88$ dBm

Wi-Fi 256-QAM requires about 30 dB $E_b/N_0$: minimum signal for data = $-88 + 30 = -58$ dBm.

Range is limited by this minimum sensitivity — at longer distances the received signal falls below −58 dBm.

**Example 2: Cellular link budget (LTE uplink)**

UE transmit power: +23 dBm (200 mW).
Path loss at 1 km, 2.1 GHz: −125 dB (urban macro model).
Base station antenna gain: +18 dBi.
Cable/feeder loss: −2 dB.

Received power: $23 - 125 + 18 - 2 = -86$ dBm.

Base station NF: 5 dB. Bandwidth: 10 MHz.
Noise floor: $-174 + 70 + 5 = -99$ dBm.

SNR at base station: $-86 - (-99) = 13$ dB.

For QPSK with turbo coding (rate 1/2), need about 3 dB $E_b/N_0$. Link margin: $13 - 3 = 10$ dB. Good.

**Example 3: Required $E_b/N_0$ for target BER**

Target: BER = $10^{-5}$ with BPSK.

BPSK BER formula: $Q(\sqrt{2E_b/N_0}) = 10^{-5}$

From Q-function tables: $Q(4.26) \approx 10^{-5}$

So $\sqrt{2E_b/N_0} = 4.26$, giving $E_b/N_0 = 4.26^2/2 = 9.07$ = **9.6 dB**.

With 3 dB implementation loss (receiver imperfections): need 12.6 dB $E_b/N_0$ in practice.

**Example 4: Noise bandwidth vs. 3-dB bandwidth**

A first-order RC low-pass filter with 3-dB bandwidth $B_{3\text{dB}}$ has a **noise bandwidth** $B_N = (\pi/2) B_{3\text{dB}} \approx 1.57 B_{3\text{dB}}$.

If you calculate the noise floor using $B_{3\text{dB}}$, you underestimate the total noise by $10\log_{10}(1.57) \approx 2$ dB. Always use noise bandwidth for noise calculations.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **One-sided vs. two-sided PSD.** $N_0/2$ is the two-sided PSD; $N_0$ is the one-sided PSD. When computing noise power in bandwidth $B$: using the two-sided PSD requires integrating from $-B/2$ to $+B/2$ (total bandwidth $B$), giving $N_0 B/2 \times 2 = N_0 B$. Using the one-sided PSD integrates from 0 to $B$, giving $N_0 B$. Same result, different limits. Get the integration limits wrong and you are off by 2× (3 dB).

2. **Noise figure applies only at the system reference temperature.** The standard NF definition uses $T_0 = 290$ K. At different temperatures, the noise is $kTB$, not $kT_0 B \cdot F$. For satellite receivers pointed at cold sky ($T \approx 10$ K), the noise floor is far below −174 dBm/Hz — the NF convention breaks down and you use noise temperature instead.

3. **Forgetting noise figure in the link budget.** The thermal floor is −174 dBm/Hz, but your receiver adds noise on top of that. A system with 10 dB NF has a noise floor 10 dB above thermal. Failing to include NF overstates the link margin by exactly the NF value.

4. **AWGN assumption in fading channels.** AWGN is always present. In a fading channel, the received signal power fluctuates — sometimes far above the noise, sometimes far below. The SNR fluctuates accordingly. The AWGN BER formula (for constant SNR) does not apply directly; you must average over the fading distribution.

5. **Noise bandwidth vs. 3-dB bandwidth.** The noise admitted by a filter depends on its noise bandwidth, not its 3-dB bandwidth. For non-rectangular filters these differ. Using the 3-dB bandwidth underestimates noise by 2–3 dB for typical filter shapes.

6. **At high SNR, quantization dominates.** ADC quantization noise is approximately $P_s / (6 \cdot 4^{N_b})$ where $N_b$ is the number of bits. For a 12-bit ADC, quantization noise is about 74 dB below the signal — negligible at low SNR, but the dominant impairment at very high SNR. AWGN models lose accuracy in this regime.
