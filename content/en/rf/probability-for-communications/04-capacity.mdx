## Why Shannon capacity?

Shannon capacity is the theoretical ceiling: the maximum data rate at which you can communicate reliably over a noisy channel. No coding scheme can exceed it; optimal coding can approach it. This single formula defines what's possible and guides every communication system design.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine a noisy phone line. Speak too fast and words blur together. Shannon's insight: there's a specific rate where, with clever enough encoding, you can communicate perfectly despite the noise — but beyond that rate, errors are inevitable no matter how smart you are.

Capacity balances signal power against noise power, bandwidth against spectral efficiency.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Shannon-Hartley theorem

For AWGN channel with bandwidth B and SNR:

$$C = B \log_2(1 + \text{SNR}) \quad \text{bits/s}$$

Or in spectral efficiency:

$$\eta = \frac{C}{B} = \log_2(1 + \text{SNR}) \quad \text{bits/s/Hz}$$

### Key relationships

**Bandwidth limited (high SNR):**
$$C \approx B \log_2(\text{SNR})$$

Doubling bandwidth doubles capacity.

**Power limited (low SNR):**
$$C \approx \frac{\text{SNR}}{\ln 2} \cdot B \approx 1.44 \cdot \text{SNR} \cdot B$$

Linear in SNR. Bandwidth helps less.

### Shannon limit

Minimum Eb/N0 for reliable communication:

$$\frac{E_b}{N_0} \geq \frac{2^{C/B} - 1}{C/B}$$

At infinite bandwidth (C/B → 0):

$$\frac{E_b}{N_0} \geq \ln 2 \approx -1.59 \text{ dB}$$

This is the Shannon limit — no system can work below -1.59 dB Eb/N0.

### Gap to capacity

Real systems operate at some gap from capacity:

$$\text{Gap} = \frac{\text{SNR}_{required}}{2^{C/B} - 1}$$

- Uncoded QPSK at BER 10⁻⁵: Gap ≈ 9 dB
- Turbo codes: Gap ≈ 1 dB
- LDPC codes: Gap ≈ 0.5 dB

### Capacity in other channels

**Rayleigh fading (ergodic):**
$$C = \mathbb{E}_h[\log_2(1 + |h|^2 \cdot \text{SNR})]$$

Lower than AWGN at same average SNR.

**MIMO:**
$$C = \log_2 \det(\mathbf{I} + \frac{\text{SNR}}{N_t}\mathbf{HH}^H)$$

Scales linearly with min(Nt, Nr).

### Practical implications

| SNR (dB) | Capacity (bits/s/Hz) | Practical modulation |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| 0 | 1.0 | BPSK |
| 6 | 2.0 | QPSK |
| 12 | 3.6 | 8-PSK, 16-QAM |
| 18 | 5.3 | 32-QAM, 64-QAM |
| 24 | 7.0 | 128-QAM, 256-QAM |

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Wi-Fi 6 capacity**

Bandwidth: 160 MHz
SNR: 25 dB (typical good conditions)

$$C = 160 \times \log_2(1 + 316) = 160 \times 8.3 = 1328 \text{ Mbps}$$

Theoretical maximum ~1.3 Gbps.

Wi-Fi 6 achieves ~1.2 Gbps with 1024-QAM + OFDM + LDPC.

**Example: Satellite link**

Bandwidth: 36 MHz
SNR: 10 dB

$$C = 36 \times \log_2(1 + 10) = 36 \times 3.46 = 125 \text{ Mbps}$$

With DVB-S2 LDPC: achieve ~100 Mbps (80% of capacity).

**Example: Shannon limit verification**

BPSK uncoded at BER = 10⁻⁵ requires Eb/N0 = 9.6 dB.

Shannon limit: -1.59 dB.

Gap: 9.6 - (-1.59) = 11.2 dB.

Modern LDPC at BER = 10⁻⁵ requires Eb/N0 ≈ 0.7 dB.

Gap: 0.7 - (-1.59) = 2.3 dB — approaching Shannon!

**Example: Bandwidth vs. power trade-off**

Fixed capacity C = 100 Mbps.

Narrow bandwidth (10 MHz): need SNR = 31.6 (15 dB)
Wide bandwidth (100 MHz): need SNR = 1 (0 dB)

Spread spectrum trades bandwidth for power savings.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Exceeding capacity.** You cannot reliably exceed Shannon capacity. Claims of "breaking Shannon" are either errors or different channel models.

2. **Confusing bits/s and bits/s/Hz.** Capacity formula gives bits/s; divide by B for spectral efficiency.

3. **Forgetting "reliably."** You can transmit faster than capacity, but errors approach 50%. Capacity is about error-free transmission.

4. **Using wrong SNR.** Shannon uses linear SNR. Converting from dB: SNR = 10^(SNR_dB/10).

5. **Ignoring practical gaps.** Real systems are 1-3 dB from capacity. Budget for this gap.

</section>
