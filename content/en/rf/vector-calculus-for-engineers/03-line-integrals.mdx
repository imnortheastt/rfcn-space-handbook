<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why line integrals?

Voltage between two points is not just the electric field at a single location — it is the accumulation of field contributions along the entire path. A line integral sums up a vector field along a curve, giving you quantities like voltage, work, and circulation.

Every time you measure a voltage with a multimeter, you are implicitly computing a line integral of the electric field from one probe to the other.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine walking through a windy field. At each step, you feel the wind pushing (or resisting) you. The total effort over your entire walk is the sum of all those little pushes — a line integral of the wind field along your path.

In electromagnetics:
- **Voltage** is the line integral of electric field: how much energy per charge is gained or lost along a path.
- **Circulation** is the line integral around a closed loop: does the field tend to push you around the loop, or does it cancel out?

If circulation is zero for all loops, the field is **conservative** — like gravity. If circulation is nonzero, the field has curl — like the magnetic field around a wire.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Definition

For a vector field $\mathbf{F}$ and a curve $C$ parameterized by $\mathbf{r}(t)$ from $t=a$ to $t=b$:

$$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\, dt$$

The dot product picks out the component of $\mathbf{F}$ tangent to the path. Only the tangent component contributes; perpendicular components do no "work."

### Voltage as a line integral

Voltage from point A to point B:

$$V_{AB} = -\int_A^B \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

The negative sign follows convention: voltage drops in the direction of $\mathbf{E}$.

### Circulation

For a closed loop $C$ (starting and ending at the same point):

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

The circle on the integral sign denotes a closed path.

### Path independence

If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla \phi$ (gradient of a scalar), then:

$$\int_A^B \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \phi(B) - \phi(A)$$

The integral depends only on endpoints, not the path. This is why voltage between two points is unique in electrostatics.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Voltage in a uniform field**

Electric field: $\mathbf{E} = 100\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ V/m (uniform, pointing in $+x$)

Path: straight line from $(0,0)$ to $(0.5, 0)$ (0.5 m along $x$-axis)

$$V = -\int_0^{0.5} 100\, dx = -100 \times 0.5 = -50 \text{ V}$$

Voltage drops by 50 V as you move in the direction of the field.

**Example: Circulation around a current-carrying wire**

Ampere's law states:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_\text{enc}$$

The circulation of the magnetic field around a closed loop equals $\mu_0$ times the enclosed current. This is why current creates circulating magnetic fields.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Sign convention.** The direction you traverse the path matters. Reversing direction flips the sign of the integral.

2. **Path dependence.** Only conservative fields (curl-free) have path-independent integrals. In the presence of changing magnetic fields, voltage between two points can depend on the path taken — this is the basis of electromagnetic induction.

3. **Parameterization details.** You must consistently parameterize the curve. Check that $d\mathbf{l} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}dt$ matches your path direction.

4. **Closed vs. open paths.** Circulation (closed loop) and voltage (open path) are related but different quantities. Kirchhoff's voltage law says circulation is zero in DC circuits — but not when magnetic flux is changing.
