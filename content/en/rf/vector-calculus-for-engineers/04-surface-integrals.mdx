## Why surface integrals?

A line integral asks "how much of the field pushes along a path?" A surface integral asks "how much of the field passes through a surface?" These are distinct physical questions that arise constantly in RF engineering.

How much total power does an antenna radiate? Integrate the Poynting vector over any closed surface surrounding it. How much electric field pierces a sphere around a charge? That is Gauss's law — one of Maxwell's four equations — and it is a surface integral. How much magnetic flux links a transformer core? Surface integral of $\mathbf{B}$. Understanding surface integrals is prerequisite to understanding any electromagnetic boundary problem.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### Rain on a tilted roof

Imagine rain falling straight down. A roof facing straight up catches the most rain — the full rate per square meter. A tilted roof catches less, because the rain hits it at an angle. A vertical wall catches nothing.

The amount of rain collected depends on the component of the rain's velocity vector that is **perpendicular (normal) to the surface**. The tangential component slides along the roof without going through it.

This is the core idea of flux: only the field component perpendicular to the surface contributes. The dot product $\mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}$ extracts that perpendicular component at each point, and the surface integral accumulates it over the whole surface.

### Flux and its sign

For an open surface, you must choose which side is "positive normal." The sign of the flux depends on this choice — positive flux means the field passes through in the chosen direction, negative means it passes back.

For a **closed surface** (a sphere, a box, any surface with no holes), the convention is that $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ points **outward**. Positive total flux then means net field lines leaving the volume. Gauss's law says this net outgoing flux equals the total charge inside (scaled by $\varepsilon_0$).

### Why closed surfaces are powerful

Consider any closed surface around a charge. The exact shape doesn't matter — sphere, cube, tetrahedron, whatever you like. Gauss's law guarantees the same total flux regardless of surface shape. This is a profound result: it means you can choose the most convenient shape for calculation.

For a point charge, a sphere centered on the charge makes $\mathbf{E}$ constant in magnitude and always pointing radially outward (parallel to $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$). The dot product becomes trivial and the integral reduces to multiplication. Without this symmetry shortcut, every surface would require a different multi-variable integration.

### Magnetic flux and Faraday's law

Magnetic flux through a surface bounded by a loop $C$ is:

$$\Phi_B = \iint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

Faraday's law says the EMF around $C$ equals $-d\Phi_B/dt$. The time derivative of a surface integral — the rate at which magnetic flux is changing — drives the EMF. To maximize induced voltage in a transformer, you maximize $\Phi_B$ by making the core high-permeability and the cross-sectional area large.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Definition

For a vector field $\mathbf{F}$ and a surface $S$ with outward unit normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$:

$$\Phi = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}\, dA$$

The area element $d\mathbf{A} = \hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dA$ is a vector: its direction is the surface normal, its magnitude is the infinitesimal area patch.

### Computing a surface integral

**Step 1:** Choose a parameterization $\mathbf{r}(u, v)$ that sweeps over the surface.

**Step 2:** Compute the area element vector:
$$d\mathbf{A} = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v}\, du\, dv$$

The cross product automatically gives the normal direction and the correct area scaling.

**Step 3:** Substitute and integrate:
$$\Phi = \iint \mathbf{F}\!\left(\mathbf{r}(u,v)\right) \cdot \left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v}\right) du\, dv$$

**Sphere parameterization** (often needed in antenna problems):

$$\mathbf{r}(\theta, \phi) = R\sin\theta\cos\phi\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + R\sin\theta\sin\phi\,\hat{\mathbf{y}} + R\cos\theta\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$$

Area element: $d\mathbf{A} = R^2 \sin\theta\, d\theta\, d\phi\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}$

### Gauss's law (electric)

$$\oint_S \mathbf{D} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = Q_\text{enc}$$

Total electric flux through any closed surface equals the enclosed free charge. Using $\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon_0 \mathbf{E}$ in free space:

$$\oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

The shape of $S$ does not matter — only the enclosed charge.

### Gauss's law (magnetic)

$$\oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0$$

For any closed surface, ever. Magnetic field lines never begin or end — they always close on themselves. There are no magnetic monopoles.

### Poynting flux — total radiated power

The Poynting vector $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}$ carries electromagnetic power. Total power radiated by a source:

$$P_\text{rad} = \oint_S \mathbf{S} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \oint_S (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}) \cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

This integral over any closed surface surrounding the antenna gives the total transmitted power, regardless of the surface chosen.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

### Example 1: Flux through a sphere from a point charge

A point charge $q$ at the origin. Gaussian surface: sphere of radius $R$.

Electric field: $\mathbf{E} = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ (constant magnitude on the sphere, pointing radially outward).

Area element: $d\mathbf{A} = R^2\sin\theta\, d\theta\, d\phi\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}$

Since $\mathbf{E}$ is parallel to $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ everywhere on the sphere:

$$\Phi_E = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R^2} \iint R^2\sin\theta\, d\theta\, d\phi = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R^2} \cdot 4\pi R^2 = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}$$

The $R^2$ cancels. Flux is independent of sphere radius — it depends only on enclosed charge. This is why field lines from a point charge "thin out" with distance: the same flux spreads over a larger sphere.

### Example 2: Magnetic flux through a transformer core

A toroidal transformer core with cross-sectional area $A_c$ and relative permeability $\mu_r$ carries a peak flux density $B_0 = 1$ T.

Flux per turn: $\Phi_B = B_0 A_c$

For a core with $A_c = 10$ cm² = $10^{-3}$ m²:

$\Phi_B = 1 \times 10^{-3} = 1$ mWb

For $N_2 = 100$ turns secondary and $f = 50$ Hz:

$$\mathcal{E}_2 = N_2 \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = N_2 \cdot 2\pi f \cdot \Phi_B = 100 \times 2\pi \times 50 \times 10^{-3} \approx 31.4 \text{ V}$$

This is transformer design in one surface integral.

### Example 3: Antenna radiated power

A short dipole antenna in free space radiates a time-averaged Poynting vector:

$$\langle S_r \rangle = \frac{\eta_0}{8\pi^2} \left(\frac{\pi I_0 \ell}{\lambda}\right)^2 \frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}$$

where $I_0$ is the peak current, $\ell$ is the dipole length, and $\eta_0 = 377\,\Omega$.

Total radiated power (integrating over a sphere of radius $r$):

$$P_\text{rad} = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\int_0^{\pi} \langle S_r \rangle\, r^2 \sin\theta\, d\theta\, d\phi$$

The $r^2$ in $\langle S_r \rangle$ cancels the $r^2$ in the area element — power is independent of integration radius, as it must be for a lossless antenna. The $\sin^2\theta$ integral evaluates to $4\pi/3$, giving the radiation resistance formula $R_\text{rad} = 80\pi^2(\ell/\lambda)^2$.

### Example 4: Shielding — why a metal box blocks fields

A perfect conductor enclosure. By Gauss's law, any free charges induced on the outer surface by external fields reside entirely on the outer surface. The interior field is determined only by interior charges. For a fully enclosed volume with no interior charges: $E = 0$ inside.

Holes in the shield violate this: any opening of area $A_\text{hole}$ allows flux $\Phi \approx E_\text{ext} A_\text{hole}$ to enter (approximately). Real shielding effectiveness calculations use surface integrals over the aperture to estimate leakage.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Normal direction ambiguity.** For closed surfaces, outward normal is standard. For open surfaces, you must define which side is positive before computing the flux. A sign error here flips the sign of every subsequent result.

2. **Only the normal component contributes.** A field that grazes a surface at 90° to the normal contributes zero flux, even if it is very strong. Flux measures "how much field passes through," not "how much field exists near."

3. **Flux vs. field intensity.** A large total flux does not imply a strong field — it could mean a large surface area with a modest field. For example, a weak field spread over a large sphere can have the same total flux as a strong field on a small sphere around the same charge.

4. **Symmetry is mandatory for Gauss's law.** Gauss's law in integral form is always true. But it is only useful for calculating fields when symmetry lets you pull $|\mathbf{E}|$ out of the integral (making it constant over the surface). For arbitrary charge distributions, the differential form or numerical methods are more practical.

5. **$\mathbf{B}$ flux must be computed through a specific surface.** When applying Faraday's law, $\Phi_B$ is through the surface bounded by the loop $C$ — not some other surface. Any surface bounded by $C$ gives the same result (Stokes' theorem guarantees this), but you must integrate over a surface that is actually bounded by your loop.

</section>
