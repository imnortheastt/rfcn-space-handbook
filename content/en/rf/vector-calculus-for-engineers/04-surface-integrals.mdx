## Why surface integrals?

How much electric field passes through a surface? How much power radiates through a sphere around an antenna? These questions require summing field contributions over an entire surface — a surface integral.

Gauss's law relates the total electric flux through a closed surface to the charge inside. This is one of Maxwell's four equations and is often the fastest way to find electric fields with high symmetry.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine rain falling on a tilted roof. The amount of water collected depends on the roof's area, the rain intensity, and the angle. A roof facing straight up catches the most; a vertical wall catches none.

**Flux** is the surface integral of a vector field through a surface. It measures "how much of the field passes through." The dot product with the surface normal picks out the perpendicular component — only that part contributes.

For a closed surface (like a sphere), flux measures net outflow. Positive flux means more field leaving than entering — there must be a source inside.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Definition

For a vector field $\mathbf{F}$ and a surface $S$ with area element $d\mathbf{A} = \hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dA$:

$$\Phi = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}\, dA$$

The unit normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ points perpendicular to the surface. For closed surfaces, convention is outward.

### Electric flux and Gauss's law

Electric flux through a closed surface:

$$\Phi_E = \oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

This is Gauss's law in integral form. The total flux depends only on enclosed charge, not on the surface shape.

### Magnetic flux

Magnetic flux through any surface:

$$\Phi_B = \iint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

For a closed surface, $\Phi_B = 0$ always — magnetic field lines have no beginning or end (no magnetic monopoles).

### Computing surface integrals

1. Choose a parameterization: $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$ where $(u,v)$ sweep the surface.
2. Compute the area element: $d\mathbf{A} = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v}\, du\, dv$
3. Integrate: $\iint \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(u,v)) \cdot d\mathbf{A}$

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Flux through a sphere**

A point charge $q$ at the origin. Sphere of radius $R$ centered at origin.

Electric field: $\mathbf{E} = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}$

Surface area element: $d\mathbf{A} = R^2 \sin\theta\, d\theta\, d\phi\, \hat{\mathbf{r}}$

Flux:
$$\Phi_E = \iint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R^2} \cdot 4\pi R^2 = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}$$

The $R^2$ cancels — flux is independent of sphere radius, depending only on enclosed charge.

**Example: Antenna power**

Total radiated power from an antenna is the flux of the Poynting vector through a surrounding sphere:

$$P_\text{rad} = \oint_S \mathbf{S} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Normal direction.** For closed surfaces, outward normal is standard. For open surfaces, you must specify which side is "positive."

2. **Only perpendicular component contributes.** Field parallel to the surface passes through without contributing to flux.

3. **Flux vs. field strength.** A large flux doesn't mean a strong field — it could mean a large area with modest field. Flux is an integral, not a local quantity.

4. **Symmetry simplifies everything.** Gauss's law is powerful when symmetry makes $\mathbf{E}$ constant over the surface. Without symmetry, the integral may be intractable.

</section>
