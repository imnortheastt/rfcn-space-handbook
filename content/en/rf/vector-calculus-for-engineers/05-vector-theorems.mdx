## Why these theorems?

You have now learned about three types of integrals: line, surface, and volume. Stokes' theorem and the divergence theorem are the bridges that connect them. They say: instead of integrating a curl over a surface, you can integrate the field around the boundary curve; instead of integrating a divergence over a volume, you can integrate the field through the enclosing surface.

These two theorems are why Maxwell's equations exist in both differential and integral forms — and why you get to choose which form is easier for any given problem. Every application of Gauss's law, Faraday's law, or Ampere's law in integral form is secretly an application of one of these theorems. Understanding them gives you a unified view of classical electromagnetism.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### Divergence theorem: local sources sum to global outflow

The divergence at a point is "net outflow per unit volume." If you integrate that over every cubic millimeter inside a closed region, you're counting all the sources and sinks. But those internal contributions don't cancel with each other — only the field escaping (or entering) through the outer boundary represents the net result.

Think of a factory district with many smokestacks. The total smoke leaving the district boundary equals the sum of what every chimney inside produces. You don't need to track internal smoke movement — only what exits.

$$\text{(sum of all internal sources)} = \text{(net outflow through boundary)}$$

This is the divergence theorem. In EM: integrate $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}$ over a volume and you get the total flux through its enclosing surface.

### Stokes' theorem: local rotations sum to boundary circulation

The curl at a point is "local rotation intensity." Imagine a large membrane covered with tiny paddle wheels, each spinning at the local curl. The total angular momentum of all those wheels is not directly observable — but its effect at the boundary is. If all the wheels spin consistently, the net effect is circulation around the membrane's rim.

Think of a field of grass. Each blade rotates slightly in the breeze. The total swirl across the whole field shows up as a circular wind pattern around its perimeter.

$$\text{(sum of all local swirls on surface)} = \text{(total circulation around edge)}$$

This is Stokes' theorem. In EM: integrate $\nabla \times \mathbf{E}$ over a surface and you get the EMF around its boundary loop.

### Why both forms of Maxwell's equations exist

Maxwell's equations were originally written in integral form by Faraday, Ampere, and Gauss — experimentalists working with loops and surfaces. Maxwell rewrote them in differential form (divergence and curl) for mathematical elegance and to derive the wave equation.

The divergence theorem converts the two Gauss equations (electric and magnetic) between forms. Stokes' theorem converts the two curl equations (Faraday and Ampere-Maxwell) between forms.

| Maxwell Law | Differential → Integral | Theorem used |
|-------------|--------------------------|--------------|
| Gauss (E) | Integrate $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}$ over volume | Divergence theorem |
| Gauss (B) | Integrate $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}$ over volume | Divergence theorem |
| Faraday | Integrate $\nabla \times \mathbf{E}$ over surface | Stokes' theorem |
| Ampere-Maxwell | Integrate $\nabla \times \mathbf{H}$ over surface | Stokes' theorem |

Both forms are always simultaneously true. Choose whichever is more convenient for your geometry.

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Divergence theorem (Gauss's theorem)

For a vector field $\mathbf{F}$, a closed volume $V$ bounded by surface $S$ (with outward normal):

$$\oint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \iiint_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})\, dV$$

Left side: flux through the closed surface.
Right side: volume integral of divergence.

**Requirements:**
- $S$ must be a closed surface (no holes, no boundary).
- $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ points outward everywhere on $S$.
- $\mathbf{F}$ and its divergence must be continuous inside $V$.

### Stokes' theorem

For a vector field $\mathbf{F}$, an open surface $S$ bounded by closed curve $C$:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

Left side: circulation of $\mathbf{F}$ around the boundary curve.
Right side: surface integral of curl over any surface bounded by $C$.

**Orientation rule (right-hand rule):** Curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of traversal around $C$; the thumb points in the direction of the surface normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$. Consistency between $C$ and $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is mandatory — getting this wrong flips the sign.

**Key flexibility:** Any surface bounded by $C$ gives the same result. For Ampere's law, this is the freedom to choose the flat disk, a bulging cap, or any other surface — they all give the same enclosed current.

### Deriving Gauss's law (integral) from the differential form

Start with differential Gauss's law: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho/\varepsilon_0$.

Integrate both sides over volume $V$:

$$\iiint_V \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}\, dV = \iiint_V \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}\, dV = \frac{Q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

Apply divergence theorem to the left side:

$$\oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

Done. The integral form follows directly from the differential form plus the divergence theorem.

### Deriving Faraday's law (integral) from the differential form

Start with $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t$.

Integrate both sides over surface $S$:

$$\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) \cdot d\mathbf{A} = -\iint_S \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

Apply Stokes' theorem to the left side:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

This is the EMF-equals-flux-rate statement of Faraday's law. The changing magnetic flux drives circulation of the electric field — which drives current in any loop.

### Complete Maxwell's equations in both forms

**Differential form** (local, point-by-point):

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho_v$$
$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$$
$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$
$$\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}$$

**Integral form** (global, over regions):

$$\oint_S \mathbf{D} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = Q_\text{enc}$$
$$\oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0$$
$$\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$
$$\oint_C \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_\text{enc} + \frac{d\Phi_D}{dt}$$

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

### Example 1: Verifying no magnetic monopoles

Take any closed surface $S$ around a region of space. By the divergence theorem:

$$\oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \iiint_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B})\, dV = \iiint_V 0\, dV = 0$$

No matter what is inside, no matter what fields are present, the total magnetic flux through any closed surface is always zero. Magnetic field lines must close on themselves — they cannot start or end anywhere. This is why every magnet has both a north and south pole: you cannot cut one off.

### Example 2: Ampere's law and capacitor gaps (why you need displacement current)

Consider a capacitor being charged. Choose a circular loop $C$ of radius $r$ in the plane of the capacitor. Apply Stokes'/Ampere's theorem:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_\text{enc} + \frac{d\Phi_D}{dt}$$

**Surface choice 1:** Flat disk bounded by $C$, not passing through the capacitor. Conduction current $I$ threads through. Result: $H \cdot 2\pi r = I$.

**Surface choice 2:** Bulging cap surface that passes between the capacitor plates. Zero conduction current threads through — no charges cross the gap. But there is a changing electric field $\partial \mathbf{D}/\partial t$ between the plates (displacement current).

Both surfaces give the same answer only if the displacement current $\epsilon \partial \mathbf{E}/\partial t$ between the plates equals the conduction current $I$ that charges the capacitor. This is precisely Maxwell's addition to Ampere's law — without it, Stokes' theorem would give inconsistent results depending on surface choice.

### Example 3: Energy conservation — Poynting's theorem from Maxwell

Taking the dot product of Faraday's law with $\mathbf{H}$ and Ampere's law with $\mathbf{E}$, then combining:

$$\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}) = -\mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{J} - \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\!\left(\frac{1}{2}\varepsilon E^2 + \frac{1}{2}\mu H^2\right)$$

Integrate over volume $V$ and apply the divergence theorem to the left side:

$$-\oint_S (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}) \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \iiint_V \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{J}\, dV + \frac{d}{dt}\iiint_V \!\left(\frac{1}{2}\varepsilon E^2 + \frac{1}{2}\mu H^2\right) dV$$

Left side: power flowing into the volume through its surface. Right side: power dissipated (Ohmic loss) plus rate of increase of stored electromagnetic energy. This is Poynting's theorem — energy conservation for EM fields — derived directly from Maxwell's equations and the divergence theorem.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Orientation consistency in Stokes' theorem.** The curve $C$ direction and the surface normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ must be related by the right-hand rule. If you reverse the traversal direction of $C$, you must also flip $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$. Getting this wrong gives a sign error that propagates through the entire calculation.

2. **Divergence theorem needs a closed surface.** The theorem requires $S$ to have no boundary — a complete enclosure. An open surface (like a hemisphere without its flat face) has a boundary curve and belongs to Stokes' theorem, not the divergence theorem.

3. **Stokes' theorem needs an open surface with a boundary.** If you apply Stokes' theorem to a closed surface, the boundary curve is empty (length zero) and the left side is zero — which forces the total curl flux to be zero, a useless result.

4. **The theorems are exact mathematical identities.** They are not approximations, not valid "only for smooth fields," and not "approximately true at low frequency." Any apparent error comes from applying them incorrectly, not from the theorems themselves.

5. **Surface independence is physical.** In Ampere's law, the fact that any surface bounded by loop $C$ gives the same current is not a mathematical curiosity — it reflects charge conservation ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \partial\rho/\partial t = 0$). If you find a surface that gives a different answer, your field model violates charge conservation.
