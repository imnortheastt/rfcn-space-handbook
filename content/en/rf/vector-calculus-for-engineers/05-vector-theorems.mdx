<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why these theorems?

Stokes' theorem and the divergence theorem are the two pillars that connect local differential operations (curl, divergence) to global integral quantities (circulation, flux). They are why Maxwell's equations have both differential and integral forms — and why you can choose whichever form suits your problem.

Every time you apply Gauss's law or Faraday's law, you are implicitly using one of these theorems.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### Divergence theorem (Gauss's theorem)

The divergence at a point measures local "outflow per unit volume." If you add up all those local outflows throughout a volume, you get the total flux through the surface enclosing that volume.

$$\text{(sum of sources inside)} = \text{(net flux out through boundary)}$$

Think of a region with many tiny faucets. The total water leaving the region equals the sum of what each faucet produces.

### Stokes' theorem

The curl at a point measures local rotation. If you add up all those local rotations over a surface, you get the circulation around the boundary curve of that surface.

$$\text{(sum of local swirls on surface)} = \text{(total circulation around edge)}$$

Think of many tiny paddle wheels on a membrane. The net spin of all the wheels equals how hard water pushes around the membrane's rim.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Divergence theorem

For a vector field $\mathbf{F}$, a volume $V$ enclosed by surface $S$:

$$\oint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \iiint_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})\, dV$$

Left side: flux through the closed surface.
Right side: volume integral of divergence.

### Stokes' theorem

For a vector field $\mathbf{F}$, a surface $S$ bounded by curve $C$:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

Left side: circulation around the boundary.
Right side: surface integral of curl.

### Connection to Maxwell's equations

| Maxwell equation | Differential form | Integral form (via theorem) |
|------------------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| Gauss's law (E) | $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho/\varepsilon_0$ | $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = Q/\varepsilon_0$ |
| Gauss's law (B) | $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ | $\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0$ |
| Faraday's law | $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t$ | $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -d\Phi_B/dt$ |
| Ampere's law | $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0\partial \mathbf{E}/\partial t$ | $\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I + \mu_0\varepsilon_0 d\Phi_E/dt$ |

The divergence theorem converts the first two. Stokes' theorem converts the last two.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Deriving integral Gauss's law**

Start with $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho/\varepsilon_0$. Integrate over volume $V$:

$$\iiint_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E})\, dV = \iiint_V \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}\, dV = \frac{Q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

Apply divergence theorem to the left side:

$$\oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_\text{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

**Example: Deriving integral Faraday's law**

Start with $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t$. Integrate over surface $S$:

$$\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) \cdot d\mathbf{A} = -\iint_S \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

Apply Stokes' theorem to the left side:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

This is why a changing magnetic flux induces an EMF around a loop.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Orientation consistency.** In Stokes' theorem, the curve $C$ and surface normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ must obey the right-hand rule: fingers curl along $C$, thumb points along $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

2. **Closed surface vs. bounded surface.** Divergence theorem requires a closed surface (no boundary). Stokes' theorem requires an open surface with a boundary curve.

3. **The theorems are NOT approximations.** They are exact mathematical identities. Any "error" comes from misapplying them, not from the theorems themselves.

4. **Choosing the right theorem.** If your field equation involves divergence, think divergence theorem. If it involves curl, think Stokes' theorem.

</section>
