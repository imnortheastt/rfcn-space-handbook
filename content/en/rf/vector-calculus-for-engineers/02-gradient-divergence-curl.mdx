<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why these three operators?

Maxwell's equations are built from exactly three vector differential operators: gradient, divergence, and curl. If you understand what each one measures physically, you can read Maxwell's equations like sentences rather than hieroglyphs.

- **Gradient** tells you how fast a scalar field changes and in which direction.
- **Divergence** tells you whether a vector field is spreading out or converging at a point.
- **Curl** tells you whether a vector field is rotating around a point.

These three concepts unlock electromagnetic theory.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### Gradient

Imagine hiking on a hill. The gradient points uphill in the steepest direction. Its magnitude is the slope. For a temperature field, the gradient points toward the hottest direction and tells you how quickly temperature increases per meter.

In electrostatics, the electric field is the negative gradient of voltage: $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V$. Field points from high voltage to low, like water flowing downhill.

### Divergence

Picture air escaping from a balloon with a pinhole. At the hole, air diverges — flows outward from that point. Divergence measures "outflow per unit volume." Positive divergence means a source; negative means a sink.

Gauss's law says the divergence of the electric field equals the charge density (divided by $\varepsilon_0$). Charges are sources or sinks of electric field lines.

### Curl

Imagine water swirling in a whirlpool. The curl measures the rotation or circulation of a vector field around a point. Stick a tiny paddle wheel in the flow — if it spins, there's curl.

Faraday's law says a changing magnetic field creates curl in the electric field. That curl drives current around a loop — the basis of every transformer and inductor.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### The del operator

The nabla symbol $\nabla$ (del) is a vector of partial derivatives:

$$\nabla = \hat{\mathbf{x}}\frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \hat{\mathbf{y}}\frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \hat{\mathbf{z}}\frac{\partial}{\partial z}$$

### Gradient (∇ applied to scalar)

For scalar field $\phi(x,y,z)$:

$$\nabla \phi = \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y}\hat{\mathbf{y}} + \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z}\hat{\mathbf{z}}$$

Result: a vector pointing in the direction of steepest increase.

### Divergence (∇ · applied to vector)

For vector field $\mathbf{F} = F_x\hat{\mathbf{x}} + F_y\hat{\mathbf{y}} + F_z\hat{\mathbf{z}}$:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial z}$$

Result: a scalar measuring net outflow per unit volume.

### Curl (∇ × applied to vector)

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{\mathbf{x}} & \hat{\mathbf{y}} & \hat{\mathbf{z}} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ F_x & F_y & F_z \end{vmatrix}$$

Result: a vector whose direction is the axis of rotation (right-hand rule) and magnitude is the rotation intensity.

### Key identities

- Curl of a gradient is zero: $\nabla \times (\nabla \phi) = \mathbf{0}$
- Divergence of a curl is zero: $\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0$

These identities have deep physical meaning: conservative fields have no curl; magnetic field lines have no beginning or end.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Electric field from voltage**

Given voltage field: $V(x,y) = 100 - 20x - 10y$ (in volts)

Gradient:
$$\nabla V = -20\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} - 10\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$$

Electric field (negative gradient):
$$\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V = 20\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + 10\,\hat{\mathbf{y}} \text{ V/m}$$

The field points in the direction of decreasing voltage with magnitude $\sqrt{20^2+10^2} \approx 22.4$ V/m.

**Example: Divergence of a radial field**

For $\mathbf{E} = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ (point charge field), the divergence is zero everywhere except at the origin, where the charge lives.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Gradient acts on scalars, divergence and curl act on vectors.** Applying gradient to a vector or curl to a scalar is undefined.

2. **Divergence gives a scalar; curl gives a vector.** Don't confuse the two output types.

3. **Coordinate system matters.** The formulas above are for Cartesian coordinates. In cylindrical or spherical coordinates, the expressions are different (and longer). Always check which system you're in.

4. **Physical interpretation first.** Before computing, ask: "Is this field spreading out? Rotating? Pointing uphill?" The math should confirm intuition, not replace it.
