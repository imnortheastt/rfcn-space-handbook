## Why these three operators?

Maxwell's equations are built from exactly three vector differential operators: gradient, divergence, and curl. They show up everywhere in RF — not as decoration, but because each one answers a distinct physical question about how a field behaves at a point.

- **Gradient** asks: in which direction does this scalar field increase most steeply, and how fast?
- **Divergence** asks: is this vector field spreading outward from this point, or converging into it?
- **Curl** asks: is this vector field swirling around this point?

If you can answer these three questions by inspection for any field, you can read Maxwell's equations the way you read plain text. The math is just formalism on top of those three physical ideas.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### Gradient — the steepest-ascent arrow

Imagine hiking across hilly terrain. At any point, there is a direction that takes you uphill most steeply. The gradient is an arrow pointing in that direction, and its length is the slope.

More concretely: if $V(x,y,z)$ is the voltage at every point in space, then $\nabla V$ at a particular point is a vector pointing toward increasing voltage. Its magnitude is the rate of voltage change per meter.

The electric field is the negative gradient of voltage:

$$\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V$$

The minus sign means field lines point from high voltage to low — from the mountain peak toward the valley. This is exactly the direction positive charges would slide if released.

**Key insight:** Gradient converts a scalar field (one number per point) into a vector field (three numbers per point). It asks "how does this number change as I step in each direction?"

### Divergence — sources and sinks

Picture a bathtub filling with water. Water molecules stream outward from the faucet — that point has positive divergence. Water molecules converge toward the drain — that point has negative divergence. A point in the middle of still water has zero divergence.

Divergence measures net outflow per unit volume at a point. Positive divergence means a **source**; negative divergence means a **sink**.

Gauss's law says $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho/\varepsilon_0$: the divergence of the electric field equals charge density (scaled by $\varepsilon_0$). Positive charges are sources of electric field lines; negative charges are sinks. Where there are no charges, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0$ — field lines neither begin nor end.

**Key insight:** Divergence converts a vector field into a scalar (one number per point). It measures "net outward flow" locally.

### Curl — the rotation meter

Drop a tiny paddle wheel at a point in a flowing river. If the current is faster on one side of the wheel than the other, the wheel spins. Curl measures how strongly and in which direction that spinning occurs.

Faraday's law says $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t$: a time-varying magnetic field creates a curling electric field. That curling E-field is what drives current around a loop in a transformer secondary. No curl, no transformer action.

Ampere's law says $\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \partial \mathbf{D}/\partial t$: currents and changing electric fields create curling magnetic fields. The circles of $\mathbf{H}$ around a wire are a direct expression of this curl.

**Key insight:** Curl converts a vector field into another vector field. The output vector points along the rotation axis (right-hand rule), and its magnitude is the rotation intensity.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### The del operator $\nabla$

The nabla symbol $\nabla$ (also called "del") packages partial derivatives into a vector-like object:

$$\nabla = \hat{\mathbf{x}}\frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \hat{\mathbf{y}}\frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \hat{\mathbf{z}}\frac{\partial}{\partial z}$$

It is an operator, not a vector — but it behaves like one when combined with dot and cross products.

### Gradient: $\nabla$ applied to a scalar field

For a scalar field $\phi(x,y,z)$:

$$\nabla \phi = \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y}\hat{\mathbf{y}} + \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z}\hat{\mathbf{z}}$$

Input: scalar field. Output: vector field pointing in the direction of steepest increase.

**Example — electric field from voltage:**

$$V(x,y,z) = 100 - 20x - 10y \quad \text{(volts)}$$

$$\nabla V = -20\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} - 10\,\hat{\mathbf{y}} + 0\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$$

$$\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V = 20\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + 10\,\hat{\mathbf{y}} \text{ V/m}$$

Magnitude: $|\mathbf{E}| = \sqrt{20^2 + 10^2} \approx 22.4$ V/m, pointing in the direction that voltage decreases fastest.

### Divergence: $\nabla \cdot$ applied to a vector field

For $\mathbf{F} = F_x\hat{\mathbf{x}} + F_y\hat{\mathbf{y}} + F_z\hat{\mathbf{z}}$:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial z}$$

Input: vector field. Output: scalar measuring net outflow per unit volume.

**Physical reading:** If each component is increasing in its own direction (field spreading outward), the divergence is positive. If each component is decreasing (field converging inward), divergence is negative.

**Example — point charge field:** For $\mathbf{E} = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}$, the divergence is zero everywhere except at $r = 0$ where the charge lives. All field lines originate from the single source point.

### Curl: $\nabla \times$ applied to a vector field

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{\mathbf{x}} & \hat{\mathbf{y}} & \hat{\mathbf{z}} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ F_x & F_y & F_z \end{vmatrix}$$

Expanding:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{\partial F_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial z}\right)\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \left(\frac{\partial F_x}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial x}\right)\hat{\mathbf{y}} + \left(\frac{\partial F_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial y}\right)\hat{\mathbf{z}}$$

Input: vector field. Output: vector field — the axis and intensity of local rotation.

**Example — magnetic field near a wire:** Current $I$ along $+z$ creates $\mathbf{H} = \frac{I}{2\pi\rho}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$ (cylindrical). This field circulates around the wire. Everywhere away from the wire, $\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = 0$. At the wire itself, $\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J}$ — the current is the source of the curl.

### Critical identities

Two identities appear constantly in EM theory:

**Curl of a gradient is always zero:**
$$\nabla \times (\nabla \phi) = \mathbf{0}$$

Physical meaning: conservative fields (those expressible as a gradient) have no rotation. Electrostatic fields satisfy this — there is no curl of $\mathbf{E}$ when $\mathbf{B}$ is constant.

**Divergence of a curl is always zero:**
$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0$$

Physical meaning: the magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ has no divergence (no magnetic monopoles) because $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ where $\mathbf{A}$ is the magnetic vector potential.

These are not approximations — they are exact mathematical identities that hold for any smooth field.

### Operator summary

| Operator | Input | Output | Physical question |
|----------|-------|--------|-------------------|
| $\nabla \phi$ (gradient) | Scalar | Vector | Which way does $\phi$ increase most steeply? |
| $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}$ (divergence) | Vector | Scalar | Is $\mathbf{F}$ spreading out or converging here? |
| $\nabla \times \mathbf{F}$ (curl) | Vector | Vector | Is $\mathbf{F}$ swirling around this point? |

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

### Example 1: Finding E from a voltage distribution

A coaxial cable with inner conductor at $V = 100$ V and outer conductor at $V = 0$ V. Between the conductors, the voltage in cylindrical coordinates is:

$$V(\rho) = \frac{100 \ln(b/\rho)}{\ln(b/a)}$$

where $a$ is the inner radius and $b$ is the outer radius. The gradient in cylindrical coordinates gives:

$$\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V = \frac{100}{\rho \ln(b/a)}\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\rho}} \text{ V/m}$$

The field points radially outward from the inner conductor — exactly the direction voltage decreases. Near the inner conductor (small $\rho$), $|\mathbf{E}|$ is large; near the outer conductor it is smaller. This inverse-$\rho$ dependence is why coaxial dielectrics experience their highest stress near the center conductor.

### Example 2: Checking Gauss's law in free space

For a plane wave traveling in $+z$:

$$\mathbf{E} = E_0 e^{-jkz}\hat{\mathbf{x}}$$

Computing divergence:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\partial E_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial E_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial E_z}{\partial z} = 0 + 0 + 0 = 0$$

Correct — there are no charges in free space. Any wave solution in empty space must have zero divergence. If your EM simulation gives a nonzero divergence in a charge-free region, something is numerically wrong.

### Example 3: Curl and Faraday induction

A time-varying magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = B_0 \sin(\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ exists throughout space. Faraday's law:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} = -B_0 \omega \cos(\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$$

The right side is nonzero, so $\mathbf{E}$ must have curl — it circulates in the $xy$-plane. By symmetry, $\mathbf{E}$ forms concentric circles. Integrating gives the induced EMF in any loop of area $A$:

$$\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -B_0 \omega A \cos(\omega t)$$

Bigger loop area, higher frequency, stronger $B_0$ — all increase the induced voltage. This is the operating principle of every loop antenna and transformer.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Gradient acts on scalars; divergence and curl act on vectors.** Applying $\nabla$ to a vector without the dot or cross is the Jacobian, a different beast entirely. Applying divergence to a scalar is undefined. Always check the input type.

2. **Divergence gives a scalar; curl gives a vector.** Confusing the two output types leads to dimensional errors that are hard to track. When in doubt, count: scalar in → gradient → vector out; vector in → divergence → scalar out; vector in → curl → vector out.

3. **Coordinate system formulas differ.** The compact formulas above are for Cartesian coordinates only. In cylindrical and spherical coordinates, the expressions for divergence and curl contain extra terms (metric factors). Always check which system you are in before writing $\nabla \cdot$ or $\nabla \times$.

4. **Physical interpretation first, computation second.** Before reaching for the determinant formula for curl, ask: "Does this field look like it's rotating around any axis?" If the field is radially symmetric (like $E_r\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ from a point charge), its curl is zero by inspection. Computation should confirm intuition, not replace it.

5. **Sign errors in E = -∇V.** The minus sign is critical. Forgetting it reverses your field direction. Electric field points from high voltage to low, which is the direction of decreasing $V$, hence the negative gradient.
