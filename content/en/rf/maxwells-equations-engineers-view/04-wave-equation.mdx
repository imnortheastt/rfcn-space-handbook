## Why the wave equation?

Maxwell's equations predict that electromagnetic disturbances propagate through space at the speed of light. The wave equation is the mathematical consequence — it governs every RF signal from DC to daylight.

Understanding this equation connects antenna radiation, transmission line behavior, and free-space propagation.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Faraday: changing B creates E. Ampere-Maxwell: changing E creates B. Together: a disturbance in E creates B, which creates E, which creates B... The disturbance propagates outward as a wave.

The speed comes from $\epsilon$ and $\mu$ — the electric and magnetic "stiffness" of space.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Derivation in source-free region

Start with Maxwell's equations ($\mathbf{J} = 0$, $\rho = 0$):

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\mu \frac{\partial \mathbf{H}}{\partial t}$$
$$\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \epsilon \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$$

Take curl of first equation:
$$\nabla \times \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \times \mathbf{H})$$

Substitute second equation:
$$\nabla \times \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\mu \epsilon \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$

Use vector identity $\nabla \times \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{E}$:

Since $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0$ in source-free region:

$$\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = \mu \epsilon \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$

### Wave equation

$$\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} - \frac{1}{v^2} \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} = 0$$

where phase velocity:

$$v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu \epsilon}}$$

In free space: $v = c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s.

### Plane wave solution

For wave traveling in +z direction:

$$\mathbf{E} = E_0 e^{j(\omega t - kz)} \hat{x}$$

where:
- $k = \omega/v = \omega\sqrt{\mu\epsilon}$ (wave number)
- $\lambda = 2\pi/k = v/f$ (wavelength)

### Helmholtz equation

For sinusoidal steady state ($e^{j\omega t}$ assumed):

$$\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} + k^2 \mathbf{E} = 0$$

This is the frequency-domain wave equation.

### Wave impedance

$$\eta = \frac{E}{H} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\epsilon}}$$

In free space: $\eta_0 = 377\ \Omega$.

### Relationship between E and H

For a plane wave:
- E and H are perpendicular
- E and H are in phase
- $|\mathbf{E}| = \eta |\mathbf{H}|$
- Both perpendicular to propagation direction

### Power flow

Poynting vector:
$$\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}$$

Average power density:
$$\langle S \rangle = \frac{1}{2} \text{Re}(\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}^*) = \frac{|E|^2}{2\eta}$$

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Wavelength calculation**

Frequency: 2.4 GHz.

In free space: $\lambda = c/f = 3 \times 10^8 / 2.4 \times 10^9 = 0.125$ m = 12.5 cm.

In dielectric ($\epsilon_r = 4$): $\lambda = 12.5 / \sqrt{4} = 6.25$ cm.

**Example: Power density**

Electric field: 1 V/m RMS in free space.

Power density: $S = E^2/(2 \times 377) = 1/(754) = 1.33$ mW/m².

**Example: Propagation in lossy medium**

For lossy dielectric with conductivity σ:

$$k = \omega\sqrt{\mu\epsilon}\sqrt{1 - j\sigma/(\omega\epsilon)}$$

The imaginary part gives attenuation.

At 1 GHz in seawater (σ = 4 S/m):

Skin depth: ~8 cm. Submarine communication requires very low frequencies.

**Example: Velocity in transmission line**

Coax with $\epsilon_r = 2.3$ (polyethylene).

$v = c/\sqrt{2.3} = 0.66c$.

Signal takes 50% longer to traverse same length vs. free space.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing phase and group velocity.** In dispersive media, $v_p = \omega/k$ differs from $v_g = d\omega/dk$. Energy travels at group velocity.

2. **Forgetting medium effects.** $\epsilon_r$ and $\mu_r$ change wavelength and impedance. Always specify the medium.

3. **Using wrong impedance.** Wave impedance $\eta$ differs from transmission line characteristic impedance $Z_0$. Related but not equal.

4. **Ignoring losses.** Real materials have loss tangent. At high frequencies, even small losses cause significant attenuation.

5. **Assuming plane waves everywhere.** Near sources, fields don't look like plane waves. The wave equation still holds, but solutions are more complex.
