## Why the wave equation?

Maxwell's equations predict that electromagnetic disturbances propagate through space at the speed of light. The wave equation is the mathematical consequence of coupling Faraday's law and Ampere's law — it governs every RF signal from the lowest ELF submarine communication band to millimeter-wave 5G.

Understanding this equation is the bridge between abstract field theory and practical RF engineering. Antenna radiation patterns come from wave equation solutions in spherical coordinates. Transmission line theory is the wave equation in one dimension. Free-space path loss is a statement about how wave amplitude decreases with distance. Waveguide cutoff frequencies are eigenvalues of the wave equation constrained by boundary conditions.

One equation — everything in RF propagation follows from it.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### The chase that creates a wave

Faraday's law: a changing $\mathbf{B}$ creates curl in $\mathbf{E}$. Ampere-Maxwell: a changing $\mathbf{E}$ creates curl in $\mathbf{H}$. Together, they set up a feedback loop.

Imagine a pulse of electric field. By Ampere's law, this creates a ring of magnetic field around it. The growing magnetic ring, by Faraday's law, drives a new ring of electric field slightly further out. That new electric field ring drives another magnetic ring... The disturbance hops outward, each step recreating the conditions for the next.

This is not merely a metaphor — it is the exact physics. The rate at which the disturbance hops is determined by how quickly each law responds: $\varepsilon$ controls how easily $\mathbf{E}$ is produced from $\mathbf{D}$, and $\mu$ controls how easily $\mathbf{H}$ is produced from $\mathbf{B}$. Stiffer medium (higher $\varepsilon$ or $\mu$) → slower propagation. Free space has the minimum "stiffness" — its wave speed is $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0} \approx 3 \times 10^8$ m/s.

### Why E and H are perpendicular

When the wave moves in the $+z$ direction, $\mathbf{E}$ points in $x$ and $\mathbf{H}$ points in $y$. These three directions are mutually perpendicular ($\hat{x} \times \hat{y} = \hat{z}$), and the Poynting vector $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}$ points in the propagation direction.

This is not arbitrary — it falls directly out of the curl equations. A plane wave with $\mathbf{E}$ parallel to propagation would require $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} \neq 0$ (nonzero divergence), which contradicts Gauss's law in a source-free region. So the transverse nature of electromagnetic waves is enforced by all four Maxwell equations simultaneously.

### Wave impedance as E-to-H ratio

In free space, the ratio $|\mathbf{E}|/|\mathbf{H}| = \eta_0 = 377\,\Omega$. This is the intrinsic impedance of free space. It is a fixed property of the vacuum — not a circuit element, not a material property, but the ratio at which the electromagnetic field must hold when propagating freely.

When a wave enters a material with $\varepsilon_r > 1$ (denser dielectric), the impedance drops below 377 Ω. This mismatch between air and substrate causes reflections at interfaces — the origin of antenna ground-plane effects, PCB transmission line impedance changes, and radar target reflectivity.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Derivation in source-free region

Start with Maxwell's equations in a source-free, linear, isotropic region ($\mathbf{J} = 0$, $\rho = 0$, $\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon\mathbf{E}$, $\mathbf{B} = \mu\mathbf{H}$):

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\mu \frac{\partial \mathbf{H}}{\partial t}$$

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \varepsilon \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$$

Take the curl of the first equation:

$$\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = -\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \times \mathbf{H})$$

Substitute the second equation into the right side:

$$\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = -\mu\varepsilon \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$

Apply the vector identity $\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{E}$. In a source-free region, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0$:

$$\boxed{\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = \mu\varepsilon \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}}$$

An identical derivation gives the same equation for $\mathbf{H}$.

### Phase velocity

Comparing with the standard wave equation $\nabla^2 f = (1/v^2)\partial^2 f/\partial t^2$:

$$v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon}} = \frac{c}{\sqrt{\mu_r \varepsilon_r}}$$

- Free space: $v = c = 2.998 \times 10^8$ m/s
- FR4 substrate ($\varepsilon_r = 4.4$, $\mu_r = 1$): $v = c/\sqrt{4.4} = 0.477c$
- RG-58 coax ($\varepsilon_r = 2.3$): $v = c/\sqrt{2.3} = 0.659c$

The velocity factor (VF) of a transmission line is $v/c = 1/\sqrt{\varepsilon_r}$ for non-magnetic materials — directly from the wave equation.

### Plane wave solution

For a uniform plane wave traveling in the $+z$ direction with $\mathbf{E}$ polarized in $\hat{x}$:

$$\mathbf{E}(z,t) = E_0 \cos(\omega t - kz)\,\hat{x}$$

or in complex phasor notation:

$$\tilde{\mathbf{E}}(z) = E_0 e^{-jkz}\,\hat{x}$$

where the wave number (propagation constant in lossless media):

$$k = \frac{\omega}{v} = \omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon} = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$$

The associated magnetic field from Faraday's law:

$$\tilde{\mathbf{H}}(z) = \frac{E_0}{\eta} e^{-jkz}\,\hat{y}, \quad \eta = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\varepsilon}}$$

### Helmholtz equation (frequency domain)

Substituting $e^{j\omega t}$ time dependence, $\partial/\partial t \to j\omega$, the wave equation becomes:

$$\nabla^2 \tilde{\mathbf{E}} + k^2 \tilde{\mathbf{E}} = 0$$

This is the Helmholtz equation — the time-independent wave equation. All frequency-domain antenna and circuit analysis starts here. The parameter $k$ is the free-space wave number; in lossy media it becomes the complex propagation constant $\gamma = \alpha + j\beta$, where $\alpha$ is the attenuation constant (Np/m) and $\beta$ is the phase constant (rad/m).

### Propagation constant in lossy media

Replace $\varepsilon$ with the complex permittivity $\varepsilon_c = \varepsilon(1 - j\tan\delta)$ where $\tan\delta$ is the loss tangent. The propagation constant becomes complex:

$$\gamma = j\omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon_c} = \alpha + j\beta$$

For a wave propagating in $+z$: $e^{-\gamma z} = e^{-\alpha z} e^{-j\beta z}$

- $e^{-\alpha z}$: amplitude decays exponentially with distance
- $e^{-j\beta z}$: phase advances with distance

The skin depth $\delta_s = 1/\alpha$ in a good conductor. The 1/e amplitude point.

### Wave impedance

$$\eta = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\varepsilon}} = \eta_0\sqrt{\frac{\mu_r}{\varepsilon_r}}, \quad \eta_0 = 376.73\,\Omega \approx 377\,\Omega$$

In a lossy medium:

$$\eta_c = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\varepsilon_c}} = \eta_0\sqrt{\frac{\mu_r}{\varepsilon_r(1 - j\tan\delta)}}$$

The impedance becomes complex: the phase difference between $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ is no longer zero.

### Power flow: Poynting vector

The instantaneous power flow density (W/m²):

$$\mathbf{S}(t) = \mathbf{E}(t) \times \mathbf{H}(t)$$

Time-averaged power density (what instruments measure):

$$\langle \mathbf{S} \rangle = \frac{1}{2}\,\text{Re}(\tilde{\mathbf{E}} \times \tilde{\mathbf{H}}^*) = \frac{|E_0|^2}{2\eta}\,\hat{z}$$

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

### Example 1: Wavelength across frequency bands

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{c}{f\sqrt{\mu_r\varepsilon_r}}$$

| Band | Frequency | Free-space $\lambda$ | In FR4 ($\varepsilon_r = 4.4$) |
|------|-----------|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| WiFi 2.4G | 2.4 GHz | 12.5 cm | 5.96 cm |
| WiFi 5G | 5.8 GHz | 5.17 cm | 2.47 cm |
| mmWave | 28 GHz | 10.7 mm | 5.1 mm |
| V-band | 60 GHz | 5.0 mm | 2.4 mm |

A half-wave dipole at 2.4 GHz is 6.25 cm long in free space. In FR4, the same resonant structure fits in 2.98 cm — which is why PCB antenna dimensions are strongly affected by substrate permittivity.

### Example 2: Power density budget for a link

A 2.4 GHz access point transmits 100 mW EIRP (20 dBm). At 10 m range:

Received power density (isotropic spreading):

$$S = \frac{P_t}{4\pi r^2} = \frac{0.1}{4\pi \times 100} \approx 79.6\,\mu\text{W/m}^2$$

Electric field amplitude:

$$|E| = \sqrt{2\eta_0 S} = \sqrt{2 \times 377 \times 79.6 \times 10^{-6}} \approx 245\,\text{mV/m}$$

This is the field strength your device antenna sees. Regulatory emission limits (e.g., FCC Part 15) are typically expressed as field strength (V/m at a reference distance) — directly from this power-to-field conversion.

### Example 3: Propagation in lossy media — seawater

Seawater: $\varepsilon_r \approx 81$, $\sigma \approx 4$ S/m at 1 GHz.

Loss tangent: $\tan\delta = \sigma/(\omega\varepsilon) = 4/(2\pi \times 10^9 \times 81 \times 8.854 \times 10^{-12}) \approx 0.89$

This is a high-loss medium (tan δ close to 1). The skin depth:

$$\delta_s = \frac{1}{\alpha} \approx \sqrt{\frac{2}{\omega\mu\sigma}} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{2\pi \times 10^9 \times 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 4}} \approx 8.0\,\text{cm}$$

After just 8 cm, the wave amplitude drops to 37% (1/e). After one meter, the signal is attenuated by $e^{-12.5} \approx -54$ dB. Submarine communication at 1 GHz is impossible — this is why the US Navy uses extremely low frequency (ELF, 3–30 Hz) transmitters where skin depth in seawater is tens of meters.

### Example 4: Transmission line velocity factor and time delay

RG-58 coaxial cable: $\varepsilon_r = 2.3$ (polyethylene dielectric), $\mu_r = 1$.

Phase velocity: $v = c/\sqrt{2.3} = 0.659c$

Velocity factor: VF = 65.9%

Time delay per meter: $t_d = 1/v = \sqrt{2.3}/c = 5.08$ ns/m

For a 1.5 m cable feeding a 2.4 GHz antenna:

Phase delay: $\phi = \omega t_d L = 2\pi \times 2.4 \times 10^9 \times 5.08 \times 10^{-9} \times 1.5 \approx 115\,\text{rad} = 6,590°$

This is 18.3 full wavelengths of phase shift. At microwave frequencies, even small variations in cable length (millimeters) cause meaningful phase shifts. This is why phased array antenna calibration requires precise physical cable length matching or electronic delay compensation.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing phase velocity and group velocity.** Phase velocity $v_p = \omega/k$ is the speed of constant-phase surfaces — it can exceed $c$ in anomalous dispersion regions, but this does not violate relativity. Group velocity $v_g = d\omega/dk$ is the speed of energy transport and signal envelopes — this is always $\leq c$. In waveguides, $v_p > c$ and $v_g < c$, with $v_p v_g = c^2$.

2. **Forgetting that wavelength changes in a medium.** The frequency $f$ stays the same when a wave enters a dielectric — the source still oscillates at the same rate. What changes is the wavelength: $\lambda = v/f = \lambda_0/\sqrt{\varepsilon_r}$. This is why a patch antenna designed for free space has the wrong resonant frequency when placed on a PCB.

3. **Mixing up wave impedance $\eta$ and transmission line impedance $Z_0$.** Wave impedance $\eta = \sqrt{\mu/\varepsilon}$ is a property of the medium. Transmission line $Z_0$ depends on the geometry (conductor spacing, radius ratio) as well as the medium. For a coaxial line, $Z_0 = (60/\sqrt{\varepsilon_r})\ln(b/a)$ — same $\varepsilon_r$ but different because of geometry.

4. **Ignoring loss tangent at high frequencies.** A substrate like Rogers 4003C has $\tan\delta = 0.0027$ at 10 GHz. Over a 10 cm trace, the attenuation is $\alpha L \approx 0.15$ dB/cm × 10 cm = 1.5 dB — significant. Always check the substrate loss tangent at the operating frequency; data sheets often specify it at 1 MHz where it is much smaller.

5. **Assuming plane wave behavior in the near field.** The plane wave solution is valid only in the far field ($r \gg \lambda$). Near an antenna or a circuit element, the fields have reactive components (evanescent fields, stored energy) that are not described by the propagating wave solution. The ratio $E/H \neq 377\,\Omega$ in the near field — near a small electric dipole, $E$ dominates; near a small magnetic dipole (loop), $H$ dominates.
