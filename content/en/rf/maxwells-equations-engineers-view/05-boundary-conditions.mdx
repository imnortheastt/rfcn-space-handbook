## Why boundary conditions?

Whenever electromagnetic waves hit a material interface — antenna to air, substrate to metal, waveguide wall — the fields must satisfy boundary conditions. These conditions determine reflection, transmission, and surface currents.

Every RF problem with real materials involves boundary conditions.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Fields can't jump arbitrarily at an interface — physics imposes constraints. Some field components must be continuous (tangential E); others can jump by the amount of surface charge or current.

Think of it like water flow: velocity tangent to a wall is continuous, but pressure normal to the wall determines the force.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Tangential electric field

$$E_{t1} = E_{t2}$$

Tangential E is continuous across any interface.

From Faraday's law: if E_t jumped, there would be infinite $\partial B/\partial t$ in zero thickness.

### Normal electric displacement

$$D_{n1} - D_{n2} = \sigma_s$$

Normal D jumps by surface charge density.

From Gauss's law applied to thin pillbox.

### Tangential magnetic field

$$H_{t1} - H_{t2} = K$$

Tangential H jumps by surface current density K (A/m).

From Ampere's law applied to thin rectangular loop.

### Normal magnetic flux

$$B_{n1} = B_{n2}$$

Normal B is continuous (no magnetic charges).

### Summary table

| Component | Condition |
|-----------|-----------|
| $E_t$ | Continuous |
| $D_n$ | Jumps by $\sigma_s$ |
| $H_t$ | Jumps by $K$ |
| $B_n$ | Continuous |

### Perfect conductor

Inside: E = 0, H = 0.

At surface:
- $E_t = 0$ (tangential E vanishes)
- $H_t = K$ (surface current supports tangential H)
- $D_n = \sigma_s$ (surface charge supports normal D)
- $B_n = 0$ (no normal B)

### Dielectric interface (no charges or currents)

$$\frac{\tan\theta_1}{\tan\theta_2} = \frac{\epsilon_1}{\epsilon_2}$$

Field lines bend at dielectric boundaries.

### Reflection coefficient

For normal incidence from medium 1 to medium 2:

$$\Gamma = \frac{\eta_2 - \eta_1}{\eta_2 + \eta_1}$$

where $\eta = \sqrt{\mu/\epsilon}$.

Transmission coefficient:
$$T = 1 + \Gamma = \frac{2\eta_2}{\eta_2 + \eta_1}$$

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Air-to-conductor**

Wave in air hits perfect conductor.

$\eta_{air} = 377\ \Omega$, $\eta_{conductor} = 0$.

$\Gamma = (0 - 377)/(0 + 377) = -1$.

Total reflection, 180° phase shift. Standing wave forms.

**Example: Air-to-dielectric**

Wave from air ($\epsilon_r = 1$) to glass ($\epsilon_r = 4$).

$\eta_{air} = 377\ \Omega$, $\eta_{glass} = 377/\sqrt{4} = 188.5\ \Omega$.

$\Gamma = (188.5 - 377)/(188.5 + 377) = -0.33$.

33% power reflected, 67% transmitted.

**Example: Microstrip line**

Metal trace on dielectric substrate.

At metal surface: $E_t = 0$, surface current K flows.

At dielectric interface: E and H continuous (no surface charges).

Fields exist both above and below trace — the effective $\epsilon_r$ is between 1 and substrate $\epsilon_r$.

**Example: Waveguide walls**

Metal walls force $E_t = 0$.

Only certain field patterns (modes) satisfy this everywhere.

TE and TM modes have different cutoff frequencies.

**Example: Antenna on ground plane**

Image theory: conductor acts as mirror.

Horizontal dipole at height h has image at depth h with opposite current.

Pattern doubles in forward hemisphere.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing E and D.** E_t is continuous; D_n has jump. Don't mix up tangential and normal, E and D.

2. **Ignoring surface currents.** On good conductors, H_t is supported by surface current K. This current is real and causes loss.

3. **Assuming continuity everywhere.** Fields are continuous in homogeneous regions. At interfaces, apply boundary conditions explicitly.

4. **Forgetting finite conductivity.** Real metals have skin depth. Fields penetrate slightly, surface current has thickness, and there's loss.

5. **Confusing reflection coefficient for power vs. amplitude.** $\Gamma$ is amplitude ratio. Power reflection is $|\Gamma|^2$.

6. **Wrong sign convention.** Different books use different conventions for $\Gamma$. Be consistent within your analysis.

</section>
