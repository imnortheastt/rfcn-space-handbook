## Why boundary conditions?

Every real RF system contains material interfaces: metal traces on dielectric substrates, connector launches from coax to PCB, antenna radiating into air, waveguide walls that confine modes. At every interface, the electromagnetic field cannot behave arbitrarily — Maxwell's equations impose four specific conditions on how field components cross the boundary.

These conditions are not additional physics. They are Maxwell's equations applied to a limit: an infinitely thin surface. They determine reflection coefficients, transmission coefficients, standing wave ratios, and surface currents that cause conductor losses. Without them, you cannot analyze a transmission line, design a matching network, or explain why a microstrip antenna has its resonant frequency.

Boundary conditions are where Maxwell's abstract equations become the practical design rules of RF engineering.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### Why some components are continuous

Imagine Faraday's law applied to a tiny rectangular loop straddling an interface. The loop has width $\Delta w$ (crossing the boundary) and length $\Delta l$ (parallel to the surface). As the loop thickness $\Delta w \to 0$:

$$\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

The magnetic flux $\Phi_B$ through the loop goes to zero (area → 0) unless $\mathbf{B}$ is infinitely large, which is unphysical. So the circulation integral must go to zero. The only terms that survive are the tangential $\mathbf{E}$ contributions on each side of the interface:

$$E_{t1}\,\Delta l - E_{t2}\,\Delta l = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad E_{t1} = E_{t2}$$

Tangential $\mathbf{E}$ must be continuous. A jump in tangential $\mathbf{E}$ would require an infinite time-varying $\mathbf{B}$ — unphysical.

Similarly, applying Gauss's law for $\mathbf{B}$ to a thin pillbox gives $B_{n1} = B_{n2}$: normal $\mathbf{B}$ is always continuous because there are no magnetic charges to create a jump.

### Why some components can jump

Normal $\mathbf{D}$ can jump — because real electric surface charges $\sigma_s$ exist. The pillbox version of Gauss's law gives:

$$D_{n1} - D_{n2} = \sigma_s$$

On a conductor surface, charges redistribute freely. The surface charge density $\sigma_s$ is whatever value is needed to make $\mathbf{E} = 0$ inside the conductor.

Tangential $\mathbf{H}$ can jump — because real surface currents $\mathbf{K}$ (A/m) exist. At a perfect conductor surface, the surface current $\mathbf{K}$ supports the magnetic field outside while $\mathbf{H} = 0$ inside.

### A useful mnemonic

"Tangential E and normal B are always continuous. Everything else can jump if surface sources are present."

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### The four boundary conditions

| Component | General condition | At perfect conductor | At dielectric interface (no sources) |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Tangential $\mathbf{E}$ | $E_{t1} = E_{t2}$ | $E_{t} = 0$ | $E_{t1} = E_{t2}$ |
| Normal $\mathbf{D}$ | $D_{n1} - D_{n2} = \sigma_s$ | $D_{n} = \sigma_s$ | $D_{n1} = D_{n2}$ |
| Tangential $\mathbf{H}$ | $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times (\mathbf{H}_1 - \mathbf{H}_2) = \mathbf{K}$ | $H_{t} = K$ | $H_{t1} = H_{t2}$ |
| Normal $\mathbf{B}$ | $B_{n1} = B_{n2}$ | $B_{n} = 0$ | $B_{n1} = B_{n2}$ |

$\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ points from medium 2 into medium 1. $\sigma_s$ is surface charge density (C/m²). $\mathbf{K}$ is surface current density (A/m).

### Derivation: tangential E (Faraday's law)

Apply Faraday's law to a thin rectangular loop of height $\delta \to 0$ and length $\Delta l$ straddling the interface:

$$\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = E_{t1}\Delta l - E_{t2}\Delta l + O(\delta) = -\frac{d}{dt}\iint_S \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

Right side: $|B|\,\Delta l\,\delta \to 0$ as $\delta \to 0$. Left side must also vanish:

$$E_{t1} = E_{t2}$$

### Derivation: normal D (Gauss's law)

Apply Gauss's law to a thin pillbox of height $\delta \to 0$ and cross-section area $\Delta A$:

$$\oint \mathbf{D}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = (D_{n1} - D_{n2})\Delta A = Q_\text{enc} = \sigma_s \Delta A$$

$$D_{n1} - D_{n2} = \sigma_s$$

### Reflection and transmission at normal incidence

For a plane wave incident normally from medium 1 (impedance $\eta_1$) onto medium 2 (impedance $\eta_2$):

Continuity of tangential $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ at the interface gives:

$$\Gamma = \frac{\eta_2 - \eta_1}{\eta_2 + \eta_1}$$

$$\tau = \frac{2\eta_2}{\eta_2 + \eta_1} = 1 + \Gamma$$

- $\Gamma$ = voltage reflection coefficient (amplitude ratio, can be negative)
- $\tau$ = voltage transmission coefficient
- Power reflection: $|\Gamma|^2$
- Power transmission: $1 - |\Gamma|^2 = \tau^2 \eta_1/\eta_2$

### Field bending at dielectric interface

At an interface between two dielectrics with no free charges ($\sigma_s = 0$, $\mathbf{K} = 0$):

- Tangential $\mathbf{E}$ is continuous: $E_{t1} = E_{t2}$
- Normal $\mathbf{D}$ is continuous: $\varepsilon_1 E_{n1} = \varepsilon_2 E_{n2}$

If $\varepsilon_2 > \varepsilon_1$, the normal component is compressed in medium 2 relative to medium 1. The field lines bend toward the normal — the angle with the normal decreases:

$$\frac{\tan\theta_1}{\tan\theta_2} = \frac{\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_2}$$

This is the electromagnetic analog of Snell's law.

### Perfect electric conductor (PEC) boundary

Inside a PEC: $\mathbf{E} = 0$, $\mathbf{H} = 0$. At the surface (medium 1 = PEC, medium 2 = outside):

$$\mathbf{E}_t = 0 \quad \text{(no tangential E at surface)}$$

$$\mathbf{D}_n = \sigma_s \quad \text{(normal D equals surface charge)}$$

$$\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{K} \quad \text{(surface current supports H outside)}$$

$$\mathbf{B}_n = 0 \quad \text{(no normal B at surface)}$$

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

### Example 1: Total reflection at a perfect conductor

A plane wave in air ($\eta_1 = 377\,\Omega$) hits a perfect conductor ($\eta_2 = 0$).

$$\Gamma = \frac{0 - 377}{0 + 377} = -1$$

$|\Gamma| = 1$: 100% of power is reflected. The negative sign means the reflected $\mathbf{E}$ is 180° out of phase with the incident $\mathbf{E}$ at the surface — required to make $E_t = 0$ at the conductor.

The superposition of incident and reflected waves creates a standing wave. Nodes of $\mathbf{E}$ (where $\mathbf{E} = 0$) occur at the surface and every $\lambda/2$ away. Anti-nodes of $\mathbf{E}$ occur at $\lambda/4$ from the surface.

This is the physics of a shorted transmission line, a cavity resonator, and a dipole antenna driven against a ground plane.

### Example 2: Air-to-FR4 dielectric interface

A wave in air ($\varepsilon_r = 1$, $\eta_1 = 377\,\Omega$) hits a bare FR4 substrate ($\varepsilon_r = 4.4$, $\mu_r = 1$).

$$\eta_2 = \frac{377}{\sqrt{4.4}} \approx 179.6\,\Omega$$

$$\Gamma = \frac{179.6 - 377}{179.6 + 377} = \frac{-197.4}{556.6} \approx -0.355$$

Power reflection: $|\Gamma|^2 = 0.126$ → 12.6% of incident power reflected.

Power transmission: $1 - 0.126 = 87.4$%.

This 12.6% reflection loss occurs at every air-substrate interface in a PCB antenna. A patch antenna on FR4 has lower radiation efficiency partly because of this mismatch (plus substrate loss). Rogers substrates with lower $\varepsilon_r$ (e.g., 2.2) have $\eta_2 = 254\,\Omega$ → $\Gamma = -0.195$ → only 3.8% reflected.

### Example 3: Microstrip effective permittivity from boundary conditions

A microstrip line has a metal trace on top of FR4, with air above. The fields exist in both media — partly in FR4 (below the trace) and partly in air (above). Neither boundary condition is fully applied: the wave "sees" an average medium.

The effective permittivity is approximately:

$$\varepsilon_{eff} = \frac{\varepsilon_r + 1}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon_r - 1}{2}\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 12\,h/w}}$$

where $h$ is the substrate thickness and $w$ is the trace width. For FR4 ($\varepsilon_r = 4.4$) and a 50 Ω line where $w/h \approx 2$:

$$\varepsilon_{eff} \approx \frac{5.4}{2} + \frac{3.4}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+6}} \approx 2.7 + 0.64 \approx 3.34$$

Phase velocity: $v = c/\sqrt{3.34} = 0.547c$. This is why microstrip has a velocity factor of ~55%, not 48% (which you would get for a fully embedded line in FR4). The boundary conditions at the air-dielectric interface directly determine the wave speed.

### Example 4: Waveguide mode cutoff from boundary conditions

A rectangular waveguide has perfectly conducting walls at $x = 0$, $x = a$, $y = 0$, $y = b$.

Boundary condition: $E_t = 0$ at all conductor surfaces.

For the TE$_{10}$ mode (dominant mode), $E_y$ is the only field component. Applying $E_y = 0$ at $x = 0$ and $x = a$:

$$E_y(x) = E_0 \sin\left(\frac{\pi x}{a}\right)$$

This $\sin(\pi x/a)$ is the only function that satisfies $E_y = 0$ at both walls. The wave number in $x$ is fixed: $k_x = \pi/a$.

For propagation in $z$ to exist, we need $k_z^2 = k^2 - k_x^2 > 0$:

$$k > \frac{\pi}{a} \quad \Rightarrow \quad f > f_c = \frac{c}{2a}$$

For standard WR-90 waveguide ($a = 22.86$ mm): $f_c = c/(2 \times 0.02286) = 6.56$ GHz. Below this, no propagation — purely evanescent fields. This is the boundary condition determining waveguide cutoff.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Mixing up which component is continuous.** Memory tool: tangential **E** and normal **B** are always continuous (they come from the "curl" equations with no source terms). Normal **D** and tangential **H** can jump (they come from "curl" equations where sources — charges and currents — appear). One sentence: "E_t and B_n are holy; D_n and H_t can jump."

2. **Confusing voltage reflection coefficient and power reflection coefficient.** $\Gamma$ is the amplitude (voltage) reflection coefficient. Reflected power is $|\Gamma|^2$. Return loss in dB = $-20\log_{10}|\Gamma|$, not $-10\log_{10}|\Gamma|$. This factor-of-2 mistake in dB is extremely common.

3. **Forgetting that real conductors have finite conductivity.** A PEC has $E_t = 0$ exactly. A real conductor (copper, aluminum) has small but nonzero $E_t$ at its surface, which drives the surface current and causes resistive loss. This is why conductor Q in a cavity resonator is finite, and why transmission line attenuation increases with frequency due to increasing surface resistance $R_s = 1/(\sigma\delta_s) \propto \sqrt{f}$.

4. **Applying the dielectric interface Snell-like formula to metals.** The angle formula $\tan\theta_1/\tan\theta_2 = \varepsilon_1/\varepsilon_2$ applies to static or quasi-static fields at lossless dielectric interfaces. For lossy materials or for wave propagation (not just field direction), use the full Fresnel equations with complex $\varepsilon$.

5. **Ignoring the effective permittivity for planar structures.** When fields exist in two dielectric regions (air and substrate in microstrip), you cannot use just the substrate $\varepsilon_r$ or just $\varepsilon_r = 1$. The actual phase velocity is determined by the weighted average set by the boundary conditions at the dielectric interface. Use $\varepsilon_{eff}$ formulas from microwave CAD references for accurate results.

</section>
