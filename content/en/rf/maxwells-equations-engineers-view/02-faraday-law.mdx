## Why Faraday's law?

Faraday's law is the engine of electromagnetic induction — and induction is the mechanism behind roughly half of all electromagnetic phenomena you encounter in RF work. Without it, there would be no transformers, no inductors, no loop antennas, no receive coils in NMR/MRI, and no electromagnetic wave propagation.

More specifically, Faraday's law is the first of two "coupling" equations in Maxwell's system: it says that a time-varying magnetic field creates an electric field with curl — a circulating E-field that drives currents around loops. The second coupling equation (Ampere-Maxwell) says the reverse: a time-varying electric field creates a magnetic field. Together, they create the self-sustaining oscillation we call an electromagnetic wave.

For the RF engineer: Faraday's law explains inductor behavior ($V = L\,dI/dt$), transformer coupling, why loop antennas are sensitive to $\mathbf{H}$ not $\mathbf{E}$, why probe lead placement matters, and why the skin effect drives current to the conductor surface at high frequencies.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### The time-varying B acts like a pump

Imagine you freeze time and look at a snapshot of a magnetic field. Static — nothing interesting happens to charge carriers. Now allow the field to change. The moment $\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t \neq 0$, an electric field appears around the region where $\mathbf{B}$ is changing. This E-field forms closed loops — it has curl. Place a wire loop in this region and the circulating E-field pushes charges around the loop, producing a current. No wire? The E-field still exists; it just has nothing to push.

The faster $\mathbf{B}$ changes, the stronger the induced E-field. This is why the EMF of an inductor scales with $dI/dt$ (faster current change = faster flux change = larger back-EMF), and why all induction effects grow with frequency.

### Lenz's law: nature resists change

The minus sign in $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi_B/dt$ is Lenz's law in mathematical form. The induced EMF always opposes the change that caused it. If the magnetic flux through a loop is increasing, the induced current creates a magnetic field opposing the increase. If the flux is decreasing, the induced current tries to maintain it.

This self-opposing behavior is not optional — it is required by energy conservation. If induced currents aided the change instead of opposing it, you would get runaway amplification: unlimited energy from nothing.

In RF, Lenz's law is why inductors resist sudden changes in current (the back-EMF opposes $dI/dt$), why eddy currents form in conducting material in the presence of time-varying fields (opposing the field change), and why high-permeability cores at high frequencies develop significant losses.

### The EMF is a closed-loop property

A key subtlety: the EMF $\mathcal{E} = \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l}$ is a property of a closed path, not a property of two endpoints. In static fields, $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = 0$ (conservative field) and you can assign a unique voltage to every point. When $\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t \neq 0$, the loop integral is nonzero and "voltage" between two points becomes path-dependent.

This is what makes RF circuit analysis with Faraday's law surprising: two probes touching the same nodes but forming different loop areas will read different voltages. This is correct physics, not a measurement error. The correct statement is always the full loop integral, not a point-to-point potential difference.

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Integral form

$$\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -\frac{d}{dt}\iint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

The EMF around any closed loop $C$ equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through any surface $S$ bounded by $C$.

- $\Phi_B = \iint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$ = magnetic flux through loop (Wb)
- Negative sign enforces Lenz's law
- For a coil with $N$ turns: $\mathcal{E} = -N\,d\Phi_B/dt$

### Differential form

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$

The curl of $\mathbf{E}$ equals the negative time derivative of $\mathbf{B}$ at every point. This is the local, instantaneous form of Faraday's law. It holds in free space, in dielectrics, in conductors, everywhere.

### Phasor domain (sinusoidal steady state)

For time dependence $e^{j\omega t}$, replace $\partial/\partial t \to j\omega$:

$$\nabla \times \tilde{\mathbf{E}} = -j\omega \tilde{\mathbf{B}} = -j\omega\mu \tilde{\mathbf{H}}$$

The phasor form is essential for antenna analysis, transmission line theory, and microwave circuit design. The factor $j\omega$ introduces a 90° phase shift and linear frequency scaling — this is why inductive reactance is $X_L = \omega L$.

### EMF scaling

For a loop of area $A$ and $N$ turns in a uniform sinusoidal field $\mathbf{B} = B_0\cos(\omega t)\hat{\mathbf{z}}$:

$$\Phi_B = B_0 A \cos(\omega t)$$

$$\mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = N B_0 \omega A \sin(\omega t)$$

Peak EMF: $\mathcal{E}_\text{peak} = N B_0 \omega A$

EMF is proportional to:
- $N$ — number of turns
- $B_0$ — field amplitude
- $\omega$ — angular frequency (EMF doubles for each octave)
- $A$ — loop area

### Motional EMF (moving conductor in static B)

When a conductor moves with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ through a static magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$:

$$\mathcal{E} = \int_C (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

This is the same phenomenon from a different reference frame. In a rotating generator, the wire loop moves through a static $\mathbf{B}$ — the motional EMF drives the output voltage.

### Boundary condition from Faraday's law

Apply Faraday's law to a thin rectangular loop spanning a material interface, then take the limit as loop height → 0:

$$E_{t1} = E_{t2}$$

Tangential $\mathbf{E}$ is continuous across any interface. This is the fundamental reason why reflected and transmitted waves at a boundary have the same tangential E-field.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

### Example 1: Inductor voltage and back-EMF

An RF choke has inductance $L = 100$ nH. Current through it: $i(t) = I_0\sin(\omega t)$ with $I_0 = 100$ mA and $f = 100$ MHz.

Flux linkage: $\Lambda = Li$.

By Faraday's law: $v = -d\Lambda/dt = -L\,di/dt$.

$$v(t) = -L \cdot I_0\omega\cos(\omega t) = -100 \times 10^{-9} \times 0.1 \times 2\pi \times 10^8 \cos(\omega t)$$

$$v_\text{peak} = 2\pi \approx 6.28 \text{ V}$$

The 100 nH choke creates a 6.28 V back-EMF for a 100 mA signal at 100 MHz. Its inductive reactance is $X_L = \omega L = 2\pi \times 10^8 \times 10^{-7} \approx 62.8\,\Omega$.

This is directly Faraday's law: $V = j\omega L I$ in phasor form, derived from $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t$ and the definition of inductance.

### Example 2: Loop antenna sensitivity and design trade-offs

A circular loop antenna with $N = 5$ turns and radius $r = 3$ cm receives an AM signal at $f = 1$ MHz with $B_0 = 10$ pT.

Loop area: $A = \pi r^2 = \pi \times (0.03)^2 = 2.83 \times 10^{-3}$ m²

Peak EMF:

$$\mathcal{E}_\text{peak} = N B_0 \omega A = 5 \times 10^{-11} \times 2\pi \times 10^6 \times 2.83 \times 10^{-3}$$

$$\mathcal{E}_\text{peak} \approx 0.89\,\mu\text{V}$$

**Design insight:** To double the received signal, you can:
- Double $N$ (5 → 10 turns): same result, more wire, more series resistance.
- Double the area by increasing radius by $\sqrt{2}$ (3 → 4.24 cm): often preferable.
- Double the frequency: works for wideband antennas but not tunable loops.

In practice, ferrite cores are used to boost the effective permeability, increasing the effective area without increasing physical size. This is the "loopstick" antenna in portable AM radios.

### Example 3: Transformer EMF ratio

A transformer with $N_1 = 200$ primary turns and $N_2 = 40$ secondary turns. Core cross-section $A_c = 4$ cm², peak flux density $B_0 = 1$ T at $f = 50$ Hz.

Peak flux: $\Phi_0 = B_0 A_c = 1 \times 4 \times 10^{-4} = 4 \times 10^{-4}$ Wb

Primary EMF:

$$\mathcal{E}_1 = N_1 \omega \Phi_0 = 200 \times 2\pi \times 50 \times 4 \times 10^{-4} = 25.1 \text{ V peak}$$

Secondary EMF:

$$\mathcal{E}_2 = \frac{N_2}{N_1} \mathcal{E}_1 = \frac{40}{200} \times 25.1 = 5.0 \text{ V peak}$$

The EMF ratio equals the turns ratio. This is the transformer equation, derived entirely from Faraday's law applied to two coils sharing the same magnetic flux path.

### Example 4: Skin effect origin

Inside a good conductor carrying AC current, a time-varying current creates a time-varying $\mathbf{B}$ (Ampere's law). That time-varying $\mathbf{B}$ creates a curl in $\mathbf{E}$ (Faraday's law). The induced $\mathbf{E}$ opposes the original current inside the conductor.

The net effect: current is pushed toward the surface. The depth at which current density falls to $1/e$ of the surface value is the skin depth:

$$\delta = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\omega\mu\sigma}}$$

At 1 GHz for copper ($\sigma = 5.8 \times 10^7$ S/m, $\mu \approx \mu_0$):

$$\delta = \sqrt{\frac{2}{2\pi \times 10^9 \times 4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 5.8 \times 10^7}} \approx 2.1\,\mu\text{m}$$

At 1 GHz, all current flows within 2 μm of the surface. A 1 mm diameter copper wire behaves as a thin hollow tube at GHz frequencies. This is why RF conductors must be plated (silver or gold) — only the outer skin layer matters.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Forgetting the minus sign (Lenz's law).** The induced EMF opposes the flux change. Getting the sign wrong gives a current that aids the change — which would violate energy conservation. In circuit analysis, the back-EMF of an inductor ($V = -L\,dI/dt$) has the minus sign precisely because of Faraday's law.

2. **Confusing EMF with voltage.** EMF is the closed-loop line integral of $\mathbf{E}$. In a resistive loop, the EMF drives a current through the resistance. The "voltage" across each element of the loop sums to the EMF. In a purely reactive loop (superconductor), the EMF is balanced by the back-EMF of the induced current's own inductance — no net current, just flux conservation.

3. **Assuming path-independent voltage near inductors.** When $\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t \neq 0$, the electric field has curl and $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} \neq 0$. "Voltage" is path-dependent. Probe placement near inductors, transformers, and RF traces can give different readings for different lead routing — all are correct.

4. **Applying Faraday's law to open segments.** The EMF requires a closed loop. For an open circuit, the induced EMF equals the open-circuit voltage at the terminals, but only when the external path is defined. Always close the loop mentally before applying Faraday's law.

5. **Ignoring frequency scaling.** Induced EMF scales with $\omega$. A sensor calibrated at 1 kHz reads 1000× higher at 1 MHz (if it's wideband). A ferrite core that works at 100 kHz may be useless at 100 MHz due to core loss. Always check frequency range.

</section>
