<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why Faraday's law?

Faraday's law explains how changing magnetic fields create electric fields. Without it, there would be no transformers, no radio reception, no electromagnetic waves. For RF, this law is half of the wave-propagation mechanism — time-varying B creates E.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A changing magnetic field is like a pump that pushes electric charges around. The faster B changes, the harder it pushes. This "push" is an induced electric field that exists even without charges.

Loops of wire convert this electric field into measurable voltage — that's how receive antennas work.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Integral form

$$\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

The EMF around any closed loop equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through it.

- $\Phi_B = \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$ = magnetic flux
- Negative sign = Lenz's law (opposes change)

### Differential form

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$

The curl of E is the negative time derivative of B.

### EMF and voltage

$$\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

For a coil with N turns:
$$\mathcal{E} = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

### Sinusoidal steady state

For $B = B_0 \sin(\omega t)$:

$$\mathcal{E} = -\omega B_0 A \cos(\omega t)$$

- EMF amplitude ∝ frequency
- EMF amplitude ∝ loop area
- EMF amplitude ∝ field strength

### Motional EMF

Moving conductor in static B field:

$$\mathcal{E} = \int (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

Same physics, different perspective.

### Boundary condition

Tangential E is continuous across any interface:

$$E_{t1} = E_{t2}$$

This holds even at conductor surfaces (E = 0 inside good conductors).

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Loop antenna**

Circular loop, radius 10 cm, 10 turns.

Incident field: $B = 1\ \mu\text{T}$ at 1 MHz.

Loop area: $A = \pi \times 0.1^2 = 0.0314$ m².

EMF: $\mathcal{E} = \omega N B A = 2\pi \times 10^6 \times 10 \times 10^{-6} \times 0.0314$

$\mathcal{E} = 1.97$ mV peak.

**Example: Transformer coupling**

Primary: 100 turns, secondary: 10 turns, core flux peak 1 mT at 50 Hz.

Primary EMF: $\mathcal{E}_1 = \omega N_1 \Phi = 2\pi \times 50 \times 100 \times 10^{-3} = 31.4$ V.

Secondary: $\mathcal{E}_2 = \mathcal{E}_1 \times (N_2/N_1) = 3.14$ V.

**Example: Why higher frequency = smaller inductors**

Inductor EMF: $V = L \frac{dI}{dt} = j\omega L I$.

At higher frequency, same voltage needs less L (or same L gives more reactance).

RF inductors: nanohenries. Power inductors: millihenries.

**Example: Magnetic field probe**

Small loop at end of coax measures local B field.

EMF ∝ $\omega \cdot A \cdot B$ → calibration depends on frequency.

Near-field probes measure $\partial B/\partial t$, not B directly.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Forgetting the negative sign.** Lenz's law: induced EMF opposes the change. Get the sign wrong and your circuit analysis fails.

2. **Confusing EMF with voltage.** EMF is the closed-loop integral of E. In a loop with resistance, some EMF drops across R. In a perfect conductor, induced E is balanced by electrostatic E.

3. **Applying to partial loops.** Faraday's law applies to closed loops. Open-circuit voltage needs a complete path to define.

4. **Ignoring frequency dependence.** Induced EMF scales with ω. A sensor designed for 1 kHz gives 1000× more voltage at 1 MHz (if it can respond).

5. **Assuming B is uniform.** Real fields have spatial variation. Integrate properly, or use average field only for small loops.

</section>
