<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why Ampere's law?

Ampere's law connects currents to magnetic fields. Maxwell's genius was adding the displacement current — changing E also creates B. This correction completed electromagnetism and predicted electromagnetic waves.

For RF, the displacement current explains how signals cross capacitor gaps and how antennas radiate.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Current creates magnetic field circles around the wire. But what happens in a capacitor where no charges flow through the gap? Maxwell realized that changing E acts like a current — the "displacement current."

Together, conduction current and displacement current create magnetic fields. This symmetry with Faraday's law enables wave propagation.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Integral form

$$\oint_C \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_{enc} + \frac{d\Phi_D}{dt}$$

Magnetic circulation equals enclosed conduction current plus rate of change of electric flux.

- $I_{enc}$ = conduction current through surface
- $\Phi_D = \int_S \mathbf{D} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$ = electric flux

### Differential form

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}$$

- $\mathbf{J}$ = conduction current density (A/m²)
- $\partial \mathbf{D}/\partial t$ = displacement current density

### Displacement current

$$\mathbf{J}_D = \frac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} = \epsilon \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$$

No charges move, but changing E creates the same magnetic effect as current.

### H vs. B

$$\mathbf{B} = \mu \mathbf{H} = \mu_0 \mu_r \mathbf{H}$$

- $\mathbf{H}$ = magnetic field intensity (A/m)
- $\mathbf{B}$ = magnetic flux density (T)
- $\mu_0$ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ H/m
- $\mu_r$ = relative permeability

### Symmetry with Faraday

| Faraday | Ampere-Maxwell |
|---------|----------------|
| $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial \mathbf{B}/\partial t$ | $\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \partial \mathbf{D}/\partial t$ |
| Changing B → E | Changing D → H |

This symmetry is why electromagnetic waves exist.

### Boundary condition

At a surface carrying surface current $\mathbf{K}$:

$$\hat{n} \times (\mathbf{H}_1 - \mathbf{H}_2) = \mathbf{K}$$

Tangential H jumps by the surface current density.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Capacitor in AC circuit**

Parallel plate capacitor, area A, separation d, applied voltage $V = V_0 \sin(\omega t)$.

Electric field: $E = V/d$.

Displacement current density: $J_D = \epsilon \frac{dE}{dt} = \epsilon \frac{\omega V_0}{d} \cos(\omega t)$.

Total displacement current: $I_D = J_D \times A = \omega \epsilon A V_0 / d \times \cos(\omega t) = \omega C V_0 \cos(\omega t)$.

This equals $I = C \frac{dV}{dt}$ — Kirchhoff's law holds even through the gap.

**Example: Coaxial cable field**

Inner conductor carries current I.

By Ampere's law: $H \times 2\pi r = I$.

$$H = \frac{I}{2\pi r}$$

This is the standard result for wire fields.

**Example: Why RF flows on conductor surface**

At high frequency, displacement current in conductor is negligible compared to conduction.

But changing B induces E that opposes current flow inside — skin effect pushes current to surface.

**Example: Antenna radiation**

In the far field, no conduction current — only displacement current.

The wave propagates because $\partial E/\partial t$ creates H, and $\partial B/\partial t$ creates E.

Self-sustaining oscillation through space.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Forgetting displacement current.** In free space or dielectrics, conduction current is zero. Only displacement current maintains the magnetic field.

2. **Confusing H and B.** Use H for Ampere's law; B for Faraday's law. In free space, B = μ₀H. In magnetic materials, relationship is more complex.

3. **Wrong surface choice.** Ampere's law works for any surface bounded by the loop. For a capacitor, choose a surface that passes through the dielectric.

4. **Ignoring J in conductors.** In good conductors, conduction current dominates. Displacement current matters in dielectrics and at high frequencies.

5. **Sign conventions.** Right-hand rule: curl fingers along H circulation, thumb points in direction of J and ∂D/∂t.

</section>
