<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why Gauss's law?

Every electromagnetic phenomenon starts with charges. Gauss's law is the first Maxwell equation — it tells you how electric fields originate from charges. For RF engineers, this law explains why antennas need charge separation to radiate, why transmission lines have capacitance, and how shielding works.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Electric field lines start on positive charges and end on negative charges. Gauss's law says: count the net field lines leaving a closed surface, and you've counted the net charge inside.

More charges → more field lines → more flux.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Integral form

$$\oint_S \mathbf{D} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = Q_{enc}$$

The total electric flux through any closed surface equals the enclosed free charge.

- $\mathbf{D}$ = electric displacement field (C/m²)
- $Q_{enc}$ = total free charge inside surface

### Differential form

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho_v$$

Divergence of D equals volume charge density at every point.

- $\rho_v$ = free charge density (C/m³)

### E vs. D

$$\mathbf{D} = \epsilon \mathbf{E} = \epsilon_0 \epsilon_r \mathbf{E}$$

- $\mathbf{E}$ = electric field (V/m)
- $\epsilon_0$ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m (free space permittivity)
- $\epsilon_r$ = relative permittivity (dielectric constant)

In free space: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho_v / \epsilon_0$.

### Key insight: no magnetic charges

Gauss's law for magnetism:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$$

Magnetic field lines always close on themselves — no magnetic monopoles.

### Symmetry exploitation

Gauss's law is most useful when symmetry is present:

| Geometry | Gaussian surface | Result |
|----------|------------------|--------|
| Point charge | Sphere | $E = Q/(4\pi\epsilon r^2)$ |
| Infinite line | Cylinder | $E = \lambda/(2\pi\epsilon r)$ |
| Infinite plane | Pillbox | $E = \sigma/(2\epsilon)$ |
| Charged sphere | Sphere | $E = 0$ inside (hollow), $E \propto r$ (solid) |

### Boundary condition

At a surface with charge density $\sigma_s$:

$$D_{n1} - D_{n2} = \sigma_s$$

Normal component of D jumps by the surface charge.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Coaxial cable capacitance**

Inner conductor radius $a$, outer radius $b$, dielectric $\epsilon_r$.

Using Gauss's law with cylindrical surface:

$$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi \epsilon r}$$

Voltage: $V = \int_a^b E\, dr = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon} \ln(b/a)$

Capacitance per unit length:

$$C = \frac{\lambda}{V} = \frac{2\pi\epsilon}{\ln(b/a)}$$

For RG-58 ($a = 0.5$ mm, $b = 1.5$ mm, $\epsilon_r = 2.3$):

$C = 2\pi \times 2.3 \times 8.854 \times 10^{-12} / \ln(3) = 116$ pF/m.

**Example: Parallel plate capacitor**

Plate separation $d$, area $A$, charge $Q$.

Field between plates: $E = \sigma/\epsilon = Q/(\epsilon A)$.

Capacitance: $C = \epsilon A / d$.

RF relevance: this is the fringe field that couples between traces.

**Example: Shielding effectiveness**

A metal enclosure with a hole. Gauss's law says fields inside depend on induced charges on the inner surface. Any net charge outside creates corresponding image charges.

A complete shield has $E = 0$ inside (Faraday cage). Holes allow field leakage proportional to hole area.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing D and E.** In dielectrics, $D = \epsilon E$. Use D for Gauss's law with free charges, E for boundary conditions and forces.

2. **Applying to asymmetric geometries.** Gauss's law is true everywhere but only useful when symmetry lets you pull E out of the integral.

3. **Forgetting polarization charges.** In dielectrics, bound charges contribute to P but not to free charge. Use D to avoid this complication.

4. **Surface vs. volume charges.** Real conductors have surface charges. Gauss's law with infinitesimal pillbox gives boundary conditions.

5. **Dynamic fields.** Gauss's law holds for time-varying fields too, but doesn't tell the whole story — need Faraday and Ampere.

</section>
