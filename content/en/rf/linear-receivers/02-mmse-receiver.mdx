## Why MMSE?

Zero-forcing eliminates interference completely but amplifies noise, sometimes catastrophically. What if we allowed a small amount of residual interference in exchange for much lower noise?

The Minimum Mean Square Error (MMSE) receiver finds the optimal balance. It minimizes the total error — interference plus noise — rather than forcing interference to zero. In most practical scenarios, MMSE outperforms ZF.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Think of a noisy room where someone is talking. You could use noise-canceling headphones (ZF approach) that try to eliminate all background noise. But aggressive cancellation might introduce artifacts.

Alternatively, you could use a more gentle filter that reduces noise but doesn't eliminate it completely. The result sounds more natural and easier to understand.

MMSE takes this approach: it accepts some interference leakage to avoid the noise explosion that ZF suffers in ill-conditioned channels. At high SNR, MMSE converges to ZF. At low SNR, it acts more like a matched filter.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### MMSE criterion

MMSE minimizes the mean square error between the estimate and the true transmitted symbols:

$$\mathbf{W}_{MMSE} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{W}} \mathbb{E}[||\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{W}\mathbf{y}||^2]$$

### MMSE receiver formula

The solution is:

$$\mathbf{W}_{MMSE} = (\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H} + \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H$$

Or equivalently:

$$\mathbf{W}_{MMSE} = \mathbf{H}^H (\mathbf{H}\mathbf{H}^H + \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1}$$

### Comparison with ZF

| Property | ZF | MMSE |
|----------|----|----- |
| Formula | $(\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H$ | $(\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H} + \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H$ |
| Interference | Zero | Small residual |
| Noise enhancement | Can be severe | Controlled |
| SNR dependence | None | Yes (needs $\sigma^2$) |

The $\sigma^2 \mathbf{I}$ term is **regularization** — it prevents the inverse from exploding when $\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H}$ has small eigenvalues.

### Post-detection SINR

For stream $k$, the signal-to-interference-plus-noise ratio:

$$\text{SINR}_k = \frac{|[\mathbf{W}_{MMSE}\mathbf{H}]_{kk}|^2}{\sum_{j \neq k}|[\mathbf{W}_{MMSE}\mathbf{H}]_{kj}|^2 + \sigma^2||\mathbf{w}_k||^2}$$

where $\mathbf{w}_k$ is the $k$th row of $\mathbf{W}_{MMSE}$.

### High-SNR limit

As $\sigma^2 \to 0$:

$$\mathbf{W}_{MMSE} \to \mathbf{W}_{ZF}$$

MMSE converges to ZF at high SNR, where noise enhancement matters less than interference.

### Low-SNR limit

As $\sigma^2 \to \infty$:

$$\mathbf{W}_{MMSE} \to \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \mathbf{H}^H$$

MMSE converges to a matched filter, maximizing SNR rather than suppressing interference.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 2×2 MIMO comparison**

Channel matrix:
$$\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.8 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.9 \end{pmatrix}$$

At SNR = 10 dB ($\sigma^2 = 0.1$):

ZF: $\mathbf{W}_{ZF} = (\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H$

MMSE: $\mathbf{W}_{MMSE} = (\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H} + 0.1\mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H$

The $0.1\mathbf{I}$ term prevents the noise enhancement that ZF suffers. Typical improvement: 1–3 dB in BER at moderate SNR.

**Example: Ill-conditioned channel**

For a channel with condition number 100 (eigenvalues differ by 100×):

- ZF: worst stream has 20 dB noise enhancement
- MMSE: regularization limits noise enhancement to ~10 dB

MMSE can be 5–10 dB better in BER for poorly conditioned channels.

**Example: Massive MIMO**

In massive MIMO ($N_r \gg N_t$), the channel becomes well-conditioned with probability 1 (channel hardening). ZF and MMSE converge. MMSE's advantage is smaller but computation is the same — might as well use MMSE.

**Example: SNR estimation**

MMSE requires knowing $\sigma^2$. In practice:

1. Use training symbols to estimate noise variance
2. Or use the residual after initial detection
3. Or assume a nominal SNR and update iteratively

Misestimating $\sigma^2$ by 3 dB degrades MMSE by ~0.5 dB — it's robust.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Needs noise variance.** MMSE requires $\sigma^2$, unlike ZF. In rapidly varying noise environments, estimation lag can hurt.

2. **Still linear.** MMSE is optimal among linear receivers but not globally optimal. Nonlinear receivers (ML, sphere decoding) can do better at high SNR.

3. **Symbol power assumption.** The derivation assumes $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}\mathbf{x}^H] = \mathbf{I}$. Non-uniform power allocation or correlated symbols require modified formulas.

4. **Per-stream SNR varies.** Even with MMSE, different streams have different post-detection SINR. Rate adaptation or power allocation can help.

5. **Iterative variants.** MMSE-SIC (successive interference cancellation) outperforms linear MMSE but has error propagation. Order matters — decode strongest stream first.

6. **Complexity.** Matrix inversion is still $O(N_t^3)$. For large systems, iterative methods (conjugate gradient, Neumann series) approximate the MMSE solution.

</section>
