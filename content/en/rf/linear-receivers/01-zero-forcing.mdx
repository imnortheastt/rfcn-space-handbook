<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why zero-forcing?

In a MIMO system, multiple transmit antennas send different data streams simultaneously. At the receiver, these streams arrive mixed together. How do you separate them?

The zero-forcing (ZF) receiver inverts the channel matrix to completely eliminate inter-stream interference. It's the simplest linear approach and the starting point for understanding MIMO detection.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Imagine two people talking at once. Your brain separates their voices using spatial cues — each voice comes from a different direction. A MIMO receiver does something similar: different transmit antennas create different spatial signatures at the receive array.

The channel matrix $\mathbf{H}$ captures how each transmit antenna's signal appears at each receive antenna. Zero-forcing computes the inverse (or pseudo-inverse) of $\mathbf{H}$ to undo the mixing.

The name "zero-forcing" comes from forcing the interference from other streams to exactly zero. The cost: noise gets amplified, sometimes severely.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### MIMO system model

For $N_t$ transmit and $N_r$ receive antennas:

$$\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{n}$$

where:
- $\mathbf{y}$ is $N_r \times 1$ received signal vector
- $\mathbf{H}$ is $N_r \times N_t$ channel matrix
- $\mathbf{x}$ is $N_t \times 1$ transmitted symbol vector
- $\mathbf{n}$ is $N_r \times 1$ noise vector (AWGN, variance $\sigma^2$)

### ZF receiver

The ZF equalizer applies:

$$\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{ZF} = \mathbf{W}_{ZF} \mathbf{y}$$

where:

$$\mathbf{W}_{ZF} = (\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H$$

This is the Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse when $N_r \geq N_t$.

### How it works

$$\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{ZF} = (\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H (\mathbf{H}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{n})$$
$$= \mathbf{x} + (\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{n}$$

The transmitted symbols $\mathbf{x}$ are recovered perfectly (no interference), but noise is transformed by $(\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1} \mathbf{H}^H$.

### Noise enhancement

The post-detection noise covariance is:

$$\mathbf{R}_{nn} = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1}$$

The noise power for stream $k$ is proportional to $[(\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1}]_{kk}$.

When $\mathbf{H}$ is ill-conditioned (eigenvalues vary widely), some streams experience severe noise enhancement — SNR can drop dramatically.

### Post-detection SNR

For stream $k$:

$$\text{SNR}_k = \frac{1}{\sigma^2 [(\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1}]_{kk}}$$

The worst stream limits overall performance.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 2×2 MIMO**

Channel matrix:
$$\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.8 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.9 \end{pmatrix}$$

Compute $\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H}$:
$$\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.68 & 0.42 \\ 0.42 & 0.90 \end{pmatrix}$$

Inverse:
$$(\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H})^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 2.17 & -1.01 \\ -1.01 & 1.64 \end{pmatrix}$$

Noise enhancement factors: 2.17 and 1.64 for streams 1 and 2.

In dB: stream 1 loses 3.4 dB SNR; stream 2 loses 2.1 dB SNR compared to single-stream transmission.

**Example: Near-singular channel**

If the channel has two nearly identical columns (similar spatial signatures), $\mathbf{H}^H \mathbf{H}$ becomes nearly singular. The inverse explodes, and noise enhancement can be 20+ dB.

This happens when:
- Transmit antennas are too close (high correlation)
- Line-of-sight dominates (rank-deficient channel)
- One path is much stronger than others

**Example: Required receive antennas**

For ZF to work, $N_r \geq N_t$ (at least as many receive antennas as transmit streams). With $N_r = N_t$, performance is marginal. With $N_r > N_t$, extra receive antennas provide diversity gain and reduce noise enhancement.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Ill-conditioned channels.** ZF amplifies noise when channel columns are nearly parallel. Check the condition number of $\mathbf{H}$ before trusting ZF performance predictions.

2. **No diversity gain.** With $N_r = N_t$, ZF extracts no receive diversity. BER curves show diversity order of 1 per stream, not $N_r$.

3. **Requires channel knowledge.** ZF needs accurate $\mathbf{H}$ at the receiver. Channel estimation errors directly degrade performance.

4. **Computational cost.** Matrix inversion is $O(N_t^3)$. For massive MIMO (many antennas), approximate methods or MMSE are preferred.

5. **Not optimal.** ZF ignores noise statistics. MMSE (next lesson) trades some interference suppression for reduced noise enhancement and generally outperforms ZF.

6. **Underdetermined systems.** If $N_r < N_t$, ZF cannot separate all streams. Use stream selection, SIC, or reduce the number of transmitted streams.

</section>
