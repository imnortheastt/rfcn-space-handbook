<section class="lesson-hook">

## Choosing the right receiver

You've learned ZF and MMSE — but which should you use? And what about matched filtering, maximum likelihood, or successive cancellation?

This lesson compares linear receivers across performance, complexity, and practical applicability. The right choice depends on your SNR regime, channel conditions, and computational budget.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Linear receivers form a spectrum from simple to sophisticated:

1. **Matched filter (MF):** Maximizes SNR, ignores interference. Good when interference is negligible (single user, orthogonal channels).

2. **Zero-forcing (ZF):** Eliminates interference completely. Good when SNR is high and channels are well-conditioned.

3. **MMSE:** Optimal linear trade-off. Best general-purpose linear receiver.

4. **Maximum likelihood (ML):** Optimal overall but exponential complexity. The gold standard for comparison.

The gaps between these shrink or grow depending on the scenario. Understanding when matters more than memorizing formulas.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Receiver formulas summary

| Receiver | Formula | Criterion |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Matched filter | $\mathbf{W}_{MF} = \mathbf{H}^H$ | Maximize SNR (ignore interference) |
| Zero-forcing | $\mathbf{W}_{ZF} = (\mathbf{H}^H\mathbf{H})^{-1}\mathbf{H}^H$ | Zero interference |
| MMSE | $\mathbf{W}_{MMSE} = (\mathbf{H}^H\mathbf{H} + \sigma^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{H}^H$ | Minimize MSE |
| ML | $\hat\{\mathbf\{x\}\} = \arg\min\_\{\mathbf\{x\} \in \mathcal\{A\}^\{N\_t\}\} \|\|\mathbf\{y\} - \mathbf\{H\}\mathbf\{x\}\|\|^2$ | Maximum likelihood |

### Performance comparison

**At low SNR:**
- MF ≈ MMSE (both maximize SNR)
- ZF performs poorly (noise enhancement dominates)
- Gap: MMSE can be 5–10 dB better than ZF

**At high SNR:**
- MMSE → ZF (regularization vanishes)
- ZF works well
- Gap: MMSE ≈ ZF ≈ ML for linear modulation

**Ill-conditioned channels:**
- ZF fails catastrophically
- MMSE degrades gracefully
- ML is robust but expensive

### Complexity comparison

| Receiver | Complexity | Memory |
|----------|------------|--------|
| MF | $O(N_r N_t)$ | $O(N_r N_t)$ |
| ZF | $O(N_t^3 + N_r N_t^2)$ | $O(N_t^2)$ |
| MMSE | $O(N_t^3 + N_r N_t^2)$ | $O(N_t^2)$ |
| ML (brute force) | $O(M^{N_t} N_r N_t)$ | $O(1)$ |

Where $M$ = constellation size. ML complexity explodes with streams and modulation order.

### Diversity order

For $N_r \times N_t$ MIMO with $N_r > N_t$:

| Receiver | Diversity order |
|----------|----------------|
| MF | 1 (no interference suppression) |
| ZF | $N_r - N_t + 1$ |
| MMSE | $N_r - N_t + 1$ (same as ZF) |
| ML | $N_r$ (full receive diversity) |

MMSE and ZF achieve the same diversity but MMSE has better coding gain (shifts BER curve left).

### Decision regions

ML partitions the receive space into optimal Voronoi regions around each possible transmit vector. Linear receivers create linear (hyperplane) boundaries — suboptimal but tractable.

At high SNR, linear boundaries approximate Voronoi well. At low SNR, the approximation is poor.

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: When to use MF**

Single-user SISO: No interference, MF is optimal.

CDMA with orthogonal codes: If codes are perfectly orthogonal after the channel, MF suffices.

Massive MIMO with $N_r \gg N_t$: Channel becomes nearly orthogonal; MF approaches MMSE performance.

**Example: When ZF suffices**

Wi-Fi MIMO in good conditions: Indoor channels are often well-conditioned. At typical 20–30 dB SNR, ZF and MMSE perform similarly.

Line-of-sight mmWave: Pure LOS channels can be low-rank, making ZF problematic. But with analog beamforming pre-applied, the effective channel may be well-conditioned.

**Example: When MMSE is essential**

Urban cellular: Varied channel conditions, moderate SNR. MMSE's robustness wins.

Low-SNR IoT: Battery-limited devices operate at low SNR where ZF's noise enhancement is fatal.

**Example: When ML is worth it**

Small constellations (BPSK, QPSK) with few streams (2×2 MIMO): $4^2 = 16$ hypotheses — ML is tractable.

Safety-critical applications: When performance matters more than power (military, space).

Sphere decoding: Reduces ML complexity to polynomial (average case) by pruning unlikely hypotheses.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **MMSE isn't always better.** For well-conditioned channels at high SNR, MMSE's overhead provides negligible gain over ZF.

2. **MF isn't useless.** In massive MIMO with channel hardening, MF achieves near-optimal performance with minimal complexity.

3. **ML complexity is deceptive.** Average-case complexity (with sphere decoding) is much lower than worst-case, but worst-case matters for real-time systems.

4. **Channel estimation dominates.** Perfect CSI comparisons hide the reality: estimation errors affect all receivers, and simpler receivers may be more robust to imperfect CSI.

5. **Implementation matters.** Fixed-point arithmetic, pipeline depth, and silicon area often favor simpler receivers despite theoretical performance gaps.

6. **Nonlinear receivers exist.** MMSE-SIC, V-BLAST, and turbo equalization bridge the gap between linear and ML at intermediate complexity.
