<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why spatial multiplexing?

A 4×4 MIMO system can transmit 4 independent data streams simultaneously — 4× the throughput of a single antenna. This isn't time or frequency multiplexing; it's spatial multiplexing, using multiple antennas to create parallel pipes through the air. It's why modern wireless delivers gigabits, not megabits.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

With multiple transmit and receive antennas, the channel matrix has multiple singular values. Each singular value represents an independent "pipe" through the wireless medium. If you have 4 strong pipes, you can send 4 different symbols at the same time, on the same frequency. The receiver separates them using the spatial signatures.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### System model

$$\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{n}$$

- M transmit antennas, N receive antennas
- $\mathbf{H} \in \mathbb{C}^{N \times M}$ — channel matrix
- Send $\min(M, N)$ independent streams

### SVD decomposition

$$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^H$$

- $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ = diagonal matrix of singular values $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq ... \geq \sigma_r$
- $r = \text{rank}(\mathbf{H}) \leq \min(M, N)$

Each singular value defines a parallel channel with gain $\sigma_i^2$.

### Capacity

With water-filling power allocation:

$$C = \sum_{i=1}^r \log_2\left(1 + \frac{p_i \sigma_i^2}{\sigma_n^2}\right)$$

At high SNR, approximately:

$$C \approx r \cdot \log_2(\text{SNR})$$

Capacity scales linearly with $r$ — the multiplexing gain.

### Multiplexing gain

**Definition:** The pre-log factor in capacity scaling.

$$r^* = \lim_{\text{SNR} \to \infty} \frac{C(\text{SNR})}{\log_2(\text{SNR})}$$

For full-rank channel: $r^* = \min(M, N)$.

**Example:** 4×4 MIMO with full-rank channel achieves 4× the capacity of SISO at high SNR.

### Conditions for spatial multiplexing

1. **Rich scattering:** Channel must have full (or near-full) rank. LOS with no scattering = rank 1.
2. **Antenna spacing:** Typically λ/2 or more to decorrelate.
3. **Sufficient SNR:** Low SNR makes weak streams unusable.
4. **Channel knowledge:** Receiver needs CSI to separate streams.

### V-BLAST: Practical spatial multiplexing

Transmit independent symbols on each antenna (no precoding).

Receive: ZF, MMSE, or successive interference cancellation (SIC).

SIC: Detect strongest stream, subtract, repeat. Approaches capacity.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 4×4 MIMO throughput**

20 MHz bandwidth, 256-QAM (8 bits/symbol), 4 streams.

Throughput: $20 \times 10^6 \times 8 \times 4 = 640$ Mbps

Compare SISO: 160 Mbps. 4× improvement.

**Example: Stream allocation**

Channel singular values: [10, 8, 3, 0.5].

At high SNR: use all 4 streams (4× multiplexing gain).
At moderate SNR: use 3 streams (stream 4 too weak).
At low SNR: use 1-2 streams (focus power on strong channels).

Water-filling automatically optimizes this.

**Example: Rank deficiency**

LOS rooftop link: channel has rank ≈ 1.
Only 1 stream useful despite 4×4 antennas.

Rich urban scattering: full rank 4.
All 4 streams usable.

Environment determines multiplexing capability.

**Example: 802.11ac/ax**

8×8 MU-MIMO: up to 8 spatial streams.
Each user may get 1-4 streams depending on their channel.

5 GHz, 160 MHz, 1024-QAM, 8 streams: 9.6 Gbps peak rate.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Assuming full rank.** Real channels often have rank deficiency (correlated antennas, LOS, keyhole effects).

2. **Ignoring SNR regime.** At low SNR, multiplexing wastes power on weak streams. Focus power on best stream.

3. **Confusing with diversity.** Diversity combats fading (reliability). Multiplexing increases rate (throughput). They trade off.

4. **Forgetting antenna count.** Max streams = $\min(M, N)$. 2×4 system has at most 2 streams.

5. **Over-counting practical gains.** Overhead (pilots, guard intervals) and imperfect CSI reduce real throughput below theory.

</section>
