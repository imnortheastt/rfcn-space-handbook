<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why this trade-off?

With 4 antennas, you can either: (a) use all 4 to combat fading (maximum diversity), (b) use all 4 to focus energy toward the user (maximum beamforming gain), or (c) something in between. These aren't the same thing, and you can't have both simultaneously. Understanding this trade-off guides system design.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

**Diversity:** Transmit the same signal through multiple independent paths. When one path fades, another survives. Reduces outage probability.

**Beamforming:** Coherently combine antennas to focus energy in one direction. Increases average SNR but doesn't reduce fading variance.

With perfect CSI at the transmitter, beamforming gives the highest average SNR. But without CSI, diversity is safer — it protects against bad fades. The choice depends on what you optimize and what you know.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Diversity perspective

Transmit same symbol from all antennas with different phases/delays.

Receiver combines signals. Even if some paths fade, others remain.

**Diversity order:** Number of independent fading paths. With M transmit and N receive antennas and rich scattering: $d = M \cdot N$.

**Error rate:** $P_e \propto \text{SNR}^{-d}$ at high SNR.

### Beamforming perspective

With CSI, transmitter adjusts phases so signals add coherently at receiver.

**Array gain:** M antennas → M× power at receiver (coherent combining).

**Beamforming gain:** $10\log_{10}(M)$ dB increase in received SNR.

But if the channel fades, all that focused energy fades together — no diversity protection.

### The tension

Both use the same antenna resources differently:

| Strategy | Uses antennas for... | Benefit | Drawback |
|----------|---------------------|---------|----------|
| Diversity | Independent paths | Reduces fading depth | Lower average power |
| Beamforming | Coherent focus | Higher average SNR | No fading protection |

### Optimal strategy depends on scenario

**No CSIT (channel state info at transmitter):** Can't beamform. Use diversity coding (space-time codes).

**Perfect CSIT:** Beamform to maximize instantaneous SNR. But channel must be tracked accurately.

**Partial CSIT:** Hybrid approaches — some beamforming gain, some diversity.

### Mathematical framework

Outage probability at rate R:

$$P_{out} = P(\log_2(1 + \gamma) < R)$$

Beamforming maximizes average $\gamma$ but doesn't reduce variance.

Diversity reduces variance of $\gamma$, lowering outage probability for fixed R.

**Key insight:** At low outage targets (high reliability), diversity dominates. At average throughput targets, beamforming wins.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 4-antenna transmitter, no CSIT**

Option A: Alamouti code (2-antenna diversity scheme extended).
Diversity order: 4 (with 1 Rx), protects against fades.

Option B: Transmit from 1 antenna at 4× power.
No diversity, same average SNR but high outage risk.

For reliability-critical links (control channels), use diversity.

**Example: 4-antenna transmitter, perfect CSIT**

Maximal ratio transmission (MRT): weight antennas by channel conjugate.

$$\mathbf{w} = \frac{\mathbf{h}^*}{\|\mathbf{h}\|}$$

Beamforming gain: +6 dB compared to isotropic.

Use for throughput-optimized data channels.

**Example: Hybrid beamforming with partial diversity**

4 antennas → 2 beamform to user direction, 2 provide polarization diversity.

Moderate beamforming gain + moderate diversity protection.

Common in mmWave systems where both matter.

**Example: 5G NR design**

Control channels: diversity-focused (reliability matters).
Data channels: beamforming-focused (throughput matters).

System dynamically switches based on channel conditions and traffic type.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Treating them as the same.** "More antennas = better" is true, but HOW they're used matters. Diversity ≠ beamforming.

2. **Ignoring CSIT quality.** Beamforming with outdated CSI can be worse than diversity. High-mobility channels favor diversity.

3. **Forgetting the objective.** Maximize average rate → beamforming. Minimize outage → diversity. Match strategy to goal.

4. **Over-beamforming at low SNR.** Beamforming gain is multiplicative. At low SNR, the multiplied value is still low. Diversity helps more.

5. **Confusing beamforming with spatial multiplexing.** Beamforming = 1 stream, focused. Multiplexing = multiple streams. Different goals.
