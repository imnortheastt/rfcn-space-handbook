<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why the channel matrix?

A SISO system has one path gain. A MIMO system with $N_t$ transmit and $N_r$ receive antennas has $N_t \times N_r$ path gains — one for each TX-RX antenna pair. The channel matrix H organizes these gains into a mathematical object that enables linear algebra tools.

Everything in MIMO — capacity, beamforming, precoding — derives from H.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Think of H as a routing table. Each entry $h_{ij}$ tells you how much of the signal from transmit antenna j reaches receive antenna i. If you know H, you can predict what any receiver sees for any transmitted signal.

With multiple antennas, the channel becomes a transformation, not just a scalar gain.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### System model

$$\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{n}$$

- $\mathbf{x}$ = transmitted signal vector ($N_t \times 1$)
- $\mathbf{y}$ = received signal vector ($N_r \times 1$)
- $\mathbf{H}$ = channel matrix ($N_r \times N_t$)
- $\mathbf{n}$ = noise vector ($N_r \times 1$), typically $\mathcal{CN}(0, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$

### Channel matrix structure

$$\mathbf{H} = \begin{bmatrix} h_{11} & h_{12} & \cdots & h_{1N_t} \\ h_{21} & h_{22} & \cdots & h_{2N_t} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ h_{N_r 1} & h_{N_r 2} & \cdots & h_{N_r N_t} \end{bmatrix}$$

Entry $h_{ij}$: complex gain from TX antenna j to RX antenna i.

### Rayleigh fading model

For rich scattering (NLOS):

$$h_{ij} \sim \mathcal{CN}(0, 1)$$

Each entry is independent, complex Gaussian.

Properties:
- $|h_{ij}|$ is Rayleigh distributed
- $|h_{ij}|^2$ is exponential distributed
- Phase is uniform on $[0, 2\pi)$

### Rician fading model

For LOS + scattered paths:

$$h_{ij} = \sqrt{\frac{K}{K+1}} h_{ij}^{LOS} + \sqrt{\frac{1}{K+1}} h_{ij}^{NLOS}$$

K-factor: ratio of LOS power to scattered power.

### Correlation

**Kronecker model:**
$$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{R}_r^{1/2} \mathbf{H}_w \mathbf{R}_t^{1/2}$$

- $\mathbf{H}_w$ = i.i.d. Rayleigh matrix
- $\mathbf{R}_t$ = TX correlation matrix
- $\mathbf{R}_r$ = RX correlation matrix

Correlation reduces effective degrees of freedom.

### Key properties

| Property | Expression | Meaning |
|----------|------------|---------|
| Rank | rank(H) | Max independent streams |
| Frobenius norm | $\|\mathbf{H}\|_F = \sqrt{\sum \|h_{ij}\|^2}$ | Total channel power |
| Condition number | $\sigma_{max}/\sigma_{min}$ | Channel quality |

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 2×2 MIMO**

$$\mathbf{H} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 + 0.3j & -0.2 + 0.4j \\ 0.1 - 0.6j & 0.7 + 0.1j \end{bmatrix}$$

Transmitted: $\mathbf{x} = [1, 1]^T$

Received (noiseless): $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{x} = [0.3 + 0.7j, 0.8 - 0.5j]^T$

Each receive antenna sees a weighted sum of both transmitted signals.

**Example: Channel normalization**

For fair comparisons, normalize so $\mathbb{E}[\|\mathbf{H}\|_F^2] = N_r N_t$.

For i.i.d. Rayleigh with unit variance entries, this is automatic.

**Example: Antenna spacing effect**

ULA with spacing d, wavelength λ.

Correlation between adjacent elements: $\rho \approx J_0(2\pi d/\lambda)$

At d = λ/2: $\rho \approx 0$ (uncorrelated)
At d = λ/4: $\rho \approx 0.5$ (moderately correlated)
At d = λ/8: $\rho \approx 0.9$ (highly correlated)

**Example: Rank deficiency**

LOS channel with parallel arrays:

$$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{a}_r \mathbf{a}_t^H$$

Rank = 1, regardless of array sizes.

Only one spatial stream possible — no multiplexing gain.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing dimensions.** H is $N_r \times N_t$, not $N_t \times N_r$. Rows are receive antennas, columns are transmit antennas.

2. **Ignoring normalization.** Different papers use different normalizations. Always check whether H has unit power per entry or total power constraint.

3. **Assuming independence.** Real channels have correlation from antenna spacing, scattering geometry, and mutual coupling.

4. **Forgetting time variation.** H changes with mobility. Coherence time determines how long H estimate remains valid.

5. **Using wrong model.** Rayleigh assumes rich scattering. Indoor LOS, satellite links, and mmWave often need Rician or geometric models.
