<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why Foschini's analysis?

In 1996, Foschini showed that MIMO capacity grows linearly with min(N_t, N_r) — not logarithmically like bandwidth or SNR. This was revolutionary: adding antennas multiplies capacity, not just adds to it. This insight launched the entire field of spatial multiplexing.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

SISO capacity hits a ceiling at high SNR — you can only extract log₂(1 + SNR) bits per symbol. But with N independent spatial channels (eigenmodes), you get N times that. More antennas = more parallel pipes = more total throughput.

It's the difference between widening a single lane versus adding more lanes to a highway.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### MIMO capacity formula

For channel H known at receiver:

$$C = \log_2 \det\left(\mathbf{I}_{N_r} + \frac{\rho}{N_t} \mathbf{H}\mathbf{H}^H\right) \text{ bits/s/Hz}$$

- $\rho = P/\sigma^2$ = total SNR
- Factor $1/N_t$ normalizes for equal power per TX antenna

Equivalently, using SVD:

$$C = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \log_2\left(1 + \frac{\rho}{N_t} \sigma_i^2\right)$$

where r = rank(H) and $\sigma_i$ are singular values.

### Channel known at transmitter

With CSI at TX, use waterfilling:

$$C_{CSIT} = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \log_2\left(1 + \frac{\sigma_i^2 p_i}{\sigma^2}\right)$$

with optimal $p_i$ from waterfilling algorithm.

### High-SNR approximation

At high SNR:

$$C \approx r \cdot \log_2(\rho/N_t) + \log_2\left(\prod_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2\right)$$

First term: linear growth with rank (multiplexing gain)
Second term: depends on channel quality

### Multiplexing gain

$$r^* = \lim_{\rho \to \infty} \frac{C(\rho)}{\log_2 \rho} = \min(N_t, N_r)$$

At high SNR, capacity grows as $\min(N_t, N_r) \cdot \log_2(\rho)$.

### Ergodic capacity (random channels)

For i.i.d. Rayleigh fading:

$$\bar{C} = \mathbb{E}_H[C] \approx \min(N_t, N_r) \cdot \log_2\left(1 + \frac{\rho \cdot \max(N_t, N_r)}{\min(N_t, N_r)}\right)$$

Key insight: capacity scales linearly with $\min(N_t, N_r)$.

### Outage capacity

For slow fading, use outage probability:

$$P_{out}(R) = P(C < R)$$

Outage capacity at 10% outage: rate R such that $P_{out}(R) = 0.1$.

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 4×4 MIMO vs. SISO**

SNR = 20 dB (ρ = 100).

SISO: $C_{SISO} = \log_2(1 + 100) = 6.7$ bits/s/Hz

4×4 MIMO (i.i.d. Rayleigh, high SNR):
$C_{MIMO} \approx 4 \cdot \log_2(100/4) = 4 \cdot 4.6 = 18.4$ bits/s/Hz

2.7× capacity increase from 4× more antennas.

**Example: Effect of antenna imbalance**

2×4 system vs. 4×4 system at 20 dB SNR.

2×4: $r^* = \min(2, 4) = 2$, so ~2× multiplexing gain
4×4: $r^* = \min(4, 4) = 4$, so ~4× multiplexing gain

The limiting factor is the smaller array.

**Example: Capacity vs. SNR**

4×4 MIMO capacity at different SNRs:

| SNR (dB) | Capacity (bits/s/Hz) |
|----------|---------------------|
| 0 | ~4 |
| 10 | ~12 |
| 20 | ~22 |
| 30 | ~32 |

Roughly linear with SNR in dB at high SNR.

**Example: Waterfilling gain**

4×4 channel with singular values [2, 1, 0.5, 0.1].

At 10 dB SNR:
- Equal power: 11.2 bits/s/Hz
- Waterfilling: 11.8 bits/s/Hz

Modest gain — more significant at low SNR.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Confusing multiplexing gain with array gain.** Multiplexing gain is multiple streams. Array gain is power focusing. Both increase capacity but differently.

2. **Ignoring channel rank.** Capacity scales with rank, not antenna count. A 64×64 system with rank-2 channel behaves like 2×2.

3. **Assuming perfect CSI.** Real systems have estimation errors. Capacity degrades with imperfect knowledge.

4. **Forgetting correlation.** Correlated channels have effective rank < min(N_t, N_r). Capacity falls accordingly.

5. **Misinterpreting ergodic vs. outage.** Ergodic is average over many channel realizations. Outage is for quasi-static channels where you can't average.
