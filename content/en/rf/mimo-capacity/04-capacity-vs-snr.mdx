<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why study capacity vs. SNR?

MIMO systems behave differently at low and high SNR. At low SNR, diversity matters most — use all antennas to improve reliability. At high SNR, multiplexing wins — use antennas as parallel channels. Understanding this transition is key to choosing the right operating mode.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

At low SNR, noise dominates. Every extra antenna helps fight noise by combining signals. At high SNR, noise is manageable — now the bottleneck is how many independent streams you can fit. 

It's like a road system: in bad weather (low SNR), you need all lanes working together for safety. In good weather (high SNR), you want maximum throughput with parallel lanes.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Low-SNR regime

When $\rho \ll 1$:

$$C \approx \rho \cdot \mathbb{E}[\|\mathbf{H}\|_F^2] \cdot \log_2 e = \rho \cdot N_r N_t \cdot \log_2 e$$

Capacity scales linearly with SNR. Array gain helps: use all antennas coherently.

### High-SNR regime

When $\rho \gg 1$:

$$C \approx \min(N_t, N_r) \cdot \log_2 \rho + O(1)$$

Capacity scales logarithmically with SNR. Multiplexing gain dominates.

### Transition region

The crossover depends on antenna configuration and channel.

Rule of thumb: transition around SNR = 0-10 dB.

Below transition: beamforming (all power to one eigenmode)
Above transition: spatial multiplexing (spread power across modes)

### Diversity-multiplexing tradeoff

Zheng and Tse's fundamental result:

$$d(r) = (N_t - r)(N_r - r)$$

- r = multiplexing gain (rate scales as $r \cdot \log_2 \rho$)
- d(r) = diversity gain (error probability scales as $\rho^{-d}$)

You can't maximize both simultaneously:
- $r = 0$: full diversity $d = N_t N_r$
- $r = \min(N_t, N_r)$: full multiplexing, $d = 0$

### Practical operating points

| Application | SNR range | Strategy |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| Cell edge | Low (< 5 dB) | Beamforming, diversity |
| Typical UE | Medium (5-15 dB) | 2-4 layers |
| Near cell | High (> 15 dB) | Max multiplexing |

### Capacity with CSIT vs. no CSIT

**With CSIT:** waterfilling, ~0.5-2 dB gain at low-medium SNR.

**Without CSIT:** equal power, still good at high SNR.

At very low SNR, CSIT enables beamforming → significant gain.

### Antenna scaling at fixed SNR

Fixing total power and adding antennas:

$$C \approx \min(N_t, N_r) \cdot \log_2\left(1 + \frac{\rho \cdot \max(N_t, N_r)}{N_t}\right)$$

Adding RX antennas: array gain + multiplexing gain
Adding TX antennas: multiplexing gain (with CSIT, also array gain)

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Low-SNR beamforming**

4×4 MIMO at SNR = -5 dB (ρ = 0.32).

With beamforming (all power to strongest eigenmode):
$C \approx \log_2(1 + 4 \cdot 0.32) = 1.2$ bits/s/Hz

With equal power multiplexing:
$C \approx 4 \cdot \log_2(1 + 0.08) = 0.45$ bits/s/Hz

Beamforming wins by 2.7× at low SNR.

**Example: High-SNR multiplexing**

4×4 MIMO at SNR = 30 dB (ρ = 1000).

With 4-stream multiplexing:
$C \approx 4 \cdot \log_2(250) = 32$ bits/s/Hz

With beamforming:
$C \approx \log_2(4000) = 12$ bits/s/Hz

Multiplexing wins by 2.7× at high SNR.

**Example: 5G NR rank adaptation**

UE reports CQI (channel quality) and RI (rank indicator).

| CQI | Approximate SNR | Typical RI |
|-----|-----------------|------------|
| 1-3 | < 0 dB | 1 |
| 4-7 | 0-10 dB | 1-2 |
| 8-12 | 10-20 dB | 2-4 |
| 13-15 | > 20 dB | 4-8 |

System adapts layers to operating point.

**Example: Diversity-multiplexing tradeoff curve**

3×3 MIMO:
- Full diversity: d = 9, r = 0
- Balanced: d = 4, r = 1
- Balanced: d = 1, r = 2
- Full multiplexing: d = 0, r = 3

Space-time codes operate on this curve.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Always using max layers.** At low SNR, fewer layers with better coding outperforms many weak layers.

2. **Ignoring the transition.** The optimal strategy changes with SNR. Fixed strategies leave capacity on the table.

3. **Confusing per-stream SNR with total SNR.** With N streams, per-stream SNR = total SNR / N (approximately). Each stream is weaker.

4. **Forgetting practical constraints.** Ideal capacity assumes Gaussian codebooks. Real systems with finite constellations hit ceilings.

5. **Overlooking channel estimation overhead.** More streams need more pilots. At low SNR, pilot overhead can dominate.
