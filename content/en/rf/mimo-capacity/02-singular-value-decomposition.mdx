<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why SVD?

The channel matrix H couples transmit and receive antennas in complex ways. SVD diagonalizes the channel — transforming the messy MIMO channel into parallel, independent SISO pipes. Each pipe has its own gain (singular value), and you can optimize power allocation across pipes.

This is the theoretical foundation for optimal MIMO transmission.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

SVD finds the "natural axes" of the channel. Instead of fighting the coupling between antennas, you transmit along these axes. The channel becomes a set of independent tunnels — some wide (high gain), some narrow (low gain).

It's like finding the grain direction in wood: work with it, not against it.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### SVD definition

Any matrix H can be decomposed:

$$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{U} \mathbf{\Sigma} \mathbf{V}^H$$

- $\mathbf{U}$: $N_r \times N_r$ unitary matrix (receive eigenvectors)
- $\mathbf{\Sigma}$: $N_r \times N_t$ diagonal matrix of singular values
- $\mathbf{V}$: $N_t \times N_t$ unitary matrix (transmit eigenvectors)

Unitary: $\mathbf{U}^H\mathbf{U} = \mathbf{I}$, $\mathbf{V}^H\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{I}$

### Singular values

$$\mathbf{\Sigma} = \text{diag}(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, ..., \sigma_r, 0, ..., 0)$$

- $r = \text{rank}(\mathbf{H}) \leq \min(N_t, N_r)$
- $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq ... \geq \sigma_r > 0$
- $\sigma_i^2$ = eigenvalue of $\mathbf{H}^H\mathbf{H}$ (or $\mathbf{H}\mathbf{H}^H$)

### Eigenmode transmission

Transmit: $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{V}\mathbf{s}$ (precode with V)
Receive: $\mathbf{U}^H\mathbf{y}$ (combine with U)

Effective system:
$$\tilde{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{U}^H\mathbf{H}\mathbf{V}\mathbf{s} + \mathbf{U}^H\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{\Sigma}\mathbf{s} + \tilde{\mathbf{n}}$$

Result: r independent channels with gains $\sigma_1, ..., \sigma_r$.

### Eigenmode SNR

For stream i with power $p_i$:

$$\text{SNR}_i = \frac{\sigma_i^2 p_i}{\sigma^2}$$

Total power constraint: $\sum_i p_i = P$

### Waterfilling power allocation

Optimal power distribution:

$$p_i = \left(\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_i^2}\right)^+$$

where $(x)^+ = \max(0, x)$ and $\mu$ chosen so $\sum p_i = P$.

Intuition: pour water into vessel with bottom heights $\sigma^2/\sigma_i^2$. Deeper channels get more power.

### Condition number

$$\kappa = \frac{\sigma_{max}}{\sigma_{min}} = \frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_r}$$

- $\kappa \approx 1$: well-conditioned, all streams similar
- $\kappa \gg 1$: ill-conditioned, weak streams barely usable

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 2×2 MIMO SVD**

$$\mathbf{H} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

SVD: $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{2}$, $\sigma_2 = \sqrt{2}$

This is a well-conditioned channel — both eigenmodes have equal gain.

$$\mathbf{V} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Precoding with V transmits along sum and difference directions.

**Example: Waterfilling**

3 eigenmodes: $\sigma_1^2 = 4$, $\sigma_2^2 = 1$, $\sigma_3^2 = 0.25$.

Noise: $\sigma^2 = 0.1$. Total power: P = 3.

Inverse SNRs: $0.1/4 = 0.025$, $0.1/1 = 0.1$, $0.1/0.25 = 0.4$

Water level μ found numerically: μ ≈ 1.175

Powers: $p_1 = 1.15$, $p_2 = 1.075$, $p_3 = 0.775$

Weak channel still gets power (above threshold).

**Example: Rank-deficient channel**

LOS channel: rank = 1.

Only $\sigma_1 > 0$, all other $\sigma_i = 0$.

Only one eigenmode usable — beamforming to that direction.

**Example: Equal power vs. waterfilling**

At high SNR: equal power approaches waterfilling capacity.
At low SNR: concentrate power on strongest eigenmode.

Waterfilling provides 0.5-2 dB gain in practical scenarios.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Assuming full CSI.** Eigenmode transmission requires channel knowledge at transmitter. In FDD, this means feedback overhead.

2. **Forgetting noise enhancement.** Weak eigenmodes have low SNR even with power allocation. Sometimes better to not use them.

3. **Confusing SVD with eigendecomposition.** SVD applies to any matrix. Eigendecomposition only to square matrices. H is typically non-square.

4. **Ignoring implementation complexity.** SVD requires matrix operations at symbol rate. May not be feasible for wideband OFDM.

5. **Overlooking channel estimation errors.** Imperfect CSI means imperfect U, V. Performance degrades gracefully but eigenmode orthogonality is lost.
