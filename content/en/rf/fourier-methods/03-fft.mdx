## Why the FFT?

The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) computes the spectrum of sampled data. But a direct DFT of $N$ points requires $N^2$ complex multiplications. For $N = 1024$, that's over a million operations — too slow for real-time spectrum analysis.

The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) computes the same result in $N \log_2 N$ operations. For $N = 1024$, that's about 10,000 operations — a 100× speedup. This makes real-time spectral analysis possible in oscilloscopes, spectrum analyzers, software-defined radios, and every signal processing system you'll encounter.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

The FFT exploits symmetry. The DFT has redundant calculations — many intermediate products get computed multiple times. The Cooley-Tukey algorithm (the most common FFT) splits the problem recursively:

1. Separate even-indexed and odd-indexed samples
2. Compute smaller DFTs on each half
3. Combine results using "butterfly" operations

Each split halves the problem size, giving $\log_2 N$ levels. Each level does $N$ operations. Total: $N \log_2 N$.

The trick: the same exponential factors (twiddle factors) appear repeatedly, so you compute them once and reuse them.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Discrete Fourier Transform

Given $N$ samples $x[0], x[1], \ldots, x[N-1]$, the DFT is:

$$X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] \cdot W_N^{nk}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1$$

where $W_N = e^{-j2\pi/N}$ is the primitive $N$th root of unity.

### FFT algorithm (radix-2)

For $N = 2^m$, split into even and odd:

$$X[k] = \sum_{r=0}^{N/2-1} x[2r] W_N^{2rk} + \sum_{r=0}^{N/2-1} x[2r+1] W_N^{(2r+1)k}$$

$$X[k] = E[k] + W_N^k \cdot O[k]$$

where $E[k]$ is the DFT of even samples and $O[k]$ is the DFT of odd samples.

The butterfly operation combines two $N/2$-point DFTs into one $N$-point DFT.

### Frequency resolution

For sample rate $f_s$ and $N$ points:

- **Frequency resolution:** $\Delta f = f_s / N$
- **Frequency bins:** $f_k = k \cdot f_s / N$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1$
- **Nyquist:** Bins $0$ to $N/2$ cover DC to $f_s/2$; bins $N/2+1$ to $N-1$ are negative frequencies (aliased)

### FFT output interpretation

| Bin | Frequency | Typical use |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| 0 | DC (0 Hz) | Average value |
| 1 to N/2−1 | Positive frequencies | Main spectrum |
| N/2 | Nyquist ($f_s/2$) | Folding point |
| N/2+1 to N−1 | Negative frequencies | Mirror of positive (for real input) |

For real input, $X[N-k] = X^*[k]$. You only need bins 0 through N/2.

### Computational cost

| Algorithm | Multiplications | Additions |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Direct DFT | $N^2$ | $N^2$ |
| Radix-2 FFT | $(N/2) \log_2 N$ | $N \log_2 N$ |

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Spectrum analyzer settings**

You want 1 Hz frequency resolution at a 1 MHz sample rate.

Resolution: $\Delta f = f_s / N = 1$ Hz
Required $N = f_s / \Delta f = 1,000,000$ points

FFT time: at 1 MHz, collecting 1M samples takes 1 second. Your spectrum updates once per second.

Trade-off: higher resolution requires more samples and longer acquisition time.

**Example: Identifying a signal**

An SDR receives a 10 MHz band at 20 Msps. You run a 4096-point FFT.

Frequency resolution: $20\text{ MHz} / 4096 = 4.88$ kHz per bin.

A carrier at 7.5 MHz above the center frequency appears at bin $k = 7.5 \text{ MHz} / 4.88 \text{ kHz} = 1536$ (approximately).

**Example: Zero-padding**

You have 256 samples but want smoother spectral display. Zero-pad to 1024 points before FFT.

This doesn't improve resolution (you don't have more data), but it interpolates between bins for a smoother plot. True resolution is still $f_s / 256$.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Power-of-two lengths.** Classic radix-2 FFT requires $N = 2^m$. Mixed-radix and prime-factor FFTs handle other lengths, but power-of-two is fastest. Pad with zeros if needed.

2. **Leakage.** The FFT assumes the signal repeats. If it doesn't, you get spectral leakage — energy smears across bins. Windowing (next lesson) reduces this.

3. **Aliasing.** Frequencies above $f_s/2$ fold back. Always lowpass filter before sampling.

4. **Scaling.** FFT libraries differ in normalization. Some scale by $1/N$, some by $1/\sqrt{N}$, some don't scale. Check your library's convention.

5. **Frequency axis.** Bin 0 is DC, not the lowest negative frequency. Many plotting tools expect you to `fftshift` the output to center DC.

6. **Real vs. complex input.** Real input produces conjugate-symmetric output. Use `rfft` for real data — it returns only the non-redundant bins and runs faster.
