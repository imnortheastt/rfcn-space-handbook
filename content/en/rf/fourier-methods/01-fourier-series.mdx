<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why Fourier series?

A square wave doesn't look like a sine wave, yet your oscilloscope's FFT shows distinct frequency spikes. That's because any periodic signal — no matter how jagged — can be built from a sum of sine waves at integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.

Fourier series is the mathematical tool that breaks a periodic signal into its harmonic components. It's why we can talk about the "3rd harmonic distortion" of an amplifier or the "bandwidth" of a pulse train.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

Think of a chord on a piano. You hear one sound, but it's actually multiple notes vibrating together. Similarly, a periodic electrical signal is a superposition of pure tones (sinusoids) at the fundamental frequency and its harmonics.

The **fundamental** $f_0 = 1/T$ sets the pitch. The **harmonics** at $2f_0, 3f_0, \ldots$ add color and shape. A square wave needs many odd harmonics to form sharp edges. A triangle wave has fewer harmonics and softer edges.

The Fourier series gives you the recipe: which frequencies, and how much of each.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Fourier series representation

A periodic signal $x(t)$ with period $T$ can be written:

$$x(t) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[ a_n \cos(n\omega_0 t) + b_n \sin(n\omega_0 t) \right]$$

where $\omega_0 = 2\pi/T$ is the fundamental angular frequency.

### Fourier coefficients

$$a_0 = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T x(t)\, dt$$

$$a_n = \frac{2}{T} \int_0^T x(t) \cos(n\omega_0 t)\, dt$$

$$b_n = \frac{2}{T} \int_0^T x(t) \sin(n\omega_0 t)\, dt$$

### Complex exponential form

More compact using Euler's formula:

$$x(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{jn\omega_0 t}$$

where:

$$c_n = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T x(t) e^{-jn\omega_0 t}\, dt$$

This form is standard in RF because it handles phase naturally.

### Common signals

| Signal | Non-zero harmonics | Coefficient pattern |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Square wave | Odd only (1,3,5,...) | $\propto 1/n$ |
| Triangle wave | Odd only | $\propto 1/n^2$ |
| Sawtooth | All harmonics | $\propto 1/n$ |
| Pulse train | All harmonics | $\propto \text{sinc}(n\cdot\text{duty})$ |

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: Square wave spectrum**

A square wave of amplitude $A$ and period $T$:

$$x(t) = \frac{4A}{\pi} \left[ \sin(\omega_0 t) + \frac{1}{3}\sin(3\omega_0 t) + \frac{1}{5}\sin(5\omega_0 t) + \cdots \right]$$

The fundamental has amplitude $4A/\pi \approx 1.27A$. The 3rd harmonic is 1/3 of that. The 5th is 1/5.

To pass a square wave cleanly, your channel must handle bandwidth up to at least the 5th or 7th harmonic. A 1 MHz square wave needs ~7 MHz bandwidth for reasonable fidelity.

**Example: Harmonic distortion**

An amplifier outputs $v(t) = A_1\sin(\omega t) + A_3\sin(3\omega t)$. The third harmonic distortion (THD3) is:

$$\text{THD3} = \frac{A_3}{A_1} \times 100\%$$

Fourier analysis of the output reveals how much distortion the amplifier adds.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Periodic signals only.** Fourier series assumes the signal repeats forever. For non-periodic signals, use the Fourier transform instead.

2. **Gibbs phenomenon.** Near discontinuities (like square wave edges), the Fourier series overshoots by about 9% no matter how many terms you include. This is fundamental, not a truncation error.

3. **Convergence.** The series converges to the signal at continuous points. At discontinuities, it converges to the midpoint of the jump.

4. **DC component.** $a_0$ is the average value (DC offset). Forgetting it shifts your reconstruction.

5. **Even vs. odd symmetry.** Even functions have only cosine terms ($b_n = 0$). Odd functions have only sine terms ($a_n = 0$). Recognizing symmetry halves your work.

</section>
