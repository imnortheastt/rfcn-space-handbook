## Why Fourier series?

A square wave doesn't look like a sine wave, yet your oscilloscope's FFT shows distinct frequency spikes at odd multiples of the fundamental. A clock signal on a PCB is not a pure 100 MHz tone — it radiates at 100 MHz, 300 MHz, 500 MHz, and beyond. An amplifier fed a single sine wave might output harmonics at 2f, 3f, 4f because it is nonlinear.

Fourier series is the mathematical foundation for all of this. It says: any periodic signal — no matter how jagged, no matter how complex — can be exactly represented as a sum of sinusoids at integer multiples of the fundamental frequency. This decomposition maps the time-domain waveform to a discrete line spectrum: the signal's fingerprint in the frequency domain.

For RF and digital engineering, Fourier series is the entry point to spectrum analysis, harmonic distortion, EMI compliance, and signal integrity. You cannot reason about why a 10 MHz clock causes interference at 70 MHz without understanding harmonics. You cannot specify the bandwidth required for a pulse signal without knowing its spectral envelope. Fourier series gives you that understanding.

## Intuition

### The piano chord analogy

Think of a chord on a piano. You hear one complex sound, but it is built from multiple strings vibrating at specific frequencies — the fundamental note plus its overtones (harmonics). The resonant body of the piano shapes which harmonics are loud and which are quiet, giving each instrument its characteristic timbre.

A periodic electrical signal works the same way. The **fundamental** at $f_0 = 1/T$ is the "base note." The **harmonics** at $2f_0, 3f_0, 4f_0, \ldots$ are the overtones. The Fourier series gives you the amplitude and phase of each harmonic — the complete recipe for the signal's spectrum.

### Why edge sharpness requires high harmonics

A perfect square wave has infinitely sharp edges — vertical transitions between two voltage levels. Sharp transitions in the time domain mean energy spread across many frequencies. Specifically:

- The $n$-th harmonic of a square wave has amplitude $\propto 1/n$
- Only odd harmonics are present (1st, 3rd, 5th, ...)
- The series never terminates — you need infinitely many terms for a perfectly sharp edge

This is not a bug. It is the fundamental trade-off of digital signals: faster edges require more bandwidth. A 1 ns rise time needs bandwidth up to roughly $0.35/t_r \approx 350$ MHz. All that bandwidth is carrying the higher harmonics of the waveform.

### Orthogonality: why the decomposition works

The reason you can extract each harmonic independently is orthogonality. The sinusoids $\cos(m\omega_0 t)$ and $\cos(n\omega_0 t)$ are orthogonal over one period: their integral product is zero when $m \neq n$.

$$\int_0^T \cos(m\omega_0 t)\cos(n\omega_0 t)\,dt = \begin{cases} 0 & m \neq n \\ T/2 & m = n \end{cases}$$

This means each harmonic "lives" in its own dimension. To find the amplitude of the $n$-th harmonic, you multiply the signal by $\cos(n\omega_0 t)$ and integrate — everything else cancels. This is exactly what a spectrum analyzer does electronically with bandpass filters centered at each harmonic.

## Mechanism

### Fourier series representation

A periodic signal $x(t)$ with period $T$ can be written:

$$x(t) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[ a_n \cos(n\omega_0 t) + b_n \sin(n\omega_0 t) \right]$$

where $\omega_0 = 2\pi/T = 2\pi f_0$ is the fundamental angular frequency.

### Fourier coefficients

$$a_0 = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T x(t)\, dt \quad \text{(DC component — average value)}$$

$$a_n = \frac{2}{T} \int_0^T x(t) \cos(n\omega_0 t)\, dt \quad \text{(cosine amplitude of } n\text{-th harmonic)}$$

$$b_n = \frac{2}{T} \int_0^T x(t) \sin(n\omega_0 t)\, dt \quad \text{(sine amplitude of } n\text{-th harmonic)}$$

The amplitude of the $n$-th harmonic is $\sqrt{a_n^2 + b_n^2}$ and the phase is $\arctan(-b_n/a_n)$.

### Complex exponential form

More compact and standard in RF and communications using Euler's formula $e^{j\theta} = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta$:

$$x(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{jn\omega_0 t}$$

$$c_n = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T x(t)\, e^{-jn\omega_0 t}\, dt$$

The complex coefficients $c_n$ encode both amplitude ($|c_n|$) and phase ($\angle c_n$). The relationship to real coefficients: $c_n = (a_n - jb_n)/2$ for $n > 0$, $c_0 = a_0$, and $c_{-n} = c_n^*$ (conjugate symmetry for real signals).

The complex form generalizes directly to the Fourier transform (for non-periodic signals) and the DFT (for discrete-time periodic signals).

### Harmonic spectrum of common waveforms

| Signal | Non-zero harmonics | Coefficient $c_n$ | Notes |
|--------|-------------------|--------------------|-------|
| Square wave (50% duty) | Odd only: 1, 3, 5, ... | $2A/(jn\pi)$ for odd $n$ | $\propto 1/n$ falloff |
| Triangle wave | Odd only | $\pm 2A/(n\pi)^2$ for odd $n$ | $\propto 1/n^2$ falloff — smoother |
| Sawtooth | All: 1, 2, 3, ... | $jA/(n\pi)$ | $\propto 1/n$ falloff |
| Pulse train (duty $D$) | All | $AD\,\text{sinc}(nD)$ | Nulls at $n = 1/D, 2/D, ...$ |

The $1/n$ rolloff of square and sawtooth means their spectrum falls at $-20$ dB/decade. Triangle waves fall at $-40$ dB/decade — much more bandwidth-efficient. This is why some digital standards specify rise-time limits to reduce harmonic energy and EMI.

### Parseval's theorem

Total power in the time domain equals total power in the frequency domain:

$$P = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T |x(t)|^2\,dt = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} |c_n|^2$$

Each harmonic $c_n$ contributes $|c_n|^2$ watts to the total power. This is the basis for harmonic power measurements — each spectral line on a power analyzer represents real power in that harmonic.

## Apply

### Example 1: Square wave spectrum and bandwidth requirement

A digital clock signal: square wave, amplitude $A = 1$ V (0 to 1 V swing, so effectively $\pm 0.5$ V around 0.5 V DC), frequency $f_0 = 100$ MHz, 50% duty cycle.

Fourier series (centered at 0 V for simplicity, $A = 1$ V peak):

$$x(t) = \frac{4A}{\pi}\left[\sin(\omega_0 t) + \frac{1}{3}\sin(3\omega_0 t) + \frac{1}{5}\sin(5\omega_0 t) + \cdots\right]$$

Harmonic amplitudes:

| Harmonic | Frequency | Amplitude | Level (dBc) |
|----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| 1st (fundamental) | 100 MHz | $4/\pi \approx 1.273$ V | 0 dBc |
| 3rd | 300 MHz | $4/(3\pi) \approx 0.424$ V | -9.5 dBc |
| 5th | 500 MHz | $4/(5\pi) \approx 0.255$ V | -14.0 dBc |
| 7th | 700 MHz | $4/(7\pi) \approx 0.182$ V | -16.9 dBc |

**Bandwidth rule of thumb:** To transmit a square wave with acceptable fidelity (edge overshoot < 10%), you need at least the 5th harmonic. For a 100 MHz clock: 500 MHz minimum bandwidth. This is why a PCB trace that looks fine at DC can become an EMI radiator at 500 MHz — it is carrying the 5th harmonic of the clock.

### Example 2: Harmonic distortion in an amplifier

A power amplifier is specified for 3rd-order harmonic distortion (HD3). Input: $v_{in}(t) = 0.5\sin(2\pi \times 1\,\text{MHz} \times t)$ V. Output measured on spectrum analyzer:

- Fundamental at 1 MHz: 5.00 V
- 2nd harmonic at 2 MHz: 0.15 V
- 3rd harmonic at 3 MHz: 0.08 V

HD2 = $0.15/5.00 = 3\%$ = $-34.4$ dBc

HD3 = $0.08/5.00 = 1.6\%$ = $-36.0$ dBc

Total harmonic distortion: $\text{THD} = \sqrt{0.15^2 + 0.08^2}/5.00 \approx 3.4\%$

Fourier analysis of the output waveform directly gives you these numbers. Fourier series is the theoretical foundation for the spectrum analyzer display and for harmonic distortion specifications in amplifier datasheets.

### Example 3: Pulse train spectral envelope and duty cycle

A digital signal bus runs at 10 Mbps with pulse width $\tau = 50$ ns (duty cycle $D = \tau T = 0.5$, $T = 100$ ns, $f_0 = 10$ MHz).

The complex Fourier coefficients:

$$c_n = A D\,\text{sinc}(nD) = A \cdot 0.5 \cdot \text{sinc}(0.5n)$$

Nulls occur at $n = 1/D = 2$: the 2nd harmonic (20 MHz) has zero power for 50% duty cycle — this is why a 50% duty clock has no even harmonics, only odd.

Now consider a narrower pulse: $\tau = 10$ ns, $D = 0.1$. The first spectral null moves to $n = 10$ (100 MHz). Below that null, all harmonics from 10 MHz to 90 MHz are present. This concentrated high-frequency energy near the null causes the pulsed bus to radiate strongly up to 100 MHz — a common EMI compliance issue for high-speed PCBs.

### Example 4: Gibbs phenomenon at edges

Truncate the square wave series at $N$ terms. At the transition edge, the partial sum always overshoots by approximately 8.9% of the step height, regardless of how large $N$ is. Mathematically:

$$\lim_{N\to\infty} x_N\left(0^+\right) \approx A + 0.089 \cdot 2A = 1.178A$$

This 9% overshoot at a rising edge corresponds to the resonance of the truncated Fourier series — the stopband ripple of a brick-wall (ideal sinc) filter applied to a step. In RF systems, this manifests as:

- Ringing on a pulse passed through a bandlimited channel
- Sidelobes in a spectrum analyzer with rectangular time-domain windowing
- Antenna impedance variations near filter band edges

Windowing functions (Hann, Hamming, Blackman) in FFT analysis reduce Gibbs phenomenon at the cost of frequency resolution — an engineering trade-off directly rooted in Fourier theory.

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Fourier series is for periodic signals only.** If the signal does not repeat with a fixed period, Fourier series does not apply. Use the Fourier transform for arbitrary finite-energy signals, or the DFT/FFT for sampled data. Applying a Fourier series formula to a non-periodic signal gives nonsense.

2. **Gibbs phenomenon is not a numerical error.** The 9% overshoot near a discontinuity is a mathematical property of Fourier series truncation — it does not disappear with more terms, only the transition region narrows. This is why ideal bandlimited systems (brick-wall filters) produce ringing on pulse inputs.

3. **The DC component $a_0$ is the average value.** Forgetting it shifts the reconstructed signal. A pulse train with positive pulses only has nonzero DC offset — $a_0 = D \cdot A$ where $D$ is duty cycle. A spectrum analyzer measurement must include the DC component for correct total power.

4. **Even vs. odd symmetry halves the work.** Even signals ($x(t) = x(-t)$) have only cosine terms: $b_n = 0$. Odd signals ($x(t) = -x(-t)$) have only sine terms: $a_n = 0$. Half-wave symmetry ($x(t + T/2) = -x(t)$) eliminates all even harmonics. Recognizing these symmetries before computing coefficients saves significant effort.

5. **dBc is amplitude-relative, not power-relative.** Harmonic levels expressed in dBc are $20\log_{10}(V_n/V_1)$, not $10\log_{10}(P_n/P_1)$. Since power $\propto V^2$, both expressions equal the same in dB for equal impedances — but the factor of 20 (not 10) is the voltage ratio convention. Mixing these up produces 2× errors in dB.
