<section class="lesson-hook">

## Why codebook beamforming?

Optimal beamforming requires full channel knowledge — expensive to acquire. Codebook beamforming offers a practical alternative: pick the best beam from a predefined set. The UE evaluates candidates and reports an index. No complex CSI feedback, just a few bits.

5G NR uses codebooks extensively for FDD systems where channel reciprocity isn't available. The codebook constrains the beam space but dramatically reduces feedback overhead.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

A codebook is a menu of beams. Instead of the base station computing the perfect beam, it offers N options. The UE measures which works best and reports the selection.

Think of restaurant ordering: custom cooking takes forever, but a fixed menu with good options serves most customers quickly. Same trade-off: codebook loses some optimality for practical speed and simplicity.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## Mechanism

### Codebook definition

A codebook $\mathcal{C}$ contains $M$ predefined beamforming vectors:

$$\mathcal{C} = \{\mathbf{c}_0, \mathbf{c}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{c}_{M-1}\}$$

Each $\mathbf{c}_i$ is an N × 1 weight vector.

### DFT codebook

The canonical choice — uniformly spaced beams from the DFT matrix:

$$\mathbf{c}_m = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ e^{j\frac{2\pi m}{M}} \\ e^{j\frac{4\pi m}{M}} \\ \vdots \\ e^{j\frac{2\pi(N-1)m}{M}} \end{bmatrix}$$

Each column points to direction:

$$\sin\theta_m = \frac{\lambda}{d} \cdot \frac{m}{M}$$

With $M = N$ and $d = \lambda/2$: beams at $\theta = 0°, 7.2°, 14.5°, \ldots$ (for N = 8).

### Oversampled codebook

Use $M > N$ beams for finer angular resolution:

$$\mathbf{c}_m = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ e^{j\frac{2\pi m}{ON}} \\ \vdots \\ e^{j\frac{2\pi(N-1)m}{ON}} \end{bmatrix}$$

where O = oversampling factor (typically 2 or 4).

### Beam selection

UE measures RSRP or SINR for each beam:

$$\text{PMI} = \arg\max_m |\mathbf{h}^H \mathbf{c}_m|^2$$

Report PMI (precoding matrix indicator) to base station.

### 5G NR Type I CSI

**Single-panel codebook:**
- $N_1 \times N_2$ dual-polarized array
- Two indices: $i_1$ (wideband beam direction), $i_2$ (co-phasing between polarizations)
- DFT beams with oversampling $O_1, O_2$

**Feedback bits:**
- $i_1$: $\lceil\log_2(N_1 O_1 N_2 O_2)\rceil$ bits (wideband)
- $i_2$: 2 bits per subband (QPSK co-phasing)

### Quantization loss

Best codebook beam vs. optimal matched filter:

$$\text{Loss} = 10\log_{10}\left(\frac{|\mathbf{h}^H\mathbf{c}_{PMI}|^2}{|\mathbf{h}^H\mathbf{h}|}\right)$$

Average loss with M beams covering ±60°:
- M = 8: ~1.2 dB
- M = 16: ~0.6 dB
- M = 32: ~0.3 dB

### Hierarchical codebooks

Search efficiently with multi-resolution:

1. **Wide beams** (level 1): Cover full sector, identify rough direction
2. **Narrow beams** (level 2): Refine within the winning wide beam
3. **Fine beams** (level 3): Final precise alignment

5G NR: SSB uses wide beams (8–64), CSI-RS uses narrow refinement beams.

### Codebook rank

Multi-layer transmission uses codebook matrices:

- Rank 1: Single beam vector
- Rank 2: Two orthogonal beam vectors (spatial multiplexing)
- Rank 4+: Combinations from codebook structure

</section>

<section class="lesson-apply">

## Apply

**Example: 8-beam DFT codebook**

8 elements, $d = \lambda/2$, 8-beam DFT codebook.

Beam directions: $\theta = \arcsin(m/8)$ for m = 0, 1, ..., 7.

θ = [0°, 7.2°, 14.5°, 22.0°, 30°, 38.7°, 48.6°, 90°] (endfire).

**Example: PMI selection**

Channel: $\mathbf{h} = \mathbf{a}(25°)$ (signal from 25°).

Codebook powers:
- $|\mathbf{h}^H\mathbf{c}_3|^2$ (22°): 7.8
- $|\mathbf{h}^H\mathbf{c}_4|^2$ (30°): 7.6
- Others: lower

PMI = 3 selected. Loss vs. matched filter: ~0.3 dB.

**Example: 5G NR feedback**

16 × 2 dual-pol array, O = 4 oversampling.

Wideband beam index: $\log_2(16 \times 4 \times 2) = 7$ bits.

Co-phasing per subband: 2 bits × 20 subbands = 40 bits.

Total: 47 bits per report (vs. full CSI: thousands of bits).

**Example: Beam tracking**

UE moving at 30 km/h at 28 GHz.

Beam coherence time: ~10 ms.

Hierarchical search: level 1 (8 wide) every 20 ms, level 2 (refinement) every 5 ms.

Overhead: ~5% of resources for beam management.

</section>

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Codebook mismatch.** DFT codebook assumes ULA. Real arrays have coupling, failures, calibration errors.

2. **Angular quantization.** Codebook covers discrete angles. Signal between beams sees ~3 dB loss at half-power points.

3. **Rank mismatch.** Codebook rank should match channel rank. Over-ranking degrades SINR; under-ranking wastes capacity.

4. **Multi-path channels.** DFT beams assume single dominant path. Rich scattering spreads energy across beams.

5. **Outdated feedback.** By the time feedback reaches BS, channel may have changed. Fast fading needs faster reports.

6. **Interference unawareness.** Codebook selection maximizes signal, not SINR. Doesn't null interference optimally.

</section>
