## Why this matters

"How fast is this satellite moving right now?" The vis-viva equation answers in one step — no angular momentum, no time-of-flight math, just current position and orbit size.

Every delta-v calculation you will ever do for a Hohmann transfer, a GTO insertion, or an interplanetary departure starts here. Starlink, GPS, Ariane 5, the Voyager probes — all designed with vis-viva at the core. It is the single most useful formula in orbital mechanics.

</section>

<section class="lesson-intuition">

## Intuition

### The equation

$$v^2 = \mu \left(\frac{2}{r} - \frac{1}{a}\right)$$

Where:
- $v$ = orbital speed at current position [km/s]
- $\mu$ = gravitational parameter = $GM$ [km³/s²]
- $r$ = current distance from central body center [km]
- $a$ = semi-major axis of the orbit [km]

For Earth: $\mu = 398600.4418$ km³/s²

### Energy perspective

The vis-viva equation is just conservation of energy restated.

Specific orbital energy $\varepsilon$ (energy per unit mass) has two parts:

$$\varepsilon = \underbrace{\frac{v^2}{2}}_{\text{kinetic}} - \underbrace{\frac{\mu}{r}}_{\text{potential}} = -\frac{\mu}{2a} = \text{constant}$$

The total energy is fixed by the semi-major axis $a$ alone. Rearranging:

$$\frac{v^2}{2} = \frac{\mu}{r} - \frac{\mu}{2a} = \mu\left(\frac{1}{r} - \frac{1}{2a}\right)$$

Double both sides and you have vis-viva.

```
Energy Trade-off Along an Ellipse:

     Apoapsis (slow)                Periapsis (fast)
          ○                               ●
     KE low, PE high              KE high, PE low
     v_a ≈ 1.6 km/s               v_p ≈ 10.2 km/s
     (GTO example)                (GTO example)

     Total ε = -μ/2a = constant throughout
```

### Orbit type from energy sign

| Energy $\varepsilon$ | Semi-major axis $a$ | Orbit type |
|---|---|---|
| $\varepsilon < 0$ | $a > 0$ | Ellipse (bound) |
| $\varepsilon = 0$ | $a \to \infty$ | Parabola (marginally unbound) |
| $\varepsilon > 0$ | $a < 0$ | Hyperbola (escape trajectory) |

### Speed variation around an ellipse

At periapsis ($r = r_p = a(1-e)$) and apoapsis ($r = r_a = a(1+e)$), velocity is purely tangential, so:

$$v_p = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{a} \cdot \frac{1+e}{1-e}} \qquad v_a = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{a} \cdot \frac{1-e}{1+e}}$$

Their ratio:

$$\frac{v_p}{v_a} = \frac{1+e}{1-e} = \frac{r_a}{r_p}$$

For GTO ($r_p = 6678$ km, $r_a = 42164$ km): ratio = 6.3 — perigee is more than 6× faster than apogee.

</section>

<section class="lesson-mechanism">

## How it works

### Derivation from energy conservation

Start with specific mechanical energy, constant throughout the orbit:

$$\varepsilon = \frac{v^2}{2} - \frac{\mu}{r} = \text{const}$$

Evaluate at periapsis where $r = a(1-e)$ and $v = v_p$. Using angular momentum conservation ($h = r_p v_p$ at periapsis where velocity is perpendicular):

$$h = \sqrt{\mu a (1-e^2)}$$

$$v_p = \frac{h}{r_p} = \frac{\sqrt{\mu a(1-e^2)}}{a(1-e)} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu(1+e)}{a(1-e)}}$$

Therefore:

$$\varepsilon = \frac{v_p^2}{2} - \frac{\mu}{r_p} = \frac{\mu(1+e)}{2a(1-e)} - \frac{\mu}{a(1-e)} = -\frac{\mu}{2a}$$

Since $\varepsilon$ is constant, at any other point:

$$\frac{v^2}{2} - \frac{\mu}{r} = -\frac{\mu}{2a}$$

$$\boxed{v^2 = \mu\left(\frac{2}{r} - \frac{1}{a}\right)}$$

### Special cases

**Circular orbit** ($r = a$ everywhere):

$$v_c = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{r}}$$

ISS at $r = 6778$ km: $v_c = \sqrt{398600/6778} = 7.667$ km/s

**Escape velocity** (parabola, $a \to \infty$, $\varepsilon = 0$):

$$v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2\mu}{r}} = \sqrt{2} \cdot v_c$$

From Earth's surface: $v_{esc} = \sqrt{2 \times 398600 / 6378} = 11.18$ km/s

**Hyperbolic excess speed** ($v_\infty$):

As $r \to \infty$ on a hyperbola: $v^2 \to -\mu/a = v_\infty^2$.

At any radius: $v^2 = v_{esc}^2 + v_\infty^2$

This is the C3 metric used in interplanetary mission design ($C_3 = v_\infty^2$ in km²/s²).

### Delta-v calculations via vis-viva

Every orbital maneuver is a delta-v: the velocity change between two orbits at the same point.

**Hohmann transfer example — LEO to GEO:**

Transfer orbit semi-major axis: $a_t = (r_{LEO} + r_{GEO})/2 = (6778 + 42164)/2 = 24471$ km

Burn 1 at $r = r_{LEO} = 6778$ km:
- Current circular: $v_1 = \sqrt{398600/6778} = 7.667$ km/s
- Transfer orbit at periapsis: $v_{t,p} = \sqrt{398600(2/6778 - 1/24471)} = 10.10$ km/s
- $\Delta v_1 = 10.10 - 7.667 = 2.43$ km/s

Burn 2 at $r = r_{GEO} = 42164$ km:
- Transfer orbit at apoapsis: $v_{t,a} = \sqrt{398600(2/42164 - 1/24471)} = 1.63$ km/s
- GEO circular: $v_2 = \sqrt{398600/42164} = 3.07$ km/s
- $\Delta v_2 = 3.07 - 1.63 = 1.44$ km/s

Total $\Delta v = 2.43 + 1.44 = 3.87$ km/s to go from LEO to GEO.

```
Delta-v Budget: LEO → GEO Hohmann

  r_LEO = 6,778 km   v = 7.667 km/s   → Δv₁ = +2.43 km/s
                            │
                    Transfer orbit
                    a = 24,471 km
                            │
  r_GEO = 42,164 km  v = 1.63 km/s   → Δv₂ = +1.44 km/s
                            ↓
                    GEO v = 3.07 km/s

  Total Δv = 3.87 km/s
```

### Energy and orbit size

Two orbits with the same semi-major axis have identical energy and period — regardless of eccentricity. A circular orbit at $a = 42164$ km and a highly elliptical orbit with $r_p = 1000$ km, $r_a = 83328$ km both have the same energy and same 24-hour period.

This has a practical consequence: to raise/lower your orbit, you must change energy, which means changing $a$. Plane changes, by contrast, rotate the velocity vector without changing speed (if done at the right point), so they don't change energy or $a$.

## Apply

**Complete vis-viva toolkit:**

```python
import numpy as np

MU_EARTH = 398600.4418  # km³/s²
R_EARTH = 6378.137      # km

def vis_viva(r, a, mu=MU_EARTH):
    """
    Orbital speed at radius r for orbit with semi-major axis a.
    r, a in km; returns km/s.
    a < 0 for hyperbola.
    """
    return np.sqrt(mu * (2/r - 1/a))

def circular_velocity(r, mu=MU_EARTH):
    return np.sqrt(mu / r)

def escape_velocity(r, mu=MU_EARTH):
    return np.sqrt(2 * mu / r)

def specific_energy(a, mu=MU_EARTH):
    """Specific orbital energy in km²/s²."""
    return -mu / (2 * a)

def periapsis_velocity(a, e, mu=MU_EARTH):
    return np.sqrt(mu / a * (1 + e) / (1 - e))

def apoapsis_velocity(a, e, mu=MU_EARTH):
    return np.sqrt(mu / a * (1 - e) / (1 + e))

def hohmann_dv(r1, r2, mu=MU_EARTH):
    """
    Total delta-v for Hohmann transfer between circular orbits.
    Returns (dv1, dv2, dv_total) in km/s.
    """
    a_t = (r1 + r2) / 2
    # Burn 1: circular orbit 1 → transfer periapsis
    v1_circ = circular_velocity(r1, mu)
    v_tp    = vis_viva(r1, a_t, mu)
    dv1 = v_tp - v1_circ
    # Burn 2: transfer apoapsis → circular orbit 2
    v_ta    = vis_viva(r2, a_t, mu)
    v2_circ = circular_velocity(r2, mu)
    dv2 = v2_circ - v_ta
    return dv1, dv2, dv1 + dv2

# --- Common orbits ---
print("=== Orbital Velocities ===")
orbits = [
    ("LEO (ISS, 400 km)",  R_EARTH + 400),
    ("MEO (GPS, 20180 km)", R_EARTH + 20180),
    ("GEO (35786 km)",     R_EARTH + 35786),
]
for name, r in orbits:
    vc  = circular_velocity(r)
    ve  = escape_velocity(r)
    eps = specific_energy(r)  # circular: a = r
    print(f"{name}")
    print(f"  v_circ = {vc:.3f} km/s  v_esc = {ve:.3f} km/s  ε = {eps:.1f} km²/s²")

# --- GTO worked example ---
r_p = R_EARTH + 300    # 300 km perigee
r_a = R_EARTH + 35786  # GEO apogee
a   = (r_p + r_a) / 2
e   = (r_a - r_p) / (r_a + r_p)
print(f"\n=== GTO (300 km × GEO) ===")
print(f"  a = {a:.0f} km,  e = {e:.4f}")
print(f"  v_perigee = {vis_viva(r_p, a):.3f} km/s")
print(f"  v_apogee  = {vis_viva(r_a, a):.3f} km/s")

# --- Hohmann deltas ---
dv1, dv2, total = hohmann_dv(R_EARTH + 400, R_EARTH + 35786)
print(f"\n=== LEO (400 km) → GEO Hohmann ===")
print(f"  Δv₁ = {dv1:.3f} km/s  (LEO → GTO perigee)")
print(f"  Δv₂ = {dv2:.3f} km/s  (GTO apogee → GEO)")
print(f"  Total Δv = {total:.3f} km/s")
```

Expected output:
```

  v_circ = 7.667 km/s  v_esc = 10.843 km/s  ε = -29.4 km²/s²
```

<section class="lesson-pitfalls">

## Pitfalls

1. **Sign of $a$ for hyperbolas.** Hyperbolic trajectories have $a < 0$, so $-1/a > 0$. Vis-viva still gives a real positive $v$ — do not take the absolute value of $a$ before plugging in.

2. **Radius vs altitude.** $r$ is measured from Earth's center: $r = R_E + h$. Using altitude $h$ directly gives velocities off by ~15%.

3. **Escape is not "v=0 now".** Escape velocity means the object reaches $r \to \infty$ with $v \to 0$. At the launch point you still need $v_{esc}$ — it is the minimum speed to leave the gravitational well from that radius.

4. **$v_{esc} = \sqrt{2} \cdot v_c$.** This ratio holds exactly at any radius for a spherical body. Useful sanity check.

5. **Units.** $\mu_{Earth} = 398600.4418$ km³/s² with $r, a$ in km gives $v$ in km/s. Mixing SI (m, m³/s²) with km silently produces wrong answers.

6. **Vis-viva assumes Keplerian (unpowered) motion.** During a burn, the satellite is not on a Keplerian orbit. Calculate the vis-viva velocity at the start and end of each burn, treating each as an impulsive delta-v at a single point.

7. **Same $a$ ≠ same orbit.** Two orbits with identical semi-major axes have the same energy and period but differ in eccentricity, inclination, and geometry. Vis-viva tells you speed, not direction.

8. **Delta-v from vis-viva is a scalar difference.** For a Hohmann burn at periapsis or apoapsis, velocity vectors are parallel, so $\Delta v = |v_{new} - v_{old}|$. For general burns at other points, the vectors are not aligned and you must use the cosine rule: $\Delta v = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 - 2v_1 v_2 \cos\theta}$.
